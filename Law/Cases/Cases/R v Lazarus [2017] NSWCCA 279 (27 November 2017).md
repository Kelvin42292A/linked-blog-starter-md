Court of Criminal Appeal

## Material Facts

- The respondent (Lazarus) was found **not guilty** of sexual intercourse without consent in a judge-alone re-trial.
- When giving her oral reasons, the trial judge accidentally omitted the word "not" when discussing the law on intoxication. She later revised her written reasons to add the word "NOT" after a defence solicitor pointed out the error.
- The Crown appealed the acquittal, arguing the judge impermissibly altered her judgment after the fact and failed to apply the correct laws regarding the assessment of consent.

## Issues

- Whether the judge **impermissibly altered her judgment** by adding the word "NOT", effectively changing whether she took the accused's self-induced intoxication into account.
- Whether the trial judge erred by **failing to consider the "steps taken"** by the accused to ascertain if the complainant was consenting.
- If an error occurred, whether the interests of justice required ordering the respondent to face a **third trial**.

## Rule/Law

- Under **s 61HA(3)(e)** of the _Crimes Act 1900_ (NSW), a judge **must not** consider an accused's self-induced intoxication when deciding if they had reasonable grounds to believe the complainant was consenting.
- Under **s 61HA(3)(d)**, a judge **must** consider "any steps taken" by the accused to find out if the other person consents. A "step" means taking some positive physical or mental act to determine consent.
- A judge is permitted to revise their reasons to fix a "slip" or mistake so it reflects what they actually meant, but they **cannot alter the substance** of the legal reasoning.

## Application/Analysis

- The Court ruled the addition of "NOT" was allowed. Looking at the whole trial, the judge repeatedly stated the correct law on intoxication; omitting the word orally was a genuine slip, not a substantive legal error.
- The trial judge made a clear legal error by failing to mention or apply **s 61HA(3)(d)**. A judge must explicitly show they considered the "steps taken" by the accused; simply summarizing the facts of the night is not enough to satisfy this mandatory legal requirement,.
- Even though the judge made a legal error, the Court refused to order a re-trial. It would be **oppressive and unfair** to make the respondent face a third trial for an event from 2013, especially since the legal errors were not his fault and he had already served 10 months in jail before his first conviction was overturned.

## Conclusion

Appeal dismissed. No retrial. While the trial judge did make an error regarding the "steps taken" to ascertain consent, the Court used its discretion to clear the respondent, deciding that a third trial was not in the interests of justice.
