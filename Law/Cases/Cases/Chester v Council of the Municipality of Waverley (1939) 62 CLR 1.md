High Court of Australia

## Material Facts

- The defendant Council excavated a trench in a road under its control. Over the weekend, it filled with water ranging from 2.5 to 7 feet deep.
- The trench was guarded by a railing, but it was inadequate and children could easily get under it.
- The plaintiff’s 7.5-year-old son accidentally fell into the trench while playing and drowned.
- The child's body was not found until several hours later, after the plaintiff (his mother) had been frantically searching for him.
- The plaintiff was present when her son’s dead body was pulled from the trench and she suffered a severe, prolonged nervous shock (more than just temporary fright or distress).
- The plaintiff sued the Council for damages for negligence. The trial judge and Full Court of NSW rejected her claim, and she appealed to the High Court.

## Issues

1. **Duty of Care:** Did the Council owe a legal duty of care directly to the mother, as opposed to just the child using the road?
2. **Reasonable Foreseeability:** Is a mother suffering severe nervous shock from seeing her child's dead body hours after an accident a "reasonably foreseeable" consequence of the Council negligently leaving a trench unguarded?

## Rule/Law

- **Duty of Care & Foreseeability (_Donoghue v Stevenson_):** A person owes a duty of care only to those who are so "closely and directly affected" by their act that they ought reasonably to have them in contemplation. To determine if an act is negligent, you must ask if a reasonable person would foresee that the act would cause damage.
- **Reasonable vs. Fantastic Possibilities (_Fardon v Harcourt-Rivington_):** When fulfilling a duty of care, people must guard against "reasonable probabilities," but they are not legally bound to guard against "fantastic possibilities". The law only requires you to foresee things within the ordinary range of human experience.
- **Liability for Nervous Shock (_Hambrook v Stokes_):** A negligent defendant can be held liable for causing severe nervous shock to a mother, but historically this is limited to cases where the shock was due to a reasonable fear of _immediate_ personal injury to herself or her children.

## Application/Analysis

- **No Reasonable Foreseeability:** The majority of the High Court ruled that while the Council was negligent toward the child, they could not reasonably foresee the mother's severe nervous shock. The judges stated that while death is distressing, it is not a "common experience of mankind" for a relative to suffer a permanent, severe physical illness simply from seeing a dead body hours after an accident. Therefore, her reaction was a "fantastic possibility" rather than a reasonable probability.
- **Not Directly Affected (Drawing the Line):** The mother was not using the road and did not actually witness the accident happen. The Court ruled that her injury was only "indirectly connected" to the unguarded trench. The law must fix a point where it stops handing out compensation, otherwise, the Council could theoretically be sued by any relative, friend, or bystander who felt sick after seeing the body.
- **The Famous Dissent (Evatt J):** _Note for your exam:_ Justice Evatt wrote a famous dissenting judgment, arguing the exact opposite. He stated that any reasonable person leaving a deadly trench in a residential area _would_ foresee that a child might fall in, and that the child's mother would desperately search for them and suffer severe nervous shock upon finding them dead.

## Conclusion

The appeal was **dismissed** by a 3:1 majority. The High Court ruled that the Council owed no duty of care to the mother and was **not liable** to pay her damages for her nervous shock.
