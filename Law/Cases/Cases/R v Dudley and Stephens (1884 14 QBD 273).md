### Material Facts

- The defendants, **Thomas Dudley and Edwin Stephens**, along with a man named Brooks and a 17-year-old cabin boy named **Richard Parker**, were stranded in an open lifeboat in the middle of the ocean after their yacht, the _Mignonette_, sank in a storm.
- The crew drifted for **nearly three weeks with virtually no sustenance**. After their meager provisions (two tins of turnips and a caught turtle) ran out, they spent several days with absolutely no food or fresh water.
- The cabin boy, Parker, was the weakest among them and had fallen severely ill after drinking seawater.
- Believing they would all die otherwise, Dudley and Stephens suggested that one person should be sacrificed to save the rest. Brooks dissented from this plan.
- On the 20th day, with Stephens' assent, **Dudley deliberately killed the unresisting cabin boy**.
- The three surviving men fed upon the boy's body and blood for four days until they were rescued by a passing ship.
- Upon their return to England, Dudley and Stephens were charged with **willful murder**.

### Issues

- **Can the extreme necessity of starvation and the imminent threat of death justify the killing of an innocent person?**
- Does the law recognize necessity as a valid legal defense to a charge of murder?

### Rule

- **Necessity is not a valid legal defense to a charge of murder.**
- The common law does not permit a person to deliberately take an innocent life in order to preserve their own.

### Application

- Chief Justice Lord Coleridge reasoned that while the men were in a desperate and terrible situation, legal duty and morality cannot be separated. The law recognizes that **a person may sometimes have a moral and legal duty to sacrifice their own life rather than take the life of an innocent**.
- The court emphasized the dangerous precedent a necessity defense would set. It raises impossible questions: Who is qualified to judge the necessity? How does one choose the victim? By what measure is one life deemed more valuable or expendable than another?
- There was no legal or moral principle that justified valuing the lives of the defendants over the life of the cabin boy. Allowing such a defense would leave the definition of "necessity" open to subjective abuse and could provide a legal cloak for horrific crimes under the guise of survival.
- Because the killing of Richard Parker was a deliberate act against an innocent who posed no direct threat to them, the legal requirements for murder were satisfied without any valid defense.

### Conclusion

The men were found guilty of murder. They were formally sentenced to the mandatory penalty of death. However, recognizing the extreme circumstances, the Crown later exercised its prerogative of mercy and commuted their sentence to six months' imprisonment.
