## Material Facts

- **The Lawsuit:** The plaintiff sued the defendant, Governor Eyre of Jamaica, in an English court for assault and false imprisonment.
- **The Defense:** Governor Eyre pleaded that the acts occurred during a rebellion and that the Jamaican Legislature had subsequently passed an **Act of Indemnity** making his actions lawful and indemnifying him.
- **The Dispute:** The plaintiff argued that this local Jamaican law should not be given extra-territorial effect to block an action in England.

## Issues

What is the choice of law rule for wrongs committed abroad, and can a defendant rely on a local law of indemnity as a defense when sued in a foreign court?

## Rule/Law

To bring a successful lawsuit for a wrong committed abroad, the plaintiff must satisfy a **two-part test**:

1. **Actionability in the Forum:** The wrong must be of such a character that it would have been actionable if committed in the country of the court hearing the case (e.g., England).
2. **No Justification Abroad:** The act must **not have been justifiable** by the law of the place where it was actually done (_lex loci actus_). An act is legally "justified" if it is neither civilly actionable nor criminally punishable under the local law.

## Application/Analysis

- **Actionable in England:** Assault and false imprisonment are standard torts that are fully actionable in England, satisfying the first arm of the rule.
- **Justified in Jamaica:** Because the Jamaican Legislature passed an Act of Indemnity declaring the Governor's acts lawful, his actions were **legally justified** in Jamaica.
- **Focus on the Act, Not the Injury:** The second arm of the rule is governed by the law of the place of the _physical act_ (_lex loci actus_) rather than where the injury was ultimately felt (_lex loci delicti_). Because the act itself was legally excused where it occurred, the second arm of the test was not met.

## Conclusion

The court held that because Governor Eyre had a valid defense under Jamaican law, he **could not be found liable** in an English court.

### Judges Rules

- **The Double-Actionability Threshold:** To sue someone locally for a civil wrong committed in another country, the conduct must be **both** a recognized civil wrong in your home court and legally indefensible in the place where it occurred.
- **Legal Excuse Clears Liability:** If the government of the country where an act was committed officially immunizes or declares that act lawful (such as through an Act of Indemnity), the actor **cannot be sued for it in a foreign court**.
- **Place of the Act Governs Legality:** Whether an action is "justifiable" is judged strictly by the law of the **exact territory where the physical act occurred** (_lex loci actus_), not where the resulting damage or injury took place.
- **Moral Wrongs Are Not Enough:** "Unjustifiable" means legally actionable or punishable. An act that is only morally wrong under local foreign law, but carries no legal penalty or civil liability there, is considered legally "justified" and cannot be sued upon.
