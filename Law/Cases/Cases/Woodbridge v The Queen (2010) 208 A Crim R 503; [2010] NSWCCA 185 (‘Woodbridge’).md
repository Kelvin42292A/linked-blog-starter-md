## Material Facts

Woodbridge drove with a very high blood alcohol level (0.269), crashed head-on into another car, and killed a passenger. She claimed she had a mental blackout (a dissociative state) triggered by an abusive phone call from her ex-husband, meaning her driving was involuntary.

## Issues

Should the trial judge have let the jury consider the defence of "sane automatism" (acting involuntarily with a healthy mind)?

## Rule/Law

Sane automatism is a healthy mind reacting to an extreme external shock. If a blackout is caused by an underlying mental illness or a condition prone to happen again, it is legally "insane automatism" (a disease of the mind).

## Application/Analysis

Woodbridge's blackout was caused by her pre-existing mental health disorders. An abusive phone call is not the kind of extraordinary shock that would cause a normal, healthy mind to black out.

## Conclusion

The appeal was dismissed because the judge was correct to block the sane automatism defence.

### Judges Rules

- **Rule 1: Sane vs. Insane Automatism** Sane automatism is a healthy mind reacting to an extreme external shock (like a physical blow). Insane automatism is an unhealthy mind reacting because of an underlying mental weakness.
- **Rule 2: What is a "Disease of the Mind"** A "disease of the mind" includes any temporary mental disorder or breakdown that is prone to happen again (recur).
- **Rule 3: Judges Decide the Law** Whether a mental condition legally counts as a "disease of the mind" is a question of law for the judge to decide, not for medical experts.
- **Rule 4: Standard to Raise the Defence** A judge will only let a jury consider sane automatism if the defendant points to real evidence showing a reasonable possibility that their actions were completely involuntary.
