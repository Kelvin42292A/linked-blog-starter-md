Victorian Court of Appeal

## Material Facts

- The complainant (a **13-year-old girl**) stayed overnight at the house of the applicant, who was her grandfather.
- After drinking vodka, she became very sick with stomach cramps, vomited, and went to bed.
- She was screaming in pain and asked the applicant to help, so he rubbed her stomach to ease the cramps.
- In her initial evidence, she claimed he put his hand under her underpants and put his fingers inside her vagina.
- However, during cross-examination, she agreed with her earlier statements that **he did not put his fingers into her vagina**.
- The applicant admitted rubbing her stomach but completely denied penetrating her vagina, stating that if he had touched her there, it was entirely an accident.

## Issues

- Did the trial judge make a fundamental mistake by failing to instruct the jury that the act of penetration had to be **intentional**?
- Was the evidence actually strong enough for a jury to conclude beyond a reasonable doubt that any penetration occurred?

## Rule/Law

- Under the _Crimes Act 1958 (Vic)_, a person commits incest if they take part in sexual penetration with a lineal descendant.
- The act of sexual penetration must be a **voluntary and intentional** (or willed) act. Intent is a fundamental element of the crime that the prosecution must prove beyond a reasonable doubt.
- A trial judge must instruct the jury on all essential elements of an offence. Failing to direct a jury on "intent" can cause a substantial miscarriage of justice.

## Application/Analysis

- The trial judge made a major error by only telling the jury they needed to find two things (penetration and the grandfather-granddaughter relationship) to convict him. The judge completely forgot to tell the jury that the act had to be intentional. Because the grandfather's entire defense was that any touching was a pure accident, this missing instruction ruined his chance at a fair trial.
- The appeal court found that there was not enough reliable evidence to prove penetration happened at all. Because the victim contradicted herself and agreed in cross-examination that he did not put his fingers inside her, a reasonable jury could not have found him guilty beyond a reasonable doubt.

## Conclusion

The appeal was **allowed**, the conviction was **quashed** (thrown out), and a **new trial was ordered**.
