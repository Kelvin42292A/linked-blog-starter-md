New South Wales Court of Criminal Appeal

## Material Facts

- The applicant (Edwards) entered an ATM room with her three-year-old son while the victim was withdrawing $200.
- The applicant distracted the victim by saying, "Look at my boy, isn't he beautiful?" and then grabbed the money from his hand, squeezing it and causing him pain.
- She then yelled for help and falsely told attending police that the victim had tried to grab her son.
- In October 2006, the sentencing judge imposed a two-year prison sentence (with a 15-month non-parole period) for stealing from the person, but suspended it upon the applicant entering into a good behaviour bond.
- The applicant had "very strong" subjective circumstances, including having seven children, being pregnant, a history of drug dependence, and a developmental disability.
- The applicant subsequently breached the bond by committing further offences (including larceny and shoplifting) and failing to report.
- In December 2008, the court revoked the bond and ordered her to serve the suspended sentence. The applicant sought an extension of time (over two years late) to appeal the original 2006 sentence, and leave to appeal the 2008 order.

## Issues

- Should an appellate court grant an extension of time to appeal an original suspended sentence years after it was imposed, just because the offender has now breached the bond?.
- Did the sentencing judge violate the _De Simoni_ principle by taking into account the "violence offered" (squeezing the hand), effectively sentencing her for the more serious uncharged offence of robbery?.
- Is an offender automatically entitled to credit for pre-sentence custody if they were held on remand for multiple different offences?.

## Rule/Law

- **s 94 _Crimes Act 1900_:** The offence of stealing from the person, which carries a maximum penalty of 14 years' imprisonment.
- **_The Queen v De Simoni_:** A fundamental sentencing principle that a judge cannot take into account circumstances of aggravation that would constitute a separate, more serious offence than the one charged.
- **_R v Finney_ & _Huntingdon v R_ (Pre-sentence Custody):** There is no obligation for a sentencing judge to give automatic credit for time spent in pre-sentence custody if that custody was not wholly referable to the specific offence for which the offender is being sentenced.
- **Appealing Suspended Sentences:** The Court discourages a "two-stage approach" where offenders accept a lenient suspended sentence without complaint at trial, holding appeal arguments in reserve to activate only if they later breach the bond.

## Application/Analysis

- **No _De Simoni_ Breach:** Both "robbery" and "stealing from the person" are offences under s 94 and carry the exact same maximum penalty. The Court noted that stealing from the person is a variant of robbery (rather than a variant of simple larceny) and inherently involves the potential for personal confrontation and force. Therefore, noting that the applicant squeezed the victim's hand did not constitute sentencing her for a more serious crime.
- **Aggravating Factors & Subjective Case:** The judge gave adequate weight to the applicant's intellectual disability and drug issues. However, the objective seriousness of the crime was elevated because she used her three-year-old child as a decoy to facilitate the theft and made a patently false, serious allegation to the police to fend off arrest.
- **Pre-Sentence Custody Credit:** The applicant claimed she was unfairly denied credit for 17 days of pre-sentence custody. The Court rejected this because during that period, she was refused bail on a number of offences, not just the ATM theft. Thus, she had no automatic legal entitlement to credit for those specific days.
- **Summary Disposal:** The applicant argued the sentence was excessive because the matter could have been dealt with in the Local Court. The Court dismissed this, noting the Crown elected to proceed on indictment due to the objective seriousness of the crime and her criminal history, making it inappropriate to reduce the sentence simply based on a theoretical possibility of summary disposal.

## Conclusion

The extension of time to seek leave to appeal the 2006 sentence was **refused**. Leave to appeal the 2008 sentence was also **refused**. The Court found no error in the original sentence or its subsequent implementation following the breach.
