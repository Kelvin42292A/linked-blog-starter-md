## Material Facts

- **The Parties and Charge:** An Aboriginal man named Ballard (also referred to as "Dirty Dick") was committed for trial for the murder of Robert Barrett, another Aboriginal man, during a fight between their tribes near Sydney.
- **The Legal Question:** The Attorney General asked the Supreme Court of New South Wales for direction on whether an Aboriginal person could be prosecuted under English law for a murder committed against another Aboriginal person.
- **The Historical Practice:** Since the settlement of the Colony, New South Wales courts and the government had never interfered in disputes or quarrels confined solely among the Aboriginal people.

## Issues

Does the Supreme Court of New South Wales have legal jurisdiction to try and punish an Aboriginal person for an offence committed against another Aboriginal person?

## Rule/Law

- **Limited Jurisdiction:** The Supreme Court has jurisdiction over disputes and wrongs committed between Aborigines and Europeans, but it lacks jurisdiction when the only parties involved are Aborigines.
- **No Natural Supremacy:** Aboriginal people are entitled to their own laws and institutions. They owe no fealty to the English, and the English have no natural claim of supremacy or acknowledgment over them.
- **The Requirement of Consent:** Until Aboriginal people consent—either actually or by implication—to the interposition of English law, there is no legal reason or justification to interfere with their customary institutions.

## Application/Analysis

- **Tribal State:** The accused and the deceased were both living in a state of natural, uncontrolled freedom.
- **Sufficient Customary Systems:** Aboriginal tribes make their own laws, which they preserve and act upon. They decide their differences using a principle of retaliation, which is perfectly suited to their dispositions and is productive of as much good for them as any novel or strange English institution.
- **Impracticability of English Law:** It is unjust to apply the technical English legal system to a people who are entirely unacquainted with English laws, customs, and language.
- **Limits of Municipal Law:** While English law applies to protect both parties in transactions _between_ British settlers and Aboriginal people, no principle of municipal or national law subjects indigenous inhabitants to the laws of the settlers for disputes confined strictly among themselves.

## Conclusion

The Supreme Court ruled that it **lacked jurisdiction** over the case, and the prisoner was **discharged.**

### Judges Rules

Colonial courts do not have criminal jurisdiction over offences committed solely between indigenous people according to their own customary laws. Because they have not consented to English sovereignty, owe no fealty to the Crown, and have their own functional traditional systems of justice, they must be left to govern their internal affairs without British legal interference.
