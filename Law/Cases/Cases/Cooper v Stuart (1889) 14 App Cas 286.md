Privy Council

## Material Facts

- **The Crown Grant:** In 1823, the Governor of New South Wales granted 1400 acres of land in Sydney to William Hutchinson. The grant included a reservation allowing the Crown to resume up to 10 acres of the land if needed for "public purposes".
- **The Resumption:** In 1882, the Governor of the Colony acted on this reservation and resumed 10 acres of the land to create a public park.
- **The Lawsuit:** Cooper, who had succeeded Hutchinson as the owner of the land, sued the government. He argued that the reservation was invalid because it was a "repugnant" exception to the grant and because it violated the common law rule against perpetuities.

## Issues

1. **The Exception Issue:** Did the Crown's right to resume the land constitute a void "exception" because it was repugnant to the overall grant?
2. **The Perpetuities Issue:** Did the English common law rule against perpetuities apply to Crown grants in New South Wales in 1823, rendering the reservation void?

## Rule/Law

- **Exceptions vs. Defeasance:** A legal "exception" instantly excludes part of the land from passing to the buyer. A "defeasance" allows the whole land to pass to the buyer immediately, but subject to a condition that might take back a portion of it in the future.
- **The Doctrine of Reception:** When English subjects settle a practically unoccupied territory, they carry English common law with them, but only to the extent that those laws are "applicable to the condition of an infant Colony". Laws that are unsuitable or inconvenient for a young colony do not apply from the outset.
- **The Rule Against Perpetuities:** The rule against perpetuities (which prevents property from being tied up indefinitely) applies to transactions between private citizens in New South Wales. However, it does not apply to Crown reservations made in the public interest if doing so would harm the development of a young colony.

## Application/Analysis

- **The Defeasance Succeeds:** The reservation was not a void exception because it did not immediately exclude the 10 acres. Instead, the entire 1400 acres passed to the grantee in 1823, subject to a future "defeasance" if the Crown ever needed to resume 10 acres for the public.
- **Unsuitability of the Rule:** The Privy Council decided that applying the rule against perpetuities to Crown grants in 1823 was entirely unsuitable for a young colony. It is impossible to foresee what land will be needed for public uses (such as roads or parks) before immigrants arrive.
- **Public Policy Prevails:** Preventing the Crown from resuming land for the public good would defeat the government's duty to manage and settle the colony. Therefore, the rule against perpetuities did not apply to the Crown's reservation in 1823.

## Conclusion

The Privy Council **dismissed the appeal**. The reservation was declared **valid**, meaning the government's resumption of the 10 acres for a public park was legally sound.

### Judges Rules

English common law is received into a peacefully annexed (settled) colony from its outset only to the extent that it is reasonably applicable to the infant colony's unique circumstances. While highly technical property rules like the rule against perpetuities apply to transactions between private citizens, they are inapplicable to Crown grants and public land reservations from the early colonial era because the Crown requires ongoing flexibility to reclaim land for public infrastructure as the colony grows.
