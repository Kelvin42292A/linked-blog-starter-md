## Material Facts

- **The Parties and Charge:** Jack Congo Murrell, an Aboriginal man, was charged with the murder of another Aboriginal man, Bill Jabingi, in New South Wales.
- **The Jurisdictional Challenge:** Murrell’s counsel argued that the Supreme Court of New South Wales had no jurisdiction to try him.
- **The Argument for Tribal Law:** The defense claimed that Aboriginal people were governed by their own customs and laws rather than the laws of England, were not British subjects, and were already subject to tribal punishment.

## Issues

1. **The Jurisdiction Question:** Does the Supreme Court of New South Wales have the legal jurisdiction to try an Indigenous person for a crime committed against another Indigenous person within the colony?
2. **The Sovereignty Question:** Are Aboriginal tribes recognized as independent sovereign states governe

## Rule/Law

- **Territorial Jurisdiction:** The English Crown exercised exclusive rights of "Domain and Empire" (sovereignty) over the entire territory of New South Wales.
- **No Competing Sovereignty:** English law does not recognize any independent sovereign power or competing customary legal system within British territory.
- **Equal Protection and Liability:** Both subjects and aliens living within the colony are entitled to the same legal protection and are equally answerable to English criminal law.

## Application/Analysis

- **No Recognized Statehood:** The court ruled that the various Aboriginal tribes had not attained the level of population, government, or laws required to be recognized as sovereign states.
- **Unappropriated Territory:** Because the land of New South Wales was treated as legally unappropriated (effectively _terra nullius_) at the time the British King took possession, English law became the sole law of the land.
- **Boundaries of Law:** Because the alleged murder took place within the geographic limits of the colony, English criminal law applied fully, making Murrell answerable for crimes against the peace of the King.

## Conclusion

The Supreme Court **overruled the plea** and declared that it had full jurisdiction to try Murrell. _(He was subsequently tried and acquitted on the facts)._

### Judges Rules

All persons residing within the geographical boundaries of a British colony—including indigenous inhabitants committing offences solely amongst themselves—are fully subject to the jurisdiction of the colonial courts and the application of English criminal law, as the Crown recognizes no independent sovereign states or competing customary legal systems within its territory.
