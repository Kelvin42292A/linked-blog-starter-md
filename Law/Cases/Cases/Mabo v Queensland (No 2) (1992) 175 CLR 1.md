High Court of Australia

## Material Facts

- The plaintiffs are members of the **Meriam people** who have lived in an organized, custom-governed community on the **Murray Islands** (Mer, Dauar, and Waier) in the Torres Strait since time immemorial.
- The Murray Islands were formally **annexed to Queensland in 1879**.
- The plaintiffs brought a lawsuit seeking legal recognition of their **traditional land rights** under the common law.
- The State of Queensland argued that the Crown acquired **absolute beneficial ownership** of all land upon annexation, which automatically wiped out any pre-existing indigenous rights.

## Issues

1. Does the Australian common law recognize a form of **traditional native title** over ancestral lands where it has not been validly extinguished?
2. Did the Crown's acquisition of sovereignty over the Murray Islands in 1879 vest **complete beneficial ownership** in the Crown, or did it only vest a **radical (ultimate) title**?

## Rule/Law

- **The Doctrine of Native Title:** The Australian common law **recognizes a form of native title** which, where it has not been extinguished, reflects the entitlements of indigenous inhabitants, in accordance with their laws and customs, to their traditional lands.
- **Radical Title vs. Beneficial Ownership:** Upon acquiring sovereignty, the Crown acquires **radical title** (ultimate legal title) rather than absolute beneficial ownership. Radical title is a sovereign power to govern and deal with the land; it does not automatically wipe out native possession.
- **Extinguishment of Native Title:** Native title survived the acquisition of sovereignty as a **burden on the Crown’s radical title**. However, it can be extinguished by a valid exercise of sovereign legislative or executive power that reveals a **"clear and plain intention"** to do so, such as through an inconsistent Crown grant (like a freehold or leasehold estate).

## Application/Analysis

- **The Meriam Connection:** The facts established that the Meriam people lived in a highly organized, land-based society with a complex system of custom-governed individual and family landholdings.
- **Preservation Upon Annexation:** When Queensland annexed the Murray Islands, the Crown acquired radical title, but the Meriam people's traditional rights survived as a **qualification and burden** on that title.
- **No General Extinguishment:** Subsequent actions by the Queensland government, such as reserving the islands "for the use of the Aboriginal Inhabitants," did not show a clear and plain intention to extinguish native title, but were rather intended to protect it.
- **Specific Extinguishment:** However, native title was lost over specific parcels that the Crown validly leased to third parties (such as a 2-acre lease granted to the London Missionary Society) because a lease is legally inconsistent with the concurrent enjoyment of native title.

## Conclusion

The High Court (in a 6:1 majority) declared that the **Murray Islands are not Crown land** and that the **Meriam people are entitled** as against the whole world to possession, occupation, use, and enjoyment of the island of Meriam (except for specifically leased or validly appropriated administrative parcels). The court declared that this native title is subject to future extinguishment by the Queensland Parliament, provided that any such action is **fully consistent with Commonwealth (federal) laws**, such as the _Racial Discrimination Act 1975_.

### Judges Rules

Australian common law recognizes a form of native title which preserves the traditional land rights of indigenous inhabitants in accordance with their customary laws and customs. The Crown's acquisition of sovereignty vests only a "radical title" in the state, which remains burdened by this native title unless the government validly extinguishes it through a clear and plain legislative or executive act (such as granting an inconsistent freehold or leasehold estate).
