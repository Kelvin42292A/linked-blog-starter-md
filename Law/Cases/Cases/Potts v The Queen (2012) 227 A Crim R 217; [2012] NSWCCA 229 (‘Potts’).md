## Material Facts

Potts, who suffered from chronic paranoid schizophrenia, stabbed a sex worker to death in his home and hid her body. He claimed the partial defence of substantial impairment. However, he also gave a non-psychotic explanation for his actions, claiming he only stabbed her because she first threatened him with a knife.

## Issues

Did Potts' schizophrenia actually cause or link to the killing, or did he act for ordinary, non-psychotic reasons?

## Rule/Law

Simply having a diagnosed mental illness does not automatically prove the defence of substantial impairment. The accused must prove a direct causal link (nexus) showing that the mental illness actually drove or influenced the criminal act.

## Application/Analysis

The jury was entitled to reject his defence. His description of defending himself against a knife was a rational, non-psychotic reaction to a situation, meaning his mental illness did not cause the crime.

## Conclusion

Potts’ appeal was dismissed, and his murder conviction was upheld.

### Judges Rules

A mental illness does not automatically excuse a crime or reduce murder to manslaughter. To successfully use the defence of substantial impairment, there must be a **direct, proven link showing the mental illness actually caused or influenced the act**, rather than the accused acting for ordinary, non-psychotic reasons (such as anger, panic, or a physical reaction to a threat).
