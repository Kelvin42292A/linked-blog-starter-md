High Court of Australia

## Material Facts

- The applicant (Parker) and his wife were staying at a farm where the deceased (Dan Kelly) worked.
- Kelly paid noticeable attention to Parker’s wife, and she eventually agreed to run away with him.
- On the day of the incident, Kelly taunted Parker with grossly insulting language, mocking his small stature and boasting about his adulterous intentions.
- Despite Parker’s pleas for his wife to stay for their children, she left down the road on Kelly's bicycle.
- Parker followed them in his car. He ran them down, breaking Kelly's legs and knocking his wife into a drain.
- Parker then viciously attacked the helpless Kelly with a knuckle-duster, and finally slit his throat with a knife, killing him.
- At trial, the judge withdrew the issue of provocation from the jury, and Parker was convicted of murder. He appealed to the High Court.

## Issues

1. **Withdrawing Provocation:** Did the trial judge err in law by refusing to let the jury consider the defence of provocation?
2. **Statutory Construction of Intent:** Under s 23 of the _Crimes Act 1900_ (NSW), does an intentional killing with a deadly weapon automatically prevent a jury from reducing murder to manslaughter based on provocation?

## Rule/Law

- **Insulting Language as Provocation (s 23(1)):** Section 23 of the _Crimes Act 1900_ (NSW) expanded the traditional common law to allow "grossly insulting language or gestures" to be considered as provocation, treating it the same way as a physical blow.
- **The Proviso for Provocation (s 23(2)):** To successfully reduce murder to manslaughter in NSW, the jury must affirmatively find three strict statutory elements:
    1. The provocation was not intentionally caused by the accused;
    2. It was reasonably calculated to deprive an ordinary person of self-control (and actually deprived the accused of it); and
    3. The act causing death was done suddenly, in the heat of passion, **"without intent to take life"**.
- **Proportionality Requirement:** Even under the statute, the loss of self-control must be of such an extent that it provides a reasonable explanation for the actual acts causing death. Therefore, the retaliation (the weapon/force used) must bear a reasonable relationship to the provocation received.

## Application/Analysis

- **The Intent to Kill Barred the Defence:** The majority of the High Court found that the statutory requirement that the act be done "without intent to take life" was fatal to Parker's case. Parker either followed Kelly with the clear intention of killing him (meaning he had intent), or he followed him simply to bring his wife back (meaning he hadn't actually lost his self-control at that stage).
- **Disproportionate Retaliation:** The majority ruled that the sequence and extreme violence of Parker's acts (running Kelly down with a car, beating him with a knuckle-duster, and slitting his throat) were completely disproportionate. No ordinary person would react to insulting words and an absconding spouse with such calculated, overwhelming lethal force.
- Because no reasonable jury could find that Parker acted "without intent to take life" or that his extreme violence was a proportionate response to the provocation, the trial judge was entirely correct to withdraw the issue of provocation from the jury.

## Conclusion

The application for special leave to appeal was **refused** by a 3:2 majority. The trial judge's ruling was upheld, and the applicant's conviction for murder remained in place.
