Court of Chancery

## Material Facts

- **The Scheme:** A Cambridge college sold land to Benedict Spinola despite a statute preventing the college from selling ("alienating") its land. To bypass this statute, the college surrendered the land to the Queen, who then granted it to Spinola.
- **The Dispute:** Thirty years later, the land had significantly increased in value and was now owned by the Earl of Oxford. The college regretted the sale and engineered a lawsuit at common law to reclaim the land, relying on a strict reading of the statute which made all such land dealings void.
- **The Common Law Win:** The common law court ruled strictly in favor of the college, declaring the original transfer void and leaving the Earl of Oxford with nothing.
- **The Chancery Appeal:** The Earl of Oxford appealed to the Court of Chancery, petitioning the Lord Chancellor for discretionary relief on the basis that the common law outcome was profoundly unfair.
- **The Conflict:** The Lord Chancellor arrested the college's representatives for refusing to answer questions. In response, the common law court issued a writ of _habeas corpus_ to release them, creating a massive constitutional deadlock over which court held supreme power.

## Issues

In the event of a direct conflict between a ruling of the common law courts and a ruling of the Court of Chancery (Equity), which system of law prevails?

## Rule/Law

- **The Supremacy of Equity:** Where the rules of common law and the rules of equity conflict, **the rules of equity shall always prevail**.
- **Acting on the Conscience (_In Personam_):** Equity does not set aside or invalidate common law judgments due to legal errors. Instead, it acts _in personam_ (upon the individual) to prevent a party from unconscionably enforcing their strict legal rights if they obtained that judgment through "oppression, wrong and a hard conscience".

## Application/Analysis

- **Strict Law vs. Conscience:** While the college possessed a valid, legal right to the land under the strict wording of the statute, enforcing that right to strip the Earl of his land after thirty years of peaceful ownership was unconscionable.
- **Royal Decree:** To resolve the deadlock, King James I issued a royal decree on July 14, 1616, officially siding with the Court of Chancery. He ruled that the Chancellor must continue to provide relief to citizens facing harsh or oppressive common law judgments to ensure justice is done.

## Conclusion

The Court of Chancery granted the Earl of Oxford relief, preventing the college from enforcing its common law judgment. The principle that **equity prevails over common law** was permanently established.

### Judges Rules

- **Equity Corrects the Rigidity of Law:** The common law operates on rigid, binary rules. Equity exists to alleviate this harshness by applying flexible maxims and judicial discretion based on changing community values.
- **Equity Prevails in Conflicts:** If a person's strict legal rights under common law clash with what is fair under the principles of equity, **the equitable principles override the common law**.
- **The Concurrent Channel:** Although the administration of common law and equity was eventually merged into a single court system under the _Judicature Acts_, the underlying legal and equitable principles **have not merged**. They remain two distinct streams of law running in the same channel.
