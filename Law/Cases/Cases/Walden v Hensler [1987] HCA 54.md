Full Court of Supreme Court of Queensland

## Material Facts

- Mr Walden, an Aboriginal Elder, shot a wild "plain turkey" for his family to eat, and his son kept a turkey chick as a pet. He was acting in accordance with traditional Aboriginal custom, hunting on land where he had the manager's permission.
- A wildlife officer seized the birds because plain turkeys are protected under the Queensland _Fauna Conservation Act_.
- Mr Walden was completely unaware of this law. He honestly believed he was doing nothing wrong and was legally entitled to take bush tucker as his people always had.

## Issues

Could Mr Walden's honest belief in his traditional Aboriginal rights serve as a legal defence (an "honest claim of right") to excuse him from the criminal charge of keeping protected fauna?

## Rule/Law

- **Ignorance vs. Claim of Right:** Under Section 22 of the Queensland _Criminal Code_, ignorance of the law is generally **not an excuse** . However, the law provides an exception: a person is not criminally responsible for an "offence relating to property" if they act in the exercise of an "honest claim of right".
- **Absolute Discharge:** Under Section 657A of the _Criminal Code_, a court has the power to absolutely discharge an offender without recording a conviction or punishment if it believes punishing them would be unjust (due to triviality, extenuating circumstances, etc.).

## Application/Analysis

- **The Defence Failed:** The majority of the High Court (Justices Brennan, Dawson, and Deane) decided that the "honest claim of right" defence did _not_ apply to Mr Walden. They concluded that the wildlife law he broke was an offence against the Crown's licensing system, not an "offence against property". Because of this, his honest mistake was treated simply as ignorance of the law, making him technically guilty.
- **The Injustice of Punishment:** Even though he was technically guilty, Justice Brennan stated that the primary purpose of criminal law is deterrence. Punishing a person who honestly doesn't know they are doing something wrong fails this purpose. The court declared it an "intolerable injustice" to slap an Aboriginal man with a criminal conviction and a massive fine for simply gathering food on his own country in honest ignorance of the law.

## Conclusion

The appeal was **allowed in part**. While his technical defence failed, the High Court quashed his original conviction and heavy fines. Instead, they applied Section 657A to **discharge him absolutely**, meaning he faced no punishment for his honest mistake.

### Judges Rules

**1. Honest Claim of Right:** Simply not knowing the law is no excuse, unless you are making an "honest claim of right." This means you genuinely believed you were legally entitled to do what you did with the property. Your belief only needs to be honest; it does not have to be reasonable or legally correct .

**2. Property Crimes Only:** This defence strictly applies to property offences, and cannot be used for offences against government regulatory or licensing systems, such as wildlife protection laws.

**3. Absolute Discharge:** If a person is technically guilty but punishing them for an honest mistake would be an "intolerable injustice," the court can completely discharge the offender without recording a conviction or handing down a punishment.
