Supreme Court of Nothern Territory

## Material Facts

- The plaintiffs were Aboriginal people representing the **Rirratjingu and Gumatj clans** of the Gove Peninsula in the Northern Territory.
- They brought a lawsuit against the Commonwealth and a mining company (**Nabalco**), claiming that bauxite mining on their ancestral lands was unlawful.
- They argued that their clans held **communal native title** over the land from time immemorial, which survived the British acquisition of sovereignty in 1788 and could not be extinguished without their consent.

## Issues

1. **Native Title at Common Law:** Does the Australian common law recognize a doctrine of "communal native title" in a settled colony?
2. **The Nature of the Connection:** Was the clans' traditional, custom-governed relationship with their land recognizable as a **proprietary (property) interest**?

## Rule/Law

- **The Settled Colony Rule:** Australia is classified as a **"settled" colony** as a matter of law. Upon peaceful settlement, the Crown acquired absolute legal ownership of all unalienated land, leaving no room for pre-existing native title unless it is expressly recognized by statute.
- **The Test for Property Rights:** For a traditional interest to be recognized as a property right under the common law, it must generally consist of **three core rights**: the right to use or enjoy the land, the right to exclude others, and the right to alienate (sell or transfer) the land.

## Application/Analysis

- **Customary Law Proved:** Blackburn J found that the plaintiffs successfully proved they lived in an organized community governed by a highly sophisticated, stable system of customary law.
- **Spiritual, Not Proprietary:** However, the court ruled that this relationship was fundamentally **religious and spiritual rather than proprietary**.
- **Failed Property Test:** The clans failed the common law property test because:
    - They did not have a clear right to exclude other clans from using the land.
    - Their customary laws explicitly forbade them from alienating (selling) the land.
    - Bands of different clans moved and hunted freely across the territory without seeking permission.
- Therefore, their spiritual connection—while real—did not constitute a property interest recognizable by the courts.

## Conclusion

The Supreme Court of the Northern Territory **dismissed the plaintiffs' claim**. The court ruled that communal native title was not a part of Australian law, meaning the mining leases granted to Nabalco were entirely lawful.

### Judges Rules

Under the common law of a settled colony, pre-existing indigenous land rights are not legally recognized as native title unless expressly established by statute. To be recognized as a proprietary interest, a traditional relationship to land must exhibit the standard characteristics of property—specifically the rights to use, exclude others, and alienate the territory. A purely spiritual or customary connection to land, without these proprietary elements, is not legally enforceable against the Crown or its grantees.
