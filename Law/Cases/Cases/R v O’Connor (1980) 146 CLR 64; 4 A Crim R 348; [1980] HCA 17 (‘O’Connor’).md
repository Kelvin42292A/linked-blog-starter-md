## Material Facts

Mark Norman O'Connor was caught stealing from a car by an off-duty police officer. While resisting arrest, O'Connor stabbed the officer with a knife he had taken from the glove box. O'Connor claimed he had no memory of the event because he had voluntarily taken 15 hallucinatory car-sickness tablets (Avil) and consumed alcohol, leaving him in a blackout state. The trial judge instructed the jury that self-induced intoxication could not be used as a defence to the alternative charge of unlawful wounding (a crime of "basic intent"). He was convicted of unlawful wounding, but the Victorian Full Court overturned his conviction. The Crown appealed to the High Court of Australia.

## Issues

Under Australian common law, is evidence of voluntary (self-induced) intoxication admissible to show that an accused's physical act was involuntary, or that they lacked the necessary mental element (mens rea), for a crime of basic intent?

## Rule/Law

Yes. In Australian common law, **evidence of self-induced intoxication is relevant and admissible for _all_ criminal charges** to determine if the accused acted voluntarily or had the required mental element (mens rea). The prosecution must always prove voluntariness and intent beyond reasonable doubt, and the court cannot create a legal fiction that presumes a "guilty mind" simply because the accused chose to get drunk or high.

## Application/Analysis

The charge of unlawful wounding requires a voluntary physical act (stabbing) and an actual intent to do that physical act. Since O'Connor’s extreme drug and alcohol consumption was capable of raising a reasonable doubt about whether he acted voluntarily or with intent, the trial judge erred by instructing the jury to ignore his state of intoxication.

## Conclusion

The High Court of Australia (by a 4–3 majority) dismissed the Crown's appeal and upheld O'Connor's acquittal, refusing to follow the English approach in _DPP v Majewski._

### Judges Rules

- **Rule 1: Rejection of the "Basic vs. Specific Intent" Distinction** Unlike English law, Australian common law **rejects the arbitrary distinction between crimes of "basic intent" (like assault or wounding) and "specific intent" (like murder)** regarding voluntary intoxication. Evidence of self-induced intoxication is admissible to raise a reasonable doubt about the mental element or voluntariness of **any** crime.
- **Rule 2: Voluntariness is Non-Negotiable** Save for statutory absolute liability or negligence offences, **no person can be held criminally responsible unless their physical act was voluntary (willed)**. The voluntary act of getting intoxicated does not automatically "supply" or substitute for a lack of voluntariness or actual intent at the moment the physical crime was committed.
