High Court of Australia

## Material Facts

- **The Prelude:** Everard Miller, Wayne Smith, Johnas Presley, and Joshua Betts spent the day drinking heavily. In the evening, Betts and Presley had a heated exchange with two local men (Clifford Hall and Wayne King) during which Betts was punched in the face.
- **The Return:** After returning home with a bloodied lip, Betts reported he was jumped. Presley grabbed a baseball bat and said, "let's go back and see what these people... go and see what the problem is".
- **The Weapons:** The group headed back. Presley had the bat, Smith carried a shovel, and Betts secretly armed himself with a 332 mm knife.
- **The Killing:** During the ensuing confrontation, King was beaten. Hall (the deceased) was struck from behind with the shovel and then fatally stabbed in the back by Betts.
- **The Conviction:** Miller, Smith, and Presley were all convicted of murder under the doctrines of joint criminal enterprise (JCE) or extended joint criminal enterprise (EJCE).
- **The Appeal:** The appellants argued they were too intoxicated to form any joint agreement or to foresee that Betts would use a knife. They also asked the High Court to completely abandon or restrict EJCE liability in light of the UK Supreme Court decision in _R v Jogee_, which had recently thrown out the doctrine.

## Issues

1. Should Australia follow the UK and **abandon or restrict the doctrine of extended joint criminal enterprise (EJCE)**?
2. Did the Court of Criminal Appeal err by failing to properly evaluate how the appellants' **severe intoxication** affected their capacity to form a joint agreement or foresee the incidental killing?

## Rule/Law

- **Joint Criminal Enterprise (JCE) Restated:** JCE exists when 2 or more people agree (expressly or tacitly inferred from conduct) to commit a crime. All participants are equally guilty of the planned crime regardless of their physical role at the scene.
- **Extended JCE (EJCE) Confirmed:** A participant in a JCE is also guilty of an _incidental_ crime committed by a partner if they **subjectively foresaw the possibility** that the partner might commit that crime (with the requisite intent) and continued to participate in the venture anyway.
- **Rejection of UK Law (_Jogee_):** The High Court explicitly declined to follow _R v Jogee_. EJCE remains a valid part of the common law of Australia, and the standard remains foresight of a **possibility** rather than a probability.
- **The Negligible Risk Exception:** Foresight is **not** established if the accused contemplated the incidental crime only "fleetingly" and genuinely dismissed it as a "negligible risk".
- **The Intoxication Standard:** To secure a conviction under JCE or EJCE, the prosecution must prove beyond reasonable doubt that the accused's **intoxication did not prevent them** from actually forming the agreement or foreseeing the incidental crime.

## Application/Analysis

- **Moral Culpability of Group Action:** The High Court held that deliberate participation in a joint criminal enterprise that carries a foreseen risk is highly culpable. A group acts as a "more potent force" with "higher levels of moral disinhibition" than an individual.
- **Failure to Assess Intoxication:** While the legal doctrine of EJCE was upheld, the High Court found that the lower Court of Criminal Appeal made a serious procedural error. They simply rubber-stamped the jury’s verdict without actually reviewing the highly specific evidence of intoxication.
- **Severe Impairment:** For instance, Miller’s estimated blood alcohol concentration at the time of the fight was between 0.241 and 0.342, with diazepam and cannabis also in his system. Expert evidence showed this would severely impair his foresight, decision-making, and planning. The prosecution had to exclude the reasonable possibility that this level of intoxication prevented him from forming the agreement or foreseeing the knife attack.

## Conclusion

The High Court **allowed the appeals**, set aside the lower court’s orders, and **remitted the matters back to the Court of Criminal Appeal** to properly evaluate the sufficiency of the evidence regarding the appellants' intoxication.

### Judges Rules

- **Foresight of a "Possibility" is the Australian Law:** The prosecution does not need to prove you intended or even expected the incidental crime to occur. It is enough to prove you subjectively foresaw the crime as a **possibility** and chose to keep participating in the joint venture.
- **Fleeting Thoughts of Risk Do Not Count:** If the idea of your partner committing a further crime crossed your mind only "fleetingly" and you **genuinely dismissed it as a negligible risk**, you do not possess the legal foresight required for conviction.
- **Intoxication Can Defeat the Agreement:** Severe intoxication can legally prevent you from coming to a tacit, unspoken understanding or agreement with others to commit the primary crime (such as a planned assault).
- **Intoxication Can Defeat Foresight:** Even if you agreed to a simple assault, severe intoxication is a highly relevant defense that can prevent you from actually foreseeing that a co-venturer might escalate the violence (such as pulling out a hidden knife and stabbing someone).
- **Appellate Courts Must Inspect the Proof:** Appellate courts cannot simply list the facts and declare the jury was entitled to convict. They have a strict duty to independently review the evidence of intoxication to ensure the prosecution has successfully excluded it as a reasonable possibility.
