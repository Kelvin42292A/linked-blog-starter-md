House of Lords (UK)

## Material Facts

- The appellant (Mrs. Donoghue) went to a café in Paisley where a friend bought her a bottle of ginger beer.
- The ginger beer was manufactured by the respondent (Stevenson).
- The café proprietor opened the bottle and poured some over ice cream. After the appellant drank some, her friend poured the rest of the bottle into the tumbler, and a decomposed snail floated out.
- The bottle was made of dark, opaque glass, which meant nobody (not the manufacturer, the café owner, or the consumer) could visually inspect the contents before it was poured.
- The appellant suffered from severe shock and gastro-enteritis (illness) as a result of drinking the contaminated ginger beer.
- Because her friend bought the drink, the appellant had no direct contract with the café owner or the manufacturer.

## Issues

**Duty of Care:** Does a manufacturer of a product owe a legal duty of care directly to the ultimate consumer to take reasonable precautions that the article is free from defects, even when there is absolutely no contractual relationship between them?

## Rule/Law

- **The "Neighbour Principle" (_Donoghue v Stevenson_ AC 562 per Lord Atkin):** You must take reasonable care to avoid acts or omissions which you can reasonably foresee would be likely to injure your neighbour. In law, a "neighbour" is defined as persons who are so closely and directly affected by your act that you ought reasonably to have them in contemplation as being so affected when directing your mind to the acts or omissions in question.
- **The Manufacturer's Duty (_Donoghue v Stevenson_ AC 562):** A manufacturer of products who sells them in such a form as to show he intends them to reach the ultimate consumer in the exact form in which they left him, with **no reasonable possibility of intermediate examination**, owes a duty to the consumer to take reasonable care that the product will not cause injury.
- **Overcoming the Privity of Contract Barrier (Distinguishing _Winterbottom v Wright_ (1842)):** Historically, courts held that without a contract, you could not sue for a defective product (as seen in _Winterbottom v Wright_). However, this new rule establishes that **liability for negligence (tort) exists independently of a contract**.

## Application/Analysis

- **Direct Relationship:** The manufacturer deliberately sealed the ginger beer in a dark, opaque glass bottle with a metal cap. By doing this, the manufacturer intentionally excluded any possibility that the retailer or consumer could inspect or interfere with the contents before consumption. This action brought the manufacturer into a direct legal relationship with the ultimate consumer.
- **Reasonable Foreseeability:** A reasonable person who manufactures food or drink for gain directly contemplates that it will be consumed by the public. Therefore, the manufacturer should have reasonably foreseen that negligently leaving empty bottles in places where snails could access them, and failing to inspect them, would cause direct harm to the consumer's health.
- Because the manufacturer owed a duty to the consumer, the absence of a contract does not bar the appellant from seeking damages for negligence.

## Conclusion

The appeal was **allowed** by a 3:2 majority in the House of Lords. The court ruled that the manufacturer **does** owe a duty of care to the ultimate consumer, establishing a valid cause of action for negligence.
