Supreme Court of South Australia

## Material Facts

- **The Cheques:** The appellant, O'Connor, lived in a home previously occupied by Carole Marie Arnold. She received cheques worth $1,040 in the mail addressed to Arnold and kept them.
- **The Scheme:** O'Connor took the cheques to the Hindmarsh Building Society to cash them. Told she must first open an account, she filled out the forms using Arnold's name, even temporarily misspelling "Arnold" before signing.
- **The Halt:** The teller deposited the cheques, gave O'Connor a receipt, and told her to return in a week with identification proving she was Arnold. O'Connor never returned because she realized she could not obtain fake ID and knew it was wrong.
- **The Charge:** She was charged with and convicted of attempting to obtain money by false pretenses. She admitted to the police that her actual intention was to get the money.
- **The Appeal:** O'Connor appealed, arguing her acts were mere "preparation" and not close enough (proximate) to committing the actual theft, as she never actually tried to withdraw the money or present fake ID.

## Issues

Were the accused's actions sufficiently proximate to constitute the physical act (_actus reus_) of an attempt, even though she had not completed the "last act" within her power (withdrawing the money) and still had further steps to complete?

## Rule/Law

- **The Last Act is Not Required:** To prove the _actus reus_ of an attempt, the prosecution does not need to show the accused completed the final physical step of the crime. The "last act" theory is not a strict requirement for conviction.
- **Beyond Preparation:** The prosecution must prove the actions went beyond mere preparation and were immediately connected to committing the crime.
- **Intent Governs Proximity:** Where the accused's criminal intent is clearly established (such as by direct confession), acts done towards committing the crime can be legally sufficient to constitute an attempt, even if those same acts might seem too remote if the intent were unclear.

## Application/Analysis

- **Plan Put into Execution:** O'Connor did not merely prepare to commit fraud; she actively began executing her plan by taking the stolen cheques, pretending to be Arnold, and successfully opening a bank account in Arnold's name.
- **Unambiguous Intent:** Because O'Connor admitted her goal was to steal the money, her acts of opening the account and depositing the cheques were clearly purposive and immediately connected to the fraud.
- **Remaining Steps Irrelevant:** The fact that she was stopped by a request for ID and had to make a further withdrawal demand did not erase the fact that she had already taken a highly proximate step toward committing the crime.

## Conclusion

The Supreme Court dismissed the appeal and **affirmed the conviction for attempt.**

### Judges Rules

- **No "Last Act" Barrier:** You can be convicted of an attempt even if you did not perform the final step required to complete the crime (such as making a withdrawal request).
- **Intent Powers Proximity:** If your criminal intent is clearly proven (such as through a confession to the police), the court will find acts done toward the crime to be sufficiently proximate, even if those same acts would be considered "mere preparation" if your intent were unclear.
- **Execution vs. Preparation:** An attempt is committed the moment you move from planning to actually executing the offense (such as opening a fraudulent account with stolen cheques rather than just holding onto them).
- **Abandonment Does Not Clear Guilt:** Once you have performed a sufficiently proximate act with criminal intent, you cannot escape liability for the attempt simply because you decided to back out or stop because of an obstacle (like being asked for ID).
