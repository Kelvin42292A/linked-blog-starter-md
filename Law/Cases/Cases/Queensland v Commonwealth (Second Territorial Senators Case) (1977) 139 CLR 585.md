## Material Facts

The State of Queensland and its Attorney-General challenged federal laws that gave the Northern Territory and the ACT voting representatives in the Senate and the House of Representatives. Just two years earlier, the High Court had upheld these laws in a 4-3 decision in _Western Australia v Commonwealth_. Queensland argued the Court should overrule that decision, pointing out that one of the majority judges had since retired and been replaced.

## Issues

Should the High Court overrule a recent constitutional precedent simply because the personal composition of the bench has changed?

## Rule/Law

A change in the personal composition of the bench is not, on its own, a sufficient reason to review or overrule a recent, fully considered precedent. Continuity and consistency in judicial decisions are vital to the administration of justice.

## Application/Analysis

Although Justices Gibbs and Stephen still personally believed the 1975 decision was incorrect, they determined it was their judicial duty to follow it to preserve consistency and protect the democratic expectations of Territory citizens who had already elected senators. The remaining majority judges (Justices Mason, Jacobs, and Murphy) reaffirmed that the original decision was correct on its merits.

## Conclusion

By a 5-2 majority, the High Court allowed the demurrers and dismissed the challenges, upholding the validity of the Territory representation laws.

### Judges Rules

- **Rule 1: The Precedent Rule (_Stare Decisis_)** A shift in the Court’s personnel must never be used as a shortcut to overturn a recent, binding constitutional precedent. Continuity, stability, and public confidence in the judiciary require judges to respect established rulings, even if they personally disagree with them.
- **Rule 2: Territory Representation** Section 122 of the Constitution gives the Commonwealth Parliament the power to allow Australian Territories to be represented by **fully voting members** in both the Senate and the House of Representatives.
