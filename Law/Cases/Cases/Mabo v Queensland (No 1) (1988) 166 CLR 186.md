High Court of Australia

## Material Facts

- The plaintiffs are Murray Islanders (members of the **Miriam people**) who have exclusively occupied and possessed the Torres Strait Islands of Mer, Dawar, and Waier since time immemorial.
- In 1982, they brought a lawsuit against the State of Queensland to obtain legal recognition of their traditional native title, customary ownership, and usufructuary rights over their ancestral lands.
- The legal action was triggered because the Queensland government threatened to set up a local council and grant the islands' lands to that council as a trustee, which would give it the power to lease the land and terminate the Islanders' traditional occupation.
- In 1985, three years after the lawsuit commenced, the Queensland Parliament passed the _**Queensland Coast Islands Declaratory Act 1985**_.
- This Act retrospectively declared that the islands belonged strictly to the Crown upon annexation in 1879, **extinguishing all traditional native title rights** and completely denying any right to compensation.
- The plaintiffs challenged Queensland's defense under **Section 109 of the Australian Constitution**, arguing that the 1985 State Act was invalid because it was inconsistent with the federal **_Racial Discrimination Act 1975_ (Cth)** (specifically Sections 9 and 10), which guarantees equal rights to own and inherit property.

## Issues

1. **The Specificity Issue:** Did the Queensland Act successfully extinguish the Miriam people's traditional rights even though it did not specifically name or identify those precise rights?
2. **The State Power Issue:** Does the Queensland Parliament have the constitutional power to retrospectively strip individuals of their property rights without paying compensation?
3. **The Discrimination Issue:** Is the Queensland Act invalid under Section 109 of the Constitution because it selectively destroys Indigenous property rights while leaving non-Indigenous property rights secure, violating the federal _Racial Discrimination Act 1975_?

## Rule/Law

- **Federal Supremacy (Section 109 of the Constitution):** When a State law conflicts with a federal law, the federal law wins, and the State law becomes completely invalid and inoperative to the extent of that conflict.
- **Equality Before the Law (Section 10(1) of the RDA):** A State cannot pass a law that takes away property or other legal rights from one racial or ethnic group while leaving those same rights secure for everyone else. If a State law does this, the federal _Racial Discrimination Act_ overrides it to restore their rights to the same level as the rest of the community.
- **The Right to Own Property:** Under federal human rights law, the right to own and inherit property includes a strict **immunity from being arbitrarily dispossessed** of your property.
- **Limited State Power:** While a State Parliament generally has broad "plenary" power to make laws and deal with land, this power is **strictly subject to the federal Constitution and federal laws**.
- **The Section 9 Limit:** Section 9 of the RDA (which bans discriminatory acts) only applies to a "person" doing an act—it does not apply to a Parliament passing a statute.

## Application/Analysis

- **The State Law Wiped Out Rights:** On its face, the Queensland Act declared that the Crown owned the islands "freed from all other rights," which successfully extinguished the plaintiffs' traditional native title under State law.
- **Section 9 Failed:** The plaintiffs' argument under **Section 9** of the federal _Racial Discrimination Act_ (RDA) failed because passing a statute is not a "person" doing an "act".
- **Section 10(1) Succeeded:** **Section 10(1)** of the RDA guarantees racial equality, including the human right to own property and be immune from arbitrary land deprivation. By destroying only the traditional land rights of the Murray Islanders while keeping other Queenslanders' property rights secure, the State law selectively discriminated against them.
- **The Constitution Win:** Because the Queensland Act was inconsistent with the protective federal law, **Section 109 of the Constitution** overrode the State Act, making it invalid.

## Conclusion

The High Court **allowed the plaintiffs' demurrer**. The court ruled that the _Queensland Coast Islands Declaratory Act 1985_ was **invalid and inoperative** under Section 109 of the Constitution because it was inconsistent with Section 10(1) of the federal _Racial Discrimination Act 1975_. Consequently, Queensland's attempt to retrospectively wipe out the Miriam people's traditional land rights **failed**.

### Judges Rule

**A State law cannot retrospectively extinguish traditional native title if doing so selectively deprives a specific racial or ethnic group of their property rights while leaving the property rights of other groups in the community secure.** Such a law directly conflicts with Section 10(1) of the federal _Racial Discrimination Act 1975_ (Cth) (which protects the human right to own property and inherit free from discrimination) and is therefore rendered **invalid and inoperative** under Section 109 of the Australian Constitution.
