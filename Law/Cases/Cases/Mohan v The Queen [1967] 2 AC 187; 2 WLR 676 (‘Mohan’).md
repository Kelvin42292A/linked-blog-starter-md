Privy Council

## Material Facts

- **The Confrontation:** A father (Ramnath Mohan) and his 18-year-old son (Deodath Ramnath) got into a violent street dispute with the deceased (Mootoo Sammy).
- **The Attack:** Both the father and son armed themselves with cutlasses, cornered the deceased, and jointly attacked him, with both landing blows.
- **The Death:** The deceased died in the hospital from a blood clot (embolism) caused by a very severe cutlass wound to his leg.
- **The Uncertainties:**
    - The evidence was conflicting and left it highly **uncertain which of the two accused actually struck the fatal leg blow**.
    - There was **no evidence of any pre-arranged plan** or plot between the father and son prior to the sudden fight.
- **The Conviction & Appeal:** Both were convicted of murder. They appealed, arguing they must both be acquitted because the prosecution could not prove a pre-arranged plan or show which individual struck the fatal blow.

## Issues

Can two co-accused be convicted of murder if they simultaneously attack a victim with a shared intent, even if there is **no pre-arranged plan** and it is **uncertain which of them struck the fatal blow**?

## Rule/Law

- **Presence and Assistance:** A person who is physically present and actively aiding and abetting the commission of an offense is guilty of that offense as a **principal in the second degree**.
- **No Pre-Arrangement Needed:** Unlike conspiracy, a charge of aiding and abetting (secondary liability) **does not require proof of a pre-arranged plan or plot**.
- **Attribution of Fatal Act:** If one participant strikes the fatal blow while the other is present aiding and abetting him, **each is guilty of murder** as a principal offender.

## Application/Analysis

- **Simultaneous Joint Assault:** The father and son attacked the same victim at the same time, using similar highly dangerous weapons (cutlasses).
- **Shared Intent:** Their simultaneous conduct established a **common intention** to inflict grievous bodily harm upon the deceased.
- **Aiding and Abetting:** Because they were actively participating together in the assault, each was physically present **aiding and abetting** the other in wounding the victim.
- **Plan Irrelevant:** Since they were actively assisting each other at the scene, the prosecution did not need to prove a prior plan or identify whose hand struck the fatal blow to find both guilty of murder.

## Conclusion

The Privy Council dismissed the appeal and upheld the murder convictions.

### Judges Rules

- **No Pre-Arranged Plan Required:** To convict multiple attackers of murder, the prosecution **does not need to prove a pre-arranged plan or plot**.
- **Simultaneous Action Equals Aid:** If co-accused attack the same victim at the same time with similar weapons and a **common intention** to cause serious bodily harm, they are legally aiding and abetting one another.
- **Principal in the Second Degree:** The participant who did not physically deliver the fatal blow is still fully liable for murder as a **principal in the second degree** because their active presence and participation assisted the killer.
- **Uncertainty of the Fatal Blow is No Shield:** If a joint attack with a shared intent results in death, the accused cannot be acquitted simply because the medical evidence cannot prove **which specific hand** delivered the fatal injury.
