## Material Facts

Trotter sexually assaulted a two-and-a-half-year-old boy. He then threw the conscious boy into a river to drown in order to hide the evidence of his crime. He raised the defence of diminished responsibility (substantial impairment) based on schizophrenia and a mild intellectual disability.

## Issues

Was Trotter's mental impairment "substantial" enough to reduce his charge from murder to manslaughter?

## Rule/Law

To be "substantial," an impairment must be more than trivial or minimal and must **really make a great difference** to the accused's mental responsibility. Deciding if an impairment is "substantial" is a common-sense value judgment for the court to make, not a medical fact for doctors to decide.

## Application/Analysis

While Trotter had a real mental disability, throwing the child into the river was a natural, calculated reaction to get rid of the evidence. Any impairment he suffered did not make a great difference to his mental responsibility for the killing.

## Conclusion

The defence of diminished responsibility was rejected, and Trotter was found **guilty of murder**

### Judges Rules

- **The Key Test:** To prove substantial impairment, a mental abnormality must be more than minimal and must **really make a great difference** to the accused's mental responsibility for the crime.
- **The Court's Decision:** Whether an impairment is "substantial" is a **common-sense value judgment** for the jury or judge to decide. Medical experts can explain the mental condition, but they cannot decide if it is legally "substantial" enough to reduce murder to manslaughter.
