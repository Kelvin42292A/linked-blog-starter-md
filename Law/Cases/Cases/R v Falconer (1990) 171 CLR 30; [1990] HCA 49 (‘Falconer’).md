## Material Facts

After suffering years of abuse, Mrs. Falconer was sexually assaulted and threatened by her husband. She experienced a mental blackout and shot him, but had no memory of doing so. The trial judge refused to let psychiatrists testify that she was sane but acting in an involuntary "dissociative state".

## Issues

Can a severe emotional or mental shock cause a sane person to act involuntarily, and is psychiatric evidence of this state admissible?

## Rule/Law

A person is not criminally responsible for an involuntary (unwilled) act. A healthy mind can malfunction temporarily due to a severe psychological blow, which is classified as **sane automatism**.

## Application/Analysis

The psychiatric evidence was relevant because it showed her blackout was a sane mind's reaction to extreme trauma, not an underlying mental disease. The trial judge was wrong to block this evidence.

## Conclusion

The appeal was dismissed, and a **new trial was ordered** so the jury could hear the psychiatric evidence.

### Judges Rules

- **The Psychological Blow Rule:** A severe psychological shock from external events can cause a sane person to experience a temporary mental blackout.
- **No Physical Blow Needed:** To prove your actions were involuntary, you do not need to prove you suffered a physical injury (like a concussion). A severe emotional shock is enough.
- **The Burden of Proof:**
    - **Sane Automatism:** The prosecution must prove beyond reasonable doubt that the accused acted voluntarily.
    - **Insanity (Insane Automatism):** The accused must prove on the balance of probabilities that they had an underlying disease of the mind.
