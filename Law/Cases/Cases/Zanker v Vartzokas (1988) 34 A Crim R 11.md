Supreme Court of South Australia

## Material Facts

- A young woman accepted a lift in the defendant's van.
- While driving, the defendant offered her money for sexual favours, which she rejected.
- When she demanded to be let out, he accelerated the van. He then threatened her, saying: "I am going to take you to my mate's house. He will really fix you up".
- Terrified by the threat, she jumped out of the moving van (travelling at 60 km/h) and suffered bodily injuries.
- The magistrate originally dismissed the charge of assault occasioning actual bodily harm, arguing that the defendant's threat created a fear of _future_ violence, not _immediate_ violence.

## Issues

Can a threat of _future_ physical harm constitute an assault if the victim is unlawfully trapped and at the mercy of the attacker?

## Rule/Law

- **Assault definition:** Creating an expectation or fear of physical violence in the victim's mind.
- **_MacPherson v Brown (1975)_:** If a victim is unlawfully imprisoned, a threat can create a **continuing fear**. The feared physical violence does not need to be strictly immediate if the fear itself is ongoing.
- **_Barton v Armstrong_:** Threats can be an assault even if the victim doesn't know exactly when the violence will occur.
- **_Rosza v Samuels_:** The core of assault is creating fear that unlawful force will be used.

## Application/Analysis

- The woman was trapped in the van at the defendant's mercy (compared to a "mouse to a playful cat" with no escape).
- Applying _**MacPherson v Brown**_, the threat did not just happen once. Because she was trapped, the words were "ringing presently in her ears," creating a **continuing fear**. This ongoing fear satisfied the legal requirement for immediacy.

## Conclusion

The appeal was **allowed**. The Court ruled that the defendant's conduct did constitute an assault, and ordered the magistrate to record a conviction against the defendant for assault occasioning actual bodily harm.
