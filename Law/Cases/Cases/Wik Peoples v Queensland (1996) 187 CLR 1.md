High Court of Australia

## Material Facts

- **The Land Claims:** The Wik and Thayorre peoples claimed **native title** over large areas of land in Northern Queensland (the Holroyd River Holding and the Mitchellton Holdings).
- **The Government Leases:** The Queensland Government had previously granted **statutory pastoral leases** over these lands under the _Land Act 1910_ (Q) and the _Land Act 1962_ (Q).
- **The Dispute:** The State and pastoralists argued that the grant of these leases automatically extinguished native title. The Aboriginal claimants argued that their traditional title survived and **coexisted** with the pastoralists' grazing rights.

## Issues

1. Did the statutory pastoral leases grant the lessees a legal right to **exclusive possession** of the land?
2. Did the grant of these pastoral leases **necessarily extinguish** all pre-existing native title rights?

## Rule/Law

- **The Clear and Plain Intention Test:** Native title is not extinguished by a statute or executive act unless Parliament reveals a **clear and plain intention** to do so.
- **Statutory Construction of Tenures:** Statutory interests (like pastoral leases) are creatures of statute, and their rights must be derived from the legislation itself rather than by importing common law leasehold concepts.
- **Inconsistency of Rights:** Native title is only extinguished by a Crown grant to the extent that the legal rights granted are **wholly inconsistent** with the continued enjoyment of native title.

## Application/Analysis

- **No Exclusive Possession:** The High Court (4:3 majority) found that the pastoral leases did **not** grant the lessees exclusive possession.
- **Limited Purpose & Carve-outs:** The leases were granted for **"pastoral purposes only"** and were heavily restricted by statutory reservations allowing third parties (such as miners, timber-cutters, and the public) to access the land.
- **Coexistence is Possible:** Because the pastoralists had no legal right to exclude others, the grant did not automatically extinguish native title. The two titles could legally **coexist**.
- **Yielding of Title:** However, if a specific native title right directly clashes with the pastoralist's rights, the native title must **yield** to the extent of that inconsistency.

## Conclusion

The High Court allowed the appeal. The court ruled that the pastoral leases **did not necessarily extinguish** native title. The case was returned to the lower court to determine if native title existed on the facts and if any specific elements were inconsistent.

### Judges Rules

- **No Automatic Extinguishment**: The mere grant of a statutory pastoral lease does not automatically or necessarily wipe out pre-existing native title rights.
- **No Exclusive Possession**: Statutory pastoral leases are governed strictly by their enabling Acts and specific terms—not by common law leasehold concepts—meaning they do not automatically grant the lessee a right to exclusive possession of the land.
- **Coexistence of Rights**: Because the lessee does not have an unqualified right to exclude others, native title rights can legally **coexist** alongside the rights of the pastoralist.
- **Native Title Yields to Conflict**: If a specific native title right directly conflicts with a right granted to the pastoralist under the lease, the native title right must **yield** (give way) to the extent of that inconsistency.
