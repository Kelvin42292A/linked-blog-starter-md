Full Court of the Supreme Court of Queensland (Court of Appeal)

### Material Facts

- The respondents (Rasells) purchased custom-made, high-quality tufted cut-pile wool carpet from the first defendant for their new home
- They specifically told the appellant’s (Cavalier Marketing) representative that the carpet was required to **blend well** with their exisiting decor (particularly exposed brick walls)
- The appellant imported the carpet and was deemed to be the manufacturer under the _Trade Practices Act 1974_ (Cth)
- After installation, the carpet developed **shading/pile reversal** (appearing lighter or darker depending on the viewing direction), a known characteristic of this type of carpet.
- The trial judge found the respondents were **not warned** about this phenomenon
- The respondents sued and the trial judge awarded damages against the appellant under **ss 74B** (not fit for purpose), **74D** (unmerchantable quality), and **74E** (not corresponding with sample) of the _Trade Practices Act._

### Issue

- Whether there was sufficient evidence to support the trail judge’s findings that the carpet was not reasonably fit for the particular purpose made known to the appellant (**s 74B**)
- Whether the stated purpose (carpet to blend well with existing decor) was sufficiently clear to constitute a “particular purpose” under the _Trade Practices Act._
- Whether the carpet was of merchantable quality (**s 74D**), having regard to the shading/pile reversal effect

### Rule

- Under s 92(3) District Courts Act 1967–1989 (Qld), this appeal is not a rehearing. The Court must treat it as if it were an appeal from a jury verdict.
- Under s 74A(4) Trade Practices Act 1974 (Cth), the appellant (importer) is deemed to be the manufacturer of the carpet.
- s 74B: Corporation is liable if goods are not reasonably fit for the particular purpose made known to it.
- s 74D: Goods must be of merchantable quality (as fit for the purpose(s) for which goods of that kind are commonly bought as it is reasonable to expect).

### Application

- The respondents made known to the appellant’s representative the **particular purpose** - carpet that would **blend well** with their existing home decor (exposed brick walls)
- They relied on the appellant’s sill and judgment to supply suitable custom-made carpet
- After installation, the carpet developed **shading/pile reversal**, making it look patchy (lighter/darker depending on viewing angle)
- The respondents were **not warned** about this known issue
- The carpet was not reasonably fit for the particular purpose (s 74B)
- The shading also made the carpet **not of merchantable quality** (s 74D), as it did not match the appearance the respondents reasonably expected for high-quality custom carpet.

### Conclusion

Breach of ss 74B and 74D established. Trial judge’s findings were open on the evidence. Appeal dismissed.
