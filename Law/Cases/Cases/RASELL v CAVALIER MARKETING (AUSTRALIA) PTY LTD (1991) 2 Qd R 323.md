Full Court of the Supreme Court of Queensland (Court of Appeal)

### Material Facts

- The plaintiffs (the Rasells) purchased a high-quality, expensive carpet for their residential home from a retailer, which was manufactured by Cavalier Marketing.
- Shortly after the carpet was laid, it began to show significant "pile reversal" or "watermarking". This meant the carpet looked patchy, with areas appearing as different shades of color depending on the light and angle.
- The Rasells complained and sought a refund, arguing the carpet was defective.
- The manufacturer and retailer argued that the carpet was still perfectly durable and functionally fine to walk on, meaning it technically still worked as a carpet.

### Issue

1. **Multiple Purposes:** Does the legal definition of "merchantable quality" require a product to be fit for _all_ the normal purposes it is commonly bought for, or is it enough that it simply fulfills its primary, functional purpose (i.e., walking on it)?
2. **Aesthetics vs. Function:** Can a product be declared legally "unmerchantable" strictly due to an aesthetic or cosmetic flaw, even if it is structurally sound?

### Rule

- **The Definition of Merchantable Quality:** Under the _Trade Practices Act 1974_ (and the _Sale of Goods Act_), goods are of merchantable quality if they are as fit for the **purpose or purposes** for which goods of that kind are commonly bought as it is reasonable to expect, having regard to their price and description.
- **The "All Purposes" Rule:** The courts strictly interpret the plural word "purposes". To be of merchantable quality, the goods must be fit for _all_ the normal purposes for which that kind of good is commonly purchased, not just one of them.

### Application

- **Carpet is for Decor, Not Just Walking:** The Queensland Supreme Court determined that people buy expensive carpet for a residential home for two distinct, common purposes:
    1. To insulate the floor and walk on (functional).
    2. To match the decor and look visually pleasing (aesthetic).
- **Failure of a Purpose:** Even though the carpet was durable and could be walked on without issue, the severe watermarking meant it completely failed its aesthetic purpose.
- **Price and Expectation:** Because the Rasells paid a premium price for what was described as a high-quality product, it was entirely reasonable for them to expect it to be visually flawless. Therefore, it was not fit for _all_ its common purposes.

### Conclusion

The plaintiffs' claim was **approved** (the appeal was **allowed**). The court deemed the carpet **not** to be of merchantable quality because of the aesthetic defects. The Rasells were legally entitled to reject the carpet and get their money back.
