Court of Criminal Appeal of New South Wales

### Material Facts

- The appellant (Williams) and another man (French) were involved in two fights at a licensed club.
- In the first fight, the appellant received a facial wound from a broken beer glass.
- In the second fight, which the appellant started, the appellant bit French's ear, severing a portion of it,.
- The appellant claimed the biting was not a deliberate act, but rather an "involuntary reflex" to excruciating pain caused when French grabbed his wounded face,.
- He was acquitted of malicious wounding but convicted of the alternative count of **assault occasioning actual bodily harm**,.

### Issue

- **Voluntariness:** Did the trial judge adequately direct the jury that the Crown must prove the biting was a _voluntary_ (willed) act rather than a reflex?,
- **Recklessness and Intent:** Can a reckless application of force constitute the necessary mental element (mens rea) for an assault, and does the Crown need to prove a specific intent to cause actual bodily harm?,
- **Defining Recklessness:** Must a trial judge provide a complex legal definition of "recklessness" to the jury?

### Rule

- **_Ryan (1967)_ (Applied):** An accused is not guilty of a crime if the act was not a willed or voluntary act. The Crown bears the onus to prove the act was voluntary beyond a reasonable doubt.
- **_Venna (1976)_ & _Revell (1976)_ (Applied):** The mental element for the offence of battery/assault is satisfied by proof that the defendant intentionally **or recklessly** applied force to another person,.
- **_Coulter (1988)_ (Applied):** To establish the offence of assault occasioning actual bodily harm, the Crown does **not** need to prove a specific intent to cause actual bodily harm. The only intent required is the intent inherent in the assault itself.
- **Meaning of Recklessness:** In the context of a physical fight, "recklessness" carries its ordinary meaning—acting without regard to possible consequences or foresight of the possibility of harmful consequences.

### Application

- **Voluntariness Direction:** The appellate court held that the trial judge adequately instructed the jury. The judge clearly told the jury they had to consider whether the biting was a willed act or an involuntary reflex, and that the Crown had to prove it was voluntary.
- **Intent for Actual Bodily Harm:** Following _Coulter_, the trial judge was correct in directing the jury that they could convict the appellant if they were satisfied he bit his opponent deliberately _or_ recklessly. The Crown did not have to prove the appellant specifically intended to cause actual bodily harm; proving the assault (the bite) caused the harm was sufficient.
- **Defining Recklessness:** The trial judge did not err by failing to give a complex legal definition of recklessness. Telling the jury that recklessness meant acting while "indifferent to Mr French's suffering" or "not caring whether it did or did not cause harm" was perfectly adequate and correctly applied a subjective test.
- **Jury Directions Principle:** The Court strongly emphasized that trial judges should avoid giving juries complex legal lectures. Summings up should be simple, clear, and focused only on the practical factual issues the jury needs to decide,.

### Conclusion

The appeal against the conviction was **dismissed**. The Court found no errors in the trial judge's directions to the jury regarding voluntariness, recklessness, or intent. The conviction for assault occasioning actual bodily harm was upheld.
