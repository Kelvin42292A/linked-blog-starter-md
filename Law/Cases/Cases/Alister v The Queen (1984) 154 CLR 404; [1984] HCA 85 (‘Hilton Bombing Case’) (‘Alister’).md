High Court of Australia

## Material Facts

- **The Arrest:** Paul Alister, Ross Dunn, and Timothy Anderson (members of the Ananda Marga sect) were arrested by police who intercepted their car on the way to bomb the house of a political opponent.
- **The Act:** When police intercepted the car, Dunn (in the back seat) physically tried to detonate the bomb to kill both the occupants and the police. Alister (in the passenger seat) did not physically assist in the detonation at that moment.
- **The Joint Enterprise:** The prosecution argued Alister shared a common purpose (joint enterprise) with Dunn because of earlier conversations in the car, where Alister said: _"If we are stopped, that should be exploded so that there is nothing that can possibly connect this with Ananda Marga"_.
- **The Charge:** Both Alister and Dunn were convicted of the **attempted murder** of the intercepting police officers.
- **The Appeal:** Alister appealed his attempted murder conviction, arguing that there was no proof he specifically intended to kill the police—only that he intended self-destruction or was recklessly indifferent to whether the police died.

## Issues

What is the necessary **mental state (_mens rea_)** required to convict someone of **attempted murder**? Is reckless indifference or an intent to cause serious bodily harm sufficient, or does it strictly require a specific intent to kill?

## Rule/Law

- **Specific Intent to Kill is Mandatory:** The _mens rea_ for any criminal attempt requires a **specific intent to commit the complete offence**. For attempted murder, the prosecution must prove a specific intent to cause death.
- **Attempts Are Stricter Than Completed Crimes:** Paradoxically, the mental element for an _attempted_ crime is stricter than for the completed crime itself.
- **Recklessness is Legally Insufficient:** Reckless indifference to the probability of death, or an intent to cause grievous bodily harm (GBH), is sufficient to convict someone of murder if the victim dies. However, **neither is sufficient for attempted murder** if the victim survives.
- **Joint Enterprise Intent:** To convict a co-accused of attempted murder under a joint enterprise, they must personally share a specific intent to kill, rather than just foresee death as a reckless possibility.

## Application/Analysis

- **Self-Destruction vs. Intent to Kill:** The High Court analyzed Alister’s statements in the car (_"be prepared to give your life for Baba,"_ and instructing the driver to lock the doors to prevent police from stopping the detonation).
- **The Recklessness Gap:** While this evidence proved Alister was recklessly indifferent to the probability that the intercepting police would die in the explosion, it did not prove he possessed a specific, targeted intent to kill the officers.
- **Split on the Facts:**
    - _The Dissent (Gibbs CJ & Murphy J):_ Held that since there was no evidence Alister shared an actual intent to kill the police, his conviction for attempted murder had to be quashed.
    - _The Majority (Wilson, Brennan, & Dawson JJ):_ Refused special leave on this count. They agreed with the legal rule that an intent to kill is required, but found that the jury was entitled to infer from the highly dangerous circumstances that the group's common purpose was indeed to destroy/kill anyone who intervened.

## Conclusion

The High Court unanimously affirmed that **attempted murder strictly requires a specific intent to kill**. (The majority refused special leave to appeal the conviction on the basis that the jury’s finding of this intent was open on the facts).

### Judges Rules

- **Attempts Require Specific Intent:** You cannot commit a criminal attempt recklessly or negligently. An attempt strictly requires a **specific, subjective intent** to bring about the completed offense.
- **The "Attempt Paradox":** The law of attempt is strictly narrower than the law of completed crimes. If you attack someone intending only to cause serious injury:
    - If they **die**, you are guilty of **murder** (intent to cause GBH suffices).
    - If they **survive**, you are **not guilty of attempted murder** (only wounding with intent).
- **Reckless Indifference is Excluded:** Even if you act with complete, reckless disregard for human life (such as setting off a bomb), you cannot be convicted of attempted murder unless the prosecution proves you **actively wanted or decided to cause death**.
- **Shared Intent in Joint Enterprises:** If your partner attempts to kill someone during a joint crime, you are only complicit in the _attempted murder_ if you **shared their specific intent to kill**, not just their plan to commit a lesser dangerous act.
