High Court of Australia

## Material Facts

- The applicant (Foster) took a loaded pistol belonging to Brian Baker from Baker’s house in **Goulburn**, **New South Wales**, without permission
- He brought the pistol to his parents’ home in **Canberra, Australian Capital Territory (ACT)**
- He was charged under [s. 189A of the Crimes Act, 1900 (N.S.W.)](https://anzlaw.thomsonreuters.com/Link/Document/FullText?refType=U5&docFamilyGuid=I297ec7b0d10b11e8a07cfaeacc13e49f&pubNum=1100190&originationContext=document&transitionType=DocumentItem&docVersion=Law+in+Force&ppcid=b5a8d0231f0444f79e0e41936f987241&contextData=\(sc.Default\)) with having in his possession in the ACT property which had been stolen (knowing it to have been stolen)
- At trail, the key dispute was whether the applicant had the **intent permanently to deprive** the owner of the pistol, or whether he only intended to “borrow” it and return it the same night
- He was convicted in the Supreme Court of the ACT
- He applied for leave to appeal to the High Court

## Issues

- Whether the offence of possessing stolen property under [s. 189A](https://anzlaw.thomsonreuters.com/Link/Document/FullText?refType=U7&docFamilyGuid=I297ec7b0d10b11e8a07cfaeacc13e49f&pubNum=1100190&originationContext=document&transitionType=DocumentItem&docVersion=Law+in+Force&ppcid=b5a8d0231f0444f79e0e41936f987241&contextData=\(sc.Default\)) of the Crimes Ordinance 1951 (A.C.T.) requires proof that applicant had the **intention permanently to deprive** the owner of the property (i.e. the full mens rea of larceny)
- Whether the trail judge erred in his summing up by failing to properly direct the jury on the necessary intent to support a conviction

## Rule/Law

- **Larceny** at common law requires a taking of property with **intent permanently to deprive** the owner of it (i.e. an intention to appropriate the property to oneself)
- [s. 189A](https://anzlaw.thomsonreuters.com/Link/Document/FullText?refType=U7&docFamilyGuid=I297ec7b0d10b11e8a07cfaeacc13e49f&pubNum=1100190&originationContext=document&transitionType=DocumentItem&docVersion=Law+in+Force&ppcid=b5a8d0231f0444f79e0e41936f987241&contextData=\(sc.Default\)) of the Crimes Ordinance 1951 (A.C.T.) makes it an offence to have in possession in the ACT property which has been stolen outside the Territory, knowing it to have been stolen. This offence requires proof that the property **was in fact stolen** (i.e. all elements of larceny must be satisfied)
- [*Crimes Act 1900(N.S.W.), s. 118](https://anzlaw.thomsonreuters.com/Link/Document/FullText?refType=U5&docFamilyGuid=I1abfe83bd10b11e8a07cfaeacc13e49f&pubNum=1100190&originationContext=document&transitionType=DocumentItem&docVersion=Law+in+Force&ppcid=b5a8d0231f0444f79e0e41936f987241&contextData=\(sc.Default\))* (applicable in the ACT): Where a person takes property with intent to deprive the owner permanently, it is no defence that he intended eventually to return the property

## Application/Analysis

- The applicant took the loaded pistol from Goulburn (NSW) without permission and brought it to Canberra (ACT)
- To convict under [s. 189A](https://anzlaw.thomsonreuters.com/Link/Document/FullText?refType=U7&docFamilyGuid=I297ec7b0d10b11e8a07cfaeacc13e49f&pubNum=1100190&originationContext=document&transitionType=DocumentItem&docVersion=Law+in+Force&ppcid=b5a8d0231f0444f79e0e41936f987241&contextData=\(sc.Default\)) of the Crimes Ordinance 1951 (A.C.T.), the Crown had to prove the pistol was **stolen** - that is, taken with **intent to permanently deprive** the owner
- The trail judge’s summing up failed to clearly direct the jury on this essential element of intent
- There was evidence that the applicant may have only intended to **borrow** the pistol temporarily (not permanently deprive the owner)
- Therefore, the jury may not have been properly instructed on the difference between larceny and unauthorised borrowing

## Conclusion

The high court allowed the appeal. The conviction was quashed.
