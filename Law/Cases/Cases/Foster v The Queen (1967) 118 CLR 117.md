High Court of Australia

## Material Facts

- The applicant (Foster) was staying in Goulburn, NSW with a watchman named Baker.
- Baker had a licensed pistol that he kept on a mantelpiece in his hallway.
- Foster took the pistol without permission and travelled to Canberra, ACT, where he showed it to his parents.
- When arrested that same day, Foster claimed he had only taken it to impress his parents and intended to return it to Baker that very evening (i.e., he was just "borrowing" it).
- Foster was charged under **s 189A** of the **_Crimes Ordinance 1951_ (ACT)** with having in his possession property stolen outside the Territory (in NSW).
- At trial, the judge directed the jury that taking the gun without permission (even just "borrowing" it) with the intent to deprive the owner of its _use_ or _possession_ was sufficient for the charge.

## Issues

- For the crime of larceny, must the Crown prove an intent to _permanently_ deprive the true owner of their property, or is an intent to temporarily assume possession ("borrowing" without permission) sufficient?
- Does **s 118** of the _Crimes Act_ relieve the Crown of the burden of proving an intention to permanently deprive?

## Rule/Law

- **Common Law Larceny:** The taking must be accompanied by an intention on the part of the accused to assume ownership of the goods, meaning an intention to deprive the true owner _permanently_ of the property in the goods. Intending to merely assume possession for a limited time does not constitute larceny.
- **s 118 _Crimes Act 1900_ (NSW) / _Crimes Ordinance 1951_ (ACT):** A statutory provision stating that if an accused "appropriated" the property to their own use but intended "eventually to restore the same," they are not entitled to an acquittal by reason only of that intended return.
- **Interpretation of s 118:** The High Court clarified that this section applies _only_ to an accused who has already actually "appropriated" the property (i.e., exercised ownership over it), not to someone who only assumed temporary possession. It is designed to prevent a thief from escaping liability by claiming they meant to give the property back eventually, but it does _not_ negate the fundamental requirement that an intention to appropriate the goods must be proven.

## Application/Analysis

- **The Misdirection:** The trial judge's directions to the jury were fundamentally erroneous. By telling the jury that the Crown only needed to prove Foster intended to deprive Baker of the _use_ or _possession_ of the gun, the judge incorrectly equated unauthorized borrowing with stealing.
- **Misunderstanding s 118:** The Crown Prosecutor incorrectly relied on **s 118** to argue that they did not need to prove an intention to permanently deprive Baker of the gun. This gave the jury an entirely wrong legal basis upon which to evaluate the case.
- **The True Issue:** The fact that the jury likely disbelieved Foster's claim that he intended to return the gun that night did not relieve the Crown of its legal burden. The jury still needed to be properly instructed that they had to find, beyond a reasonable doubt, that Foster had the intention to _permanently_ deprive Baker of the pistol at the time he took it.

## Conclusion

Leave to appeal was **granted** and the appeal was **allowed**. The High Court quashed the conviction, set aside the sentence, and ordered a **new trial** because the jury was misdirected on the fundamental elements of larceny.
