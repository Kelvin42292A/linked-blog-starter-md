Divisional Court of the Queen’s Bench Divison, England

## Material Facts

- **The Shipwreck:** Three seamen (Dudley, Stephens, and Brooks) and a 17-year-old cabin boy (Richard Parker) were cast away in an open lifeboat 1,600 miles from land with virtually no food or water.
- **The Starvation:** After 20 days of drifting and going 8 days without food and 5 days without water, the boy was extremely weak, helpless, and near death.
- **The Killing:** Dudley, with Stephens’ agreement, killed the helpless boy so they could feed on his flesh to survive. Brooks dissented but also fed on the body. They were rescued four days later.
- **The Defense:** They argued they acted out of absolute **necessity** to preserve their own lives.

## Issues

Can extreme hunger or the threat of starvation constitute a legal "necessity" that excuses the deliberate killing of an innocent person to save others?

## Rule/Law

- **No Necessity for Murder:** The common law does **not recognize necessity as a defense to murder**.
- **No Absolute Duty of Self-Preservation:** There is no absolute legal or moral duty to preserve one's own life; in many circumstances, sacrifice is a higher duty.
- **Protection of the Innocent:** You cannot legally kill an innocent, unoffending, and unresisting person to save yourself.

## Application/Analysis

- **No Special Excuse:** While the temptation and physical suffering were extreme, they do not constitute legal "necessity".
- **Unfair Selection:** Admitting such a defense would be incredibly dangerous, allowing individuals to arbitrarily decide whose life is worth more. There was no greater necessity to kill the weak cabin boy than any of the grown men.

## Conclusion

The court held that Dudley and Stephens were **guilty of murder** and sentenced them to death (which was later commuted by the Crown to six months' imprisonment).

### Judges Rules

- **Necessity Cannot Justify Murder:** Under common law, the defense of necessity can **never** be used to justify or excuse the deliberate taking of an innocent person's life, regardless of how extreme the circumstances.
- **Self-Preservation is Not Paramount:** The law does not recognize an absolute right to save your own life at the expense of an innocent neighbor.
- **Equal Value of Human Life:** No person has the legal authority to measure the comparative value of lives or sacrifice the weakest or most vulnerable member of a group for their own survival.
