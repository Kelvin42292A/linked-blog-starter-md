Supreme Court of Victoria

## Material Facts

- **The Escape:** The applicant, a prison inmate, was convicted of escaping from prison.
- **The Threat:** At his trial, he raised the defense of necessity, claiming he escaped because he honestly feared that other prisoners were going to attack and kill him.
- **The Trial Ruling:** The trial judge ruled that there was no evidence fit to be left to the jury regarding the defense of necessity and kept the defense from them. The prisoner applied for leave to appeal his conviction.

## Issues

Under what conditions does the common law recognize the defense of necessity to excuse a crime (such as prison escape), and did the trial judge err in keeping this defense from the jury?

## Rule/Law

The common law recognizes the defense of necessity, which requires three essential elements:

1. **Irreparable Evil:** The criminal act must have been done only to avoid certain consequences which would have inflicted irreparable evil upon the accused or others they were bound to protect.
2. **Imminent Peril:** The accused must honestly believe on reasonable grounds that they were placed in a position of imminent peril.
3. **Proportionality:** The physical acts done to avoid the peril must not be out of proportion to the peril to be avoided.

## Application/Analysis

- **Lack of Proportionate Evidence:** The majority of the Court (Young CJ and Crockett J) held that there was no evidence upon which a jury could have reasonably found that the applicant's escape was proportionate to the threat he claimed to fear.
- **Keeping the Defense from the Jury:** Because a key element of the defense (proportionality) was completely unsupported by the evidence, the trial judge was legally correct to withhold the defense of necessity from the jury's consideration.

## Conclusion

The application for leave to appeal was dismissed, and the conviction was upheld.

### Judges Rules

- **The Three-Part Cumulative Test:** To successfully raise the defense of necessity, a defendant must satisfy three strict requirements: they acted only to avoid **irreparable evil**, they held an honest and reasonable belief in **imminent peril**, and their criminal act was **proportionate** to the threat.
- **Proportionality as a Threshold Rule:** A court will not allow a jury to consider the defense of necessity if the defendant's criminal response is wildly out of proportion to the threatened harm. The response must be a balanced, last-resort action to protect life or safety.
- **Statutory Interpretation Principle:** Just because a criminal statute fails to explicitly include words like "unlawfully" or "without excuse" does not mean common law defenses (like necessity) are automatically excluded.
