Supreme Court of New South Wales

## Material Facts

- Andrew (AV) gave Vicki (VP) an engagement ring worth about $15,250 in contemplation of marriage.
- Ten days later, VP called off the wedding, told AV "take the ring, I don't want it," and placed it on the table in front of him.
- AV responded, "I do not want the ring it is a gift for you, you can keep it," and left without taking it.
- VP boxed up the ring and later told AV to come and pick it up.
- Eventually, VP's father (on her behalf) threw the box containing the $15,250 ring into the garbage bin.
- AV sued VP in detinue to recover the ring or its value, and the Local Court magistrate ordered VP to pay the value of the ring. VP appealed to the Supreme Court.

## Issues

1. **Nature of the Gift:** Did AV's statement ("you can keep it") transform the engagement ring from a _conditional_ gift into an _absolute_ gift?
2. **Duties of a Bailee:** If a person rejects a gift but retains physical control of it, what are their legal obligations regarding that property?
3. **Estoppel:** Was AV legally blocked (estopped) from claiming the ring back because he told her to keep it and left it at her house?

## Rule/Law

- **Engagement Rings (_Cohen v Sellar 1 KB 536_):** An engagement ring is a conditional gift. If a woman who has received a ring in contemplation of marriage refuses to fulfil the conditions of the gift (i.e., refuses to carry out the promise of marriage without a recognised legal justification), she must return the ring.
- **The Law of Bailment (_Papathanasopoulos v Vacopoulos NSWSC 502_):** A party cannot be forced to take or accept a gift. However, as Justice Smart AJ established in this very case, if a person rejects a gift but retains physical control of it, they become a **bailee** of that item. A bailee cannot intentionally cause the bailed item to be thrown into the garbage bin, especially a valuable item.
- **Estoppel (_Thompson v Palmer (1933)_ & _Grundt v The Great Boulder Pty Gold Mines Ltd (1937)_):** A party is not permitted unjustly to depart from a representation by words or conduct of an existing fact if the other party has acted in reliance on it. (The court also cited *Hoath v Connect Internet Services Pty Ltd * for this principle). However, the reliance must be legally permissible, and a bailee destroying property is not a permissible form of reliance.

## Application/Analysis

- **The Gift Remained Conditional:** The Supreme Court agreed with the magistrate that AV's statement ("you can keep it") happened in a highly emotional environment where he was desperately trying to preserve the relationship. It did not change the essential legal nature of the ring from a conditional engagement gift to an absolute "keep it no matter what" gift.
- **Breach of Bailment:** Because VP was the one who called off the wedding, she had no right to keep the ring. When she rejected the gift but kept it in her house, she became a bailee. By having her father throw the ring into the garbage bin, she severely breached her duties as a bailee. You cannot legally tell an owner to "collect it from the garbage".
- **No Estoppel:** AV was not estopped from suing her. Her action of throwing the ring away out of spite was not a valid or legal way to "rely" on AV leaving it on the table.

## Conclusion

VP's appeal was **dismissed**. The court ruled in favor of the plaintiff (AV). Because the ring was destroyed/lost and could not be physically returned, VP was ordered to pay AV the monetary value of the ring, plus his legal costs.
