## Material Facts

The House of Representatives passed a Bill on 12 December 1973 and sent it to the Senate on 13 December (the last sitting day of the year). The Senate immediately read the Bill once but then adjourned the debate until the next year (February 1974). The Senate finally rejected the Bill on 2 April 1974. Only six days later, on 8 April 1974, the House passed it again. The Bill was eventually passed at a Joint Sitting following a double dissolution, but Victoria challenged its validity, arguing the constitutional rules were violated.

## Issues

1. Did the Senate "fail to pass" the Bill on 13 December 1973 simply by adjourning the debate?
2. Does the constitutional three-month waiting period run from when the House first passes a Bill, or from when the Senate rejects/fails to pass it?
3. Can the High Court rule on whether Parliament followed proper constitutional lawmaking procedures?

## Rule/Law

- **Justiciability:** The High Court has the power to check if Parliament followed constitutional lawmaking procedures. The rules in s 57 of the Constitution are **mandatory**; if they are not followed, the law is invalid.
- **"Fails to Pass":** This does not mean "does not pass instantly". The Senate must be allowed a **reasonable time** to debate and consider a Bill before it can be legally said to have "failed to pass".
- **The 3-Month Interval:** The three-month waiting period before the House can pass a Bill a second time **runs from the date of the Senate's rejection or failure to pass**, not from when the House first passed it.

## Application/Analysis

- The Senate did not fail to pass the Bill on 13 December 1973. It had only just received it that day, and adjourning debate to the next session was a normal part of the deliberative process.
- Therefore, the Senate's first failure to pass/rejection occurred on 2 April 1974. Because the House passed the Bill again on 8 April 1974, the mandatory three-month waiting period had not elapsed.

## Conclusion

The High Court held that the Act was invalid because Parliament failed to wait the required three months.

### Judges Rules

- **Rule 1: High Court as the Guardian of the Constitution** The High Court has the power and duty to oversee Parliament’s lawmaking procedures. If Parliament ignores the mandatory requirements of the Constitution (such as s 57), the courts will declare the resulting law invalid.
- **Rule 2: The Deliberative Role of the Senate** The Senate is a co-equal house, not a rubber stamp. "Failure to pass" only occurs after the Senate has been given a **reasonable opportunity** to debate, negotiate, or take a definite stand on a Bill, yet unreasonably delays doing so.
- **Rule 3: Calculation of the 3-Month Interval** Under s 57, the three-month deadlock-resolving interval strictly **starts when the Senate rejects, fails to pass, or unacceptably amends a Bill**—never when the House of Representatives first passes it.
