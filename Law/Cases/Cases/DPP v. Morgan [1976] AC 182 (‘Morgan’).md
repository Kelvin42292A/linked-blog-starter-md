House of lords

## Material Facts

- The appellant (Morgan) invited three younger men to his home to have sexual intercourse with his wife.
- Morgan told the men that his wife was "kinky" and that any resistance she put up would just be play-acting to stimulate her own sexual excitement.
- The men arrived, dragged the sleeping wife into another room, and held her limbs apart. All four men had intercourse with her despite her violent struggles and screams for the police.
- At trial, the men admitted the physical acts but claimed the wife actively and enthusiastically participated in a "sexual orgy." The jury completely rejected this story as a lie and convicted them.

## Issues

- Can a defendant be convicted of rape if he honestly believed the woman was consenting, but that belief was completely _unreasonable_?
- Should the convictions be upheld despite the judge's incorrect legal instructions, because there was no actual miscarriage of justice?

## Rule/Law

- Under **s 1 of the Sexual Offences Act 1956**, it is an offence for a man to rape a woman.
- Rape consists of having unlawful sexual intercourse with a woman without her consent by force, fear, or fraud (_based on 1 Hale's Pleas of the Crown 627_).
- The required mental element in rape is the intention to have intercourse without the woman's consent, or recklessly not caring whether she consents (_based on Reg. v. Wright_).
- If a man genuinely believes a woman is consenting, he lacks the intent to rape. Unlike bigamy (_which requires reasonable grounds under Reg. v. Tolson_), in a rape case, this honest belief **does not need to be based on reasonable grounds** to be a valid defence.
- Under the proviso to **s 2(1) of the Criminal Appeal Act 1968**, an appellate court can uphold a conviction despite a legal misdirection by the trial judge if no miscarriage of justice actually occurred.

## Application/Analysis

- The trial judge made an error of law. He instructed the jury that for the defendants to be acquitted, their mistaken belief in the wife's consent had to be both honest _and reasonable_. The House of Lords ruled this was wrong: an honest belief, even if foolish and unreasonable, is a full defence because it cancels out the intent to rape.
- Even though the judge gave the wrong legal rule, the House of Lords found it did not matter on these specific facts.
- Because the defendants lied in court by claiming the victim enthusiastically participated in an orgy—a story the jury entirely rejected—and because of the undeniable violence used, no reasonable jury could ever conclude that the men _honestly_ believed she was consenting.

## Conclusion

The appeals were **dismissed** and the convictions were upheld. The House of Lords established the major legal rule that an honest belief in consent does not have to be reasonable, but they applied the proviso to uphold the guilty verdicts because the men clearly held no such honest belief.
