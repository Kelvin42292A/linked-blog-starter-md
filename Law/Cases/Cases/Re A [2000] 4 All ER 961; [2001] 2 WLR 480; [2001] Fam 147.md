England and Wales Court of Appeal (Civil Division)

### Material Facts

The Twins: Conjoined twins were born sharing a common aorta. One twin was relatively healthy, while the other's body was completely incapable of sustaining independent life.

The Dilemma: Surgical separation would result in the certain death of the unhealthy twin. However, if the surgery was not performed, the healthy twin's heart would fail, resulting in the inevitable death of both twins within a short period.

The Dispute: The hospital sought to perform the surgery to save the healthy twin. The parents, on religious grounds, refused to give their consent.

### Issues

Can doctors lawfully perform a life-saving separation surgery without parental consent when they know it will directly cause the death of one conjoined twin?

Is the defense of necessity available to protect the doctors from a charge of murder?

### Rule/Law

An operation that causes the certain death of one person to save another can be lawfully performed under the defense of necessity if:

It is impossible to preserve the life of the viable person without bringing about the death of the other.

The non-viable person, by their very continued existence, would inevitably cause the death of the viable person within a short period of time.

The viable person is capable of living an independent life, but the other is completely incapable of a viable independent existence under any circumstances (including all medical interventions).

### Application/Analysis

Inevitable Double Death: Without the surgery, both twins will die very quickly.

No Viability for One: The weaker twin has no possibility of independent survival under any circumstances, meaning the surgery is not depriving them of a viable life.

Choosing Life over Death: In this unique conflict of interests, performing the surgery is the only way to save the one life that actually has a chance to survive. Therefore, the doctors' actions are justified by necessity.

### Conclusion

The Court of Appeal ruled that the separation surgery could be lawfully performed without parental consent, and the doctors would have a valid defense of necessity.

#### Judges Rules

Necessity in Extreme Conflict: While necessity is generally not a defense to murder, a narrow exception exists where two lives are physically bound together in a way that will inevitably kill both. If one life is entirely non-viable, the law permits a protective intervention to save the viable life.

Court Can Override Parents: When there is an objective, irreconcilable conflict between a child's best interests and their parents' religious objections, the court has the authority to override the parents' decision to preserve a child's life.
