Royal Courts of Justice
## Material Facts

- The appellant (Nedrick) had a grudge against a woman named Viola Foreshaw and threatened to "burn her out".
- In the early hours of the morning, he poured paraffin through her letterbox and set the front door on fire without giving any warning.
- The house burned down, and a 12-year-old boy inside died from asphyxiation and burns.
- The appellant confessed to starting the fire but claimed his only motive was "just to wake her up and frighten her." He explicitly stated, "I didn't want anyone to die".
- At trial, the judge directed the jury that if the appellant knew it was "highly probable" his act would cause serious bodily injury, that was sufficient to prove the intent for murder. He was convicted of murder.

## Issues

- Did the trial judge misdirect the jury by equating foresight of a "high probability" of serious injury with actual intention?
- In cases where a defendant performs a manifestly dangerous act but claims they had no _desire_ or motive to harm anyone, how should a jury be directed on the mental element (intent) required for murder?

## Rule/Law

- **The Intent for Murder:** The _mens rea_ for murder strictly requires an intention to kill or do serious bodily harm.
- **_Moloney_ and _Hancock_ (Applied):** The House of Lords established that foreseeing a consequence (even a highly probable one) is not the exact same thing as intending it. Foresight is merely evidence from which a jury _might_ infer intention.
- **The "Virtual Certainty" Test:** A jury is not entitled to infer the necessary intention for murder unless they feel sure of two things:
    1. Death or serious bodily harm was a **virtual certainty** (barring some unforeseen intervention) as a result of the defendant's actions.
    2. The defendant actually appreciated (subjectively knew) that this was the case.

## Application/Analysis

- **The Misdirection:** The trial judge's instruction was wrong because it set the bar too low. Telling the jury that a "high probability" of serious injury equals murder contradicts the strict rules set in _Moloney_ and _Hancock_.
- **Intention vs. Desire:** The Court clarified that a person can intend a result without actually desiring it (e.g., a man who boards a plane to Manchester just to escape pursuers still _intends_ to go to Manchester, even if he doesn't _want_ to be there).
- **Drawing the Inference:** If a defendant realizes that it is practically inevitable (a virtual certainty) that their dangerous act will result in death or serious harm, the jury can easily infer that they intended that result, no matter how little they actually desired it to happen. However, the jury must be properly directed on this "virtual certainty" standard, which didn't happen here.

### Conclusion

Because the jury was misdirected with the "highly probable" test rather than the "virtual certainty" test, the murder conviction was unsafe. The Court of Appeal substituted the verdict of murder for a conviction of **manslaughter** and sentenced the appellant to 15 years' imprisonment
