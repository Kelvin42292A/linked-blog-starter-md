Victoria Court of Criminal Appeal

## Material Facts

- The appellant (Meyers) and the deceased (Tracey McNamara) had a violent, drunken argument at his home.
- Neighbours heard screaming, the deceased pleading "Stop, stop, you're hurting me", and a series of loud thumps or bangs. The appellant was heard yelling, "I'll fix you..." and "Get off the fucking floor, you weak bitch".
- The deceased was found unconscious on the floor with clumps of her hair pulled out underneath her head, and died in hospital from severe intra-cranial head injuries.
- The appellant claimed he only pushed her to get to the phone, and she must have accidentally hit her head on a table or the floor. He argued he did not have the specific intent to cause "really serious injury" at the exact moment she fell.

## Issues

1. **Coincidence of Act and Intent:** In a prolonged fight, does the prosecution have to prove the accused had the intent to cause really serious injury at the _exact moment_ the fatal blow was struck?
2. **Inferring Intent:** Can the jury look at the entire altercation to figure out the intent behind the specific act that caused the death?

## Rule/Law

- **Act and Intent Must Coincide:** The required intent for murder must exist at the exact time the fatal act is committed.
    - _Quoted Cases:_ Citing **_Ryan v R_ (1967)** and **_Royall v R_ (1991)**, the Court affirmed: "Act and intent must coincide".
- **Specific Intent for the Fatal Act:** If a fight involves many different acts, "no conviction for murder can be returned unless there is evidence on which the jury can reasonably find that the act which caused the death was one of those done with the necessary intent".
- **Inferring Intent from Circumstances:** The jury is allowed to look at the "whole of the circumstances" to determine the intent behind the fatal act.
- **The Rational Inference Test:** An accused can be convicted if intending really serious injury is "the only rational inference to draw from the proved circumstances" (citing **_Barca v R_ (1975)**).

_(Note: The court accepted that in Victoria, "really serious injury" is used as the modern synonym for the traditional term "grievous bodily harm")._

## Application/Analysis

- **Looking at the Whole Picture:** The appellant argued he only had a minor intent (just pushing her away). If true, he would only be guilty of manslaughter. However, the physical evidence—the series of loud bangs, the pulled hair under her head, and her screams—did not match a simple, accidental fall.
- **Proving the Intent of the Fatal Blow:** By looking at the entire sequence of events, the jury had a sufficient foundation to rationally infer that the appellant didn't just push her; he repeatedly banged her head against a hard surface. This proved that at the exact moment he inflicted the fatal head injury, he had the specific intent to cause her really serious injury.

## Conclusion

The appeal was **dismissed**. The High Court upheld the murder conviction because it was perfectly reasonable for the jury to look at the whole fight and infer he had the necessary intent when he caused the fatal injury.

### Judges Rule

- **Exact Timing:** The physical act and the mental intent must happen at the exact same time. The court's golden rule is: "Act and intent must coincide".
- **The Fatal Strike:** In a messy fight, it is not enough to have bad intentions at _some_point. You must have the intent to cause serious harm at the **exact second** you deal the specific fatal blow.
