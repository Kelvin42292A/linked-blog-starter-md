High Court of Australia

## Material Facts

- **The Killing:** Gerald Preston entered a car repair workshop with a loaded gun and shot two men dead (including Les Knowles) and wounded a third.
- **The Accomplice's Role:** Kevin Gillard stole a van, disguised himself, drove Preston to the workshop, and called ahead to confirm the victim was there. He waited in the driver's seat of the van as a get-away driver and later destroyed the van.
- **The Robbery Hypothesis:** Gillard did not give evidence but claimed in a police interview that he had no idea Preston intended to kill anyone or was carrying a gun; he believed they were only executing a robbery.
- **The Trial Rulings:** At trial, the prosecution argued that even if Gillard only intended robbery, a verdict of manslaughter should be left to the jury. Gillard’s defense counsel strongly resisted this, preferring a "murder or acquittal" choice. The trial judge agreed and did not instruct the jury on manslaughter. Gillard was convicted of murder.
- **The Appeal:** Gillard appealed, arguing the trial judge erred by not leaving manslaughter to the jury. The Full Court dismissed his appeal, and he appealed to the High Court.

## Issues

1. Was there a viable case of manslaughter to be left to the jury under the doctrine of common purpose on the armed robbery hypothesis?
2. Does the failure to leave a viable manslaughter verdict to the jury constitute a substantial miscarriage of justice, even if the jury was properly instructed on murder and chose to convict?

## Rule/Law

- **Common Purpose Culpability:** Criminal liability under a joint criminal enterprise depends on the scope of the shared criminal design and what the secondary participant subjectively foresees as a possible incident of carrying out that design.
- **Differential Verdicts (Murder vs. Manslaughter):** If a joint enterprise involves violence, and the level of violence contemplated by one participant exceeds that contemplated by the other, they can be convicted of different crimes.
- **The Manslaughter Threshold:** A secondary participant is guilty of manslaughter (rather than murder) if they foresaw as a possibility that the principal offender might use a loaded weapon during the crime, but did **not** foresee that the principal would do so with an intent to kill or cause grievous bodily harm.
- **Duty to Instruct on Lesser Verdicts:** A trial judge has a strict legal duty to instruct the jury on a viable lesser verdict (like manslaughter) if it is open on the evidence, even if the defense lawyer explicitly objects to it.
- **No Proviso for Depriving Choice:** An appellate court cannot dismiss a misdirection as having "no substantial miscarriage of justice" if the jury was deprived of an intermediate verdict like manslaughter. Juries cannot be forced into an all-or-nothing choice between murder and total acquittal when a middle ground is legally open.

## Application/Analysis

- **A Viable Manslaughter Case:** The jury could have concluded that Gillard knew Preston was carrying a loaded gun but honestly believed they were only committing an armed robbery.
- **Foresight of the Gun's Use:** Pointing or presenting a loaded gun in a robbery is an unlawful and dangerous act. If Gillard foresaw the possibility that the gun might be fired, but did not foresee that Preston would do so with murderous intent, Gillard’s legal liability was strictly manslaughter.
- **A Lost Chance at Justice:** Depriving the jury of the intermediate option of manslaughter was a fatal error. Forced to choose between convicting a getaway driver of murder or letting him go entirely free, the jury may have chosen a murder conviction to avoid an outright acquittal, creating a severe miscarriage of justice.

## Conclusion

The High Court allowed the appeal, quashed Gillard's convictions, and ordered a new trial.

### Judges Rules

- **Same Physical Act, Different Crimes:** Participants in a joint criminal enterprise do **not** have to receive the same verdict. One participant can be convicted of murder while the other is convicted of manslaughter based on their differing intentions and foresight.
- **Subjective Foresight Governs Liability:** Your criminal liability for an additional act committed by your partner is determined strictly by what you **subjectively foresaw as a possible consequence** of continuing to participate in the enterprise.
- **The Manslaughter Standard for Weapons:** If you participate in an enterprise knowing a loaded weapon will be used to intimidate (e.g., an armed robbery), you are guilty of **manslaughter** if your partner kills the victim, provided you did not subjectively foresee that they would shoot with an intent to kill or cause serious bodily harm.
- **Shared Actions, Not Shared Minds:** A joint enterprise does **not** require a perfect "meeting of minds" regarding the ultimate crime or motive. It is enough that you agreed on the **physical actions** to be performed (e.g., confronting someone with a loaded weapon), even if you both had completely different intentions for why you were doing it.
- **The Jury Must Have the Choice:** If the evidence open at trial supports a lesser charge like manslaughter, the trial judge **must** offer it to the jury. This duty exists even if your defense lawyer objects to the instruction to try and force a total acquittal.
