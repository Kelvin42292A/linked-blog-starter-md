Supreme Court of New South Wales

## Material Facts

- **The Accused and the Charge:** Jackey, an Aboriginal man also known as Wong-ko-bi-kan, was indicted for the wilful murder of a white settler, John Flynn, by wounding him with a spear at William's River.
- **The Armed Raid:** Following an alleged attack on a nearby station, a constable and a group of settlers armed themselves without a warrant and tracked Jackey's tribe to their camp.
- **The Incident:** When the armed party arrived at the camp, Jackey threw a spear that struck Flynn under the shoulder blade. Flynn died of the wound days later.
- **The Trial Objections:** Jackey's defense counsel, Mr. G.R. Nichols, objected to the court's jurisdiction, arguing that Aboriginal people were the primary tenants of the soil and that the conflict was "open warfare" between two armed parties rather than a civil crime. Jackey also requested to be tried by a "jury of black-fellows," which the court ruled it had no power to grant.

## Issues

1. **Jurisdiction:** Does the colonial court have criminal jurisdiction over an Indigenous person for a conflict occurring on traditional lands with British settlers?
2. **Mitigation (Provocation):** Does an unauthorized, armed pursuit of an Indigenous tribe by settlers constitute legal provocation sufficient to reduce a charge of murder to manslaughter?

## Rule/Law

- **Equal Application of Law:** Under colonial law, Indigenous people are considered subjects of the Crown and are entitled to the equal protection and liabilities of English criminal law.
- **Provocation by Armed Pursuit:** A homicide is mitigated from murder to manslaughter if it occurs during a clash where the victim was part of an armed group actively pursuing the accused, as this show of hostility constitutes legal provocation.
- **Excusable Homicide:** To claim excusable self-defense, there must be a visible disposition on the part of the accused to avoid the encounter or fly away from the attack.

## Application/Analysis

- **Crown Jurisdiction Confirmed:** Chief Justice Forbes rejected the defense's "open warfare" argument, ruling that the protection of life is a reciprocal obligation under both the law of nature and English law, meaning the court had full authority to try the prisoner.
- **Hostility as Provocation:** The Chief Justice ruled that the charge could not be wilful murder because the armed settlers went in pursuit of the tribe, creating a "show of hostility" that amounted to legal provocation.
- **No Self-Defense:** However, the act was not excusable homicide (self-defense) because the tribe showed no disposition to retreat or avoid the encounter, but rather prepared for a fight. Therefore, the appropriate charge to put to the jury was manslaughter.

## Conclusion

The jury found Jackey guilty of **manslaughter** and recommended him to mercy. The Chief Justice sentenced Jackey to be **transported from the Colony for the term of his natural life**.

### Judges Rules

Indigenous inhabitants are considered subjects of the Crown and are fully subject to the jurisdiction and protections of colonial criminal law. However, if a settler is killed during an unauthorized, armed pursuit of an Indigenous group, the pursuers' show of hostility constitutes legal provocation that mitigates the killing from murder to manslaughter
