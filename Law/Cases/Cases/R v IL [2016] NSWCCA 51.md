NSW Court of Criminal Appeal

## Material Facts

- The respondent (IL) and the deceased (Mr Lan) were engaged in a joint criminal enterprise (JCE) to manufacture a large commercial quantity of methylamphetamine (a foundational crime punishable by life imprisonment).
- During the manufacturing process in a small bathroom, a ring burner was ignited, causing a fire that killed Mr Lan.
- The Crown could not prove whether IL or Mr Lan actually lit the burner.
- At the original trial, the judge directed the jury to acquit IL of murder and manslaughter. The judge reasoned that IL's liability was merely "derivative" (meaning she was just an accessory to Mr Lan's actions) and because Mr Lan could not be guilty of his own murder, IL could not be guilty either.
- The Crown appealed this directed acquittal under **s 107(2) of the _Crimes (Appeal and Review) Act 2001_**.

## Issues

1. **Constructive Murder & JCE:** Did the trial judge apply the wrong legal test by finding that IL's liability was "derivative" and that she could not be guilty of constructive murder if the deceased accidentally killed himself?
2. **Manslaughter:** Did the judge err in finding no case for manslaughter simply because there was no evidence IL and Mr Lan physically "acted together" to light the burner?

## Rule/Law

- **Constructive Murder (Statute):** Defined under **s 18(1)(a) of the _Crimes Act 1900 (NSW)_**, which states murder is committed where the act causing death is done "during or immediately after the commission, by the accused, or some accomplice with him or her, of a crime punishable by imprisonment for life or for 25 years".
- **Joint Criminal Enterprise (Direct Liability):** The liability of participants in a JCE is direct and equal for acts within the scope of the enterprise.
    - _Quoted Case:_ Relying on **_McAuliffe v The Queen_ HCA 37**, the court affirmed: "If one or other of the parties to the understanding or arrangement does... all those things which are necessary to constitute the crime, they are all equally guilty of the crime regardless of the part played by each in its commission".
- **Malicious Act (Statute & Common Law):** To be murder, the act must be "malicious" under **s 18(2)(a) of the _Crimes Act 1900 (NSW)_**.
    - _Quoted Case:_ Adopting the rule from **_R v Cunningham_ 2 QB 396**, the court noted that "the word 'maliciously' in a statutory crime postulates foresight of consequence" or "recklessness as to whether such harm should occur or not".

## Application/Analysis

- **The Trial Judge's Error:** The NSWCCA found the trial judge completely missed the point by focusing on whether IL intended or foresaw Mr Lan's _death_. The correct question under JCE was simply whether the _act of lighting the burner_ was within the scope of their drug manufacturing enterprise.
- **Direct, Not Derivative Liability:** Because lighting the burner was an agreed part of making the drugs, both IL and Mr Lan were equally and directly responsible for that physical act, regardless of who actually struck the match. Therefore, IL's liability was not "derivative" of Mr Lan's suicide; it was her own direct liability for an act committed during a life-imprisonment felony.
- **Manslaughter Error:** For manslaughter by an unlawful and dangerous act, it was legally irrelevant whether they physically acted together to light the burner. As long as lighting the burner was contemplated as part of the drug enterprise, IL was responsible for the act.

## Conclusion

The Crown's appeal was **allowed**. The trial judge's directed verdicts of acquittal for both murder and manslaughter were **quashed**. The Court ordered a **new trial** on the charges of murder and manslaughter.

### Judges Rule

- **The Act:** You are guilty of murder if you do a "malicious" (intentional or reckless) act that causes a death while committing a serious crime punishable by 25 years or life in prison.
- **Must Kill Someone Else:** Constructive murder strictly requires the killing of _another_ person _(as established in the 2017 High Court ruling from our previous discussion)_.
- **Self-Killing is Exempt:** If your accomplice accidentally kills _themselves_ during your shared crime, you cannot be charged with their murder because killing oneself is no longer a legal offence _(from our previous discussion)_.
