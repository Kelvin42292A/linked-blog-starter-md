Court of Appeal, Criminal Division (UK)

## Material Facts

- The appellant stabbed his wife nine times with a kitchen knife, resulting in a deep penetrating wound to her abdomen.
- She underwent surgery to repair her intestine and appeared to be making an uneventful recovery.
- Days later, she suddenly collapsed due to a massive pulmonary embolism (a blood clot), which is a common complication of major abdominal surgery.
- During emergency surgery to remove the clot, her heart stopped beating for about 30 minutes, causing severe anoxic damage to her brain. She was subsequently placed on a ventilator (life support machine).
- After her condition deteriorated and medical tests confirmed she had suffered irretrievable brain damage, doctors consulted with her relatives and made the decision to disconnect the life support machine. She was declared dead shortly after.
- At trial, the appellant was convicted of murder. The trial judge withdrew the issue of causation from the jury, leaving them only to decide the issue of intent.
- The appellant appealed the conviction, arguing that the judge had been wrong to withdraw the issue of causation from the jury. He argued there was evidence that the doctors, by switching off the life support machine, were the actual cause of death, rather than his initial assault.

## Issues

1. **Breaking the Chain:** Did the doctors break the "chain of causation" when they decided to turn off the victim's life support machine?
2. **Who Caused the Death?:** Is an attacker allowed to blame the doctors for causing the death, just because the doctors turned off the machines after their medical efforts failed?
3. **The Judge's Role:** Was the trial judge right to take the question of causation away from the jury because the answer was medically and legally obvious?

## Rule/Law

- **Discontinuing Life Support (_The Malcherek Rule_):** If doctors act in good faith and use accepted medical criteria to conclude a patient is practically dead, their decision to disconnect a life support machine **does not** break the chain of causation. The attacker remains responsible.
- **Operating and Substantial Cause (_R v Smith_):** If the original wound is still an "operating and a substantial cause" at the time of death, then the attacker caused the death.
- **Abnormal Medical Treatment (_R v Jordan_):** This is a highly exceptional rule. Medical treatment will only break the chain of causation if the original wound has practically healed, and the subsequent medical treatment is "palpably wrong" and abnormal.
- **The Judge's Power (_R v Blaue_):** If there is no conflict in the medical evidence about how the victim died, the trial judge is entitled to withdraw the issue of causation from the jury and simply direct them that the attacker's wound was the operative cause of death.

## Application/Analysis

- **The Continuing Cause:** At the time the doctors disconnected the life support machine, the original stab wound was still a "continuing, operating and indeed substantial cause" of the victim's death. A doctor trying their conscientious best to save a patient, but ultimately failing and discontinuing treatment, does not break the chain of causation.
- **Normal Medical Care:** There was no evidence that the doctors mistreated the deceased or gave abnormal care. Because the medical treatment was "normal and conventional," the attacker could not blame the doctors for causing the death.
- **The Judge Was Right:** Because there was no medical evidence to suggest that the attacker did _not_ cause the death, the trial judge was completely correct to withdraw the issue of causation from the jury.

## Conclusion

The appeal was **dismissed**. The murder conviction was upheld. The Court ruled that the doctors turning off the life support machine did not break the chain of causation.
