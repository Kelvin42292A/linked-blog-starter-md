Court of Criminal Appeal

## Material Facts

- The respondents (Desmond and Hall) broke into a bakery at night to steal money from a safe.
- A night watchman (Scott) and a maintenance engineer (Lai) were on duty.
- The respondents attacked the two men, overpowered them, blindfolded them, tied their hands and feet, and locked them in a lavatory.
- The respondents then broke into the cash office, which was 33 yards away from the lavatory and separated by two locked doors.
- Using an oxy-acetylene burner, they broke open the safe and stole over £10,000.
- While imprisoned for over three hours, Scott and Lai heard the sounds of breaking glass, heavy banging, and the burner.
- The trial judge convicted the respondents of robbery with violence, but the Court of Criminal Appeal (CCA) reduced the convictions to simple larceny, reasoning that because the victims were locked in a distant room, the goods were not technically taken "in their presence". The Crown appealed.

## Issues

- For the common law offence of robbery, must the victim be physically present (e.g., in the same room) at the time of the actual taking, or does taking goods "in the presence of" the victim extend to situations where the victim is forcibly removed from the property they are guarding?
- Is the victim's ability to see or hear the theft (their "awareness") the correct legal test for "presence"?

## Rule/Law

- **Common Law Robbery:** The essence of robbery is stealing by violence; it combines an offence against the property with an offence against the person. The taking must be from the victim's person or **"in his presence"**.
- **Constructive Presence:** Extending the strict definition, early legal authorities (like Hawkins and Hale) established that taking property from someone's **"immediate and personal care and protection"** constructively qualifies as taking it from their person or in their presence.
- **The "One Transaction" Principle:** If violence or putting in fear is used to overcome the resistance of a custodian, and the theft follows as part of the same planned sequence, it constitutes robbery.
- _**Reg. v. Selway and Wynn**_ **(Discussed):** An older case which suggested the victim needed to be "aware" of the goods being stolen for them to be under their protection. The House of Lords modified this approach, ruling that a strict test of sensory awareness is arbitrary and inappropriate.

## Application/Analysis

- **Rejecting the "Awareness" Test:** The House of Lords rejected the CCA's narrow view and the trial judge's reliance on whether the victims could _hear_ the safe being blown. The law does not require the victim to be perceptually aware of the exact moment of the theft. It would be absurd if a thief could escape a robbery charge simply by blindfolding the victim, rendering them unconscious, or dragging them out of earshot before stealing the goods.
- **The True Test - Custodianship:** The correct approach is to ask whether the victim was the custodian of the property and whether violence was used to oblige them to submit to the theft.
- **Applying the Facts:** Scott and Lai were in the building specifically to guard and protect its contents. The safe was under their immediate and personal care and protection. The respondents deliberately applied continuous force to overpower the men because they stood between the thieves and the money. Because the violence and the theft were inextricably linked as one planned transaction, the thieves' act of forcibly removing the guards meant the money was legally taken "in their presence".

## Conclusion

The appeal by the Crown was **allowed**. The House of Lords ruled that forcibly removing a custodian to a different location to facilitate a theft satisfies the requirement of taking property "in the presence" of the victim. The CCA's verdicts of simple larceny were reversed, and the original convictions and sentences for **robbery with violence were restored**.
