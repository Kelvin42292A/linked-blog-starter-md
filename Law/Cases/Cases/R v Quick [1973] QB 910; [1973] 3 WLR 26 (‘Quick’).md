## Material Facts

Quick, a diabetic nurse, took his prescribed insulin but drank alcohol and ate very little food. He had a severe low blood sugar episode (hypoglycaemia) and violently assaulted a paraplegic patient while unconscious.

## Issues

Is a temporary mental blackout caused by taking prescribed insulin a "disease of the mind" (insanity), or is it automatism (which allows a full acquittal)?

## Rule/Law

A "disease of the mind" is a malfunctioning of the mind caused by an internal sickness. It does **not** include temporary mental malfunctions caused by an **external factor** applied to the body, such as drugs, alcohol, or physical violence.

## Application/Analysis

Quick’s mental blackout was caused by an external factor (the drug insulin), not his internal disease of diabetes. Therefore, his condition was not a "disease of the mind".

## Conclusion

Quick’s conviction was quashed. He was entitled to have the defence of automatism left to the jury.

### Judges Rules

To decide if a mental blackout is legally a "disease of the mind" (insanity) or "automatism" (complete excuse), courts use the **Internal vs. External Test**:

- **Internal Causes (Disease of the Mind):** The mental malfunction must come from an **internal disease or bodily disorder**.
- **External Causes (Not a Disease of the Mind):** Any temporary mental malfunction caused by an **external factor** is **not** a disease of the mind. External factors include:
    - Taking drugs or prescribed medication (like insulin).
    - Drinking alcohol.
    - Being administered anaesthetics.
    - Suffering physical violence (like concussion from a blow).
