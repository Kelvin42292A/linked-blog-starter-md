New South Wales Criminal Court of Appeal

## Material Facts

- **The Confrontation:** Cornelissen and Sutton went to Tony Byrne’s house to confront him over allegations that Byrne had sexually abused Cornelissen's sister years earlier.
- **The Fight:** A physical altercation occurred outside the house. Byrne, who had been drinking , was knocked to the ground but got back up, took off his jumper, and advanced toward Cornelissen while swinging punches.
- **The Punch:** Cornelissen threw a single punch in response. Byrne fell, suffered a ruptured blood vessel in his brain (subarachnoid hemorrhage), and died shortly after.
- **The Conviction & Appeal:** Both men were convicted of manslaughter by an "unlawful and dangerous act". They appealed their convictions.

## Issues

1. **The Legal Test Error:** Did the trial judge err by failing to instruct the jury that the "reasonable person" must be placed **"in the accused's position"** when deciding if the punch was "dangerous"?
2. **Unreasonable Verdict:** Was it open to the jury to find beyond reasonable doubt that the punch was objectively dangerous and that Cornelissen was not acting in self-defense?

## Rule/Law

- **The "Dangerous Act" Test:** An unlawful act is "dangerous" only if a reasonable person **in the accused’s position** would have realized that the act exposed the victim to an appreciable risk of serious injury.
- **Self-Defense:** A person is not criminally liable if they acted in self-defense. The prosecution must prove beyond reasonable doubt that the accused did not believe their actions were necessary to defend themselves, or that their response was unreasonable in the circumstances as they perceived them .

## Application/Analysis

- **Omission in the Legal Test:** The trial judge failed to include the words "in the accused's position" in his instructions. This omission was crucial because witnesses saw Byrne staggering, but Cornelissen did not; the jury had to judge the risk based strictly on what was apparent to Cornelissen.
- **The Act Was Not Proven "Dangerous":** Medical evidence showed that a subarachnoid hemorrhage can be triggered by relatively minor, non-dangerous blows, meaning the fatal outcome did not prove the punch itself was inherently dangerous .
- **Self-Defense Was Not Disproven:** Cornelissen was backing away and only threw a single, non-forceful punch to defend himself from Byrne's active physical attack. The jury should have had a reasonable doubt about whether Cornelissen's response was unreasonable .

## Conclusion

The Court of Criminal Appeal allowed the appeals, **quashed the convictions**, and entered verdicts of **acquittal** for both appellants

### Judges Rules (Unlawful and dangerous act doctrine)

- **Subjective Context for Objective Test:** When applying the objective test for a "dangerous act," the court must place the hypothetical reasonable person **directly in the accused's position**. The danger of the act must be judged based only on the facts and circumstances that were apparent to the accused at the time, not on what outside observers saw.
- **Hindsight Cannot Prove Danger:** A court cannot use hindsight to label an act "dangerous" simply because it resulted in death. If an unexpectedly minor blow causes a fatal injury due to a victim's vulnerability, the act itself does not automatically meet the "appreciable risk of serious injury" threshold required for manslaughter .
