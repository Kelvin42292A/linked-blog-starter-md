High Court of Australia

## Material Facts

- In 1975, the High Court decided _Western Australia v The Commonwealth_ (the First Territory Senators Case). By a narrow 4:3 majority, the Court upheld the _Senate (Representation of Territories) Act 1973_, which allowed the ACT and NT to elect voting senators.
- Following that 1975 decision, elections were held, and territory senators and members of the House of Representatives took their seats.
- Shortly after, one of the judges in the 1975 majority (McTiernan J) retired and was replaced by a new judge (Aickin J).
- Hoping the new judge would tip the balance, Queensland launched a new action to challenge the Senate Act again. Western Australia also launched actions to challenge the _Northern Territory Representation Act 1922_ and the _Australian Capital Territory Representation (House of Representatives) Act 1973_.
- The States asked the Court to completely overrule its 1975 decision and declare that territories cannot have voting representatives in the federal Parliament.

## Issues

1. **Stare Decisis (Precedent):** Should the High Court overrule a very recent, fully considered previous decision simply because the composition of the bench has changed?
2. **Territory Representation:** Does s 122 of the Constitution authorize the Parliament to pass laws giving territories full voting representatives in the Senate and the House of Representatives?

## Rule/Law

- **Territory Representation (Statute - _Australian Constitution_, s 122):** Section 122 states that the Commonwealth Parliament may allow the representation of a territory in either House of the Parliament "to the extent and on the terms which it thinks fit". In 1975, this was interpreted to mean Parliament can grant territories full voting members, acting as an exception to s 7 and s 24 (which say the Parliament is composed of members chosen by the States).
- **Overruling Precedent / Stare Decisis (Common Law - _The Tramways Case (No 1) (1914)_):** The High Court is not strictly bound by its own previous decisions. However, as stated by Barton J in _Tramways_, a change in the number or identity of appointed justices can "never of themselves furnish a reason for review".
- **When to Overrule (Common Law - _Attorney-General (NSW) v Perpetual Trustee Co Ltd (1952)_):** The court will only take the exceptional step of overruling a past decision for "grave reasons." It will not reopen a recent, fully considered decision on a highly disputable question unless it conflicts with well-established principle or is "manifestly wrong".

## Application/Analysis

- **The "Reluctant" Adherence to Precedent:** Three judges (Mason, Jacobs, and Murphy JJ) still believed the 1975 case was correctly decided. Two judges (Gibbs and Stephen JJ) still believed the 1975 case was _wrong_ and that the Constitution meant for the Senate to strictly be a States' house.
- **A Change of Judge is Not Enough:** Despite personally disagreeing with the law, Gibbs and Stephen JJ applied the common law rules of _stare decisis_. They noted the 1975 case was fully argued, recent, and that people had already voted and elected territory senators based on it. Therefore, they ruled it was their duty to follow the precedent; a mere change in one justice was not a valid legal reason to destroy the established rights of territory voters.
- **Validating Both Houses:** Because the Court refused to overrule the 1975 decision regarding the Senate, it was an inevitable consequence that the Acts giving the territories representation in the House of Representatives must also be upheld under the same interpretation of s 122.
- _(Note: Barwick CJ and Aickin J dissented, arguing that maintaining the true federal nature of the Constitution was more important than following a wrong precedent. However, they were outvoted)._

## Conclusion

The Commonwealth's demurrers were **allowed** by a 5:2 majority (meaning the plaintiff States lost their cases). The Court refused to overrule its 1975 decision. The Acts were declared **valid**, confirming that the Australian Capital Territory and the Northern Territory are legally entitled to be represented by voting members in both the Senate and the House of Representatives.
