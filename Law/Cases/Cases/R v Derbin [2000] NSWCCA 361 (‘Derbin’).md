## Material Facts

Peter Derbin, a 22-year-old diagnosed with chronic schizophrenia, stopped taking his prescribed antipsychotic medication. After consuming alcohol, smoking cannabis, and inhaling butane gas, he suffered an acute psychotic episode and brutally attacked his mother, gouging out her eyes and attempting to suffocate her. Two psychiatrists agreed that while he knew what he was doing, his illness and command hallucinations meant he did not know it was wrong. Despite this undisputed medical evidence, the jury rejected his mental illness defence and convicted him.

## Issues

Can a defendant claim the defence of mental illness (insanity) if their acute psychotic state was triggered by voluntary drug and alcohol consumption superimposed on an underlying mental disease?

## Rule/Law

- **The Trigger Rule:** If a person has an underlying "disease of the mind" (such as schizophrenia), it does not matter if the acute psychotic episode was triggered or worsened by voluntary intoxication (alcohol or drugs).
- **The Sole Cause Exception:** Voluntary drug or alcohol use will only defeat the defence of mental illness if the temporary state of mind was caused _solely_ by the substances, without any underlying mental disease playing a part.

## Application/Analysis

Derbin had a long-term disease of the mind (schizophrenia). Although the alcohol, cannabis, and butane acted as the immediate triggers for the assault, they merely exacerbated his pre-existing illness. Because his capacity to reason calmly about whether his actions were wrong was destroyed by his schizophrenia (acting in combination with the drugs), the defence of mental illness was fully made out.

## Conclusion

The Court of Criminal Appeal allowed the appeal, quashed the guilty verdicts, and ordered that Derbin be detained in a secure prison psychiatric facility rather than prison .

### Judges Rules

Under Australian criminal law, **the cause of a mental illness does not matter**. If a defendant suffers from an underlying "disease of the mind" (like schizophrenia), **they are still entitled to the defence of mental illness even if their psychotic breakdown was triggered or made worse by voluntary drug or alcohol use**. Voluntary intoxication only blocks the defence if the temporary impairment was caused _solely_ by the drugs or alcohol.
