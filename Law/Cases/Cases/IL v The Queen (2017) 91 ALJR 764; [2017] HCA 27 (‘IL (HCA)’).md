High Court of Australia

## Material Facts

- The appellant (IL) and the deceased (Zhi Min Lan) were involved in a joint criminal enterprise (JCE) to manufacture a large commercial quantity of methylamphetamine (a crime punishable by life imprisonment).
- They were refining the drugs in a small bathroom using a gas ring burner and highly flammable acetone.
- The gas burner was lit, sparking a fire that severely burned the deceased, leading to his death.
- The Crown could not prove whether IL or the deceased actually lit the burner.
- The Crown argued that even if the deceased lit the burner and accidentally killed himself, IL was guilty of constructive murder because the act was done in furtherance of their JCE.

## Issues

1. **Constructive Murder:** Can an accused be held liable for constructive murder if their accomplice accidentally kills _themselves_ during the commission of a joint criminal enterprise?
2. **Joint Criminal Enterprise:** Does the law attribute an accomplice's act of self-killing to the accused to make it the "act of the accused" for the purposes of murder?

## Rule/Law

- **Murder Means Killing Another (Statute):** Under **s 18(1) of the _Crimes Act 1900 (NSW)_**, constructive murder strictly requires that one person kills _another_ person. It does not apply if someone accidentally kills themselves during a crime.
- **Joint Enterprise Attributes _Acts_, Not Liability (_Osland v The Queen_):** If you are in a joint criminal enterprise (JCE), the law attributes your accomplice's _physical acts_ to you, making you primarily responsible. However, the act attributed to you must actually be a crime (the _actus reus_ of an offence).
- **Self-Killing is Not a Crime:** Because suicide is no longer a crime in NSW, an accomplice accidentally causing their own death is not an unlawful act. Because their act was not a crime, there is no criminal act to attribute to you, meaning you cannot be guilty of their murder.

## Application/Analysis

- **The Act of Self-Killing:** Because the Crown could not prove otherwise, the Court had to assume the deceased lit the burner himself.
- **No Unlawful Act:** Because killing oneself is no longer an offence in NSW, the deceased's act of accidentally causing his own death was not unlawful. It therefore lacked the _actus reus_ (guilty act) required for murder or manslaughter.
- **Failure of Attribution:** Because the deceased's self-killing was not legally a crime, the rules of joint criminal enterprise from _**Osland**_ could not be used to attribute criminal liability to IL. You cannot be the constructive murderer of an accomplice who accidentally kills themselves. (However, the judges noted that if the fire had killed a _third party_, like a child outside, IL could have been charged with murder by attribution).

## Conclusion

The appeal was **allowed** (by majority). The trial judge's directed verdicts of acquittal for murder and manslaughter were restored. IL could not be convicted of the deceased's death.

### Judges Rules

**Self-Inflicted Death**

- **Murder Requires Killing "Another":** Under s 18 of the _Crimes Act_, the offences of murder and manslaughter strictly require that one person kills _another_ person. The law does not apply if a person kills themselves, regardless of whether they do it intentionally or accidentally during a crime.
- **Self-Killing is Not a Crime:** It is no longer a legal offence to kill oneself in New South Wales.
- **No Accomplice Liability:** Because accidentally killing yourself is not a legal offence, it lacks the _actus reus_ (guilty act) of a crime. Therefore, if your accomplice accidentally kills themselves during a joint criminal enterprise, their act is not a crime, so it cannot be legally attributed to you. You cannot be charged with their murder.

**Constructive murder**

- **Must Kill "Another":** Constructive murder strictly requires that a person kills _someone else_, not themselves.
- **Acts Must Be Illegal:** In a joint criminal enterprise, you are only responsible for your accomplice's physical acts if those acts are actually illegal crimes.
- **The Result:** Since killing yourself is no longer a crime in NSW, an accomplice accidentally killing themselves commits no crime. Because their act is not a crime, you cannot be held legally responsible for it, and cannot be convicted of their murder.
