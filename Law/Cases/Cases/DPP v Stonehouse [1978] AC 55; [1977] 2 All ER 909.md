House of Lords

## Material Facts

- **The Insurance:** John Stonehouse, a prominent British public figure facing disastrous personal finances, took out large life insurance policies in his wife's name payable upon his death. His wife was completely innocent of the scheme.
- **The Faked Death:** Stonehouse travelled to Miami, Florida, and fabricated his death by drowning by leaving his clothes on a beach and secretly fleeing to Australia under a false identity.
- **The Fraud Uncovered:** Before his wife could submit any formal claim to the insurance companies, Stonehouse was discovered alive in Australia and extradited.
- **The Charge:** He was charged with **attempting to obtain property by deception** (specifically, attempting to _enable_ his wife to obtain the insurance payouts).
- **The Defense:** Stonehouse argued his actions in Miami were merely "preparatory" and too remote to constitute the physical act (_actus reus_) of a criminal attempt because no actual insurance claim had been submitted.

## Issues

What constitutes the physical act (**actus reus**) of a criminal attempt, and can faking a death satisfy this threshold if no formal insurance claim has been submitted.

## Rule/Law

- **The Proximity Test:** The _actus reus_ of an attempt requires an act that goes beyond mere preparation and is **immediately connected** or sufficiently proximate to the commission of the complete offence.
- **Crossing the Rubicon:** An attempt is committed when the accused has "crossed the Rubicon and burnt his boats". This means they have performed all physical actions within their power to set the crime in motion, leaving nothing left to do but wait for the intended consequence.
- **Enabling Another to Obtain:** If a crime is defined as enabling another person to obtain property by deception, the attempt is complete when the accused does everything on their part to enable that outcome, even if the third party has not yet acted.

## Application/Analysis

- **Final Contribution Made:** Stonehouse had done every single physical act within his power to initiate the insurance fraud.
- **Inevitability of the Pretense:** By faking his death, he created the false pretense and ensured the news would hit the media and inevitably reach his wife and the insurers in England.
- **Rubicon Crossed:** Because there was absolutely nothing left for him to do except avoid detection, his physical conduct had progressed past mere "preparation" and was sufficiently proximate to constitute the _actus reus_ of an attempt.

## Conclusion

The House of Lords dismissed the appeal and **upheld the convictions for attempt**.

### Judges Rules

- **Beyond Mere Preparation:** The physical act (_actus reus_) of an attempt must be **immediately connected** and proximate to the complete crime, rather than just getting ready to commit it.
- **"Crossing the Rubicon" Standard:** You commit a criminal attempt when you have done everything in your power to set the crime in motion and **"burnt your boats,"** leaving nothing left for you to do but avoid detection.
- **No Final Step Required by You:** If you are charged with attempting to "enable another to obtain" property, your attempt is complete the moment you perform your final act to trigger the fraud—**even if the innocent third party has not yet submitted a formal claim**.
- **Indirect Communication Suffices:** You do not need to personally communicate a false pretense to the victim. It is enough if you perform an act (such as faking your death) **knowing and intending that media coverage or third parties will inevitably convey that false representation** to the victim.
