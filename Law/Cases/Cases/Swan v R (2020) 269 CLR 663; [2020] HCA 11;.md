High Court of Australia

## Material Facts

- The victim, Mr Kormilets, was a 78-year-old man who lived an active, healthy, and independent life prior to the assault.
- The appellant entered the victim's home and attacked him with a machete, intending to cause grievous bodily harm. The victim suffered severe, life-threatening injuries to his brain, face, kidneys, and chest.
- The victim spent four months in the hospital before being transferred to a high-level care facility. He did not recover; he suffered severe cognitive decline, required tube feeding, was doubly incontinent, and lost the ability to communicate or undertake daily living activities.
- Months later, because of his unsteady mobility and agitation, the victim fell and fractured his left femur.
- Although surgery on the fractured femur would reasonably have been expected to save his life, the victim's son and the doctors made a decision not to operate. This decision for palliative care was motivated by the victim's exceptionally low quality of life caused by the original assault.
- Because the fracture was not surgically treated, fat emboli travelled to the victim's lungs, compounding his pre-existing chest injuries from the assault and causing his death.
- The appellant was convicted of murder under s 18(1)(a) of the _Crimes Act 1900_ (NSW). He appealed, arguing that the decision not to undergo surgery broke the chain of causation, and therefore the assault was not the legal cause of death.

## Issues

1. **The Decision Not to Operate:** Was it legally open to the jury to decide that the victim's terrible quality of life (caused by the machete attack) was the _real reason_ his family and doctors chose not to operate on his broken hip?
2. **The "Quality of Life" Pathway:** Is an attacker legally responsible for a death if their attack causes such catastrophic, life-altering injuries that it later causes the victim's family to reject life-saving medical treatment?

## Rule/Law

- **Murder (s 18(1)(a) _Crimes Act 1900_ NSW):** A murder is committed when the act of the accused, which causes the death, was done with the intent to inflict grievous bodily harm.
- **The Causation Test (_Royall v The Queen_):** To prove causation, the jury must be satisfied that the accused's act was a "substantial or significant cause" (or a "sufficiently substantial" cause) of the death.
- **Boundaries of Responsibility (_Royall_):** Not every act that is strictly necessary for a death to occur is enough to impose legal responsibility for that death.
- **Jointly Necessary Conditions (_Royall_):** An accused can still be legally responsible for a death even if their act was not the _only_ cause. It is enough if their act was "one of the conditions which were jointly necessary to produce the event" (such as a severe injury combined with a decision not to operate).

## Application/Analysis

- **The Three-Step Pathway:** The High Court ruled there was sufficient evidence for the jury to legally conclude three things: (a) surgery would reasonably have saved his life; (b) the victim's son made a positive decision not to operate; and (c) this decision was motivated by the victim’s terrible quality of life resulting from the assault.
- **Substantial Contribution:** The attacker's actions did not have to be the _only_ reason the surgery was refused. It was enough that the catastrophic physical and cognitive effects of the machete attack "substantially or significantly contributed" to the family's decision to switch to palliative care.
- **No Mistake by the Appeal Court:** The High Court confirmed that the Court of Criminal Appeal did not fail to address the appeal. The lower court was completely correct to rule that the refusal of consent for the surgery was a direct consequence of the injuries suffered from the original assault.

## Conclusion

**Appeal dismissed. Conviction upheld.** The attacker is legally responsible for the death because the assault ruined the victim's quality of life, which is what caused the family to refuse the life-saving surgery.
