Supreme Court of Victoria

## Material Facts

- **The Incident:** Eric George Nydam caused a petrol explosion and fire at a ladies' hairdressing salon in St. Kilda.
- **The Deaths:** Two women (Katherine Joan Stradling and Karen Joyce Bush) died from severe burns resulting from the explosion.
- **The Conflicting Accounts:**
    - The prosecution argued Nydam deliberately threw a bucket of petrol over Stradling and lit it with a cigarette lighter because she had rejected him.
    - Nydam argued it was an accident. He claimed he only brought the petrol to threaten suicide as a dramatic gesture to get her sympathy, and that it accidentally ignited when he tried to light a match.
- **The Conviction & Appeal:** Nydam was convicted of murder. He appealed on the basis that the trial judge failed to properly instruct the jury on "murder by recklessness" and "manslaughter by criminal negligence".

## Issues

- Does manslaughter by criminal negligence require the prosecution to prove that the accused was subjectively aware of the risk of death or serious injury?
- What is the correct legal standard to establish manslaughter by criminal negligence?

## Rule/Law

- **Strictly Objective Test:** The test for manslaughter by criminal negligence is strictly objective, not subjective.
- **No Subjective Awareness Required:** The prosecution does **not** need to prove that the accused was personally conscious or aware of the likelihood that their actions would cause death or serious injury.
- **The Four Elements:** To establish this crime, the prosecution must prove:
    1. The accused had an intent to do the conscious and voluntary act that caused the death.
    2. The act was done in circumstances falling great short of the standard of care a reasonable person would have shown.
    3. The act carried a high degree of risk that death or serious bodily harm would occur if that standard of care was not observed.
    4. The falling short and the risk were so high that they warrant criminal punishment.

## Application/Analysis

- **Incorrect Jury Directions:** The trial judge failed to instruct the jury on the correct objective test for manslaughter by criminal negligence.
- **Grave Risk of Confusion:** Because both reckless murder (which requires a subjective state of mind) and negligent manslaughter (which is objective) were left to the jury, the failure to clearly separate the two tests created a serious risk of confusion.
- **Accused Placed at Risk:** Because the instructions were unsatisfactory, Nydam was at risk of being convicted of murder on a legal basis the jury might not have fully understood.

## Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Victoria allowed the appeal, quashed Nydam's murder convictions, and ordered a new trial

### Judges Rules

- **Objective Judgment:** Manslaughter by criminal negligence is judged strictly by an **objective standard** (comparing the accused's actions to a reasonable person) rather than what the accused was subjectively thinking.
- **No Personal Foresight Needed:** The prosecution **does not** have to prove that the accused actually realized or foresaw that their actions were highly dangerous to others.
- **The Liability Standard:** Criminal liability is established if the accused voluntarily did an act that fell **colossally short** of a reasonable standard of care, carrying an **obvious, high risk of death or serious injury** that deserves criminal punishment.
