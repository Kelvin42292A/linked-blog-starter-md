New South Wales courts of criminal appeal

## Material Facts

- Following a party, the complainant, who was highly intoxicated, was placed in the appellant's bed to sleep.
- The appellant, also highly intoxicated, later went into the bedroom, undressed, and attempted to have sexual intercourse with the complainant while she was asleep.
- When a friend walked into the bedroom and found the appellant on top of the sleeping complainant, the appellant apologized and claimed he mistakenly thought the complainant was his wife.
- The appellant was charged with attempting to have sexual intercourse without consent under s 61P (via s 61I) of the _Crimes Act 1900_ (NSW).
- Before the jury was empanelled, the trial judge made a preliminary ruling that s 61HA (a statutory provision regarding consent and knowledge) applied to the charge of attempt, and the appellant filed an interlocutory appeal against this ruling.

## Issues

- Whether a trial judge's preliminary advance ruling on a question of law constitutes an "interlocutory judgment or order" capable of being appealed under s 5F of the _Criminal Appeal Act 1912_ (NSW).
- Whether the statutory provisions regarding knowledge about consent in s 61HA of the _Crimes Act 1900_ (NSW) apply to the offence of _attempting_ to commit sexual assault.

## Rule/Law

- Under **s 5F** of the _Criminal Appeal Act 1912_ (NSW), an appeal can be brought against an "interlocutory judgment or order". However, to qualify, a ruling must have a requisite element of finality; preliminary rulings on evidence or points of law are generally excluded.
- Under **s 61HA** of the _Crimes Act 1900_ (NSW), a person who is asleep cannot consent, and an accused is legally taken to "know" there is no consent if they have "no reasonable grounds for believing" the other person consents.
- When a statute curtails an accused's rights under general law (such as by lowering the bar for prosecution), it requires a clear and express legislative intention to do so .

## Application/Analysis

- The trial judge's decision regarding the applicability of s 61HA was merely an expression of opinion on an anticipated legal issue, not a formal determination of the proceedings. Because it lacked finality, it did not qualify as an "interlocutory judgment or order," meaning the Court had no jurisdiction to hear the appeal.
- Although the Court lacked jurisdiction, it provided guidance on the law, noting that s 61HA explicitly lists substantive sexual offences but omits the offence of _attempt_ (s 61P). Because s 61HA diminishes an accused's common law rights by making it easier for the prosecution to prove knowledge of non-consent, the Court held that it cannot be applied to "attempts" without clear legislative language .

## Conclusion

The appeal was dismissed as incompetent because the Court did not have jurisdiction under s 5F to hear an appeal on a preliminary legal ruling.
