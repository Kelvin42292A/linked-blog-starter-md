High Court of Australia

## Material Facts

- The appellant (Pemble) and the deceased (Josephine) had been living together, but she recently left him.
- Pemble saw her sitting on a car outside a hotel. He used a hacksaw to cut down the barrel and butt of his .22 rifle.
- He approached her from behind with the loaded, cocked rifle pointing at her head. He claimed he didn't know it was loaded and only intended to "frighten" her.
- According to Pemble, as he got close, he called her name, stumbled, and the rifle accidentally discharged, shooting her in the back of the head.
- At trial, Pemble's own defense counsel explicitly told the jury that Pemble was definitely guilty of manslaughter, and just asked them to acquit him of murder.
- The trial judge directed the jury that murder included "reckless indifference", and essentially told the jury they just had to decide between murder and manslaughter. Pemble was convicted of murder and appealed.

## Issues

1. **Mens Rea (Recklessness):** Did the trial judge adequately direct the jury on what constitutes "reckless indifference" for the crime of murder?
2. **Duty of the Judge:** Is a trial judge legally required to direct the jury on all possible defenses (including a full acquittal), even if defense counsel explicitly abandons those arguments?

## Rule/Law

- **Murder by Recklessness (_Pemble_):** The test is subjective. To be guilty of murder, you must _actually foresee_ that your actions could cause death or really serious harm.
- **The Judge's Duty (_Mancini v DPP_ & _Kwaku Mensah v The King_):** The trial judge must explain all possible verdicts to the jury (like a full acquittal), even if the defense lawyer explicitly abandons them.
- **Manslaughter by Unlawful Act (_R v Larkin_):** If you accidentally kill someone while committing a dangerous and unlawful act (like threatening someone with a loaded gun), you are guilty of manslaughter.
- **Substituting Verdicts (_Northern Territory Supreme Court Act 1961, s 47(4)_):** An appeal court has the statutory power to throw out a murder conviction and substitute it with manslaughter if the jury's facts support it.

## Application/Analysis

- **The Flawed Recklessness Test:** The High Court found the trial judge's direction fundamentally defective. By simply agreeing with the jury that recklessness meant the accused "couldn't care less", the judge failed to tell the jury that Pemble had to _actually foresee_ the possibility of death or grievous bodily harm.
- **The Abandoned Acquittal:** The High Court ruled the trial judge breached his duty under the _Mancini_ rule. Just because Pemble's lawyer conceded manslaughter didn't mean the judge could just tell the jury to choose between murder and manslaughter. The judge was legally required to explain the elements of accidental death and let the jury decide if a full acquittal was appropriate.
- **No Full Acquittal Possible:** Despite the judge's error, the High Court ultimately concluded that, as a matter of law, a full acquittal wasn't actually possible anyway. Even if Pemble's story about stumbling was 100% true, brandishing a loaded, sawn-off rifle at someone's head is an unlawful and highly dangerous attempted assault. Therefore, under the rule in _R v Larkin_, the absolute best-case scenario for Pemble was manslaughter.

## Conclusion

The appeal was **allowed**. The conviction for murder was quashed due to the judge's defective directions. Using their statutory powers under **s 47(4) of the _Northern Territory Supreme Court Act 1961_**, the High Court substituted a verdict of **guilty of manslaughter** and remitted the case for resentencing.

### Judgment rule

- **Actual Foresight (Subjective Test):** To be guilty of murder by recklessness, you must _actually foresee_ that death or really serious harm (grievous bodily harm) is a probable or possible consequence of your actions.
- **Willingness to Risk:** You must foresee the specific consequences and still be willing to run the risk of them happening.
- **More Than Just Careless:** Mere carelessness or a general "couldn't care less" attitude is not enough; the jury must be strictly directed to find that you specifically foresaw the danger before you acted.
