High Court of Australia

## Material Facts

- Marion was a 14-year-old girl suffering from severe intellectual disability, deafness, and epilepsy, rendering her incapable of caring for herself or giving valid consent to medical treatment.
- Her parents applied to the Family Court of Australia for a declaration that they could lawfully authorise a hysterectomy and an ovariectomy (sterilisation) on Marion without a court order.
- The procedures were proposed for non-therapeutic purposes: to prevent pregnancy and to manage the psychological and behavioural consequences of menstruation.

## Issues

- Can the parents of an intellectually disabled child lawfully authorise a sterilisation procedure upon the child without an order of a court?
- If not, does the Family Court of Australia have the jurisdiction to authorise such a procedure?

## Rule/Law

- **_Gillick v West Norfolk AHA_ (Applied):** A minor is capable of giving informed consent to medical treatment only when they achieve sufficient understanding and intelligence to understand fully what is proposed.
- **s. 187 & 188 _Criminal Code Act 1983 (N.T.)_:** Any surgical procedure is an unlawful assault unless the patient (or their legally authorised representative) gives valid consent, or it is justified by medical emergency.
- **The Scope of Parental Power:** Parents generally have the authority to consent to therapeutic medical treatment for their children. However, the High Court established a rule that non-therapeutic sterilisation is a "special case" falling _outside_ the ordinary scope of parental power.
- **s. 63, 64 & Part VII _Family Law Act 1975 (Cth)_:** Confers a broad "welfare jurisdiction" upon the Family Court (similar to the traditional _parens patriae_ jurisdiction), empowering it to make orders protecting the welfare of children

## Application/Analysis

- **Incapacity to Consent:** Marion lacked the intellectual capacity required under the _Gillick_ test to ever give or refuse informed consent to the procedure herself.
- **Why Sterilisation is a "Special Case":** The majority held that parents cannot consent to this procedure on their own because:
    1. It requires major, invasive, and irreversible surgery that deprives the child of a fundamental right to reproduce.
    2. There is a significant risk of making the wrong decision about the child's future capacities or best interests.
    3. There is a potential conflict of interest between the independent, legitimate interests of the parents/carers (alleviating their burden of care) and the overriding best interests of the child.
- **The Court's Role:** Because of these grave risks, an independent, objective body must make the decision. The Family Court has the jurisdiction to authorise the procedure, but it must only do so as a "step of last resort" when all other alternative, less invasive procedures have failed, ensuring the decision is strictly in the best interests of the child.

## Conclusion

The parents **cannot** lawfully authorise the sterilisation of their intellectually disabled child without a court order. The Family Court of Australia **does have jurisdiction** to authorise the carrying out of the procedure under its welfare jurisdiction.
