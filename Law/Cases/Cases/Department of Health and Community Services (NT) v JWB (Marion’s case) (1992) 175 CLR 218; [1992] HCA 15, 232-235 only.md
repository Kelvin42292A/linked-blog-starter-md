High Court of Australia

## Material Facts

- Marion is a 14-year-old girl who suffers from mental retardation, severe deafness, epilepsy, has an ataxic gait and behavioural problems. She cannot care for herself
- Marion’s parents, who are married and residents of the Northern Territory, applied to the Family Court of Australia for an order authorising the performance of a hysterectomy and an ovariectomy on Marion.
- Alternatively, the parents sought a declaration that it is lawful for them, as joint guardians, to consent to the performance of those procedures.
- The hysterectomy is proposed to prevent pregnancy and menstruation with its psychological and behavioural consequences. The ovariectomy is proposed to stabilise hormonal fluxes to help eliminate consequential stress and behavioural responses.
- The question whether these operations are in Marion’s best interests is not before the Court. That inquiry continues before the Family Court.
- The appeal is from answers given by the Full Court of the Family Court to a case stated.

## Issues

- Can the applicants as joint guardians of the child (Marion) lawfully authorise the carrying out of a sterilisation procedure upon the child without an order of a Court?
- If no to the above question, does the Family Court of Australia have jurisdiction:
    - (a) to authorise the carrying out of such a procedure; or
    - (b) to enlarge the powers, rights or duties of the applicants as guardians of the child to enable them to lawfully authorise the carrying out of such a procedure; or
    - (c) to approve the consent of the applicants, as guardians of the child, to the proposed procedure to make the procedure lawful?
- Which (if any) of the steps referred to in (a), (b) or (c) is required by law?

## Rule/Law

- An act is unlawful if done without authorisation, justification or excuse (Criminal Code Act 1983 (N.T.), s.1).
- Assault includes the direct or indirect application of force to a person without consent (Criminal Code Act 1983 (N.T.), s.187).
- A person who is the guardian of a child under the Family Law Act has responsibility for the long-term welfare of the child and has all the powers, rights and duties vested by law or custom in the guardian of a child (Family Law Act 1975 (Cth), s.63E(1)).
- The Family Court has jurisdiction in relation to matters arising under Part VII of the Family Law Act (s.63(1)) and may make such order in respect of the welfare of a child as it considers proper (s.64(1)(c)).
- The parens patriae jurisdiction of the Court involves acting as supreme parent of children and exercising that jurisdiction in the manner in which a wise, affectionate, and careful parent would act for the welfare of the child.

## Application/Analysis

- The questions focus on the issue of consent: since Marion is intellectually disabled and cannot consent herself, can her parents decide alone, or is court involvement required under the Family Court’s welfare jurisdiction?
- The Full Court gave different answers, but the key issue is whether sterilisation falls outside ordinary parental powers and needs court protection for the child’s welfare.
- Nicholson C.J. held that parents cannot lawfully authorise the sterilisation without a court order, and that the Family Court has jurisdiction to authorise the procedure.

## Conclusion

The High Court held that parents **cannot authorise sterilisation** of their intellectually disabled child without court approval. The Family Court has jurisdiction to authorise it. Court order is required.
