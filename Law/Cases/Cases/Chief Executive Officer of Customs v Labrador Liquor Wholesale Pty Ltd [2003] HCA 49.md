### Material Facts

- The appellant (the Chief Executive Officer of Customs) instituted proceedings against the respondents (Labrador Liquor Wholesale Pty Ltd and its directors) for allegedly failing to pay customs and excise duties on alcohol and cigarettes.
- The Customs alleged the respondents falsely claimed the goods had been exported to the Solomon Islands and Fiji in 1996.
- Proceedings were commenced in the Supreme Court of Queensland. The appellant sought declarations of liability, convictions for offences against the _Customs Act 1901_ (Cth) and _Excise Act 1901_ (Cth), recovery of penalties, and reparation orders.
- Preliminary questions were separated for trial to determine the required standard of proof to obtain a conviction, and to clarify whether the proceedings were "criminal" for the purposes of applying section 92 of the _Evidence Act 1977_ (Qld), which regulates documentary evidence in civil cases.

### Issue

- What is the standard of proof required of the Customs in order to obtain a conviction for an offence under the _Customs Act 1901_ and the _Excise Act 1901_ (i.e., is it the civil standard of the balance of probabilities, or the criminal standard of beyond reasonable doubt)?.
- Are these Customs and Excise prosecutions considered "criminal proceedings" for the purpose of the _Evidence Act 1977_ (Qld), and consequently, do the civil rules of evidence (specifically s 92) apply?.

### Rule

- **s 247 _Customs Act_ & s 136 _Excise Act_:** Both sections stipulate that prosecutions in these matters may be commenced, prosecuted, and proceeded with in accordance with "the usual practice and procedure of the Court in civil cases".
- **s 4(1) _Crimes Act 1914_ (Cth):** Stated that, subject to specific legislation, "the principles of the common law with respect to criminal liability apply in relation to offences against laws of the Commonwealth".
- **s 92 _Evidence Act 1977_ (Qld):** Regulates the admissibility of documentary evidence as to facts in issue, but applies only in proceedings that are _not_ criminal proceedings.
- **_Daniels Corp_ Principle:** Clear statutory language is required to abrogate fundamental rights, such as depriving an accused party of the normal protection of the criminal standard of proof.

### Application

- **Standard of Proof:** Even though the statutes instruct that the "practice and procedure" of civil cases should be used, the High Court held that the standard of proof is not merely a matter of practice or procedure—it involves substantive rights. Because the legislation identifies offences which lead to a formal "conviction" and punishment, the common law principle regarding criminal liability applies. Seeking a conviction is at the heart of the criminal process; therefore, it requires proof beyond reasonable doubt. The general language in s 247 of the _Customs Act_ is not sufficient to abrogate this basic criminal standard.
- **Application of the Evidence Act:** While the standard of proof remains criminal, the rules of evidence governing admissibility clearly fall under the umbrella of "practice and procedure". Because the federal _Customs Act_ and _Excise Act_ explicitly direct the court to follow the "usual practice and procedure of the Court in civil cases," the provisions of the _Evidence Act 1977_ (Qld) that apply to civil cases (including s 92) are "picked up" and must be applied in these proceedings.
- **Rejecting Rigid Classifications:** The Court emphasized that it is unhelpful and erroneous to try to strictly classify these revenue proceedings as wholly "criminal" or wholly "civil," as they historically have a "hybrid characteristic". The issues must be resolved by looking directly at what the specific statutory provisions require.

### Conclusion

The appeal was **allowed in part**. The High Court declared that to obtain a conviction for the specified offences, the elements of the offence must be established **beyond reasonable doubt**. The Court also ordered that the provisions of the _Evidence Act 1977_ (Qld) that apply in civil cases (including s 92) **are to be applied** in the trial of these proceedings.
