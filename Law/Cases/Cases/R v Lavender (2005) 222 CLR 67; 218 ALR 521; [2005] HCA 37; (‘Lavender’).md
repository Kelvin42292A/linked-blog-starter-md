High Court of Australia

## Material Facts

- **The Incident:** Wayne Lavender, a front-end loader operator at an unfenced sand mine, drove a 25-ton loader to chase away four boys playing on the site.
- **The Death:** He pursued them into thick scrub, essentially "driving blind" because his vision was blocked by the loader's large front bucket. He accidentally ran over and killed a 13-year-old boy.
- **The Conviction & Appeal:** Lavender was convicted of involuntary manslaughter by criminal negligence. The Court of Criminal Appeal quashed his conviction, ruling that the prosecution had to prove **"malice"** (a subjective evil intent or recklessness) as an element of the crime. The Crown appealed to the High Court.

## Issues

1. Is **malice** an essential element of the crime of involuntary manslaughter by criminal negligence?
2. Is the legal test for criminal negligence **objective or subjective** (i.e., does the prosecution have to prove the accused subjectively foresaw the risk of harm)?

## Rule/Law

- **No Malice Required:** Malice is not, and has never been, an element of manslaughter at common law or under Section 18 of the _Crimes Act 1900_ (NSW).
- **The Objective Test (_Nydam_ Test):** The test for criminal negligence is strictly **objective**. The prosecution must prove that the accused's actions:
    1. Fell so far short of the standard of care a reasonable person would have exercised in those circumstances.
    2. Involved such a high risk of death or really serious bodily harm that the conduct deserves criminal punishment (often called "wicked" or gross negligence).
- **Subjective Awareness Unnecessary:** The prosecution **does not** need to prove that the accused actually realized or appreciated that their conduct was negligent or dangerous.

## Application/Analysis

- **Correct Directions:** The High Court found the trial judge’s directions to the jury were correct. The jury was properly instructed to evaluate Lavender's conduct against a reasonable person of his age and experience, knowing the circumstances he knew.
- **Gross Falling Short:** Driving a massive, noisy loader blind into thick vegetation where he knew young, unpredictable children were playing was a gross error of judgment.
- **No Subjective Defense:** It did not matter that Lavender did not intend to hurt the boys or subjectively fail to realize the danger. A reasonable person in his position would have immediately recognized the extreme risk of death or serious injury.

## Conclusion

The High Court unanimously allowed the appeal, set aside the acquittal, and **reinstated Lavender's conviction** for manslaughter

### Judges Rules

- **Strictly Objective Test:** The accused's conduct is judged strictly against how a **reasonable person** would have acted in those circumstances, rather than what the accused was actually thinking.
- **No "Malice" Required:** The prosecution does **not** need to prove any bad motive, evil intent, or statutory "malice" to establish guilt.
- **No Foresight of Danger Needed:** There is **no requirement** to prove that the accused personally realized, foresaw, or appreciated that their actions were dangerous.
- **Shocking Carelessness**: To be guilty of this crime, your actions must go far beyond a normal mistake and be so dangerously irresponsible—creating an obvious, extreme risk of death or serious injury—that they deserve actual criminal punishment
