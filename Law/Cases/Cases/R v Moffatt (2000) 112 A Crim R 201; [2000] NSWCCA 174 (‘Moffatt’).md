Supreme Court of New South Wales

## Material Facts

- The appellant, the deceased, and others were binge drinking heavily, consuming a mixture of wine and methylated spirits.
- An altercation occurred after the deceased called the appellant a "dog," resulting in the appellant striking the deceased in the chest with a hammer and manually strangling him until he stopped breathing. The appellant told police he heard a "crack" during the assault.
- **Crucial Medical Facts:** The deceased was in extremely poor health prior to the attack. He had a 75% occlusion of a coronary artery (heart disease) and a potentially lethal blood alcohol level (0.482), making him highly vulnerable to sudden death.
- Because of his poor health, medical experts could not completely rule out that the deceased died from cardiac arrest or alcohol poisoning before or during the attack.
- The appellant made several admissions to the police, but he had an extremely high blood alcohol level at the time, raising questions about whether his confession was reliable or voluntary.
- The appellant was convicted of murder by a judge alone. He appealed, arguing his confession should not have been admitted as evidence and that the Crown could not prove his actions actually caused the death.

## Issues

1. **Causation (Substantial Contribution & Pre-existing Conditions):** When a victim suffers from potentially lethal pre-existing conditions (such as severe heart disease and extreme alcohol toxicity), does a physical attack constitute the legal cause of death if the prosecution can prove the attack substantially contributed to or accelerated the death?
2. **Admissibility of Admissions (Intoxication):** Did the trial judge err in admitting the appellant's confessions (under ss 85, 90, 135, and 137 of the _Evidence Act 1995_) given his extremely high blood alcohol level, or did his established tolerance to alcohol mean the admissions were reliable and voluntary?
3. **Unsafe/Unreasonable Verdict:** Was it open to the trial judge to be satisfied beyond reasonable doubt that the appellant's actions were the substantial and operating cause of death, given the conflicting medical evidence about the possibility of sudden cardiac arrest or alcohol poisoning?

## Rule/Law

**1. Causation Rules**

- **Vulnerability of the Victim (_Mamote-Kulang_ & _Van den Bemd_):** An assailant must "take their victim as they find them." If a victim has a pre-existing condition or illness that makes them unusually susceptible to death, it does not enable the assailant to claim the death was a mere accident.
- **Multiple Causes (_Butcher_, _McKinnon_ & _Pagett_):** It does not matter if there is more than one cause of death. It is a misapplication of legal principle for a court to attempt to search for a single "principal" cause of death.
- **The Substantial Cause Test (_Royall_):** The accused's act does not need to be the only cause, but it must have more than a coincidental or insignificant effect. The act must provide a "substantial contribution" towards the death of the deceased.

**2. Admissibility of Evidence (Confessions)**

- **Reliability of Admissions (s 85 _Evidence Act 1995_):** Evidence of an admission made by a defendant during official questioning is not admissible unless the circumstances (such as the suspect's level of intoxication) make it _unlikely_ that the truth of the admission was adversely affected.
- **Unfairness and Prejudice (ss 90, 135, 137 _Evidence Act 1995_):** The court has the discretion (or duty, under s 137) to refuse to admit an admission if using the evidence would be unfair to the defendant, or if its probative value is outweighed by the danger of unfair prejudice.

## Application/Analysis

- **Causation (Substantial Cause):** Even though the victim had a bad heart and extreme alcohol levels, he was awake and fighting back right before the attack. The rapid sequence of the hammer blow, the strangulation, and his immediate death proved the attack was the "substantial and operating cause" of his death.
- **Vulnerability (Eggshell Skull):** The Court applied the rule that you must "take your victim as you find them". The fact that the victim was extremely unhealthy and vulnerable to sudden death did not excuse the appellant's actions.
- **The Drunken Confession:** The appellant was heavily intoxicated, but he was an alcoholic with an "extreme tolerance". Because he gave clear, sensible answers to the police that perfectly matched the physical evidence at the crime scene (like the broken ribs and damaged unit), the Court ruled his confession was completely reliable and fair to use.

## Conclusion

The appeal was **dismissed**. The Court upheld the murder conviction and sentence. The judges agreed that the appellant's confession was properly admitted into evidence, and that his physical attack was the substantial cause of the victim's death.
