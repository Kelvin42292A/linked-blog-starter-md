Federal Court of Australia

## Material Facts

- Mr. Treloar suffered severe and permanent facial disfigurement from skin cancers caused by his employment with Telstra.
- Under the old _Compensation (Commonwealth Government Employees) Act 1971_ (s 41), he was entitled to a lump sum for this injury. His initial claim was wrongly refused.
- Before the refusal could be reviewed, the new _Safety, Rehabilitation and Compensation Act 1988_ came into force. The transitional provisions (s 124) ensured he was still entitled to the compensation he would have received under the old 1971 Act.
- After a review, he was paid a $4,500 lump sum under s 24 of the new 1988 Act.
- Treloar then applied for **additional** compensation for "non-economic loss" under **s 27** of the new 1988 Act.
- Telstra refused the s 27 claim, but the Administrative Appeals Tribunal (AAT) ruled in Treloar's favor, relying on two previous Federal Court precedents (_Schlenert_ and _Bozicevic_).
- Telstra appealed to the Full Court of the Federal Court, arguing the AAT made an error of law regarding statutory construction, and asking the Court to explicitly overrule its own previous decisions.

## Issues

1. **Statutory Construction & The Formula:** Does the fact that compensation for facial disfigurement under s 41 of the 1971 Act is calculated as a flat lump sum (rather than a percentage) mean that the percentage-based formula for additional compensation under s 27 of the 1988 Act cannot legally or practically be applied to Mr. Treloar?
2. **Stare Decisis (Overruling Precedent):** Under what circumstances is it appropriate for an intermediate appellate court (like the Full Court of the Federal Court) to refuse to follow and overrule its own previous decisions (_Schlenert_ and _Bozicevic_) regarding the construction of a statute?

## Rule/Law

- **Transitional Compensation (Statute - _Safety, Rehabilitation and Compensation Act 1988_):** Based on the prior Federal Court precedents of _Schlenert_ and _Bozicevic_, if an employee is entitled to compensation under s 24 in respect of an impairment which was permanent before 1 December 1988, they are automatically also entitled to additional compensation under s 27 for non-economic loss.
- **Stare Decisis (Common Law):** An appellate court will act with caution before reviewing and overruling one of its own earlier decisions.
- **Overruling Statutory Construction (Common Law):** When dealing specifically with the interpretation of a statute, an intermediate appellate court should rarely throw open a past decision. Unless the previous court's error in construction is **"patent"** (obvious), or has produced unintended and irrational consequences not foreseen by the previous court, the first decision should stand. Just because a statute is ambiguous or a new judge prefers a different interpretation is not enough to overrule the precedent.

## Application/Analysis

- **The Precedent Still Applies:** The majority (Branson and Finkelstein JJ) acknowledged that Mr. Treloar's case was slightly different from previous cases because his old compensation was calculated as a lump sum under s 41, rather than a percentage under s 39. However, they ruled this difference did not change the core legal principle: because he was entitled to s 24, he was entitled to s 27.
- **Anomalies vs. Patent Errors:** Telstra pointed out major anomalies, arguing that Mr. Treloar might be double-dipping for non-economic loss and that the mathematical percentage formula in s 27 simply doesn't work for a lump sum injury. The majority accepted that these anomalies existed and admitted they might have originally interpreted the statute differently themselves.
- **Refusal to Overrule:** Despite the anomalies, the majority ruled that Telstra failed to demonstrate a "patent error" in the previous decisions. The previous courts hadn't missed the law; they simply chose a specific interpretation. Because Parliament had not stepped in to amend the legislation to fix the anomalies, the Court applied the strict rules of _stare decisis_ and refused to overturn its own past decisions.

## Conclusion

Telstra's appeal was **dismissed** by a 2:1 majority. Telstra was ordered to pay Mr. Treloar's costs. The Tribunal's decision was upheld, confirming Mr. Treloar was legally entitled to the additional compensation under s 27.
