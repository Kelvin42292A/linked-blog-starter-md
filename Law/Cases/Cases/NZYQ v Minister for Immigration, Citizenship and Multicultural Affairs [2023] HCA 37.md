High Court of Australia

## Material Facts

- The plaintiff (NZYQ) is a stateless Rohingya Muslim who arrived in Australia by boat in 2012 and was placed in immigration detention under s 189 of the _Migration Act 1958_ (Cth).
- He was granted a bridging visa in 2014, but pleaded guilty to a sexual offence against a child in 2016. After serving his prison sentence, he was taken back into immigration detention in 2018.
- A delegate of the Minister found he had a well-founded fear of persecution in Myanmar (making him a refugee owed protection), but refused to grant him a protection visa under s 36(1C)(b) because his conviction meant he was a danger to the Australian community.
- The final refusal of his visa engaged the Department's legal duty under s 198(6) of the Act to remove him from Australia as soon as reasonably practicable.
- Because he was stateless and owed protection, he could not be removed to Myanmar. He had no right to enter Saudi Arabia or Bangladesh (where he had relatives), and no country has a practice of accepting child sex offenders.
- The parties agreed that there was no real prospect of him being removed from Australia in the reasonably foreseeable future.
- The plaintiff commenced proceedings in the High Court claiming his continued detention under ss 189(1) and 196(1) was either unauthorised by the statute or contravened **Chapter III** of the Constitution.

## Issues

1. **Statutory Construction:** On their proper construction, do ss 189(1) and 196(1) of the _Migration Act_ authorise the continued detention of the plaintiff?
2. **Constitutional Validity:** If the statute does authorise it, are those provisions beyond the legislative power of the Commonwealth because they contravene Chapter III of the Constitution?
3. **Overruling Precedent:** Should the High Court grant leave to reopen and overrule the constitutional holding in the landmark 2004 case of _Al-Kateb v Godwin_?

## Rule/Law

- **The _Lim_ Principle (Chapter III):** Under Chapter III of the Constitution, the involuntary detention of a person by the State is inherently penal or punitive, and generally exists only as an incident of the exclusively judicial function of adjudging and punishing criminal guilt.
- **The Exception for Aliens:** A law authorising the Commonwealth Executive government to detain an alien without a court order will only be valid if the detention is limited to what is "reasonably capable of being seen as necessary" for a legitimate and non-punitive purpose (such as processing a visa or deporting/removing them).
- **The New _NZYQ_ Rule (Overruling _Al-Kateb_):** Overruling the constitutional holding in _Al-Kateb_, the High Court established a strict limit on executive detention. If there is **no real prospect of the removal of the alien from Australia becoming practicable in the reasonably foreseeable future**, the detention can no longer be justified by the non-punitive purpose of removal. Such detention becomes punitive and contravenes Chapter III of the Constitution.

## Application/Analysis

- **No Prospect of Removal:** The Court looked at the practical reality of the plaintiff's situation. Because he was a stateless Rohingya Muslim who was owed protection preventing his return to Myanmar, and because no other country would agree to accept him, the Court found as a fact that there was **no real prospect of his removal from Australia becoming practicable in the reasonably foreseeable future**.
- **Detention Became Punitive:** Applying the new constitutional limit, the Court ruled that because the legislative objective of removal could not actually be achieved, the plaintiff's continued detention could no longer be seen as necessary for the legitimate, non-punitive purpose of removal.
- **Constitutional Breach (Chapter III):** Because the detention could no longer be justified by a non-punitive purpose, it transformed into punitive detention. Since the power to inflict penal or punitive detention is an exclusively **judicial function** under Chapter III, the Executive government's continued detention of the plaintiff was unconstitutional and unlawful.

## Conclusion

The High Court ruled in favour of the **plaintiff (NZYQ)**. The Court declared that because there was no real prospect of his removal, his continued detention under the _Migration Act_ was **unlawful**. The Court ordered a writ of _habeas corpus_ requiring the defendants to **release the plaintiff forthwith** (immediately). The defendants were ordered to pay the costs.
