House of Lords (UK)

## Material Facts

- The respondent (Smith) was driving a car containing stolen scaffolding clips.
- A police officer (PC Meehan) stopped the car and told the respondent to pull over.
- Instead, the respondent accelerated, and the officer clung to the side of the car.
- The respondent drove erratically and zigzagged through traffic to shake the officer off.
- The officer was thrown off, fell into the path of an oncoming car, and suffered fatal injuries.
- The respondent claimed he panicked, only wanted to "shake him off," and did not intend to kill or cause serious harm.
- He was convicted of capital murder, but the Court of Criminal Appeal reduced the conviction to manslaughter because they believed the jury should have used a _subjective_ test for intent (what the respondent actually foresaw).

## Issues

- Is the legal test for proving intent (malice aforethought) to cause grievous bodily harm subjective (what the accused actually contemplated) or objective (what a reasonable man would contemplate)?
- What is the correct legal definition of "grievous bodily harm"?

## Rule/Law

- **s 1(1) and s 5 _Homicide Act, 1957_:** Defines capital murder. The Act did _not_ abolish the common law rule that malice can be constituted by a proved intention to inflict grievous bodily harm.
- **The Objective Intent Test:** The sole test for intent (once it is proved the accused did an unlawful and voluntary act) is what the ordinary, responsible "reasonable man" would have contemplated as the natural and probable result of that act.
- **_Reg. v. Ward_ (Approved):** Confirmed that the test for intent in murder must be the objective "reasonable man" standard.
- **_Reg. v. Vickers_ (Approved):** Confirmed that an intent to cause grievous bodily harm, resulting in death, amounts to murder.
- **Definition of Grievous Bodily Harm:** The words should bear their ordinary and natural meaning, which is simply **"really serious"** harm.

## Application/Analysis

- **The Intent Test:** The House of Lords rejected the subjective approach. If an accused person (who is sane and capable of forming intent) voluntarily does an act, the law does not care what they _actually_ foresaw in their own mind. Because a reasonable person would clearly foresee that shaking a man off a fast-moving car into oncoming traffic would cause really serious harm, the respondent is legally deemed to have intended that harm.
- **Applying the Presumption:** The legal presumption that "a man intends the natural and probable consequences of his acts" is simply another way of applying the reasonable man test.
- **Grievous Bodily Harm:** The trial judge directed the jury that GBH meant harm that "seriously interferes with health or comfort". The House of Lords held that the simpler "really serious harm" definition is better. However, this did not amount to a fatal misdirection because the harm the respondent caused (being thrown into traffic) was obviously of a very serious nature regardless of the specific wording used.

## Conclusion

The appeal by the Crown was **allowed**. The House of Lords ruled that the objective "reasonable man" test is the correct standard for proving intent. The original conviction for capital murder was restored.

### Judgment rules

**Mens Rea (Intent)** The test is objective. If an ordinary, **reasonable person** would expect your actions to cause harm, the law says you intended it, no matter what you actually thought or felt at the time.

**Intent to Inflict Grievous Bodily Harm (GBH)** Intending to cause GBH simply means you intended to cause **"really serious"** harm. The injury does not need to be life-threatening or permanent.
