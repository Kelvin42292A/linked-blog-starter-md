House of Lords (UK)

## Material Facts

- The respondent (Jim Smith) was driving a stolen car containing sacks of scaffolding clips.
- A police constable (Meehan) told him to pull over. Instead, the respondent accelerated.
- The constable clung to the side of the car. The car swerved erratically at speed through traffic.
- The constable was thrown off and killed by an oncoming car.
- The respondent continued, dumped the stolen items, then returned. He claimed he only wanted to "shake him off" and was frightened.

## Issues

Whether the trial judge misdirected the jury on the test for intention (specifically, the use of the "reasonable man" / objective test for whether the respondent intended grievous bodily harm).

## Rule/Law

- Murder requires malice aforethought: either an intention to kill or an intention to cause grievous bodily harm.
- A person is presumed to intend the natural and probable consequences of his voluntary acts.
- The test is objective: what the ordinary responsible (reasonable) man would have contemplated as the natural and probable result of the acts in the circumstances.
- Foresight by the accused of the consequences is not required if the acts were deliberate and of a kind that grievous bodily harm was the natural and probable result.
- The presumption is rebuttable only by incapacity to form intent, insanity, or diminished responsibility.

## Application/Analysis

- The respondent deliberately accelerated and drove erratically with the constable hanging on in traffic.
- A reasonable man would have contemplated that grievous bodily harm (serious injury) was the natural and probable result.
- The trial judge correctly directed the jury using the objective test. There was no misdirection.
- The respondent’s panic or claim that he did not foresee the result did not rebut the presumption, as he was sane and responsible for his deliberate acts.

## Conclusion

Appeal allowed. Conviction for murder restored. The objective test applies.
