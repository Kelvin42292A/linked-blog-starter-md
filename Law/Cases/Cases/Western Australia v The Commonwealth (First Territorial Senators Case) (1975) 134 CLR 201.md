## Material Facts

The Commonwealth Parliament passed the _Senate (Representation of Territories) Act 1973_ to give the Northern Territory and the ACT two fully voting senators each. State governments (Western Australia, New South Wales, and Queensland) challenged this. They also argued that the joint sitting which passed the laws was invalid because of an unprompted 5-to-8 month delay under Section 57, a prior prorogation of Parliament, and because the Governor-General's proclamation listed one invalid bill (the _Petroleum and Minerals Authority Bill_).

## Issues

1. Does Section 122 of the Constitution allow the Commonwealth to give Territories fully voting senators, even though Section 7 says the Senate must be composed of State senators?
2. Does Section 57 contain any implied time limits, or does prorogation wipe out a legislative deadlock?
3. Does listing an invalid bill in the joint sitting proclamation invalidate the entire sitting?

## Rule/Law

1. Section 122 is a broad, plenary power that allows Parliament to grant full voting membership to territory representatives "to the extent and on the terms which it thinks fit".
2. Section 57 does not contain any implied time limits; a deadlock remains an historical fact and is not wiped out by proroguing Parliament.
3. A proclamation listing an invalid bill is not entirely void; the invalid part is treated as surplusage, leaving the valid bills unaffected.

## Application/Analysis

1. The words "representation... in either House... on the terms which it thinks fit" in s 122 include the power to grant full voting rights. Section 122 acts as a valid extension of, or exception to, Section 7's general rule that the Senate is composed of State senators.
2. The delay did not undo the deadlock, and proroguing Parliament merely ended a session but did not erase the historical disagreement.
3. The joint sitting was legally convened; the inclusion of the _Petroleum and Minerals Authority Bill_ was simply disregarded as surplusage.

## Conclusion

The _Senate (Representation of Territories) Act 1973_ and the other bills passed at the joint sitting were declared valid and the challenges were dismissed.

### Judges Rules

- **Rule 1: Territory Representation** Under Section 122 of the Constitution, the Commonwealth has the plenary power to give Territories fully voting senators and members of Parliament. Section 122 operates as a valid exception or extension to the general rules of State-based representation in Sections 7 and 24.
- **Rule 2: No Implied Deadlock Time Limits (s 57)** Section 57 has no hidden time limits. A legislative deadlock is a historical fact that does not expire over a delay of several months, nor is it wiped out if Parliament is prorogued before the Governor-General dissolves both Houses.
- **Rule 3: The Surplusage Rule for Joint Sittings** If a joint sitting proclamation mistakenly includes an invalid bill alongside valid bills, the entire sitting is not ruined. The invalid bill is treated as "surplusage" (ignored), and the valid bills can still be legally debated and passed.
