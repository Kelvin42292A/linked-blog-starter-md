High Court of Australia

## Material Facts

- The appellants (who lived in QLD and VIC) formed a conspiracy to defraud a company (Collins Street Properties) out of a $6.5 million lease incentive for a building in Melbourne, VIC.
- While the building was in Melbourne and the conspiracy was formed outside South Australia, the victim company was registered in South Australia, advised by Adelaide solicitors, and ultimately owned by the South Australian government (SGIC).
- To secure the money, the appellants sent a forged facsimile from Thailand to the company's solicitors in Adelaide, knowing the final decision to approve the lease and pay the money had to be made by the SGIC Board in South Australia.
- The appellants were charged in the Supreme Court of South Australia with the common law offence of conspiracy to defraud.
- The appellants tried to quash the indictment, arguing that because the conspiracy was formed outside of South Australia, their conduct did not constitute an offence against South Australian law and the court lacked jurisdiction.

## Issues

1. **Statutory vs. Common Law Jurisdiction:** Did the South Australian statute (s 5C of the _Criminal Law Consolidation Act_) give the court jurisdiction, or did the court have to rely on the common law?
2. **The Nature of the Common Law:** Is there a separate "common law of South Australia" that was breached, or is there only one unified common law of Australia?
3. **Territorial Nexus:** Does a State Supreme Court have the legal authority (jurisdiction) to try a common law conspiracy formed entirely outside the State, and what level of "connection" is required?

## Rule/Law

- **The Limits of Statute (s 5C):** Section 5C of the _Criminal Law Consolidation Act_ requires a "territorial nexus" between the State and at least one _element_ of the offence. In conspiracy, the "agreement" is the only element; overt acts (like sending a fax) are just evidence.
- **The Unified Common Law (_Lange v Australian Broadcasting Corp_):** There is no separate "common law of South Australia". Australia has one unified common law,. Therefore, a common law conspiracy is an offence against the Australian common law, not a State law.
- **Jurisdiction & The "Real Connection" Test:** To exercise jurisdiction over a trans-jurisdictional common law offence, the strict historical rule that a crime only has a single location is abandoned. A State court has jurisdiction if there is a **"real connection"** (or real and substantial link) between the conspiracy and that State.

## Application/Analysis

- **The Statute Fails:** The High Court found that because the agreement was made outside SA, and sending the fax was just an "overt act" rather than an element of the crime, the prosecution could not rely on s 5C to prove jurisdiction.
- **The "Real Connection" Existed at Common Law:** The High Court majority ruled that despite the failure of the statute, there was objectively a real connection between the conspiracy and South Australia.
- **The Target and the Deception:** The intended victim was a South Australian company controlled by the SA government. Furthermore, the appellants deliberately sent the forged facsimile to Adelaide to deceive people in South Australia and induce the SGIC Board to act to its economic detriment.
- Because this real and substantial connection existed, the Supreme Court of South Australia was properly exercising its jurisdiction "in and for the State" to try an offence against the unified Australian common law.

## Conclusion

The appeals were **dismissed** by the High Court. The convictions of the appellants were upheld, confirming that the Supreme Court of South Australia had valid jurisdiction over the crime.
