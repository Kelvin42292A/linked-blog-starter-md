Court of Criminal Appeal for New South Wales

## Material Facts

- The appellant (Stanford) and a co-offender (Shaw) planned to enter the Sawtell Bowling and Recreation Club to steal money.
- They planned to gain access through a window that had previously been left open.
- When Shaw arrived at the window, he found it was "slightly ajar and unlocked". He simply slid the window further open and climbed inside without needing to use force.
- Once inside, Shaw stole $30 from a cleaner and severely assaulted the cleaner with a crowbar.
- The appellant, who had waited outside, was charged under the principle of joint criminal enterprise.
- The appellant was convicted of the specially aggravated offence of breaking, entering, and committing a serious indictable offence.

## Issues

- Does further opening a window that is already partly open constitute a "breaking" for the purposes of the statutory offence of break, enter, and steal?
- Does a recreational club fall under the definition of a "pavilion" (or other specified buildings) for the purposes of the _Crimes Act_ ?

## Rule/Law

- **s 112 _Crimes Act 1900_ (NSW):** The statutory offence of breaking and entering certain specified buildings (including a dwelling-house, shop, or pavilion) and committing a serious indictable offence therein.
- **Common Law "Breaking":** Obtaining entry by prising open a locked window, or opening a fully closed (but unlocked) window constitutes a break. However, further opening an already partly open window or door is **not** a breaking.
- **_R v Smith (1827)_ (Applied):** An old and long-standing common law precedent which established that merely removing an obstruction by pushing a partly open window further up does not qualify as a legal "breaking".

## Application/Analysis

- **No "Breaking" Established:** The Crown's own evidence established that Shaw did not open a closed window; he merely slid a partly open window further open. Because the court was bound by the longstanding rule from _R v Smith_, this action could not constitute a legal "breaking". Therefore, the prosecution failed to prove an essential element of the charge.
- **Outdated Laws:** The Court noted that relying on such fine historical distinctions (where taking advantage of an ajar window is treated differently than opening a closed one) is outdated and suggested the law needs reform.
