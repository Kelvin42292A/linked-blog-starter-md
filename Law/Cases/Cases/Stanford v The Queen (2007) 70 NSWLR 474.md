Court of Criminal Appeal for New South Wales

## Material Facts

- On or about 1 November 2004, the appellant (Stanford) and co-offender Dudley Ian Shaw planned to break into the Sawtell Bowling and Recreation Club to steal money from an ATM, poker machines, and a safe
- They waited for a cleaner (Wayne Richard Dennett) to be present inside the Club. Shaw entered through a partly open window carrying a crowbar
- Shaw assaulted the cleaner with the crowbar, inflicting grievous bodily harm (including the loss of an eye). The only proceeds obtained were $30 stolen from the victim’s wallet
- The appellant remained outside during the break and enter and did not personally enter the Club or inflict the injuries
- The appellant was charged with specially aggravated break, enter and commit serious indictable offence (stealing) under s 112(3) Crimes Act 1900 (NSW) (in company, armed with offensive weapon, and inflicting GBH) and, in the alternative, the aggravated form under s 112(2).
- He was convicted after trail on the specially aggravated count and sentenced to 10 years imprisonment with a 6-year non-parole period

## Issues

- Whether further opening a **partly open window** constitutes a “**break**” for the purposes of s 112 Crimes Act 1900 (NSW)
- Whether the Sawtell bowling and Recreation Club qualifies as a “**pavilion**” (or other specified building) under s112
- Whether the indictment was defective in failing to properly allege the elements of the specially aggravated offence under s 112 (3) (particularly “maliciously” inflicting grievous bodily harm and being armed with a “dangerous weapon”)
- Whether the trail judge erred in directions on **joint criminal enterprise** liability (specifically, whether the appellant must have contemplated the infliction of grievous bodily harm)
- Whether the conviction was unreasonable or unsafe

## Rule/Law

- **Section 112(1) Crimes Act 1900 (NSW)**: Whosoever **breaks and enters** any dwelling-house, pavilion, shop, warehouse, garage, or other specified building and commits a serious indictable offence (e.g. stealing) therein is guilty of an offence.
- **“Break”** at common law requires some degree of force to create or enlarge an opening. Merely **further opening a partly open window** does **not** amount to a “break” (_R v Smith_ (1827) 1 Mood CC 178 followed).
- **Section 112(2)**: Aggravated offence — liable to 20 years (e.g. in company, armed with offensive weapon, etc.).
- **Section 112(3)**: Specially aggravated offence — liable to 25 years if the aggravated offence involves **wounding or maliciously inflicting grievous bodily harm**, or being armed with a **dangerous weapon**.
- **Section 105A**: Defines “circumstances of aggravation” and “circumstances of special aggravation”.
- **Joint Criminal Enterprise**: For liability as a participant, the accused must be shown to have contemplated the possibility that the co-offender would commit the acts giving rise to the aggravation (including the infliction of GBH).

## Application/Analysis

- Entering through a **partly open window** by further opening it did **not** constitute a “**break**” under s 112. The common law definition requires force to create or enlarge an opening where none existed. This was fatal to the conviction.
- The Sawtell Bowling and Recreation Club was **doubtful** to qualify as a “pavilion” under s 112. Many modern buildings (including clubs) may fall outside the specific categories listed in the section.
- The indictment was **defective**:
    - It failed to allege that the grievous bodily harm was inflicted **maliciously**.
    - It alleged “offensive weapon” instead of the required “dangerous weapon” for s 112(3).
- On **joint criminal enterprise**, the trial judge’s directions were inadequate. The jury should have been directed that the appellant could only be liable for the specially aggravated offence if he contemplated the possibility that Shaw would inflict grievous bodily harm.
- The conviction was unsafe and unreasonable in light of the above defects.

## Conclusion

Appeal allowed. Conviction quashed.
