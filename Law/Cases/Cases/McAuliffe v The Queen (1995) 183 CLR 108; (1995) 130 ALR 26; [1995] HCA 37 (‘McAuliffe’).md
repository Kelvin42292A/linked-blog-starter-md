High Court of Australia

## Material Facts

- **The Plan:** Two teenage brothers (Sean and David McAuliffe) and their friend (Matthew Davis) drank alcohol, smoked marijuana, and decided to go to a park near Bondi Beach to "roll", "rob", or "bash" people. The park was located near high, dangerous cliffs.
- **The Attack:** Sean was armed with a hammer and Davis with a stick. Both brothers were experienced in Tae Kwon Do. Upon arriving at the park, they launched a violent attack on two men near the cliff edge. David and Davis beat the deceased while Sean attacked the other man.
- **The Fatal Act:** Sean went over to the deceased and delivered a side-kick to his chest "to finish the fight," causing him to fall over a ledge. The youths then left the scene. The next day, the deceased's body was found in the sea at the bottom of the cliff.
- **The Conviction & Appeal:** The brothers were tried and convicted of murder. They appealed, arguing they should not be liable for murder because they only agreed to a simple assault ("bashing") and did not explicitly agree or intend to inflict grievous bodily harm (GBH).

## Issues

Can a participant in a joint criminal enterprise be found guilty of a crime (like murder) committed by a co-participant if that crime was **not part of their original agreement**, but the participant **individually foresaw it as a possible incident** of carrying out the plan and still chose to continue participating?

## Rule/Law

- **Joint Criminal Enterprise (JCE):** When two or more people agree (expressly or tacitly) to commit a crime, they are acting in concert. If any participant performs the acts necessary to commit that crime, **all are equally guilty** regardless of the specific role they played.
- **Scope of the Enterprise:** Participants are also guilty of any other crime committed in carrying out the enterprise that falls within the scope of their common purpose, which is determined **subjectively** by what the parties actually contemplated.
- **Extended Common Purpose (The McAuliffe Principle):** A participant is criminally liable for an additional crime committed by a co-participant that falls _outside_ the agreed plan if they **individually contemplated/foresaw as a possibility** that the crime might be committed, but nevertheless **continued to participate** in the joint venture.

## Application/Analysis

- **The Shared Enterprise:** The three youths entered into a clear, joint enterprise to assault and rob people.
- **Foresight of Serious Harm:** Although the brothers claimed their explicit agreement did not extend to inflicting serious injury, they continued to participate in the violent assault near a cliff edge while knowing that the intentional infliction of GBH was a highly possible incident of the attack.
- **Participation Equals Guilt:** By continuing to lend themselves to the enterprise with that individual foresight, they actively assisted and encouraged the conduct. Therefore, the trial judge was correct to instruct the jury that individual contemplation of GBH as a possibility was sufficient to establish the intent required for murder.

## Conclusion

The High Court of Australia **dismissed the appeals** and upheld the murder convictions.

### Judges Rules

- **Partners Share Equal Guilt:** Under joint criminal enterprise, if you agree to commit a crime with others and participate in it, the physical actions of your partners are legally treated as your own. You are all **equally guilty of the planned crime**, regardless of who actually struck the final blow.
- **No Explicit Agreement Needed for Incidents:** You can be convicted of an additional crime committed by your partner (which was not part of the initial plan) if you **individually foresaw that crime as a possibility** and chose to keep participating anyway.
- **Subjective Foresight is the Key:** The scope of your liability is judged strictly by **what you subjectively contemplated** might happen during the enterprise, not what an ordinary person would have expected.
- **Culpability in Continued Presence:** If you know your partner is carrying a weapon or might use extreme violence, and you do not withdraw, your **continued participation** is legally treated as giving assistance and encouragement to that violence.
