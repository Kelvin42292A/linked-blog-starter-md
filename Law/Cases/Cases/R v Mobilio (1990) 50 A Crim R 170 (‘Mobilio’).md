Supreme Court of Victoria

## Material Facts

- The applicant, a radiographer, inserted an ultrasound probe (transducer) into the vaginas of three young women.
- The women consented to this, believing it was necessary for medical diagnostic purposes.
- The prosecution argued that the radiographer actually inserted the probe solely for his own sexual gratification, which meant the women's consent was not legally real.

## Issues

- Does a woman's mistake about a medical professional's _purpose_ (sexual gratification instead of medical diagnosis) cancel out her legal consent to the physical act?

## Rule/Law

- Inserting an object into a vagina without consent is rape (_based on s 2A of the Crimes Act 1958_).
- Consent is only legally cancelled if the victim is tricked about the physical act itself or the person's identity (_based on the High Court case Papadimitropoulos v R_).
- Tricking someone about the _reason_ for the act (e.g., doing it for sexual pleasure instead of medical treatment) does **not** cancel their consent to the physical act (_based on Papadimitropoulos v R_, which rejected the Canadian case _R v Harms_).
- Tricking someone into a sexual act by lying is a completely separate crime called "false representation," not rape (_based on s 54 of the Crimes Act 1958_).

## Application/Analysis

- The women consented to the specific physical act of having an ultrasound probe inserted, and that is exactly the physical act the radiographer performed,.
- Even if the radiographer performed the act for his own sexual gratification, this only changes his _purpose_—it does not change the physical _nature and character_ of the act,.
- Because the physical act was exactly what the women consented to, their consent was legally valid. (The court noted the prosecution charged him with the wrong crime; he should have been charged with procuring penetration by false representation instead of rape).

## Conclusion

The appeal was **allowed**, and the applicant's rape convictions were **quashed** (thrown out).
