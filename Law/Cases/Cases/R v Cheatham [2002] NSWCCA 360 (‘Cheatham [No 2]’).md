## Material Facts

Cheatham killed his wife and daughter, and wounded his baby, under a severe delusion that they all had AIDS. He raised the defence of **diminished responsibility**. The trial psychiatrists all agreed he suffered from an abnormality of mind, but disagreed on whether it "substantially impaired" his mental responsibility. The jury rejected his defence and convicted him of murder. Cheatham appealed, claiming the trial judge failed to properly explain "abnormality of mind".

## Issues

Did the trial judge fail to properly instruct the jury on what constitutes an "abnormality of mind" and how it impairs mental responsibility?

## Rule/Law

- An **abnormality of mind** is a state of mind so different from an ordinary human being's that a reasonable person would call it abnormal.
- It must affect at least one of three mental activities: **perception of events**, **ability to judge right from wrong**, or **ability to control one’s actions**.

## Application/Analysis

The trial judge's instructions did include all of these aspects, which gave the jury the correct legal framework to assess the psychiatric evidence.

## Conclusion

The appeal against his convictions was dismissed.

### Judges Rules

To establish an "abnormality of mind" for diminished responsibility under the law:

- **The Standard:** The accused's mind must be so different from an ordinary person's that a reasonable person would find it abnormal.
- **The Three Key Areas:** This abnormality must affect one or more of these three faculties:
    1. **Perception:** Their capacity to perceive and understand physical events.
    2. **Judgment:** Their ability to form a rational or sensible decision about whether their actions are morally right or wrong.
    3. **Willpower:** Their capacity to exercise willpower to control their physical actions.
