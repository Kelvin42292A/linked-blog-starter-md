## Material Facts

In 1929, the NSW Parliament added Section 7A to the _Constitution Act_, stating that the Legislative Council (the upper house) could not be abolished, and Section 7A itself could not be repealed, without a referendum of the electors. In 1930, a new government passed two Bills through both houses to repeal Section 7A and abolish the Council—completely bypassing the referendum.

## Issues

Could the NSW Parliament repeal the referendum requirement and abolish the upper house by simply passing ordinary Bills without holding a referendum?

## Rule/Law

Under Section 5 of the _Colonial Laws Validity Act 1865_, a legislature has the power to change its own constitution, provided it follows the **"manner and form"** (the specific legislative procedures) required by existing laws in force.

## Application/Analysis

The referendum requirement in Section 7A was a valid "manner and form" law. Therefore, any Bill trying to repeal Section 7A or abolish the Legislative Council had to follow that exact procedure to be valid.

## Conclusion

The Privy Council dismissed the appeal. The Bills could not lawfully be presented to the Governor for Royal assent because they had not been approved by a majority of the electors in a referendum.

### Judges Rules

- **The Rule:** A parliament has the power to bind its successors by establishing a special procedure (a "manner and form" requirement, such as a referendum) for making specific constitutional changes.
- **The Binding Effect:** A future parliament cannot simply ignore or pass an ordinary law to bypass this procedure; it is legally forced to comply with the established "manner and form" for any changes to be valid.
