Court of Exchequer Chamber

## Material Facts

- **The Writ:** In 1635, King Charles I issued a nationwide writ commanding all counties—including inland ones like Buckinghamshire—to pay a tax ("Ship Money") to provide and furnish warships for national defense.
- **The Refusal:** John Hampden, a wealthy landowner from Buckinghamshire, refused to pay his assessed share, arguing that the King had no legal authority to levy taxes without the consent of Parliament.
- **The King's Argument:** The Crown argued that the kingdom was in active danger, that the King's duty to defend the realm was supreme, and that the King was the sole judge of when an emergency existed.

## Issues

Does the King have the sole prerogative power to compel subjects to pay non-parliamentary taxes for defense during a self-declared national emergency?

## Rule/Law

- **The Sovereign’s Emergency Prerogative:** When the safety of the kingdom is in general danger, the King possesses the super-eminent authority to command his subjects to pay for national defense.
- **The Executive is the Sole Judge of Emergency:** The monarch is the sole legal judge of whether a national danger exists, as well as how and when that danger must be prevented.
- **State Survival Trumps Property:** Individual property rights are subordinate to collective defense; without state security, private property cannot legally exist.

## Application/Analysis

- **The State of Emergency:** The judges accepted the King’s assertion of a national emergency at face value, refusing to require any objective inquiry or legal proof of the actual danger.
- **Bypassing Parliament:** Although Hampden's counsel argued the King was legally required to call Parliament to raise funds, the court found that the immediate duty of state preservation allowed the Crown to bypass the legislature and demand direct financial service.

## Conclusion

The court ruled in favor of the Crown (7 to 5) and **affirmed that Hampden was legally obligated to pay the tax**. _(Note: This ruling was subsequently declared completely unlawful and void by an Act of Parliament in 1641)._

### Judges Rules

- **No Taxation Without Representation:** The executive branch cannot levy taxes or extract money from citizens without the explicit, statutory consent of the legislature.
- **Emergency Cannot Override Constitutional Limits:** A self-declared "emergency" does not grant the executive the absolute right to bypass legislative processes or confiscate private property.
- **Judges Must Require Proof of Emergency:** Courts must not blindly defer to executive assertions of a crisis. Granting the executive the absolute right to unilaterally define and declare an emergency destroys individual liberties and property rights.
