High Court of Australia

## Material Facts

- The appellant forcibly entered the complainant’s home at night and commenced sexual intercourse with her while she was asleep.
- When the complainant awoke and told him to stop, he ceased and left.
- About eight weeks earlier, the complainant had rejected the appellant's attempt to kiss her.
- The appellant was charged with an aggravated offence under s 112(2) of the _Crimes Act 1900_ (NSW), but the jury found him **not guilty** of that charge. Instead, he was **convicted of the non-aggravated alternative offence under s 112(1)**.

## Issues

- The primary issue was the correct legal meaning of the word "reckless" in s 61R(1) of the _Crimes Act 1900_ (NSW).
- Specifically, the Court had to decide if the trial judge erred by directing the jury that an awareness of a **possibility** that the complainant was not consenting constitutes recklessness.

## Rule/Law

- Under **s 112(1)** of the _Crimes Act 1900_ (NSW), it is an offence to break and enter a dwelling-house and commit a serious indictable offence.
- Sexual intercourse without consent knowing the other person does not consent (s 61I) is a serious indictable offence.
- Under **s 61R(1)**, a person who is "reckless" as to whether the other person consents is legally taken to know that the other person does not consent.
- The High Court ruled that "reckless" in s 61R(1) includes proceeding without even considering if the victim is consenting, or being aware of any possibility that the victim is not consenting but proceeding anyway.

## Application/Analysis

- The trial judge directed the jury that if the accused "is aware that there is a possibility that she is not consenting but he goes ahead anyway, that is recklessness".
- The High Court held that this direction was correct and reasonably approximated the legal requirements for recklessness.
- The jury was entitled to look at the appellant's actual state of mind and the surrounding circumstances—such as his prior rejection by the complainant—to conclude he was reckless as to her consent.

## Conclusion

The appeal was dismissed. The conviction under **s 112(1)** of the _Crimes Act 1900_ (NSW) was upheld because the trial judge's directions regarding recklessness and consent were correct.
