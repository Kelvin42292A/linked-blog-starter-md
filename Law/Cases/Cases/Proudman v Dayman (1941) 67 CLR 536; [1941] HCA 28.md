## Material Facts

Mrs. Proudman was convicted of permitting an unlicensed person to drive a car on a road. She claimed she did not know the driver was unlicensed and believed he had a valid licence. However, she had never actually asked him or made any inquiries to check.

## Issues

What elements must a defendant establish to successfully rely on the defence of "honest and reasonable mistake of fact"?

## Rule/Law

To successfully raise the defence of **honest and reasonable mistake of fact**, three elements must be satisfied:

1. The defendant must hold an **active, honest belief** in a specific state of facts.
2. This belief must be based on **reasonable grounds**.
3. The mistaken facts, if they were actually true, must make the defendant’s conduct **entirely innocent**. _Note: The initial burden of raising this defence rests on the defendant._

## Application/Analysis

Mrs. Proudman did not make any inquiries and likely did not think about the driver's licence at all. Simply not knowing, showing complete indifference, or failing to inquire does not count as holding an active, honest belief on reasonable grounds.

## Conclusion

Because she could not establish the necessary elements of the defence, the High Court refused her application for special leave to appeal.

### Judges Rules

To successfully defend against a strict liability charge using the **honest and reasonable mistake of fact** defence, the defendant must prove:

1. **An Active Belief (Not Ignorance):** You must actively and honestly believe in a specific set of facts. Merely not thinking about the issue, being indifferent, or saying "I didn't know" is not a legal "mistake".
2. **Objective Reasonableness:** Your belief must be based on reasonable grounds—meaning a reasonable person in your situation would have believed the same thing.
3. **Innocence if True:** The mistaken state of affairs, if it actually existed, must make your conduct completely lawful and innocent.
