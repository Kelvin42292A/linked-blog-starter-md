Supreme Court of Queensland

## Material Facts

- The defendants (Chan and Anor) were converting a large, three-level premises into a nightclub called "The Roxy".
- They purchased a grey "Giverney" carpet from the plaintiff (Carpet Call) for $68,839.
- During negotiations, the nightclub owners made general statements that they wanted a "good" grey carpet for a "big" upmarket nightclub with heavy human traffic. They had previously rejected a patterned carpet because they didn't want it to look like a Chinese restaurant.
- The carpet laid by the plaintiff was rated as "heavy duty domestic".
- Shortly after opening, the carpet became an absolute mess due to incredible patron abuse, including myriad cigarette burns, massive drink stains, and ground-in chewing gum.
- The defendants refused to pay the remaining balance of $20,040, arguing that the plaintiff breached the implied statutory condition that the carpet would be reasonably "fit for purpose" for a nightclub.

## Issues

1. **Reliance on Skill & Judgment:** Did the buyers disclose their purpose in a way that showed they actually relied on the seller's skill and judgment to pick the right carpet (under the _Sale of Goods Act_ and _Trade Practices Act_)?
2. **Definition of Consumer Goods:** Are goods like carpet, even if they are bought for a commercial nightclub, legally considered goods "ordinarily acquired for personal, domestic or household use" under the _Trade Practices Act_?
3. **Exclusion Clauses:** Does a standard contract clause stating that the written document is the "entire agreement" legally block these implied statutory warranties from applying?

## Rule/Law

- **The Test for Reliance:** Under both the _Sale of Goods Act_ and the _Trade Practices Act_, it is not enough to merely tell a seller what you are going to use the goods for. The buyer must affirmatively show that they brought it home to the seller's mind that they were **relying** on the seller's skill and judgment.
- **"Consumer" Goods (_Trade Practices Act_ s 4B):** For an expensive purchase (over the statutory limit) to attract the un-excludable implied warranties of the _Trade Practices Act_, the goods must be of a kind "ordinarily acquired for personal, domestic or household use or consumption".
- **"Entire Agreement" Clauses (_Criss v Alexander_):** A boilerplate clause stating that a buyer relied on their own judgment or that the contract is the "entire agreement" does not automatically defeat or negative an implied statutory condition of fitness.

## Application/Analysis

- **No Reliance Shown:** The Supreme Court ruled that the buyers' "imprecise generalisations" (just asking for a "good" grey carpet for a big club) did not prove they were relying on the seller's skill. The buyers actually knew much more about the extremes of nightclub customer behavior than the seller did.
- **The Real Cause was Abuse, Not Unfitness:** The judge found that the carpet failed because of extraordinary human abuse, not because it was inherently unfit. Even if the seller had supplied the strongest, most expensive "extra-heavy commercial" carpet available, it still would have been ruined by the same cigarette burns, drink spills, and chewing gum.
- **Carpet is a Domestic Good:** The judge made an important provisional ruling on the _Trade Practices Act_: **Carpet is fundamentally a commodity ordinarily acquired for domestic consumption**. It does not lose that legal "domestic" description just because it is given a commercial rating or is placed in a commercial nightclub setting.

## Conclusion

The defendants' counter-claim failed. Judgment was awarded to the **plaintiff (Carpet Call)**. The defendants were ordered to pay the outstanding balance of **$20,040**, plus substantial interest.
