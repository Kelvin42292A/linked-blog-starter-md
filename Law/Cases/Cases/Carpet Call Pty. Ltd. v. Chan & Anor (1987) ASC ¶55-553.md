Supreme Court of Queensland

## Material Facts

- Plaintiff (Carpet Call Pty Ltd) supplied and laid plain grey “Givernney” carpet in defendant’s new three-level nightclub “The Roxy” in Brisbane Valley in 1985
- Contract price: $68,839. Defendants paid $48,799, leaving $20,040 outstanding
- Defendants (experienced nightclub operators) told plaintiff they wanted a “good” quality grey carpet for large nightclub with heavy human traffic catering for young upmarket patrons.
- Plaintiff supplied “heavy duty domestic” rated carpet. No advice given about patterned or commercial-grade carpet
- Carpet suffered heavy damage (especially ground floor): cigarette burns, drink/food stains, chewing gum, and wear from patron abuse
- Defendants refused to pay the balance, claiming the carpet was not fit for purpose. They issued dishonoured post-date cheques
- Plaintiff sued for the balance owing. Defendants counter-claimed for breach of implied fitness for purpose.

## Issues

- Whether the defendants made known to the plaintiff the particular purpose for the carpet with sufficient clarity
- Whether the defendants relied on the plaintiff’s skill and judgement
- Whether the carpet was goods of a kind ordinarily acquired for personal, domestic or household use or consumption (for TPA purpose)

## Rule/Law

- Sale of Goods Act 1896 (Qld), sec 17(4): An express warranty or condition does not negative an implied warranty or condition unless inconsistent with it.
- Trade Practices Act 1974 (Cth), s71(2): Where a corporations supplies goods to **consumer** in the course of business and the consumer makes known the particular purpose, there is an implied condition that the goods are reasonably fit for that purpose
- s 4B(1): A person is a “consumer” if the goods are of a kind **ordinarily acquired for personal, domestic or household use or consumption** (or price below prescribed amount).
- s 68: Implied conditions under the TPA **cannot be excluded** or modified

## Application/Analysis

- Defendants only told plaintiff they wanted a “good” grey carpet for a big nightclub with heavy traffic for upmarket patrons. This was too vague and general.
- They did not clearly explain the expected harsh use (burns, stains, heavy abuse)
- Defendants were experienced nightclub operators. They inspected samples, rejected some carpets and shopped around
- No real reliance on plaintiff’s skill and judgement
- Therefore, no **implied condition** of fitness for purpose under **s 17 Sale of Goods Act** or **s 71(2) Trade Practices Act**
- Carpet qualifies as consumer goods under **s 4B TPA** (domestic/household use), but this did not assist defendants due to lace of reliance.

## Conclusion

No implied condition of fitness for purpose arose under s 17 Sale of Goods Act or s 71(2) Trade Practices Act. The defendants failed to show clear purpose and reliance on the plaintiff. As a result, no breach. Plaintiff wins and defendants must pay the $20,040 balance.
