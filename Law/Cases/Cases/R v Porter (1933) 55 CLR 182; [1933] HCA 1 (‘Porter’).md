## Material Facts

After a nervous breakdown and a failed reconciliation with his wife, Bertram Edward Porter administered strychnine to his 11-month-old son, causing the child's death, and attempted to poison himself. He was tried for murder and pleaded temporary insanity.

## Issues

At the time he poisoned his son, was Porter's state of mind so disordered that he was not criminally responsible (legally insane)?

## Rule/Law

- **Presumption of Sanity:** Everyone is presumed sane; the defence must prove insanity on the **balance of probabilities**.
- **The Insanity Test:** The accused is not criminally responsible if, at the time of the act, they suffered from a **disease, disorder, or disturbance of the mind** that prevented them from:
    1. **Knowing the physical nature and consequences of the act**, **OR**
    2. **Knowing that the act was wrong** according to the everyday standards of reasonable people (meaning they were incapable of reasoning about its rightness or wrongness with a moderate degree of sense and composure).

## Application/Analysis

Dixon J instructed the jury that while Porter likely understood the physical nature of his act, the key question was whether his mental disturbance made him **incapable of reasoning with a moderate degree of composure** about the wrongness of killing his child.

## Conclusion

The jury returned a verdict of **not guilty on the ground of insanity**.

### Judges Rules (Generally)

If an accused has a mental disorder, they are legally insane if:

1. They do not know their act is **wrong** according to the **everyday standards of ordinary people**.
2. **The Key Test:** Their mind is so disturbed that they are **incapable of reasoning about the act with a "moderate degree of sense and composure"**. If they cannot think calmly and rationally about the rightness or wrongness of the act, they do not "know" it is wrong.

### Judges Rules (Disease of the mind)

A "disease of the mind" is **not** a physical brain injury. It is any mental condition that temporarily or permanently stops you from using your understanding to reason with a **moderate degree of sense and composure** about whether your act is right or wrong.

### Judges Rule (Knowledge of Nature and Wrongness)

For the insanity defence, a mental disorder must prevent the accused from understanding one of two things:

1. **The Physical Act:** They do not understand the physical nature of what they are doing (such as believing they are destroying an inanimate object instead of a human life).
2. **The Wrongness:** They do not understand that the act is morally wrong by the everyday standards of ordinary people. An accused lacks this understanding if their mind is so disturbed that they **cannot reason about the act with a moderate degree of sense and composure**.
