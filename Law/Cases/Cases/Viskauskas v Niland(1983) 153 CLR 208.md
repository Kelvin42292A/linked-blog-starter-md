## Material Facts

Three Aboriginal people were allegedly refused service at a hotel bar in Kempsey, New South Wales, because of their race. Both the Commonwealth Commissioner (under the _Racial Discrimination Act 1975_) and the NSW State Counsellor (under the _Anti-Discrimination Act 1977_) started separate investigations into the same event. The hotel operators sued the State Counsellor, Carmel Niland, arguing the State had no power to investigate because the federal law had already dealt with the matter.

## Issues

Are the racial discrimination provisions in the NSW State Act inconsistent with the Commonwealth Act, making the State provisions invalid under **Section 109 of the Constitution**?

## Rule/Law

Under Section 109, when a State law is inconsistent with a Commonwealth law, the Commonwealth law prevails and the State law is invalid to the extent of the inconsistency. Inconsistency exists if the Commonwealth Parliament shows an intention to **"cover the field"** by completely, exhaustively, or exclusively stating the law on that subject. If two laws cover the same subject for the same purpose, but set up different procedures or remedies, they are inconsistent.

## Application/Analysis

Both Acts deal with the exact same subject of racial discrimination and the refusal of goods and services. The Commonwealth Act was intended as a **complete and exclusive statement** of the law for Australia to fulfill its international treaty obligations. Because the State Act set up a different tribunal, different procedures, and strict limits on damages for the exact same conduct, it interfered with the exclusive federal framework.

## Conclusion

The High Court declared that the NSW provisions were inconsistent with the Commonwealth Act and therefore invalid. The State Counsellor had **no legal power** to investigate or conciliate the complaints.

### Judges Rules

- **The "Covering the Field" Inconsistency Rule:** Under Section 109 of the Constitution, if the Commonwealth Parliament intends its law to be the complete, exhaustive, and exclusive law on a subject, any State law entering that same field is invalid.
- **Same Conduct, Different Sanctions:** Even if it is possible to obey both laws at the same time, an inconsistency arises if both parliaments legislate on the same subject for the same purpose but establish **different procedures, remedies, or penalties**.
