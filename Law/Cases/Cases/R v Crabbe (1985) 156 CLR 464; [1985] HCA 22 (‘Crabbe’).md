High Court of Australia

## Material Facts

- The respondent (Crabbe) was drinking heavily at the Inland Motel bar at Ayers Rock and was physically ejected for being a nuisance.
- In the early hours of the morning, Crabbe returned driving a massive road train (a prime mover and trailer) and deliberately drove it through the wall into the crowded bar.
- Five people were killed, and many were injured. Crabbe left the scene without helping anyone.
- At trial, the judge directed the jury that Crabbe was guilty of murder if he drove the truck into the bar foreseeing the **"possibility"** that people might be inside, and deliberately blinded himself to checking whether they were there or not.
- Crabbe was convicted of murder, but the Federal Court quashed the conviction on appeal due to the judge's directions. The Crown appealed to the High Court.

## Issues

1. **Mens Rea (Probability vs. Possibility):** Does the _mens rea_ for murder by recklessness require the accused to foresee that death or grievous bodily harm is a **probability** (likely), or is it enough that they foresee it as a mere **possibility**?
2. **Wilful Blindness:** Did the trial judge misdirect the jury by telling them they could convict if Crabbe foresaw a "possibility" of people being in the bar and failed to take steps to check?

## Rule/Law

- **Reckless Murder (The Probability Test):** The High Court established a firm rule: A person who acts without lawful justification, knowing that it is **probable** (likely) that death or grievous bodily harm will result, is guilty of murder.
    - _Clarifying Pemble:_ The High Court officially disapproved of the suggestion made by Barwick CJ in _Pemble v The Queen_ that foreseeing a mere "possibility" was enough. Knowing an act might _possibly_ cause harm is not murder unless actual intent is proven.
- **Knowledge vs. Indifference:** While it is often called "reckless indifference", the law does not strictly require indifference. The core element is the accused's **actual knowledge** that the consequences will probably occur, not just their careless attitude.
- **Wilful Blindness:** Actual knowledge or foresight is necessary; imputed knowledge (saying someone _should_ have known) is not enough. You cannot be "wilfully blind" to a risk unless you actually realize the risk is probable in the first place.

## Application/Analysis

- **The Flawed "Possibility" Direction:** The High Court ruled that the trial judge's instruction was a "material misdirection". By telling the jury they could convict if Crabbe only foresaw the "possibility" that people might be in the bar, the judge incorrectly lowered the legal threshold for murder.
- **The Flawed "Wilful Blindness" Direction:** There was no evidence that Crabbe deliberately refrained from checking the bar just to keep himself ignorant. Suggesting that he failed to take "reasonable steps" to check confused the jury, because it suggested he could be convicted for negligence (failing to check) rather than actual, subjective foresight of a probable danger.

## Conclusion

The Crown's appeal was **dismissed**. The High Court agreed with the Federal Court that the trial judge's directions were fatally flawed. The conviction for murder was set aside, and a new trial was ordered.

### Judges rules

- **The Probability Test:** To be guilty of murder by recklessness, you must have actual knowledge that your actions will **probably** (likely) cause death or grievous bodily harm. Knowing that harm is merely a "possibility" is not enough.
- **"Indifference" is Not the Element:** The court ruled that the phrase "reckless indifference" is actually misleading. It does not matter if you were indifferent to the consequences, or even if you secretly hoped no one would get hurt; the only legal requirement is your **actual knowledge** that the harm was probable.
- **Wilful Blindness:** You cannot be convicted of murder simply because you were "wilfully blind" and carelessly failed to check if people were in danger. You can only be guilty if you _actually knew_ the risk was probable in the first place.
