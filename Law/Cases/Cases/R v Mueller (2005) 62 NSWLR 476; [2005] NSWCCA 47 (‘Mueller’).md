Court of Criminal Appeal for New South Wales

## Material Facts

- The complainant was a resident at a care home due to disabilities (Asperger’s Syndrome and schizophrenia).
- The appellant worked at the care home and was responsible for providing care and assistance to residents, including the complainant.
- On 25 April, the complainant placed her feet on the appellant’s lap.
- The appellant touched and kissed the complainant’s breasts, bit her nipples, touched her vulva outside her clothing, and kissed her vulva.
- The appellant also took the complainant’s hand and forced her to touch his penis.
- The appellant was found **not guilty** on some charges but **guilty** on the charges relating to touching the complainant’s vulva, kissing her vulva, and forcing her to touch his penis.

## Issues

Whether the trial judge gave a wrong or inadequate direction to the jury regarding the definition of consent.

## Rule/Law

- In **Victoria**, consent is defined under **s 36** of the _Crimes Act 1958_ (Vic) as meaning **free agreement**.
- In **New South Wales**, there is no direct statutory definition of consent. However, **s 61R** of the _Crimes Act 1900_ (NSW) explains situations where consent is **vitiated** (cancelled), such as when it is obtained by threats, terror, or force.
- Consent must be **free and voluntary**. Mere submission because of force, threats, or fear is **not** consent.
- It is not necessary for the victim to physically resist or scream for it to be non-consensual.
- Key cases:
    - _R v Blayney_ (2003) 87 SASR 354
    - _Question of Law (No 1 of 1993)_ (1993) 59 SASR 214

## Application/Analysis

- The appeal court agreed that saying "freely and voluntarily" can be risky. In everyday language, these words mean acting without any reluctance, but legally, **reluctant consent is still valid consent**.
- The court decided the trial judge did not make a mistake because jury instructions must be looked at as a whole, not just one isolated sentence.
- The trial judge fixed any possible confusion by clearly telling the jury that **consent given after persuasion is still legal consent.**
- Because the judge clearly explained the difference between actual consent and "mere submission" (just giving in due to pressure or threats), the jury was not misled, and the conviction was upheld.

## Conclusion

The appeal was dismissed. The appellant’s conduct constituted sexual offences against a cognitively impaired person in his care.
