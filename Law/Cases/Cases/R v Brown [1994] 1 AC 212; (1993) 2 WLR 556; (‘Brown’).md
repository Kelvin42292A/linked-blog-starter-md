House of Lords (UK)

## Material Facts

- The appellants were a group of men who willingly and enthusiastically participated in sado-masochistic acts against each other in private for sexual pleasure.
- The activities included genital torture, bloodletting, and branding.
- All participants were consenting adults, there were no public complaints, and no permanent injuries were sustained.
- The men were charged with **assault occasioning actual bodily harm** (under **s. 47**) and **wounding** (under **s. 20**).
- The trial judge ruled that their consent was no defence; consequently, they pleaded guilty and appealed.

## Issues

Where an individual causes actual bodily harm or wounding during a sado-masochistic encounter, does the prosecution have to prove lack of consent? (In short: Is consent a valid defence to **s. 20** and **s. 47** charges in the context of sado-masochism?).

## Rule/Law

- **s. 20 and s. 47 _Offences against the Person Act 1861_:** The statutory offences for unlawful wounding and assault occasioning actual bodily harm.
- **_Rex v Donovan_ & _Attorney-General's Reference (No. 6 of 1980)_:** Established the general rule that it is unlawful to cause actual bodily harm to another person for "no good reason". If actual bodily harm is caused or intended, the victim's consent is legally immaterial.
- **The Public Interest Exceptions:** Consent _can_ validate the infliction of actual bodily harm, but only in recognized exceptions justified by public interest (e.g., properly conducted sports, reasonable surgery, and lawful chastisement).

## Application/Analysis

- **No "Good Reason":** The majority held that the satisfaction of sado-masochistic libido does not constitute a "good reason" to cause actual bodily harm, nor does it fall into any of the recognized public interest exceptions.
- **Public Interest:** The majority viewed sado-masochistic violence as unpredictably dangerous, degrading, and injurious to participants. It breeds cruelty, which is not in the public interest to protect or legalize.
- **Human Rights:** The appellants' arguments under Article 8 of the European Convention on Human Rights (right to privacy) were rejected; the right to private life does not invalidate laws protecting citizens from intentionally harmful violence.

## Conclusion

The appeals were **dismissed** (by a 3-2 majority). The House of Lords ruled that consent is **not** a defence to charges under **s. 20** or **s. 47** of the _Offences against the Person Act 1861_ in the context of sado-masochistic encounters. The original convictions were upheld.
