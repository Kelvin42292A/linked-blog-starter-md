Supreme Court of New South Wales

## Material Facts

- **The Parties and Charge:** Jack Congo Murrell, an Aboriginal man, was charged with the murder of another Aboriginal man, Bill Jabingi, on the Windsor road near Sydney.
- **The Jurisdictional Objection:** Murrell’s counsel, Sydney Stephen, filed a plea (demurrer) denying the court's jurisdiction. He argued that Aboriginal people were governed by their own customs, were not British subjects, and that New South Wales was not an empty, conquered, or ceded country. He maintained that the settlers were obliged to obey Aboriginal customs rather than the other way around.
- **The Government's Argument:** The Attorney General argued that the court had jurisdiction because the offense occurred within the colony's geographical limits, the King was obligated to protect natives, and English law does not recognize any independent sovereign power within British territory.

## Issues

Does the Supreme Court of New South Wales have criminal jurisdiction to try an Aboriginal person for the murder of another Aboriginal person committed within the geographical limits of the colony?

## Rule/Law

- **Territorial Sovereignty:** When the British King takes actual possession of an unappropriated territory, the English nation obtains and exercises the rights of "Domain and Empire" over that country.
- **The Law of the Land:** Under the common law and the _Australian Courts Act 1828_ (9 Geo 4 c 83), English law is established as the sole law of the land, giving the colonial Supreme Court full criminal jurisdiction equivalent to the Westminster courts in England.
- **No Competing Sovereignty:** While Aboriginal people are recognized as free and independent, their tribes do not constitute sovereign states governed by their own laws because they lacked the necessary numbers, civilization, and form of government at the time of settlement.
- **Equality of Protection and Liability:** There is no legal distinction regarding protection or liability under the law between a British subject and an alien living under the King's peace.

## Application/Analysis

- **Offense Within the Realm:** Because the alleged murder occurred on the Windsor road, it took place within the proclaimed geographical boundaries of the colony where English law strictly prevails.
- **Consistency in Prosecution:** Since the Supreme Court had repeatedly tried and executed Aboriginal people for crimes against white subjects, there was no legal basis to distinguish between crimes committed against subjects and crimes committed against each other.
- **The Necessity of Public Order:** Denying jurisdiction would cause extreme public scandal and inconvenience, as it would mean serious crimes like murder could be committed in the streets without any legal restraint.
- **Hardships are for the Legislature:** Any practical difficulties, hardships, or inconveniences in trying Aboriginal people under English law are matters for the local legislature to resolve or for the Crown to address through the royal prerogative of mercy—they do not strip the court of its legal jurisdiction.

## Conclusion

The Supreme Court of New South Wales unanimously overruled the prisoner's plea, declaring that the court had full jurisdiction to try Murrell

### Judges Rule

All persons residing within the defined geographical boundaries of a colony—including indigenous inhabitants committing offenses against one another—are fully subject to the criminal jurisdiction of the colonial courts. Because the Crown does not recognize independent sovereign states or competing legal systems within its territory, there is no distinction in criminal liability or protection between subjects and aliens living under the King's peace.
