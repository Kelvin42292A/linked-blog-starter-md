## Material Facts

DB was prosecuted for child sexual touching. He was acquitted because he was asleep at the time and acting involuntarily due to "sexsomnia" (a sleepwalking disorder involving sexual behaviour). The Crown appealed, arguing that sexsomnia is a "mental health impairment" under the _Mental Health and Cognitive Impairment Forensic Provisions Act 2020_(NSW). The Crown argued DB should have received a special forensic verdict ("act proven but not criminally responsible") rather than an outright acquittal.

## Issues

Does a natural absence of volition while asleep (such as sleepwalking or sexsomnia) count as a "disturbance of volition" and a "mental health impairment" under the 2020 Act, effectively abolishing the common law defence of sane automatism for sleep-related acts?

## Rule/Law

- At common law, sleepwalking is a classic example of **sane automatism** that entitles an accused to an outright acquittal.
- Under s 4(1)(a) of the 2020 Act, a mental health impairment requires a "temporary or ongoing **disturbance** of volition".
- A natural, universal absence of volition that occurs when any healthy person is asleep is not a "disturbance" or impairment of ordinary functioning.

## Application/Analysis

DB’s lack of volition was simply the natural state of being asleep, which is an ordinary human condition. Because parasomnias like sexsomnia do not involve awake psychopathology or require psychiatric hospital treatment, they do not meet the legal threshold for a clinical "mental health impairment".

## Conclusion

The NSW Court of Criminal Appeal dismissed the appeal by majority, confirming that DB was entitled to an **outright acquittal** under the common law of sane automatism.

### Judges Rules

- **The Rule:** A natural **absence of volition during sleep is not a "disturbance of volition"** or a "mental health impairment" under modern forensic legislation.
- **The Legal Consequence:** The common law defence of **sane automatism remains fully active for sleepwalking**. Involuntary acts committed while asleep result in a complete, outright acquittal, rather than a special verdict of mental health impairment.
