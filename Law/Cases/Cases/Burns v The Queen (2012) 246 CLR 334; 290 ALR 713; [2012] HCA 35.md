### Material

- The appellant (Mrs. Burns) and her husband supplied methadone to the deceased at their apartment.
- The deceased had already taken other prescription drugs (olanzapine) earlier that day.
- After taking the methadone, the deceased had an adverse reaction and became noticeably drug-affected ("out of it").
- Despite someone suggesting they call an ambulance, the deceased declined, and the husband told him to leave, walking him outside.
- The deceased's body was found the next day in a nearby toilet block, having died from the combined toxic effect of methadone and olanzapine.
- The appellant was convicted of manslaughter. The trial judge had directed the jury that the mere _supply_ of the methadone could constitute an "unlawful and dangerous act".

### Issue

- **Unlawful & Dangerous Act:** Does the mere _supply_ of an illicit drug to an adult constitute an "unlawful and dangerous act" for the purposes of manslaughter?
- **Causation:** Does the voluntary consumption of the drug by a fully informed, sane adult break the chain of causation between the supplier and the death?
- **Criminal Negligence (Omissions):** Does a drug supplier owe a "legal duty of care" to seek medical assistance for a person who takes drugs in their presence and suffers an adverse reaction?

### Rule

- **Unlawful and Dangerous Act:** An act is dangerous if a reasonable person would realize it exposes another to an appreciable risk of serious injury (_Wilson v The Queen_),. However, supplying a drug is not inherently dangerous; the danger only arises upon _ingestion_.
- **Chain of Causation:** The voluntary and informed act of a sane adult negatives causal connection (_Royall v The Queen_; _R v Kennedy (No 2)_),,,. What a sane adult chooses to do breaks the chain of causation from the original supplier.
- **Manslaughter by Omission:** Criminal liability for failing to act (omission) only arises if the accused owes a strict **legal duty** to the victim (e.g., created by statute, contract, special relationship, or voluntarily assuming their care) (_R v Taktak_).

### Application

- **Supply vs. Administration:** The Crown conceded that the trial judge misdirected the jury,. While supplying methadone is illegal, it is not an objectively _dangerous_ act on its own,. Therefore, supply alone cannot support a manslaughter conviction.
- **Chain Broken:** The deceased was a sane adult,. Although his decision to combine drugs was foolish, he voluntarily and knowingly chose to take the methadone. This voluntary act broke the chain of causation, meaning the appellant did not legally "cause" his death.
- **No Legal Duty of Care:** The appellant did not owe a legal duty to the deceased. She had not voluntarily assumed his care, nor did she fall into any recognized category of duty (like doctor/patient or parent/child). The danger was created by the deceased's own choice to ingest the drugs, not by the appellant's act of supplying them. Without a legal duty, there can be no manslaughter by criminal negligence for failing to call an ambulance.

### Conclusion

The appeal was **allowed**. The High Court **quashed** the manslaughter conviction and entered a verdict of **acquittal** because the trial judge misdirected the jury and there was insufficient legal basis to find the appellant liable for the death.
