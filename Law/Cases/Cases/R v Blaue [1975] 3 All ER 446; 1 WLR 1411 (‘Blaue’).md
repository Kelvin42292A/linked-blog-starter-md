Court of Appeal, Criminal Division (UK)

## Material Facts

- On May 3, 1974, the appellant entered the house of an 18-year-old girl and asked her for sexual intercourse. When she refused, he attacked her with a knife, inflicting four serious wounds, one of which pierced her lung.
- The victim lost a large quantity of blood and was taken to the hospital, where she was informed that a blood transfusion was necessary before she could undergo surgery.
- The victim was a practicing Jehovah's Witness. She consciously and deliberately refused the blood transfusion because it was contrary to her religious beliefs, even after being told that she would die without it.
- The Crown admitted that if she had accepted the blood transfusion, she would not have died. She died the following day, and the physical cause of death was bleeding into her pleural cavity caused by the stab wound.
- At trial, the appellant was convicted of manslaughter on the ground of diminished responsibility.
- The appellant appealed the conviction. His core argument was that the girl's refusal to have a blood transfusion was unreasonable and had therefore broken the chain of causation between his stabbing and her death.
- At trial, the judge directed the jury that they could still find the stab wound was an "operative cause" or a "substantial cause" of her death.

## Issues

1. **Breaking the Chain:** Did the victim's refusal to get a life-saving blood transfusion break the "chain of causation" between the stabbing and her death?
2. **Unreasonable Beliefs:** Is an attacker allowed to argue that a victim's religious beliefs were "unreasonable" in order to avoid being blamed for their death?
3. **The Whole Person:** Does the rule that you must "take your victim as you find them" apply to a person's mind and religious beliefs, or does it only apply to their physical body?

## Rule/Law

- **Refusing Medical Treatment (_R v Holland_):** An attacker cannot excuse themselves from a homicide charge by arguing that the victim could have avoided death if they had just accepted medical treatment or taken better care of themselves.
- **Operating and Substantial Cause (_R v Smith_):** If the original wound is still an "operating and substantial cause" at the time of death, then the attacker caused the death. The chain of causation is only broken if a second event is so overwhelming that it makes the original wound merely "part of the history".
- **Take Your Victim As You Find Them (The Whole Person):** The legal rule that an attacker must "take their victim as they find them" applies to the **entire person**, not just their physical body. This includes their deeply held religious beliefs.
- **Unreasonable Conduct:** Because you must take your victim's beliefs as you find them, an attacker is not legally allowed to argue that the victim's religious refusal to accept medical treatment was "unreasonable"

## Application/Analysis

- **The "Whole Person":** The Court applied the rule that an attacker must "take their victims as they find them." Importantly, this rule applies to the **whole person**, meaning their mind and religious beliefs, not just their physical body.
- **No Unreasonable Beliefs:** Because of this rule, an attacker cannot excuse their violent actions by arguing that the victim's religious beliefs (and their refusal of treatment) were "unreasonable".
- **The Chain of Causation:** The physical cause of death was the bleeding caused by the stab wound, not her decision to refuse the transfusion. The fact that the victim refused medical treatment did not break the causal connection between the stabbing and her death.

## Conclusion

The appeal was **dismissed**. The appellant's manslaughter conviction was upheld. The Court concluded that the stab wound was the operative cause of death, and the victim's religious refusal to accept a blood transfusion did not break the chain of causation.
