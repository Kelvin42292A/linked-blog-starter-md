## Material Facts

Walton shot and killed a stranger in a passing car after his girlfriend fled from him. He claimed diminished responsibility. His medical experts testified that his immature personality substantially impaired his responsibility, and the prosecution did not call any doctors to dispute this. The jury still rejected his defence and convicted him of murder.

## Issues

Is a jury legally forced to accept a mental health defence if the medical evidence is not challenged or contradicted by the prosecution?

## Rule/Law

A jury is not bound to accept medical evidence just because it is unchallenged. Juries must evaluate the quality of the medical opinions alongside all other facts and circumstances of the case.

## Application/Analysis

The jury was entitled to reject the medical opinion because the psychologist's tests showed Walton had normal intelligence, and his behaviour during the shooting did not suggest a mind bordering on insanity.

## Conclusion

The appeal was dismissed, and the murder conviction stood.

### Judges Rules

- **The Burden of Proof:** The defendant must prove diminished responsibility on the **balance of probabilities**.
- **The Jury's Authority:** A jury has the final say and is **never forced to accept medical evidence**, even if it is completely unchallenged by the prosecution.
- **The Whole-Picture Test:** To decide if the defence is proven, the jury must weigh the medical opinions against the **entire factual circumstances**, including the nature of the killing and the defendant's conduct before, during, and after the crime.
