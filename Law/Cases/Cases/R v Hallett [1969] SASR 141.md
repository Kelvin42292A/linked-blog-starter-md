### Material Facts
The accused violently assaulted the victim, rendering him unconscious, and left him lying on a beach with his feet in the sea. The victim later drowned in shallow water (either by the tide coming in or by rolling into the sea while unconscious). The accused claimed he had tired to save the victim earlier and believed he had left him in a safe position before falling asleep
### Issue
1. Did the accused's action **cause** the victim's death?
2. Did the accused have the necessary **malice aforethought** for murder
### Rule
- **Causation:** The accused causes death if his unlawful act is a substantial operating cause of death. The ordinary operation of natural forces (e.g. tide) does not break the chain of causation if the victim was left in a position of danger while unconscious.
- **Malice Aforethought:**
	 1. Includes intention to kill,
	 2. intention to cause grievous bodily harm, or
	 3. Knowledge that the act is likely to cause death or GBH (even if accompanied by indifference or a wish that it not occur)
### Application
- **Causation:** The Court held that leaving the unconscious victim at the water's edge was a substantial cause of death. The sea's ordinary action (tide or rolling) did not break the chain. The trial judge's direction was correct.
- **Malice Aforethought:** There was sufficient evidence that during the assault in the water the accused applied force with knowledge that it was likely to cause death or serious harm.
### Conclusion
The conviction for murder was upheld. The appeal was dismissed on both causation and malice aforethought.
