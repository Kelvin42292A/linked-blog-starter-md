Supreme Court in Banco
### Material

- The appellant (Hallett) and the deceased (Whiting) got into a physical altercation in the shallow water at Tumby Bay beach.
- During the struggle, the appellant choked the deceased, rendering him unconscious.
- The appellant left the unconscious deceased lying on his back on the sand with his ankles in the water, and went further up the beach to sleep.
- When the appellant woke up, he found the deceased dead, floating in the water.
- Medical evidence confirmed the deceased did not die from the physical injuries, but from drowning in shallow water while unconscious.
- The appellant was convicted of murder. He appealed, arguing he did not legally cause the death because the incoming tide drowned the victim while he was asleep.

### Issue

- **Causation:** Can the ordinary action of the sea (e.g., the tide coming in) break the chain of causation between an accused's violent assault and the victim's death?
- **Malice Aforethought (Recklessness):** Did the trial judge misdirect the jury by stating that foreseeing death or grievous bodily harm as a "likely" or "probable" consequence of an act is sufficient for the _mens rea_ of murder?

### Rule

- **Chain of Causation:** The accused's act must be a substantial and operating cause of death. The chain of causation is broken by an _extraordinary_ operation of natural forces (e.g., an unpredictable earthquake or a tidal wave), but is **not** broken by the _ordinary_ operation of natural causes (e.g., the natural flow of the tide).
- **Malice Aforethought:** Foresight that death or grievous bodily harm is "likely" or "probable" to occur from an act is legally sufficient to constitute malice aforethought for murder. The court ruled that "likely" and "probable" mean essentially the same thing (a more than 50% chance).

### Application

- **Causation Was Not Broken:** The Court held that the appellant's act of choking the victim into unconsciousness was the foundational act that caused the death. Leaving an unconscious person at the edge of the ocean exposed them to the ordinary, natural movement of the tides, which does not act as a supervening cause to break the chain of causation. The chain would only have been broken if the victim had regained consciousness and voluntarily walked back into the water himself.
- **Correct Mens Rea Directions:** The trial judge's instructions were correct. The appellant applied severe force to the victim's throat in the water. If the jury was satisfied the appellant foresaw that this violence was _likely_ to cause death or grievous bodily harm, the element of malice aforethought was satisfied at the time of the assault. It did not legally matter if the appellant subsequently hoped the victim would survive when he left him on the sand.

### Conclusion

The appeal was **dismissed**. The Court ruled that the ordinary action of the sea did not break the chain of causation, and upheld the original murder conviction.Supreme Court in Banco
