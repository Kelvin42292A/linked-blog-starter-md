## Material Facts

Willgoss, a diagnosed psychopath, broke into a house and shot an elderly man three times. He argued he was legally insane because his psychopathy prevented him from "feeling" or emotionally understanding that his actions were wrong, even though he knew it intellectually.

## Issues

Does a lack of emotional feeling or conscience count as not "knowing" an act is wrong under the insanity defence?

## Rule/Law

Legal insanity requires a disease of the mind that destroys the accused's capacity to _know_ their act is wrong. The law does not draw a distinction between intellectual knowledge and moral or emotional feelings.

## Application/Analysis

Willgoss was capable of intellectually understanding that murder was wrong. His lack of conscience or empathy (psychopathy) did not legalise or excuse his actions under the insanity test.

## Conclusion

The appeal was refused, confirming Willgoss was legally sane and guilty.

### Judges Rules

- **The Rule:** A "defect of reason" must affect the accused's **intellectual capacity** to know right from wrong.
- **The Limit:** A mere **lack of emotional feeling, empathy, or conscience** (such as psychopathy) is not a defect of reason under the law. If you intellectually know an act is wrong, you are legally responsible, even if you cannot emotionally feel that it is wrong.
