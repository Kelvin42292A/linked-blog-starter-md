Supreme Court of New South Wales

## Material Facts

- **The Punch:** During a street brawl, Ahmed Dib (the accused’s brother) was punched and knocked unconscious.
- **The Reaction:** The accused, Moustapha Dib, walked toward the fight, saw his brother on the ground, and believed his brother was at least seriously injured.
- **The Mistake:** Dib ran back to his house, armed himself with a kitchen knife, and returned to the street. Seeing 14-year-old Edward Lee running away, Dib mistakenly believed Lee was the person who had punched his brother.
- **The Killing:** Dib stabbed and killed Lee. Lee was completely innocent and had not punched the brother or engaged in any provocative conduct.
- **The Plea:** Dib sought to plead guilty to manslaughter on the basis of provocation, which the prosecution accepted, but the trial judge had to decide whether to accept the plea.

## Issues

- Can an accused's **mistaken belief** about the victim's actions or identity be regarded as "conduct of the deceased" to support the statutory defense of provocation?

## Rule/Law

- **Statutory Requirement:** Under Section 23 of the _Crimes Act 1900_ (NSW), provocation must be induced by the **"conduct of the deceased"**.
- **No Mistaken Conduct:** As a matter of ordinary English, a mistaken belief by the accused cannot be substituted for actual conduct of the deceased.
- **The Mistake Limitation:** The legal doctrine of honest and reasonable mistake only excuses an action if the imagined facts would make the accused’s conduct **completely innocent**. It does not apply if the mistake merely reduces the crime from murder to manslaughter.
- **Reasonableness:** Under Australian law, any mistake of fact must be **both honest and reasonable** to be legally considered.

## Application/Analysis

- **No Actual Provocation:** Edward Lee did absolutely nothing to provoke the accused.
- **Mistake Does Not Count:** Dib's argument relied entirely on his mistaken belief that Lee was the attacker. Because this mistake was not actual "conduct of the deceased," it could not legally trigger the defense of provocation.
- **No Innocence:** Since believing Lee was the attacker would still not make stabbing him innocent (it would only reduce the crime to manslaughter), the doctrine of mistake could not assist Dib.
- **Unreasonable Belief:** Even if a mistake could be considered, it was highly unlikely that a reasonable person in Dib's position would have held such a belief given the dark street and chaotic scene.

## Conclusion

The court **refused to accept the plea of guilty to manslaughter**, ruling that a mistaken belief cannot support a provocation defense under the law.

### Judges Rules

- **Requires Actual Conduct:** To raise provocation, the provocative acts must be the **actual, physical conduct of the deceased** (or conduct they are legally responsible for via joint enterprise/agency).
- **Mistakes Are Excluded:** A mistaken belief—such as misidentifying an innocent bystander as the provoker—does **not** count as "conduct of the deceased" and cannot be used to claim provocation.
- **Must Aim for Total Innocence:** An honest and reasonable mistake of fact is only a defense if the imagined facts would make the accused's actions **completely innocent**; it cannot be used to simply lower a charge from murder to manslaughter.
- **Must Be Reasonable:** If a mistake is to have any operation in criminal law, it must be **both honest and reasonable**. Unreasonable, panicked, or drug-induced hallucinations do not satisfy this standard.
