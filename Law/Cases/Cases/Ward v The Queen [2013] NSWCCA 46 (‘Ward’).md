## Material Facts

Sarah May Ward, who was unlicensed and heavily intoxicated by alcohol and drugs, took a car without permission. After a minor verbal and physical fight with a group of young men in a lane, the men walked away. Ward drove after them, mounted a kerb, and drove directly at Eli Westlake. He jumped out of the way. She reversed, and as he was walking down the street again, she drove diagonally across the grass verge and footpath at 27 km/h, hitting him and crushing him to death. Ward claimed she panicked and accidentally hit the accelerator. She pleaded guilty to manslaughter, but the Crown rejected it and prosecuted her for murder. She was convicted and appealed, claiming the trial judge gave faulty jury directions on the standard of proof, intoxication/intent, provocation, and substantial impairment.

## Issues

Did the trial judge misdirect the jury on the standard of proof, her rejected manslaughter plea, the effect of intoxication on forming intent, provocation, or substantial impairment?

## Rule/Law

- **Intoxication and Intent:** Intoxication can disinhibit a person and cause them to form an intention or make decisions they would not make when sober, but an **"intoxicated intention" is still legally a valid intention**.
- **The "Whole Charge" Rule:** When assessing if a judge's instructions misdirected a jury, the appellate court must **read the instructions as a whole**, rather than isolating single sentences or minor verbal slips.
- **Substantial Impairment:** The disinhibiting effects of voluntary intoxication must be excluded when assessing whether a defendant's abnormality of mind substantially impaired their capacity.

## Application/Analysis

- The judge’s directions on intoxication properly instructed the jury to determine what _actual_ intent Ward formed.
- Even if the judge made minor verbal slips, there was no miscarriage of justice because the objective evidence was overwhelming. The CCTV footage clearly showed Ward deliberately targeting the deceased twice and driving directly at him without decelerating, proving she had at least the intent to cause really serious injury.

## Conclusion

The High Court/Court of Criminal Appeal granted leave to appeal on provocation but dismissed the appeal, upholding her murder conviction and sentence.

### Judges Rules

- **Rule 1: The "Intoxicated Intention" Rule** Being drunk or high might explain _why_ someone formed a violent intention, but **an intention formed while intoxicated is still a legally valid intention to commit a crime**.
- **Rule 2: The "Summing-Up as a Whole" Rule** An appeal based on a judge's jury instructions cannot succeed by picking out isolated sentences or "armchair" errors. The court will only overturn a conviction if the **overall effect of the instructions** misled the jury or caused a substantial miscarriage of justice.
- **Rule 3: The Overwhelming Evidence Proviso Rule** If the physical, objective evidence (such as clear CCTV footage of a crime) is so overwhelming that a jury could only reasonably conclude the defendant acted deliberately, minor procedural or instructional slips by the trial judge will not invalidate the conviction.
