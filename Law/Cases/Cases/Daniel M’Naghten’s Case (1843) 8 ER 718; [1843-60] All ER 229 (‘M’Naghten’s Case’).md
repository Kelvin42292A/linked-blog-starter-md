## Material Facts

In 1843, Daniel M’Naghten suffered from severe delusions of persecution. Under the influence of these delusions, he shot and killed Edward Drummond (the secretary to the Prime Minister), believing Drummond was part of a conspiracy against him.

## Issues

What is the legal standard to establish the defense of insanity when a person commits a crime while suffering from an insane delusion?

## Rule/Law

To establish a defense of insanity, it must be clearly proved that, at the time of committing the act, the accused was laboring under such a **defect of reason**, from a **disease of the mind**, as:

1. Not to know the **nature and quality** of the act they were doing; **OR**
2. If they did know it, they did not know that what they were doing was **wrong**.

## Application/Analysis

Apply this rule to the accused's mental state at the exact moment of the act. If a person has a partial delusion, their liability is evaluated **as if the facts of their delusion were completely real** (e.g., if they believe they are killing in self-defense, they are exempt; if they kill in revenge for a supposed injury to character, they are liable).

## Conclusion

M’Naghten was found **Not Guilty on the ground of Insanity**.

### Judges Rules

- **The Presumption:** Everyone is assumed to be sane and legally responsible until proven otherwise.
- **The Test:** To prove insanity, the defense must show that a **disease of the mind** caused a **defect of reason** so severe that the accused:
    1. Did not understand the **nature and quality** (the physical reality) of what they were doing, **OR**
    2. Did not know that what they were doing was legally or morally **wrong**.
