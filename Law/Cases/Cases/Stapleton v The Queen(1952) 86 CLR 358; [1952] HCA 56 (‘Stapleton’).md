## Material Facts

Stapleton, who had a strong family history of mental illness, shot and killed a police officer during a drunken, highly irrational episode. At his trial, he raised the defence of insanity, but the jury convicted him of murder.

## Issues

Did the trial judge make a mistake by telling the jury that the test for insanity was whether Stapleton knew his act was "against the law"?

## Rule/Law

Under Australian law, "wrong" does not mean legally wrong. Instead, it means **morally wrong according to the everyday standards of ordinary, reasonable people**. An accused person does not "know" their act is wrong if a mental disease makes them **incapable of reasoning about it with a moderate degree of sense and composure**.

## Application/Analysis

The trial judge wrongly directed the jury that insanity depended on whether Stapleton knew firing a gun was against the law. This mistake prevented the jury from properly considering whether Stapleton's mental illness made him completely incapable of reasoning calmly about the moral wrongness of his actions.

## Conclusion

The High Court allowed the appeal, set aside the conviction, and ordered a new trial.

### Judges Rule

In the insanity defence, "wrong" means morally wrong according to the everyday standards of ordinary people, rather than legally wrong. If a mental disease stops a person from reasoning calmly and rationally about the rightness of their act, they do not "know" it is wrong under the law, even if they are aware that the act is illegal.
