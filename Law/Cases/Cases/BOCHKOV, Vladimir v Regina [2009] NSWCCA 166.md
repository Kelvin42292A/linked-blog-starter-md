New South Wales Court of Criminal Appeal

## Material Facts

- The appellant (Vladimir Bochkov), the complainant, and her boyfriend (Robert) were in the same apartment, and the complainant fell asleep.
- The complainant woke up with someone’s arms around her and initially assumed it was her boyfriend, Robert.
- The appellant performed three acts of sexual intercourse on her (digital, penile, and cunnilingus).
- During the first two acts, she verbally said "no" and pushed him, still assuming it was Robert. It was not until the third act, when she tried to kick the person off, that she realized it was the appellant and not Robert.
- The appellant was convicted of three counts of sexual intercourse without consent.

## Issues

- Whether the Crown was allowed to present two alternative bases for lack of consent: (1) consent under a mistaken belief as to identity, and (2) absence of consent in fact.
- Whether the trial judge properly directed the jury on the concept of "recklessness," specifically regarding which scenario it legally applied to.

## Rule/Law

- Under **s 61I** of the _Crimes Act 1900_ (NSW), having sexual intercourse without a person's consent, knowing they do not consent, is an offence.
- Under **s 61R(2)(a)**, if a person consents under a **mistaken belief as to the identity** of the other person, they are legally taken _not_ to consent.
- Under **s 61R(2)(b)**, if the accused **knows** the other person is consenting under a mistaken belief as to identity, the accused is taken to know they do not consent.
- Under **s 61R(1)**, a person who is **reckless** as to whether the other person consents is legally taken to know that they do not consent.

## Application/Analysis

- The Crown's case relied on two alternative scenarios: either the complainant consented under the mistaken belief that the appellant was Robert, or she did not consent at all (evidenced by her saying "no" and pushing him).
- The trial judge explicitly directed the jury that the concept of **recklessness only applied to the second scenario** (where she verbally and physically resisted).
- The judge stressed that recklessness did **not** apply to the mistaken identity scenario. If the jury found she consented under a mistake, the appellant either _knew_ she was mistaken or he didn't; recklessness was not a midway option for mistaken identity.
- The Court held that the trial judge's directions were correct and that the jury was entitled to find the appellant reckless based on his failure to consider her consent or his awareness that she might not be consenting after she said "no".

## Conclusion

The appeal was dismissed. The convictions were upheld because the trial judge's directions regarding the two scenarios of consent, mistaken identity, and recklessness were adequate and accurate .
