NSW Court of Criminal Appeal

## Material Facts

- **The Incident:** Three men (Chishimba, Makasa, and Mulenga) and a 15-year-old girl went to Makasa's apartment to drink. The girl became heavily intoxicated and fell asleep in a bedroom.
- **The Assault:** She woke up in a dark room to find an unidentified man having vaginal intercourse with her, while the other men were present in the bedroom.
- **The Conviction:** The prosecution could not prove which specific man physically assaulted her. Instead, they charged all three men with aggravated sexual assault under a **joint criminal enterprise (JCE)**. All three were convicted by a jury and appealed.

## Issues

1. **Presence vs. Complicity:** Can "mere presence" in a dark room where an assault is taking place satisfy the requirement of "encouragement" to prove a joint criminal enterprise?
2. **Shared Mental State:** Can statutory rules regarding the perpetrator’s mental state (like recklessness as to consent) be legally attributed to other participants in a joint criminal enterprise?

## Rule/Law

- **Primary Liability (JCE):** Unlike secondary complicity (aiding and abetting), which is derivative, liability under a joint criminal enterprise is **primary**. If an agreement exists, every participant is equally responsible for the perpetrator's physical acts.
- **The Presence Limit:** "Mere presence" or passive silence at a crime scene does not constitute encouragement. To prove participation by encouragement, the accused must take **active steps** (words, gestures, or signs of approval) showing they are "linked in purpose" with the perpetrator.
- **Attributing Recklessness:** If a JCE is established, statutory provisions that deem recklessness as to consent to be equivalent to "knowledge" apply to all participants, not just the physical perpetrator.

## Application/Analysis

- **No Active Encouragement:** The majority of the Court of Appeal found that the appellants' presence rose no higher than being "on the floor" in a dark room. There was no evidence of active encouragement (such as gestures or comments) at the exact moment the assault began.
- **Alternative Explanations:** Because the men had been drinking heavily, their presence on the floor was equally consistent with sleeping, trying to sleep, or sitting quietly, meaning joint criminal enterprise was not the only rational explanation.
- **No Error in Mental State Directions:** However, the trial judge was correct to instruct the jury that if a JCE existed, recklessness as to the girl's consent could legally be attributed to all members.

## Conclusion

The Court of Appeal **allowed the appeals and quashed the convictions** on Count 1 because the evidence was legally insufficient to prove a joint criminal enterprise.

### Judges Rules

- **Primary, Not Derivative:** When you act in a joint criminal enterprise, your liability is **primary and direct**, meaning the physical acts of your partner are legally treated as your own.
- **Active Encouragement Required:** You cannot be found guilty of a joint enterprise simply because you stood by, sat in the room, or failed to prevent a crime. You must take active steps to intentionally assist or show approval of the crime.
- **The "Only Rational Explanation" Test:** In a circumstantial case, your presence at a crime scene cannot prove a joint enterprise if there is any other reasonable, non-criminal explanation for you being there (such as sleeping or being intoxicated).
- **Shared Intent Rules:** If a joint criminal enterprise is successfully proven, any legal rules that define the mental state of the physical perpetrator (like recklessness) apply equally to everyone in the group.
