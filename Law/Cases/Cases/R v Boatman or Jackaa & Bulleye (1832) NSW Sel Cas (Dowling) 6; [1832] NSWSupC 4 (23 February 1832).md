## Material Facts

- **The Parties and Charge:** Bonjon, a Wadora man, was charged with the shooting murder of Yammowing, a member of the Colijon people, at Geelong. Both parties were Aboriginal.
- **The Jurisdiction Argument:** Bonjon's defense counsel argued that because New South Wales was occupied rather than conquered or ceded, the British Crown acquired a right to the soil but had no legal authority over the internal affairs of the Indigenous population.
- **Lack of Consent:** The defense emphasized that there was no treaty, statute, or compact showing that the Aboriginal people had ever consented to or submitted to British authority.

## Issues

1. **Precedent:** Is a single judge of the Supreme Court of New South Wales at Port Phillip bound by the earlier Full Court decision in _R v Murrell_ (1836)?
2. **Jurisdiction _Inter Se_:** Does the colonial court have criminal jurisdiction to hear and try a charge of murder committed by one Aboriginal person against another?

## Rule/Law

- **Precedent Flexibility:** A single judge of the Supreme Court of New South Wales at Port Phillip is not legally bound by the decision in _R v Murrell_.
- **Distinct and Dependent Status:** Indigenous inhabitants of a territory that was neither unoccupied nor acquired by conquest or treaty are to be regarded as **distinct though dependent allies, not British subjects**.
- **Requirement of Consent:** For the Crown to exercise absolute criminal jurisdiction over the internal disputes of an Indigenous population, there must be an **express legislative enactment or a treaty** proving their submission to British law.

## Application/Analysis

- **No Conquest or Treaty:** New South Wales was not acquired by conquest, and it was not empty of people when British settlement began.
- **Separate Sovereignty and Usages:** Because the Aboriginal people live in self-governing communities with their own established regulations, laws, and usages, they are entitled to exercise their own customary laws among themselves.
- **Limit of English Law:** While English law protects and applies to conflicts _between_ British settlers and Aboriginal people, it cannot be legally stretched to govern private conflicts confined entirely to the Indigenous population.

## Conclusion

The court held that the Supreme Court of New South Wales **has no jurisdiction** over crimes committed by Aboriginal people against one another. Justice Willis reserved his final decision to further consider the matter, strongly doubting his legal right to try the prisoner.

### Judges Rules

In a colony acquired without conquest or treaty, the colonial courts lack criminal jurisdiction over offenses committed solely between indigenous inhabitants. Because they have not consented to British sovereignty, owe no natural allegiance, and exist as distinct though dependent allies, they are entitled to govern their internal affairs under their own traditional laws and usages unless explicitly subjected to English law by a treaty or an express Act of Parliament.
