Supreme Court of Victoria

## Material Facts

- **The Incident:** Jenny Besim was on trial for involuntary manslaughter by an unlawful and dangerous act after throwing a vase at her husband, striking him in the head and causing his death.
- **The Context:** Immediately before the incident, Besim had been punched by her husband, was trying to get her children out of the house, and was threatened with being burned alive inside the home.
- **The Defense Argument:** The defense argued that the "reasonable person" in her position should be given the same overwhelming emotions of fear and panic that she felt. They claimed a reasonable person in that terrified state would be too overwhelmed to realize throwing the vase carried an appreciable risk of serious injury.
- **The Prosecution Objection:** The prosecution objected, arguing that the objective test only looks at the physical act itself and does not consider the accused's emotional state.

## Issues

In deciding whether an act is "dangerous" for manslaughter, should the hypothetical "reasonable person" be given the emotions, passions, or temporary mental state of the accused?

## Rule/Law

- **Strictly Objective Test:** The test for a "dangerous act" is strictly objective.
- **Physical Position Only:** The phrase "a reasonable person in the accused's position" refers only to the **physical circumstances** of the situation and any **actual knowledge** the accused possessed.
- **Exclusion of Emotions:** The "reasonable person" cannot be given the temporary emotions, panic, or mental state of the accused. The law assumes the reasonable person always judges with the **unclouded reasoning power** of a healthy mind.

## Application/Analysis

- **Irrelevant to Dangerousness:** While the severe physical and verbal abuse Besim suffered was highly relevant to provocation or sentencing, it could not alter the objective danger of throwing the vase.
- **Preventing Subjective Slippage:** Allowing the jury to factor in Besim's terrified state of mind would turn a strictly objective test into a subjective one, defeating the purpose of the law.
- **The Unclouded Mind Standard:** Deciding whether throwing a vase at someone’s head is dangerous must be judged by a calm, sensible mind, not a mind clouded or overwhelmed by fear.

## Conclusion

The court ruled in favor of the prosecution. Redlich J directed the jury that they must completely **ignore the accused's emotions** and whether she was "overwhelmed" by panic when deciding if her act was objectively dangerous.

### Judges Rules (Unlawful and dangerous act doctrine)

- **Emotions Are Excluded:** The hypothetical "reasonable person" used to judge danger cannot be given the accused's temporary emotions, fear, panic, or anger—even if those emotions were completely natural under the circumstances.
- **Limited to Physical Situation:** The phrase "in the accused's position" only covers the physical surroundings, the physical act, and any actual facts the accused knew.
- **Judgment Must Be Unclouded:** The test assumes the reasonable person always maintains the clear, healthy, and unclouded reasoning power to recognize danger, regardless of how terrified or overwhelmed the actual accused was.
