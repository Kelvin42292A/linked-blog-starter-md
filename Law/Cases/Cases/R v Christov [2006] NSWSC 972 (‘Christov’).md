## Material Facts

Christov strangled his ex-girlfriend with a shoelace and a dog lead after lying in wait in her home. He argued his charge should be reduced to manslaughter due to a substantial impairment by an "abnormality of mind" caused by a mix of long-term drug abuse, morbid jealousy, and a panic-induced mental blackout when she defended herself with a knife.

## Issues

Was Christov’s mental capacity substantially impaired by an abnormality of mind at the time of the killing?

## Rule/Law

Under s 23A of the _Crimes Act 1900_ (NSW), an accused is not guilty of murder if their capacity to understand events, judge right from wrong, or control themselves was substantially impaired by an abnormality of mind arising from an underlying (pre-existing and non-transitory) condition. The accused bears the burden of proving this.

## Application/Analysis

The judge rejected Christov's claims:

- His jealousy did not reach the level of a delusional mental illness .
- His history of drug use did not cause active psychosis during the crime, and self-induced drug effects must be legally ignored.
- He did not suffer a dissociative panic; he calmly planned the attack and lay in wait.

## Conclusion

Christov failed to prove a substantial impairment by an abnormality of mind . He was found **guilty of murder**.

### Judges Rules

To successfully use the defence of substantial impairment (reducing murder to manslaughter):

1. **The Cause:** The mental abnormality must come from an "underlying condition"—meaning a pre-existing mental or physical condition that is not temporary.
2. **The Effect:** It must substantially impair the accused's ability to do at least one of three things: understand what is happening, judge if their actions are right or wrong, or control their own physical actions.
3. **The Exclusions:** The court must completely ignore any temporary mental effects caused by self-induced drugs or alcohol.
