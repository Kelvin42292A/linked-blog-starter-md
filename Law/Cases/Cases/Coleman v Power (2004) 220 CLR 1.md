High Court of Australia

## Material Facts

- The appellant (Coleman) was distributing pamphlets in a Townsville mall that alleged local police officers, including Constable Brendan Power, were corrupt,.
- When Constable Power approached and asked for a pamphlet, the appellant refused, pushed the officer, and loudly said: "This is Constable Brendan Power, a corrupt police officer".
- The officer arrested the appellant for using "insulting language" under s 7(1)(d) of the _Vagrants, Gaming and Other Offences Act 1931_ (Qld),.
- The appellant violently resisted the arrest, struggling with police, kicking an officer, and attempting to bite Constable Power.
- He was convicted in the Magistrates Court of using insulting words, distributing printed matter containing insulting words, and multiple counts of assaulting and obstructing police officers.
- The Queensland Court of Appeal later set aside the printed matter conviction but upheld the s 7(1)(d) "insulting words" conviction and the assault convictions,.
- The appellant then appealed to the High Court of Australia.

## Issues

1. **Statutory Construction:** Does the legal definition of "insulting words" under s 7(1)(d) apply broadly to any words that hurt personal feelings, or is it strictly limited to words intended or reasonably likely to provoke unlawful physical retaliation,?
2. **Constitutional Validity (The _Lange_ Test):** Does s 7(1)(d) impermissibly burden the implied constitutional freedom of communication about government or political matters?
3. **Lawfulness of Arrest:** If the conviction for insulting words is set aside, does that automatically make the initial police arrest unlawful, thereby legally excusing the appellant's violent resistance and assault?

## Rule/Law

- **Constitutional Conformity:** When a statute is ambiguous, courts should prefer a construction that avoids infringing the Constitution or basic common law rights,.
- **The _Lange_ Test:** A law is constitutionally invalid if it effectively burdens freedom of communication about government or political matters, unless the law is "reasonably appropriate and adapted" to serve a legitimate end in a manner compatible with the maintenance of representative and responsible government.
- **Police Arrest Powers:** Under the _Police Powers and Responsibilities Act 1997_ (Qld), an arrest is lawful if a police officer "reasonably suspects" an offence has been committed. A police officer is protected under this law even if a court later finds the specific elements of the offence were not met, provided the underlying law itself is constitutionally valid.

## Application/Analysis

- **Narrowing the Law ("Fighting Words"):** To save s 7(1)(d) from being constitutionally invalid under the _Lange_ test, the High Court majority ruled it must be construed very narrowly. The phrase "insulting words" does not mean any rude or offensive political statement; it only prohibits words intended or reasonably likely to provoke unlawful physical retaliation.
- **No Provocation:** The majority found that because Constable Power was a trained police officer, he was expected to resist the sting of insults and was not reasonably likely to unlawfully physically retaliate. Therefore, the appellant's words did not legally meet the high threshold of "insulting words," and his conviction for that specific charge had to be quashed.
- **The Arrest Was Still Lawful:** Even though the appellant was cleared of the insulting words charge, the High Court declared that the statute itself (s 7(1)(d)) was still a valid law, just narrower in scope. Because the underlying law was valid, the police officers acted lawfully because they "reasonably suspected" an offence at the time of the arrest. Consequently, the appellant had no legal right to violently resist, and his convictions for assaulting and obstructing police were entirely valid.

## Conclusion

The appeal was **allowed in part** by a 4:3 majority. The specific conviction for using insulting words under s 7(1)(d) was **quashed** (set aside). However, the appeals against the convictions for assaulting and obstructing police officers were **dismissed** (upheld).
