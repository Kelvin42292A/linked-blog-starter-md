## Material Facts

Majewski went on a violent rampage at a pub, assaulting the landlord, customers, and several police officers during his arrest and while in a holding cell. He claimed to have no memory of the events because he had voluntarily taken a massive amount of non-prescribed drugs and alcohol.

## Issues

Can a defendant use self-induced (voluntary) intoxication as a defence to escape liability for crimes of "basic intent," such as assault?

## Rule/Law

No. **Self-induced intoxication is not a defence to crimes of basic intent** (like assault, manslaughter, or unlawful wounding). It is only relevant to crimes of **specific intent** (like murder or theft) to see if the defendant actually formed that specific, purposeful intent. The voluntary act of getting drunk or high supplies the necessary element of recklessness, which legally satisfies the required _mens rea_ (guilty mind) for basic intent offences.

## Application/Analysis

The charges against Majewski—assault occasioning actual bodily harm and assaulting a police officer—are crimes of basic intent. Therefore, his voluntary blackout state was legally irrelevant to his guilt, and the trial judge was right to tell the jury to ignore his drug and alcohol use.

## Conclusion

The House of Lords unanimously dismissed the appeal and upheld his convictions.

### Judges Rules

- **Rule 1: The Basic vs. Specific Intent Divide**  
    Voluntary intoxication can **never** be used to defend against crimes of **basic intent** (where the mental element matches the physical act, like assault or manslaughter). It can only be considered for crimes of **specific intent** (which require a further, purposeful intent beyond the physical act, like murder or burglary).
- **Rule 2: Recklessness is Transferred Fault**  
    A person who voluntarily chooses to drink or take drugs to the point of losing self-control is acting **criminally reckless**. This initial choice to get intoxicated supplies the general _mens rea_ required for any basic intent crime they commit while blacked out.
