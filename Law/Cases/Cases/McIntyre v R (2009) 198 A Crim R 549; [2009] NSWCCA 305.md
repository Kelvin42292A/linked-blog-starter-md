New South Wales Court of Criminal Appeal

## Material Facts

- The applicant attacked his elderly, physically disabled mother in her home, choking and punching her, and threatening her with a knife.
- At the police station, he punched a police officer in the face, causing bruising and swelling.
- He pleaded guilty to assault occasioning actual bodily harm (**s. 59** for his mother) and assaulting a police officer in the execution of duty (**s. 58** for the officer).
- When sentencing him for the **s. 58** offence (the police assault), the judge explicitly noted that the offence "resulted in some injury".

## Issues

- Did the sentencing judge breach the **_De Simoni_ principle** by taking the police officer's _injury_ into account when assessing the seriousness of a standard **s. 58** assault charge?
- Did the judge err in refusing to consider the availability of "summary disposal" (having the matter dealt with in the lower Local Court) as a mitigating factor?

## Rule/Law

- **s. 58 _Crimes Act 1900_:** Assaulting a police officer in the execution of duty (Max penalty: 5 years).
- **s. 60(2) _Crimes Act 1900_:** A _more serious_ offence of assaulting a police officer _occasioning actual bodily harm_ (Max penalty: 7 years).
- **_The Queen v De Simoni (1981)_ (The _De Simoni_ Principle):** A sentencing judge cannot take into account circumstances of aggravation that would warrant a conviction for a _more serious_ offence than the one the offender was actually charged with.
- **s. 6(3) _Criminal Appeal Act 1912_:** An appeal court can dismiss a sentence appeal even if the trial judge made an error, provided "no lesser sentence is warranted in law" .

## Application/Analysis

- **The _De Simoni_ Error:** The trial judge made a clear legal error. Because the applicant was only charged under **s. 58** (basic assault), the judge was legally forbidden from using the officer's bruising/injury to make the offence seem worse. If the Crown wanted to punish the applicant for the injury, they should have charged him with the more serious **s. 60(2)** offence. Therefore, a _De Simoni_ breach occurred.
- **Summary Disposal Error:** The judge also erred in her reasoning regarding the Local Court summary disposal, but the appeals court noted this ultimately did not matter because the applicant's severe criminal history meant the case rightly belonged in the District Court anyway.
- **Applying s. 6(3):** Despite the judge's errors, the appeals court found the sentence was not "manifestly excessive". The applicant's unprovoked violence against a vulnerable victim, his attack on police, and his long criminal history meant the punishment fit the crime .

## Conclusion

Leave to appeal was granted, but the appeal against the sentence was **dismissed**. Although the trial judge breached the **_De Simoni_ principle**, the appeals court utilized **s. 6(3)** to uphold the original sentence because no lesser sentence was warranted in law .
