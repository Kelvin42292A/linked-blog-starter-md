High Court of Australia

## Material Facts

- **The Provocation:** Frank Parker's wife confessed she was in love with Daniel Kelly, had planned to run away with him, and refused Parker's pleas to stay for their six children. Kelly also taunted Parker about his wife and mocked his physical inability to stop him.
- **The Killing:** After they left, Parker chased them in his car, ran them over (breaking Kelly's legs), beat Kelly with a knuckle-duster, and stabbed him to death in the throat with a sheath knife.
- **The Trial:** The trial judge refused to let the jury consider the defense of provocation, and Parker was convicted of murder. He applied for special leave to appeal.

## Issues

1. **Scope:** Does the NSW statutory defense of provocation (s 23) apply generally to all provocation, or is it limited only to words and gestures?
2. **Jury Issue:** Should the trial judge have left the question of provocation to the jury on these facts?

## Rule/Law

- **General Scope:** The statutory defense of provocation is not limited only to language or gestures; it applies to all provocation generally (including physical blows).
- **The Rationale:** The defense is a legal concession to **"human frailty"**, recognizing that sudden, severe provocation can cause a sane person to temporarily lose self-control and act in the **"heat of passion"**.
- **The NSW Onus:** The accused bears the onus of proving provocation. They must establish that the act causing death was done suddenly in the heat of passion **"without intent to take life"** (interpreted by the court to mean without a _premeditated_ or pre-planned intent to kill).

## Application/Analysis

- **The Majority View:** The majority of the High Court held that provocation was properly withheld from the jury. Parker’s actions (such as attempting to sharpen a metal rod beforehand, chasing the couple, and brutally attacking Kelly with multiple weapons) showed a clear, premeditated intent to kill rather than a sudden, un-premeditated loss of self-control.
- **The Minority View (Dixon CJ & Windeyer J):** The minority argued that the emotional pressure on Parker was cumulative, mounting, and still actively boiling when he caught up to them. They believed the jury should have been allowed to decide whether Parker acted under a sudden, un-premeditated loss of self-control.

## Conclusion

By majority, the High Court refused special leave to appeal, upholding Parker's murder conviction.

### Judges Rules

- **A Mercy for Human Weakness:** The rationale of the defense is to recognize **"human frailty"**. It reduces murder to manslaughter because a provoked person is morally less to blame than someone who kills in cold blood.
- **Applies to All Provoking Acts:** The legal scope of the defense is wide and covers all types of provoking conduct—including physical violence, gestures, and insulting words.
- **No Premeditated Revenge:** The defense is strictly limited to sudden acts done in the **"heat of passion"**. If a person has time to cool down, plans the attack, or acts out of pre-planned vengeance, the defense is completely unavailable.
- **Loss of Self-Control, Not Insanity:** Provocation only applies to sane individuals who are temporarily deprived of their self-control; it is not a defense for mental illness or a complete lack of physical intent.
