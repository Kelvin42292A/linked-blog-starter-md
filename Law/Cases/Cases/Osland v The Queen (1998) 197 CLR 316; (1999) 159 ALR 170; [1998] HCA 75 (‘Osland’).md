High court of Australia

## Material Facts

- **The Plan:** Marjorie Heather Osland and her son, David Albion, planned to kill her highly abusive husband (and his step-father), Frank Osland.
- **The Killing:** In furtherance of their plan, they dug a grave during the day. That evening, Osland mixed sedatives into her husband’s dinner to drug him. While he slept, her son struck the fatal blows with an iron pipe in her presence. They then buried his body in the pre-dug grave.
- **The Joint Trial:** Both Osland and her son were tried jointly for murder, and both raised defenses of self-defense and provocation.
- **The Discrepant Verdicts:** The jury convicted Osland of murder but was unable to reach a verdict regarding her son. (The son was subsequently retried and acquitted of all charges in a separate trial).
- **The Appeal:** Osland appealed her conviction, arguing that because she did not strike the fatal blow herself, her liability was purely "derivative". She claimed her conviction was legally inconsistent with the jury's failure to convict her son (the physical perpetrator) on the same evidence.

## Issues

1. **Nature of Complicity:** Was Osland’s criminal liability as a joint participant present at the scene purely derivative (dependent on her son being convicted), or was it primary?
2. **Inconsistent Verdicts:** Is the conviction of a co-accused acting in concert legally inconsistent with the jury's failure to reach a verdict or subsequent acquittal of the actual physical perpetrator?

## Rule/Law

- **Derivative Liability (Accessories):** An accessory before the fact or principal in the second degree (who merely aids or encourages) has derivative liability. Their guilt is historically dependent on proving that a principal offender committed the crime.
- **Primary Liability (Acting in Concert):** If two or more people reach an understanding to commit a crime, are present together at the scene, and execute the crime, they are acting in concert. In this category, their liability is **primary and direct, not derivative**.
- **Attribution of the Act:** When acting in concert, the physical acts (_actus reus_) of the actual perpetrator are legally attributed to the co-accused as if they performed them themselves.
- **Separation of Intent and Excuse:** While the physical _act_ of killing is attributed to both parties, their _criminal guilt_ is judged independently based on their individual state of mind (_mens rea_) and personal defenses (such as self-defense, provocation, or insanity).

## Application/Analysis

- **Osland Was a Joint Principal:** Because Osland was present at the scene acting in concert pursuant to a joint plan, her liability was primary, not derivative. Her son's act of striking the fatal blow was legally treated as her own act.
- **Different Evidentiary Portions:** The jury was required to evaluate the evidence against each accused separately. The prosecution introduced devastating evidence admissible _only_ against Osland—such as intercepted phone recordings proving meticulous planning, a financial motive, and attempts to threaten witnesses.
- **Defenses Independent:** While the jury had a reasonable doubt about whether the son acted in self-defense or under provocation (due to his personal history and a physical fight with the deceased on the night of the killing), they were satisfied beyond reasonable doubt that those defenses were disproven for Osland.
- **No Inconsistency:** Because her liability was primary and her defenses were evaluated on separate evidence, her conviction was legally consistent with her son’s lack of conviction.

## Conclusion

The High Court (by a 3:2 majority) **dismissed the appeal** and upheld Osland's conviction, ruling that her primary liability under the doctrine of concert was not dependent on her son's conviction.

### Judges Rules (Derivative liability)

- **Helper vs. Partner:** If you merely help or encourage a crime without acting in concert at the scene, your liability is **derivative** (dependent on proving the principal's guilt). If you plan a crime and are present at its execution, your liability is **primary**.
- **Your Partner's Hands Are Your Hands:** In a joint criminal enterprise (acting in concert), the physical acts of the perpetrator are legally attributed to you as if you did them yourself.
- **Excuses Do Not Transfer:** While the physical _act_ is shared, the _excuse_ is not. If the physical perpetrator is acquitted because of a personal defense (like self-defense, provocation, or insanity), you can still be convicted of murder if you had a guilty mind and no personal defense of your own.
- **Verdicts Can Differ:** In a joint trial of co-accused acting in concert, different verdicts (convicting one and acquitting/disagreeing on the other) are perfectly consistent if the admissible evidence or personal justifications differ between them.

### Judges Rules (Joint Criminal enterprise and the common purpose doctrine)

- **Unspoken (Tacit) Agreement is Enough:** The common agreement or understanding does not need to be formal or express. It can be a completely **unspoken (tacit) understanding inferred entirely from the circumstances** or your simultaneous actions at the scene.
- **Foresight of Additional Crimes (Common Purpose):** You are criminally liable for an additional, unplanned crime committed by your partner if you **subjectively foresaw or contemplated that crime as a possible incident** of carrying out the enterprise, yet chose to continue participating anyway.
- **Active Support Equals Primary Contribution:** You do not need to strike the fatal blow to be a primary offender. Actively planning the crime, preparing the scene, rendering the victim helpless (like administering drugs), or holding the victim down makes your **physical contribution substantial enough to be a primary partner** rather than a mere secondary accessory.
