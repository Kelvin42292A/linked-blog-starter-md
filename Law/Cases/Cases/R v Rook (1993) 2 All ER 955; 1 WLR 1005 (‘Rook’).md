Court of Appeal Criminal Divison

## Material Facts

- **The Plot:** Adrian Rook (the appellant), Armstrong, and Leivers agreed with a husband, Mohammed Afsar, to kill Afsar's wife, Shaheen, in exchange for a promised payment of £20,000.
- **The Plan:** The four men met the evening before the murder to discuss the arrangements. They agreed that Afsar would drive Armstrong, Leivers, and Rook to Colwick Park by the lake and bring his wife there once they were ready. They also decided to use a knife and a piece of wood for the killing.
- **The Absence:** On the scheduled day of the murder, Rook could not be found and deliberately failed to turn up.
- **The Killing:** Despite Rook's absence, Afsar drove Armstrong and Leivers to the lake, where they dragged his wife from the car and brutally murdered her.
- **The Defense:** Rook admitted to playing a leading role in planning the murder but claimed he never actually intended for the victim to die. He argued he only "tagged along" in the hope of getting upfront cash before disappearing, and assumed his physical absence on the day would prevent the others from going through with the crime.
- **The Appeal:** Rook never communicated his change of heart to the other men. He was convicted of murder and appealed, arguing the trial judge gave incorrect jury directions on both _mens rea_ and the legal defense of withdrawal.

## Issues

1. **Secondary Party _Mens Rea_:** To convict an absent secondary party of murder who lent prior assistance or encouragement, must the prosecution prove they intended the victim to be killed, or is subjective foresight of a real risk sufficient?
2. **Effective Withdrawal:** Can a secondary participant effectively withdraw from a crime simply by absenting themselves from the scene of the crime, or is unequivocal communication of withdrawal required?

## Rule/Law

- **Foresight of Risk Suffices:** For a secondary party who lends assistance or encouragement before a crime, the prosecution does not need to show they intended the victim to be killed or suffer serious injury. It is sufficient that they contemplated or foresaw the killing as a **real or substantial risk** (defined as a risk that cannot be dismissed as negligible).
- **Communication is the Minimum:** To effectively withdraw from a criminal enterprise and escape secondary liability, a person cannot merely change their mind, repent, or absent themselves.
- **Unequivocal Notice:** The withdrawing party **must at least unequivocally communicate their withdrawal** to the other participants.
- **Timeliness:** Where practicable and reasonable, they must give **timely communication** that serves clear notice to the other parties that if they proceed, they do so entirely without the withdrawing party's aid and assistance.

## Application/Analysis

- **Foresight Proven:** Rook played a major role in planning the murder and recruited Leivers. Because he intentionally assisted the plan while subjectively foreseeing a real risk that the murder would occur, his lack of direct intent for her to die did not excuse him.
- **Failure to Communicate:** Although Rook decided within himself that he did not want to be present, he kept this decision entirely to himself. His mere failure to show up did not serve as unequivocal notice to Armstrong and Leivers that he was withdrawing his assistance.
- **Proviso Applied:** While the trial judge’s written directions on withdrawal were confusing and legally incorrect, the Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal using the appellate proviso. Because Rook had completely failed to communicate his withdrawal, a properly directed jury would have inevitably convicted him anyway.

## Conclusion

The Court of Appeal **dismissed the appeal and upheld Rook's conviction** for murder.

### Judges Rules

- **Mental Change is Not Enough:** Merely changing your mind, repenting, or feeling horror about a planned crime is legally useless if you do not take active steps to withdraw.
- **Absenteeism is Not Withdrawal:** Simply staying away from the scene on the day of the crime or failing to show up does **not** count as an effective withdrawal.
- **Must Unequivocally Communicate:** To escape secondary liability, you **must at least unequivocally communicate your withdrawal** to the other partners in the joint enterprise.
- **Timely and Clear Notice:** Where practicable and reasonable, your communication must be **timely and serve unequivocal notice** to the other parties that if they proceed with the crime, they do so entirely without your aid, assistance, or approval.
- **The "Stepping on the Fuse" Principle:** While it remains an open question whether you must completely "neutralize" prior aid (such as recovering a supplied weapon or notifying the police), you must—at the very least—do your best to stop the momentum of the crime you helped set in motion.
