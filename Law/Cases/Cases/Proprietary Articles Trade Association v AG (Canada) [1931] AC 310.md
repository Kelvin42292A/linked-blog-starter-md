Supreme Court of Canada

## Material Facts

- The Dominion Parliament of Canada enacted the _Combines Investigation Act_ (1927) and section 498 of the _Criminal Code_, making it an indictable offence (punishable by fine or imprisonment) to form or operate a "combine" (a monopoly, trust, or agreement) that operates to the detriment of the public and restrains or injures trade or commerce.
- The Act established machinery for officials to investigate the existence of combines, with powers to examine books and summon witnesses. It also included remedies (ss. 29 and 30) allowing the government to reduce customs duties or revoke patents if they were being used to facilitate a combine.
- The Proprietary Articles Trade Association was investigated and found by a Commission to be a party to a combine.
- The Governor in Council referred questions to the Supreme Court of Canada to determine if the _Combines Investigation Act_ and section 498 of the _Criminal Code_ were _ultra vires_ (beyond the constitutional power of) the Parliament of Canada. The Supreme Court ruled the legislation was valid (_intra vires_), and the Association, along with the Attorneys-General for Ontario and Quebec, appealed to the Privy Council.

## Issues

- Is the _Combines Investigation Act_ and section 498 of the _Criminal Code_ _ultra vires_ the Dominion Parliament?
- Specifically, does the legislation improperly encroach upon the exclusive Provincial powers over "property and civil rights" or "the administration of justice," or is it validly enacted under the Dominion's power over "the criminal law" or other specified powers?

## Rule/Law

- **s. 91 & s. 92 _British North America Act, 1867_:** Section 91 grants specific legislative powers to the Dominion Parliament (e.g., head 27: criminal law; head 3: taxation; head 22: patents), while section 92 grants exclusive powers to the Provincial legislatures (e.g., head 13: property and civil rights).
- **Pith and Substance Doctrine:** If a piece of legislation, in its "pith and substance," falls within one of the enumerated powers of the Dominion under s. 91, it is constitutionally valid even if it incidentally affects property and civil rights within the Provinces.
- **The Definition of "Criminal Law" (s. 91(27)):** Criminal law means criminal law "in its widest sense" and is not confined to what was considered criminal in 1867. The defining feature of a crime is simply an act or omission that is prohibited by the State with penal consequences attached.
- **_In re Board of Commerce Act, 1919_ (Distinguished):** An earlier Privy Council decision which struck down previous combine legislation because it was merely a "colourable" attempt to regulate everyday prices, profits, and property/civil rights under the guise of criminal law.

## Application/Analysis

- **Valid Use of Criminal Law Power:** The Privy Council held that the substance of the current legislation is to define and criminalize combinations that genuinely operate to the public's detriment. Parliament has the authority to determine that certain commercial activities are harmful and to suppress them via penal consequences. Therefore, the core of the Act and s. 498 falls squarely within the Dominion's s. 91(27) "criminal law" power.
- **Distinguishing the 1919 Act:** Unlike the invalid 1919 legislation—which aggressively empowered a board to arbitrarily regulate prices, profits, and ordinary business operations—the current Act relies on a general definition of a crime and standard judicial trials. The investigative machinery established by the Act is a valid, ancillary mechanism to support this _intra vires_ objective.
- **Customs Duties and Patents:** While sections 29 and 30 (reducing tariffs and revoking patents) do not relate to criminal law, they are completely valid under the Dominion's specific powers over taxation (s. 91(3)) and patents (s. 91(22)). If Parliament grants protective tariffs or patent monopolies, it has the inherent power to withdraw that protection if it is abused to form harmful combines.
- **Property and Civil Rights:** Because the legislation is validly anchored in the Dominion's enumerated powers (criminal law, taxation, patents), its interference with Provincial property and civil rights is constitutionally permissible.

## Conclusion

The appeal was **dismissed**. The Privy Council affirmed the Supreme Court of Canada's ruling that the _Combines Investigation Act_ and s. 498 of the _Criminal Code_ are **intra vires** (constitutionally valid).
