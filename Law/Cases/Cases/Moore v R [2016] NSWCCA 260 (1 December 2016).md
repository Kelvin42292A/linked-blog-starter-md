Court of Criminal Appeal for New South Wales

## Material Facts

- The appellant (Moore) opened a "Complete Freedom" bank account with St George Bank, which had no agreed overdraft facility.
- Despite having no funds, Moore made a series of electronic withdrawals and transfers (e.g., to pay his mortgage and transfer to PayPal) that drove his account into a massive negative balance.
- Over two years, Moore used the money to purchase luxury cars (an Aston Martin, a Maserati), a boat, and music memorabilia.
- There was nothing covert about this; the bank processed every transaction, sent Moore regular statements showing his massive debt, and charged him a $9 "payment honour fee" plus 20% interest for every overdrawn transaction.
- By the time the account was closed, the negative balance exceeded $2.1 million.
- Moore was convicted of dishonestly obtaining a financial advantage by deception and dealing with proceeds of crime.

## Issues

- Did Moore obtain the financial advantage "by deception," given that he made no false representations and the bank knew the true state of the account?
- Under the expanded statutory definition of deception, was Moore "not authorised" to cause the bank's computer systems to process his transactions?

## Rule/Law

- **s 192E(1)(b) _Crimes Act 1900_ (NSW):** The offence of dishonestly obtaining a financial advantage by deception.
- **s 192B(1)(b) _Crimes Act 1900_ (NSW):** Defines "deception" to include conduct that causes a computer or electronic device to make a response that the person is **not authorised** to cause it to make.
- **_Croton v The Queen_ (Applied):** Confirms that a bank balance is a _chose in action_ (a contractual debt), not physical money that can be stolen via common law larceny.
- **_R v Evenett_ (Distinguished):** In _Evenett_, withdrawing cash from an offline ATM that exceeded the account balance was an unauthorized deception because the bank's contract strictly forbade it.

## Application/Analysis

- **No Actual Deception:** Moore never lied to the bank or made any false representations. He simply requested funds, and the bank—fully aware of his negative balance—chose to lend him the money.
- **The Contractual Authority:** The bank's own "Terms and Conditions" explicitly stated that if Moore overdrew his account, the bank had the discretion to allow it, subject to charging fees and interest.
- **The Computer's Response:** Because the bank's systems were configured to allow the transactions (due to a human error in assigning the account "relationship officer status"), every time Moore requested a transfer, the bank's computer "allowed" it and charged him for it.
- Because the bank contractually allowed the loans and acceded to his requests, Moore _was_ authorised to make them. Without an "unauthorised" computer response, the legal element of "deception" completely failed.
- **Civil vs. Criminal Liability:** The Court noted that Moore behaved dishonestly and foolishly, and he undoubtedly owed the bank a massive civil debt. However, obtaining a loan that a bank willingly (if mistakenly) approves under its own terms does not constitute the crime of fraud.

## Conclusion

The appeal was **allowed**. The Court found that the essential element of deception was entirely absent. The convictions were **quashed**, and verdicts of **acquittal** were entered for both charges.
