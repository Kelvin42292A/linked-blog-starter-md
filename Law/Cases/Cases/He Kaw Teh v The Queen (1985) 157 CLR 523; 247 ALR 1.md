High Court of Australia

## Material Facts

- The applicant (He Kaw Teh) arrived at Melbourne airport on a flight from Kuala Lumpur.
- Customs officials searched his baggage and found 2.788 kilograms of heroin hidden inside a false bottom of his bag.
- He was charged with two offences under the _Customs Act 1901_ (Cth): importing prohibited imports (section 233B(1)(b)), and possessing prohibited imports without reasonable excuse (section 233B(1)(c)).
- The trial judge told the jury that the prosecution did **not** have to prove any specific state of mind or knowledge by the accused for either charge.
- The judge also instructed that if the accused wanted to use the defense of an "honest and reasonable mistake" or "reasonable excuse," the burden of proof was entirely on the accused to prove his innocence on the balance of probabilities.
- The accused was convicted and sentenced to life imprisonment. He appealed, arguing the judge's instructions on "knowledge" and the burden of proof were wrong.

## Issues

1. **Importing (s 233B(1)(b)):** Does the offence of importing narcotics require the prosecution to prove _mens rea_ (that the accused actually knew he was importing narcotics), or is it an absolute liability offence?
2. **Possession (s 233B(1)(c)):** Does the legal concept of "possession" inherently require the prosecution to prove that the accused knew the goods were physically in his custody?
3. **The Burden of Proof:** If an accused raises the defense of an honest and reasonable mistake of fact, does the accused have to prove it, or does the prosecution have to disprove it?

## Rule/Law

- **The Presumption of Mens Rea (_Sherras v De Rutzen_):** There is a strong common law presumption that _mens rea_ (an evil intention or guilty knowledge) is an essential ingredient in every criminal offence. This presumption can only be displaced by the clear words of the statute or its subject matter.
- **The Meaning of Possession (_DPP v Brooks_ & _Williams v The Queen_):** In criminal law, "possession" is not just physical custody. It necessarily imports a mental element. You do not legally "possess" a thing unless you _know_ that you have it in your physical control.
- **The Ultimate Burden (_Woolmington v DPP_):** Following modern criminal principles, the ultimate burden of proof is always on the prosecution. If an accused raises an honest and reasonable mistake of fact, the accused does not have to _prove_ it; they just have to raise enough evidence to create a reasonable doubt. The prosecution must then disprove that mistake beyond a reasonable doubt.

## Application/Analysis

- **Grave Offences Require Guilt:** The High Court noted that importing commercial quantities of heroin is a massive crime carrying a maximum penalty of life imprisonment. Parliament could not possibly have intended to lock up completely innocent people (like someone who had drugs secretly planted in their bags) just because they physically carried a bag. Therefore, the presumption of _mens rea_ stands. The prosecution _must_ prove the accused knew they were importing narcotics.
- **Possession Requires Knowledge:** Because the word "possession" automatically implies knowing you have something, the prosecution cannot just prove the drugs were in the bag. They must prove the accused actually _knew_ the drugs were inside.
- **The Trial Judge Was Wrong:** The trial judge made a massive legal error by telling the jury the prosecution didn't have to prove knowledge. The judge made a second massive error by forcing the accused to carry the burden of proving his own innocent mistake.

## Conclusion

The appeal by He Kaw Teh was **allowed**. The High Court set aside the judgments of the lower courts because the jury was given the wrong legal instructions. The case was sent back to the Victorian Court of Criminal Appeal to determine if the legal mistakes caused a substantial miscarriage of justice requiring a new trial.
