New South Wales Criminal Court of Appeal

## Material Facts

- Simon Charles Pullman was driving a car and **crossed an unbroken centre line** on a country road to overtake traffic while approaching a blind left turn.
- An oncoming vehicle was forced to swerve off the road to avoid him, lost control, and crashed into a motorcycle, **killing the rider** and seriously injuring others.
- There was **no direct impact** between Pullman’s car and any other vehicle or person.
- Because there was no physical contact, the statutory charge of culpable driving was unavailable. The Crown instead prosecuted and convicted Pullman of **manslaughter by an unlawful and dangerous act** and causing grievous bodily harm by an unlawful act.

## Issues

Can a simple breach of road traffic regulations (such as crossing an unbroken line) constitute the "unlawful act" required to support a conviction for manslaughter by an unlawful and dangerous act?

## Rule/Law

- **No Unlawfulness by Mere Statutory Breach:** For the purposes of manslaughter, an act is **not "unlawful" merely because it breaches a statutory or regulatory prohibition** (such as a traffic regulation).
- **Must Be Unlawful in Itself:** A statutory breach can only count as the "unlawful act" if it is **unlawful in itself**—meaning it is a crime independent of the regulatory breach (such as a physical assault).
- **Criminal Negligence is the Standard:** In motor vehicle fatalities, the prosecution should almost always rely on the category of **manslaughter by criminal negligence** rather than an unlawful and dangerous act, unless the vehicle was extraordinary and used as a weapon.

## Application/Analysis

- Pullman's act of crossing the unbroken line was a breach of the traffic regulations.
- However, there was **no evidence that his conduct was unlawful in itself**—for instance, there was no suggestion that he crossed the line deliberately to threaten or assault the oncoming driver.
- Because his act was only "unlawful" because it broke a traffic regulation, it could not legally support a charge of manslaughter by an unlawful and dangerous act.

## Conclusion

The Court of Criminal Appeal **allowed the appeal and quashed Pullman’s convictions** on all counts

### Judges Rules (Unlawful and dangerous act doctrine)

- **Traffic Violations Are Not Enough:** You cannot convict a driver of manslaughter by an "unlawful and dangerous act" based solely on a breach of traffic regulations.
- **Must Be an Independent Crime:** The driver’s action must be **unlawful in itself** (such as using a car as a weapon to commit an assault) apart from simply breaking a safety law.
- **Negligence is the Correct Charge:** For standard driving fatalities, the prosecution must prove **criminal negligence** rather than relying on the unlawful and dangerous act doctrine.
