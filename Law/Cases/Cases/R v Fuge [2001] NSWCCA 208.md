NSW Court of Criminal Appeal (NSW Supreme Court)

## Material Facts

- The appellant (Fuge) was part of a group that planned and executed an attempted armed robbery at a Hungry Jack's restaurant.
- The principal offenders, RB (a former employee recently dismissed from the restaurant) and Pohl, attempted the robbery using knives.
- The appellant was found to have acted as an accessory before the fact by supplying a key to steal a getaway car, providing a bandanna and stockings for disguise, supplying a knife, and encouraging the principals to hurry.
- During the planning, RB briefly mentioned that Hungry Jack's "owed her money and that was the only way she thought she'd get it," though the group took this comment as "a bit of a joke".
- The appellant was convicted of being an accessory before the fact to assault with intent to rob while armed with an offensive weapon. She appealed on the grounds that the judge failed to direct the jury on a possible "claim of right" defense and an accomplice's motive to lie.

## Issues

- Should the trial judge have directed the jury on the defense of a "claim of right," even though trial counsel did not request it?
- Does an accessory before the fact escape liability if the principal offender has a bona fide claim of right to the property taken?
- Did the trial judge err by failing to give specific directions regarding an accomplice's (Pohl's) motive to lie?

## Rule/Law

- **Claim of Right:** If a person genuinely (bona fide) believes they are legally entitled to certain money or property, taking it does not constitute the _mens rea_ required for robbery or larceny, even if the belief is unreasonable or not grounded in law.
- **_Walden v Hensler_ & _Salvo_ (Applied):** Established that a genuine belief in a legal entitlement negates the _mens rea_ for larceny/robbery.
- **Accessorial Liability:** There can be no accessorial liability unless a foundational offence has actually been committed by the principal. If the principal offender has a valid claim of right, there is no foundational offence of robbery.
- **Rule 4 of the _Criminal Appeal Rules_:** Requires an appellant to seek leave to appeal if they are relying on misdirections or non-directions that their counsel failed to object to during the original trial. Leave is generally only granted if there is a risk of a real miscarriage of justice.
- **_Jovanovic_ & _Palmer_ (Distinguished):** Established that in certain cases, juries must be strictly warned not to reverse the onus of proof when considering a witness's "motive to lie".

## Application/Analysis

- **Insufficient Evidence for Claim of Right:** The Court held that the claim of right issue was not sufficiently raised by the evidence to require a jury direction. RB's comment was passing and treated as a joke. Furthermore, the claim must extend strictly to the entirety of the money taken, and there was no evidence the robbers intended to take only a specific sum owed rather than whatever cash was in the safe.
- **Motive to Lie:** The trial judge did not err by failing to give a complex _Palmer_ direction regarding Pohl's motive to lie to gain a lighter sentence. The judge had already given strong warnings about accomplice testimony and gave extremely clear, general directions that the Crown always bears the onus of proof and the accused has to prove nothing.
- **The "Armchair Appeal":** The Court heavily criticized the appeal, noting it was a "classic example of an 'armchair appeal'" where new counsel combed the trial transcript for minute errors that original trial counsel deliberately or carelessly ignored. Because trial counsel fought the case purely on whether the appellant actually assisted the robbers and never raised the claim of right or objected to the summing up, it was entirely inappropriate to order a new trial.

## Conclusion

Leave to appeal under Rule 4 was **dismissed**. The Court found no miscarriage of justice. The conviction and sentence were **confirmed**.
