## Material Facts

The NSW Legislative Assembly passed a Bill to abolish the Legislative Council (the upper house). The Council refused to consider the Bill. The Assembly passed it a second time, but the Council refused the Assembly's request for a "free conference" of managers and formally resolved not to participate in a "joint sitting" convened by the Governor. The Assembly then directed that the Bill be submitted to a referendum under the s 5B deadlock-resolving procedure. Opponents of the Bill sued to stop the referendum from being held, claiming s 5B was invalid and the required procedures had not been met.

## Issues

Is the s 5B deadlock procedure constitutionally valid, and does the failure to hold a free conference or a fully participated joint sitting stop the referendum process?

## Rule/Law

1. **Constituent Power:** A state parliament with plenary legislative power has the authority to alter its own constitution, which includes establishing alternative lawmaking pathways that bypass one of its Houses.
2. **No Unilateral Veto:** One House of Parliament cannot completely halt or veto a statutory deadlock-resolving procedure simply by refusing to cooperate or participate in its preliminary steps.

## Application/Analysis

Section 5B is entirely valid under the NSW Parliament's broad constituent power. The Legislative Council's refusal to take part in the free conference or joint sitting did not stop the deadlock procedure, because a House cannot defeat a statutory process designed to bypass its opposition by simply refusing to show up.

## Conclusion

Special leave to appeal was refused, meaning the s 5B process was valid and the referendum was allowed to proceed.

### Judges Rules

- **The Power to Bypass:** A parliament with full constituent power can legally establish alternative legislative methods—such as a referendum—to pass laws and even abolish a house of parliament without that house's consent.
- **The Cooperation Principle:** A house of parliament **cannot freeze or defeat a statutory deadlock procedure by refusing to participate** in its preliminary steps (like conferences or joint sittings). If one house refuses to cooperate, those steps are treated as bypassed or unnecessary, allowing the rest of the procedure to continue legally.
