Court of Criminal Appeal for New South Wales

## Material Facts

- **The Bust & Substitution:** Police intercepted a suitcase containing 30 blocks of illegally imported heroin. They replaced the heroin with blocks of plaster of paris for a controlled delivery.
- **The Apprehension:** Van Hung Mai was later arrested with one of these substituted plaster blocks in his jacket pocket. This specific block contained absolutely **no heroin**.
- **The Charge:** Mai was charged with **attempting to obtain possession** of a prohibited import (heroin) under s 233B(1)(c) of the _Customs Act 1901_ (Cth).
- **The Defense:** Mai argued that because the plaster block contained no actual heroin, it was **physically impossible** for him to possess heroin, and therefore no crime of attempt had been committed.

## Issues

Can an accused be convicted of a criminal attempt if committing the completed offense is physically impossible?

## Rule/Law

- **Physical Impossibility is No Defense:** The fact that it is physically impossible to complete the intended crime does not prevent a conviction for a criminal attempt.
- **The Two-Step Test for Impossible Attempts:** An attempt is legally proven if:
    1. **Subjective Intent:** The accused intended to commit the crime, acting on the belief that the facts were as they supposed them to be (i.e., if their belief was true, the crime would have been completed).
    2. **Proximate Act:** The accused performed an act towards committing that crime that went beyond "mere preparation" and has no other reasonable purpose than committing that crime.

## Application/Analysis

- **Subjective Belief:** Mai believed the plaster block in his jacket pocket was heroin. Had his belief been true, he would have successfully possessed a prohibited import.
- **Action Taken:** Physically taking and carrying the block was an overt act that went far beyond mere preparation and had no other logical purpose than possessing the drug. His subjective state of mind, combined with this proximate action, satisfied the requirements of criminal attempt regardless of the block's physical composition.

## Conclusion

The Court of Criminal Appeal dismissed the appeal and **upheld Mai's conviction** for attempted possession.

### Judges Rules

- **Impossibility is Irrelevant:** You can be convicted of attempting a crime even if it was **physically impossible** to complete it (e.g., attempting to possess drugs that are actually plaster, or attempting to steal from an empty pocket).
- **Judged by Your Beliefs:** An impossible attempt is evaluated strictly by **what you subjectively believed the facts to be**, not what they actually were.
- **The Preparatory Line Must Be Crossed:** To be guilty, you must still take active steps that cross the line from "mere preparation" to "execution". Your actions must clearly show a singular, criminal purpose.
