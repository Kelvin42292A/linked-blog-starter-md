## Material Facts

An engineering union had an industrial dispute with 844 employers across Australia, including government-owned trading businesses (like sawmills and engineering works) run by the State of Western Australia. The State argued that under the Constitution, State governments were immune from Commonwealth industrial arbitration laws.

## Issues

Does the Commonwealth Parliament have the constitutional power under section 51(xxxv) to make industrial arbitration laws that bind State governments and their businesses?

## Rule/Law

- **Literal Interpretation (The "Golden Rule"):** The Constitution must be interpreted by reading the text itself in its natural, ordinary sense. Courts must not read imaginary limitations or political "necessities" into the text to protect State rights.
- **Plenary Power:** The Commonwealth’s legislative powers in section 51 are complete and absolute within their limits, meaning they can bind the States unless the Constitution explicitly writes in an exception.
- **Inconsistency:** Under section 109, when a valid Commonwealth law conflicts with a State law, the Commonwealth law always wins.

## Application/Analysis

Section 51(xxxv) gives the Commonwealth power over "industrial disputes" generally, and contains no written exception for State-run businesses. Reading the text literally, the Commonwealth has the authority to regulate these disputes, and the State must obey.

## Conclusion

Yes, the Commonwealth has the power to bind the States. The court rejected the old doctrine of implied State immunity and overruled the _Railway Servants' Case_.

### Judges Rules

- **The Textualism Rule:** The ultimate "universal rule" of statutory and constitutional interpretation is that **the words of a document must be read in their natural and ordinary sense**.
- **The Power Principle:** If the Constitution grants a legislative power to the federal government in broad, general terms, **that power is complete and can bind State governments**. Courts cannot invent "implied" protections or state immunities that are not explicitly written into the text of the Constitution itself.
