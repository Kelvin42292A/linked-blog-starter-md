## Material Facts

Chauncey Aaron Bell injected a 15-year-old girl with methylamphetamine. He was charged with supplying a controlled drug to a child under s 14 of the _Misuse of Drugs Act 2001_ (Tas). Bell claimed he honestly and reasonably believed the girl was 20 years old based on what she had told him. However, supplying methylamphetamine to an adult was still a criminal offence under s 26 of the same Act.

## Issues

Is the defence of honest and reasonable mistake of fact available if the defendant's mistaken belief, if true, would still make them guilty of a different, lesser offence? What does "innocent" mean in the context of this defence?

## Rule/Law

- To successfully rely on the common law excuse of honest and reasonable mistake of fact, the mistaken belief must be such that, if true, the conduct of the accused would be **"innocent"**.
- **"Innocent" strictly means not guilty of _any_ criminal offence**. It does not mean innocent of only the particular, specific offence charged.
- A mistake that merely shifts the conduct from one statutory prohibition to another is legally irrelevant to guilt, though it may be considered during sentencing.

## Application/Analysis

Even if the complainant had been 20 years old as Bell believed, his act of supplying her with methylamphetamine would not have been "innocent" or lawful; it would still have been a crime under s 26 of the Act. Therefore, the defence of mistake was legally unavailable to him.

## Conclusion

The High Court of Australia unanimously dismissed Bell's appeal and upheld his conviction, confirming the defence of mistake of fact was properly withheld from the jury.

### Judges Rules

1. **Strict Definition of "Innocent"**: The defendant's mistaken belief must be in a state of affairs which, if true, would make their conduct **completely lawful and innocent of any criminal offence**.
2. **No "Prohibition Hopping"**: If the defendant’s mistaken belief simply means they thought they were committing a different or lesser crime (rather than behaving lawfully), **the defence is completely unavailable**. Their mistake is legally irrelevant to whether they are guilty of the charged crime, though it can still be used to argue for a lighter sentence.
