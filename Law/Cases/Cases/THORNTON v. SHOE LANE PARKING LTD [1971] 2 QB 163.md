Court of Appeal (Queen's Bench Division) (UK)

## Material Facts

- The plaintiff (Mr. Thornton) drove to a brand new multi-storey automatic car park for the first time.
- A notice on the outside simply stated the parking charges and that all cars were "Parked At Owner's Risk".
- There was no human attendant. When he drove up to the entrance, a traffic light turned green and an automatic machine pushed out a ticket.
- Mr. Thornton took the ticket, parked his car, and left. He did not read the small print on the ticket, which said it was "issued subject to the conditions of issue as displayed on the premises".
- If he had walked around the garage, he would have found a long list of conditions on a pillar. One of these conditions stated the garage would **not be liable for any personal injury to the customer**.
- When Mr. Thornton returned to collect his car, there was an accident and he was severely injured. The garage was found partly at fault, but they tried to use the hidden condition to completely exempt themselves from paying damages.

## Issues

1. **Timing of the Contract:** In cases involving automatic machines, exactly _when_ is the contract formed, and can a ticket issued by the machine legally add new conditions?
2. **Reasonable Notice:** Did the garage do what was "reasonably sufficient" to bring a drastic condition (excluding liability for personal injury) to the customer's attention?

## Rule/Law

- **The Old Ticket Cases Don't Apply:** In old cases (involving trains or ships), a ticket handed by a human clerk was treated as an "offer" that the customer could refuse. This does not apply to automatic machines because a customer cannot refuse it or get their money back. The process is completely irrevocable.
- **Offer and Acceptance for Machines:** The "offer" is made when the machine is held out as ready to receive money (or a car). The "acceptance" takes place the exact moment the customer puts their money in or activates the machine.
- **The "Red Hand" Rule:** The more unusual or destructive a restrictive condition is (like taking away a person's statutory right to sue for personal injury), the more extreme the notice must be. To give sufficient notice for such a drastic clause, it would need to be printed "in red ink with a red hand pointing to it—or something equally startling".

## Application/Analysis

- **The Ticket Came Too Late:** The contract was concluded the moment Mr. Thornton drove up and the light turned green to issue the ticket. Because the contract was already made, the garage could not legally alter it by thrusting a ticket with new conditions at him after the fact.
- **Insufficient Notice:** The sign outside only warned about property damage ("Owner's Risk"). The condition trying to block personal injury claims was a massive restriction hidden inside the garage, which Mr. Thornton could only read _after_ he had irrevocably entered. The Court ruled it would be a "farce" to expect customers to abandon their cars blocking the entrance just to wander around searching for hidden notices before parking.

## Conclusion

The garage's appeal was **dismissed**. The Court ruled that the exemption condition **did not** form part of the contract because Mr. Thornton had no knowledge of it and the garage failed to give reasonable notice. The garage was held liable and ordered to pay the damages for Mr. Thornton's injuries.
