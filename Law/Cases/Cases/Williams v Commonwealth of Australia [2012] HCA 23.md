## Material Facts

Ronald Williams challenged the Commonwealth's funding of a school chaplaincy program at his children's State school in Queensland. The program was funded through a contract between the Commonwealth and a private provider, Scripture Union Queensland. While Parliament had passed annual Appropriation Acts to allocate funds for the budget, **no specific federal law** had been passed to authorize the program or its spending.

## Issues

Does the Commonwealth Executive have the power to make contracts and spend public money on domestic programs without specific enabling legislation passed by Parliament?

## Rule/Law

- The Executive **cannot** spend public money or make contracts simply because the subject matter falls within a head of federal legislative power.
- Apart from narrow exceptions—such as royal prerogatives, national emergencies, or the everyday administration of government departments—the Executive **must have statutory (parliamentary) authorization** to spend public funds.

## Application/Analysis

The school chaplaincy program was not a national emergency, a prerogative matter, or part of the ordinary administration of a government department. Therefore, even though the Commonwealth argued it _could_ have passed a law under the "benefits to students" power (s 51(xxiiiA)), the lack of an actual statute meant the Executive could not bypass Parliament to fund the program on its own.

## Conclusion

The High Court held that the funding agreement and the payments were beyond the Commonwealth's executive power and were invalid. (A secondary freedom of religion challenge under s 116 failed because the school chaplains did not hold an "office under the Commonwealth").

### Judges Rules

- **The Rule of Executive Spending:** Under Section 61 of the Constitution, **the Executive cannot spend public money or enter into contracts on a program unless Parliament has actually passed a law authorizing that program.**
- **The Parliamentary Principle:** The mere fact that the federal parliament _has the power_ to pass a law on a topic (or that money has been set aside in a general Appropriation Act) is **not enough**. Parliament must actively debate, scrutinize, and pass the authorizing legislation to maintain democratic accountability and protect the federal balance with the States.
