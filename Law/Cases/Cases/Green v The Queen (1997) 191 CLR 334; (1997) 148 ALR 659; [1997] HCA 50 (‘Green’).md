High Court of Australia

## Material Facts

- **The Relationship:** Malcolm Green (22) was close friends with Donald Gillies (36), whom he trusted deeply as a father figure.
- **The Incident:** While Green was staying overnight, a highly intoxicated Gillies entered his bedroom naked, climbed into bed with him, and made persistent, unwanted sexual advances, grabbing Green’s backside and groin.
- **The Killing:** Green "snapped". He brutally beat Gillies with his fists, violently banged his head against the wall, and stabbed him 10 times with scissors, killing him.
- **The Trauma History:** Green claimed his violent reaction was triggered because the assault unlocked memories of his father physically abusing his mother and sexually abusing his sisters, making him exceptionally sensitive to sexual interference.
- **The Trial Rulings:** The trial judge directed the jury that Green's traumatic family history was completely irrelevant to provocation. He also ruled that the provocation must be severe enough to make an ordinary person _physically react with the same extreme violence_ as Green, rather than merely forming an _intent to kill or cause serious harm_.
- **The Appeal:** Green was convicted of murder. The Court of Criminal Appeal agreed the judge made errors but dismissed the appeal, ruling that an ordinary person would not have reacted with homicidal intent to a non-violent homosexual advance. Green appealed to the High Court.

## Issues

1. Does "an ordinary person in the position of the accused" take into account the accused's past history and personal sensitivities when assessing the gravity of provocation?
2. Does the objective test look at whether the ordinary person could have formed the _intent_ to kill/cause serious harm, or does it evaluate the actual _ferocity of the physical response_?

## Rule/Law

- **Strictly Objective Self-Control:** The hypothetical ordinary person possesses ordinary powers of self-control. The accused's personal weaknesses (such as an explosive temper or bad self-control) are completely ignored.
- **Contextual Gravity of Provocation:** However, in assessing the **gravity, sting, and severity of the provocation**, the ordinary person is placed **"in the position of the accused"**. The court must consider the accused’s age, relationships, past history, and personal sensitivities (like Green's history of family sexual abuse) to understand how deeply the deceased's conduct stung them.
- **The Synthesis:** The ultimate test is whether an ordinary person, experiencing provocation of _that specific, contextual gravity_, could have lost self-control so far as to form an **intent to kill or inflict grievous bodily harm**.
- **Intent, Not Action:** The test is strictly about the formation of the **intention to kill or cause serious harm**, not whether an ordinary person would have carried out the killing with the same extreme physical violence.

## Application/Analysis

- **Critical Misdirection:** The High Court majority found that by directing the jury to ignore Green's family history of sexual abuse, the trial judge prevented them from properly assessing the true gravity of the provocation from Green's perspective.
- **Denial of Natural Justice:** By wrongly focusing on the physical _violence of the response_ rather than the _intent formed_, the judge stopped defense counsel from presenting Green's actual case to the jury, resulting in an unfair trial.

## Conclusion

By a 3:2 majority, the High Court allowed the appeal, **quashed Green's murder conviction**, and ordered a new trial.

### Judges Rules

- **Assessing Gravity (Subjective):** To measure the "sting" of the provocation, the hypothetical ordinary person is placed **"in the position of the accused"**. This means the jury must factor in the accused's unique background, personal relationships, past history, and sensitivities (such as traumatic family abuse).
- **Assessing Self-Control (Strictly Objective):** Once the severity of the provocation is understood, the jury asks if a person with **ordinary powers of self-control** could have snapped under that pressure. The accused’s personal bad temper, aggressiveness, or general lack of self-control are completely ignored.
- **Focus on Intent, Not Action:** The objective test only asks whether the provocation could have pushed an ordinary person to form an **intent to kill or cause serious bodily harm**. It does **not** ask whether an ordinary person would have reacted with the same level of extreme, frenzied physical violence as the accused.
