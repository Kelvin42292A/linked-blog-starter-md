High Court of Australia

## Material Facts

- The appellant (Michael Jiminez) was driving a car from the Gold Coast to Sydney with three passengers.
- Before taking the wheel, the appellant had slept for four hours before the trip began, and slept again while a passenger (Janelle Stephanoni) drove the first 400 kilometres.
- The appellant took over driving at about 3:30 a.m. At 6:00 a.m., the car failed to negotiate a moderate right-hand curve on the highway.
- The vehicle went off the road, travelled along a gravel shoulder, and crashed into three trees. Ms. Stephanoni, who was in the front passenger seat and not wearing a seatbelt, was killed.
- The appellant admitted to police that he "lost control when I fell asleep". However, he also stated that just prior to the crash, he was awake and thinking about stopping at the next town for breakfast,.
- The appellant was charged with and convicted of **culpable driving** under s 52A of the _Crimes Act 1900_ (NSW),.
- The core of the prosecution's case was that his driving was dangerous because he was tired and had fallen asleep at the wheel.

## Issues

1. **Voluntary Acts:** Can a driver be legally considered to be "driving" in a dangerous manner under s 52A during a period when they are asleep, given that criminal acts must be conscious and voluntary?.
2. **Prior Driving:** Can the period of driving _immediately preceding_ the onset of sleep constitute dangerous driving if the driver knew or ought to have known they were too tired?
3. **Honest and Reasonable Mistake:** Is the defence of honest and reasonable mistake available to a driver who falls asleep if they had no warning of the onset of sleep and reasonably believed it was safe to drive?

## Rule/Law

- **s 52A _Crimes Act 1900_ (NSW):** The offence of culpable driving. It applies when a death is occasioned by the impact of a motor vehicle being driven at a speed or in a manner dangerous to the public.
- **The Voluntariness Requirement (_R v Coventry_):** Criminal liability requires the driving to be a conscious and voluntary act. Because actions performed while asleep are strictly unconscious and involuntary, a driver cannot legally be "driving in a dangerous manner" while they are actually asleep.
- **Preceding Driving & Contemporaneity (_McBride_ & _Kroon_):** Even if the driver is asleep at the moment of impact, the relevant period of driving is the time _immediately preceding_ their falling asleep. If a driver continues to drive when they know or ought to know there is a significant risk of falling asleep, that preceding driving can be considered dangerous and sufficiently contemporaneous with the crash.
- **Condition of the Driver (_McBride_):** "Manner dangerous" is an objective test. If a driver's severely tired or drowsy condition makes their driving a real, objective danger to the public, it constitutes dangerous driving, even if the vehicle itself isn't visibly behaving erratically yet.
- **Defence of Honest and Reasonable Mistake:** The offence under s 52A is not an "absolute liability" offence. The defence of honest and reasonable mistake applies. If a driver falls asleep without any prior warning, and honestly and reasonably believed it was safe for them to drive, this serves as a complete excuse.

## Application/Analysis

- **Misdirection on Voluntariness & The Relevant Period:** The High Court found that the trial judge critically misdirected the jury by allowing them to consider the appellant's driving _at the exact moment_ the car left the road. Because the appellant was asleep, his actions were strictly involuntary, and he could not be legally responsible for them,. The judge failed to instruct the jury that the only relevant period to assess for "dangerous driving" was the time _immediately preceding_ his sleep,.
- **Failure to Direct on Honest & Reasonable Mistake:** The appellant had rested for four hours before the trip, slept for another three hours while his passenger drove, had not been driving for an excessive period, and had not consumed alcohol or drugs. This lack of warning regarding the onset of sleep laid a strong evidentiary foundation that the appellant honestly and reasonably believed it was safe to drive. The trial judge failed entirely to instruct the jury that this was a complete defence to the charge.
- **Miscarriage of Justice:** Because the trial judge failed to properly direct the jury on the relevant period of driving, the requirement of voluntariness, and the defence of honest and reasonable mistake, the jury may have convicted the appellant for an involuntary act. Therefore, the trial miscarried.

## Conclusion

The appeal was **allowed**. The High Court set aside the culpable driving conviction because the trial miscarried due to the judge's misdirections. The Court decided **not** to order a new trial.
