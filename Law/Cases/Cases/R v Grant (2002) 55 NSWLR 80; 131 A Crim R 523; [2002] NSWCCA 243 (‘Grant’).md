## Material Facts

After drinking alcohol all day, Grant got a rifle from his caravan and shot a man dead from 20 to 25 feet away. He was charged with murder. The prosecution argued he either had a specific intent to kill/injure, or acted with "reckless indifference to human life" (meaning he personally foresaw that his action would likely cause death and went ahead anyway).

## Issues

Can a jury take an accused person's self-induced intoxication into account when deciding if they had the subjective foresight required for murder by reckless indifference?

## Rule/Law

Yes. Under Part 11A of the _Crimes Act 1900_ (NSW), **murder in all of its forms** (including reckless indifference and felony murder) is classified as an **offence of specific intent**. Therefore, evidence of self-induced intoxication **can** be considered in determining whether the accused actually foresaw that their action would likely cause death.

## Application/Analysis

The trial judge wrongly directed the jury that they could only consider Grant’s drunkenness for the "intent to kill or cause grievous bodily harm" paths of murder, but not for "reckless indifference". Because the jury might have convicted Grant based on reckless indifference without properly considering how his severe intoxication affected his awareness, the trial was unfair.

## Conclusion

The NSW Court of Criminal Appeal allowed the appeal, quashed the murder conviction, and ordered a new trial.

### Judges Rules

- **The Rule:** Under NSW criminal law, **murder is always treated as an offence of "specific intent," regardless of how the prosecution argues the charge.**
- **The Practical Effect:** Even when the prosecution relies on the "reckless indifference" path rather than a direct intent to kill, **the jury is legally required to consider the accused’s voluntary intoxication** to decide whether they actually, subjectively realized their actions were highly likely to cause death.
