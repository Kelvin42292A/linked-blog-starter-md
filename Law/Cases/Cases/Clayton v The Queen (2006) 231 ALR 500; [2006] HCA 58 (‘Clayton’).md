High Court of Australia

## Material Facts

- **The Slaying:** Celia Clayton, John Hartwick, and Lisa Hartwick armed themselves with weapons (metal and wooden poles, and a large carving knife) and went to Paula Rodwell’s house to assault Steven Borg.
- **The Attack:** During a prolonged 30-to-40-minute attack, Borg was severely beaten and stabbed to death.
- **The Prosecution’s Gap:** The prosecution was completely unable to prove which of the three co-accused actually delivered the fatal stab wound.
- **The Conviction & Appeal:** All three were convicted of murder. They appealed to the High Court, asking the court to abandon or modify the doctrine of **extended common purpose**. They argued it was unjust to convict a secondary party of murder based on the mere foresight of a "possibility" of death or serious harm.

## Issues

Should the High Court modify or abandon the doctrine of extended common purpose by replacing the requirement of foreseeability of a **possibility** of death/serious harm with a higher standard (such as "probability," "virtual certainty," or actual intent)?

## Rule/Law

- **Extended Common Purpose:** A participant in a joint criminal enterprise is guilty of murder if they agreed to a joint venture (such as an assault) and foresaw the **possibility** that another participant might intentionally kill or inflict really serious injury, yet continued to participate anyway.
- **The Source of Culpability:** Culpability does not depend on desiring or intending the death. Rather, the legal wrong lies in the **mutual embarkation on a crime** and the **continued participation** in that joint venture with the foresight of what might occur.
- **Strict Standard Upheld:** The High Court majority explicitly declined to change the law, confirming that foresight of a **possibility** remains the correct legal test.

## Application/Analysis

- **No Miscarriage of Justice:** The majority found that applying the "possibility" standard does not cause injustice or an unfair trial.
- **Agreement to Less Does Not Excuse:** It did not matter if Clayton or the Hartwicks only agreed to a lesser assault and did not wish for Borg to die. Because they went to the house armed (or knowing others were armed), the jury could legally infer they foresaw the possibility of serious harm, and their continued participation made them guilty of murder.
- **Simple Jury Questions:** The jury does not need to get bogged down in over-complicated legal formulas. The essential factual questions are simple:
    1. What did the accused agree was to happen?
    2. What did the accused foresee was possible?
    3. What did the accused actually do at the scene to assist?

## Conclusion

The High Court **dismissed the appeals** and upheld all murder convictions.

### Judges Rules

- **Foresight of "Possibility" is Sufficient:** To be convicted of a partner’s additional crime, the prosecution only has to prove you foresaw it as a **possibility** (not a probability or virtual certainty) and chose to keep participating anyway.
- **Culpability Lies in Continued Participation:** You do not need to agree to the killing, or even want the victim to die, to be guilty of murder. Your criminal guilt comes entirely from **continuing to take part in the group enterprise** while knowing the dangerous risks.
- **Mutual Embarkation is the Wrong:** Unlike ordinary aiding and abetting (which requires proving you specifically intended to help a crime), joint enterprise liability is triggered because you **mutually embarked on a criminal venture with others** and are therefore responsible for the foreseen outcomes of that group action.
- **Weapons Support the Inference:** If you go to a crime scene armed, or knowing your partners are carrying weapons (like knives or poles), a jury can easily infer that you **subjectively foresaw the possibility** of serious harm or death occurring.
