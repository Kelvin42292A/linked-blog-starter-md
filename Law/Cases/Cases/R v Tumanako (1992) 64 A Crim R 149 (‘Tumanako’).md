## Material Facts

Tumanako stabbed his ex-girlfriend to death in a jealous rage and claimed the defence of diminished responsibility. Two of his defence experts testified that he had a "dependent personality disorder" (an inherent cause), but a third defence expert testified he only had ordinary "dependency traits". The jury rejected his defence and convicted him of murder.

## Issues

What must an accused person prove to successfully establish the defence of diminished responsibility?

## Rule/Law

To prove diminished responsibility under s 23A of the _Crimes Act 1900_ (NSW), the accused must establish three distinct elements on the **balance of probabilities**:

1. They suffered from an abnormality of mind at the time of the killing.
2. The abnormality arose from a specified cause (such as an inherent cause).
3. The abnormality substantially impaired their mental responsibility.

## Application/Analysis

Because the medical experts disagreed on whether Tumanako’s condition arose from a true inherent disorder or just ordinary emotional traits, the jury was fully entitled to decide that the defence had failed to prove the second element (the underlying cause) of the defence.

## Conclusion

The appeal was dismissed, and the murder conviction stood.

### Judges Rules

- **The Burden of Proof:** The accused person has the legal burden of proving the defence of diminished responsibility on the **balance of probabilities** (meaning it is more likely than not to be true).
- **The Requirement for Medical Evidence:** The accused **must** provide expert medical evidence to prove the underlying cause (etiology) of their mental abnormality. A jury cannot simply guess or find an inherent cause without expert testimony.
- **The Jury's Final Say:** The jury is not forced to accept unanimous medical opinions if there is other factual evidence that displaces or throws doubt on those medical views.
