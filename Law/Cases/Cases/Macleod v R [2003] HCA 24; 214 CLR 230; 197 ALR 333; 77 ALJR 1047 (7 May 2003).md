High Court of Australia

## Material Facts

- The appellant (Macleod) was a director and the sole beneficial shareholder of a company named Trainex Pty Ltd.
- Trainex promoted a film production investment scheme, raising over $6 million from thousands of investors seeking tax concessions.
- The invested funds were supposed to be held in trust accounts specifically for film production.
- While only about $718,000 was actually spent on making films, the appellant applied over $2 million of the funds for his own personal benefit, including purchasing a home unit on the Gold Coast in his own name and paying off personal loan accounts.
- To conceal the reality, the appellant sent investors false "income statements" and instructed an employee to hide company records from the Australian Securities Commission.
- The appellant was convicted of five counts of **fraudulently applying company property** for his own use or a use other than the company's purpose, under **s 173** of the **_Crimes Act 1900_ (NSW)**.

## Issues

- **Consent:** Can the sole directing mind and shareholder of a company legally "consent" on the company's behalf to the taking of corporate property, thereby negating the charge of a fraudulent application under **s 173**?.
- **Dishonesty / _Mens Rea_:** Did the trial judge err in directing the jury on the meaning of "fraudulently" by failing to require proof that the appellant subjectively realised his actions were dishonest by ordinary standards (the **_Ghosh_ test**)?.

## Rule/Law

- **s 173 _Crimes Act 1900_ (NSW):** The statutory offence prohibiting a director or officer from fraudulently taking or applying company property for their own use or for purposes other than the company's.
- **_Salomon v Salomon & Co_:** Established the foundational principle that a corporation is a separate legal entity, distinct from its shareholders or directors.
- **_R v Gomez_ (Approved) & _R v Roffel_ (Disapproved):** The House of Lords in _Gomez_ correctly established that a controlling shareholder committing a crime _against_ the company cannot use their own consent to authorize the misappropriation. The High Court explicitly rejected the Victorian case of _Roffel_ (which controversially held that a sole shareholder's consent meant there was no "appropriation" of company property).
- **_Peters v The Queen_ (Applied):** "Fraudulently" means "dishonestly." The correct legal test for dishonesty is simply whether the conduct was dishonest according to the standards of ordinary, decent people. The High Court reaffirmed that the English **_Ghosh_ test** (requiring the accused to subjectively _know_ they were violating those ordinary standards) is rejected in Australia.

## Application/Analysis

- **The "Consent" Defence:** The High Court firmly rejected the appellant's argument that, as the sole shareholder, he could not defraud his own company. Because a company is a separate legal entity with its own distinct rights and duties, the self-interested "consent" of a shareholder acting to further a crime against the company does not equate to the legally valid consent of the company. The funds were taken for the appellant's personal benefit, directly contradicting the lawful purposes of the company (which was bound by a trust deed to use the money for films).
- **Applying the _Peters_ Test:** The trial judge's directions to the jury were perfectly adequate. The judge told the jury to evaluate the appellant's actions based on the current standards of ordinary, decent people. Following _**Peters**_, there was no need to instruct the jury to consider whether the appellant personally realized his actions were dishonest by those standards. Such a subjective direction would only distract the jury.
- **Claim of Right:** The appellant claimed he believed the company owed him the money and that he had a "claim of right" to take it. However, the court found there was overwhelming evidence of deceit—such as faking income statements and hiding documents from investigators—which completely undermined any assertion of an honest belief.

## Conclusion

The appeal was **dismissed**. The High Court ruled that a sole shareholder's consent does not cure the fraudulent misappropriation of company property, and reaffirmed that the objective standard from _**Peters**_ is the correct test for assessing dishonesty. The original convictions were upheld.
