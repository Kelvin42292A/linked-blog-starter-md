High Court of Australia

## Material Facts

- After a night of drinking, the accused (Getachew) and the complainant lay on a mattress to sleep.
- Before falling asleep, the complainant told the accused, "just don't touch me and let me sleep".
- The complainant awoke to find her clothes dishevelled and the accused sexually penetrating her.
- At his trial, the accused did not give evidence. His sole defence was disputing that penetration occurred; he **never asserted or led evidence that he believed the complainant was consenting**.
- He was convicted of rape, but the Court of Appeal overturned the conviction, stating the judge should have directed the jury on the possibility that the accused mistakenly believed she was consenting. The prosecution appealed to the High Court.

## Issues

- Whether the trial judge was required to direct the jury regarding the accused's mistaken belief in consent, even though the accused never asserted or led evidence of such a belief at trial.

## Rule/Law

- Under **s 38(2)** of the _Crimes Act 1958_ (Vic), a person commits rape if they intentionally sexually penetrate another person without consent, while being aware that the person is not consenting or might not be consenting.
- Under **s 36(d)**, "consent" means free agreement. A person who is **asleep** is legally incapable of freely agreeing.
- Under **s 37AA**, a judge must direct a jury about an accused's belief in consent (and whether it was reasonable) **only if** evidence is led or an assertion is made at trial that the accused held that belief.

## Application/Analysis

- Because the accused never claimed or led evidence that he believed the complainant was consenting, **the requirements to trigger a direction under s 37AA were not met**.
- The trial judge was correct in not giving a direction on mistaken belief, and the Court of Appeal was wrong to conclude this was a misdirection.
- The High Court held that if a complainant is asleep, she cannot consent. Since the accused knew she was or might be asleep, **the only open conclusion was that he was aware she might not be consenting**. The jury did not need to exclude a hypothetical mistaken belief that the accused himself never claimed to hold.

## Conclusion

The prosecution's appeal to the High Court was **allowed**. The Court of Appeal's orders were set aside, and the appeal to the Court of Appeal was dismissed, effectively upholding the original rape conviction.
