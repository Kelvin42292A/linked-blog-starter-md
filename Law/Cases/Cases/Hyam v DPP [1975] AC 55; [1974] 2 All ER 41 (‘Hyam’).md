House of Lords (UK)

## Material Facts

- The appellant (Pearl Hyam) had been J’s mistress, but the relationship ended due to the appellant suffering from gynaecological trouble.
- The appellant became jealous and suspicious that J was about to marry a Mrs B.
- In the early hours of the morning, the appellant drove to Mrs B's house (where Mrs B was sleeping with her three children). She poured half a gallon of petrol through the letterbox and ignited it.
- The appellant did nothing to alert the occupants or the fire brigade, and drove home.
- Mrs B and her son escaped, but her two daughters died from asphyxia caused by the fire fumes.
- At trial, the appellant argued she only set the fire to _frighten_ Mrs B into leaving the neighbourhood and had no intention to kill or do serious bodily harm.
- The trial judge directed the jury that the prosecution only needed to prove that when the appellant set the fire, she knew it was **highly probable** that the act would result in death or serious bodily harm.
- The appellant was convicted of murder. She appealed to the Court of Appeal (which was dismissed) and then appealed to the House of Lords.

## Issues

1. **Foresight as Intent:** Is "malice aforethought" in the crime of murder established by proof beyond a reasonable doubt that the accused knew it was **highly probable** that their act would result in death or serious bodily harm, even if they did not actively desire that result?
2. **Intent to cause Grievous Bodily Harm:** In order to sustain a conviction for murder, is an intent to cause "serious bodily harm" sufficient, or must the prosecution prove a strict intent to kill or to cause harm that is likely to endanger life?

## Rule/Law

- **Mens Rea for Murder (_Criminal Justice Act 1967_):** The test for intent is subjective. The prosecution must prove the accused _actually knew_ it was **highly probable** their act would cause death or grievous bodily harm—even if their motive was just to frighten someone.
- **Grievous Bodily Harm (_DPP v Smith_ & _R v Vickers_):** Intending to cause "grievous bodily harm" (GBH) is legally enough to be convicted of murder. GBH simply means **"really serious bodily harm"** and does _not_ need to be life-threatening.

## Application/Analysis

- **Knowledge Equals Intent:** The majority of the House of Lords ruled that the trial judge did not make a mistake. Even though the appellant's _motive_ was just to frighten Mrs B into moving away, the law distinguishes between motive and intent. Because she deliberately poured half a gallon of petrol into an occupied house in the middle of the night, the jury was perfectly entitled to conclude she knew it was highly probable that someone would suffer really serious harm.
- **Deliberate Exposure to Risk:** By acting with the knowledge of that high probability, she deliberately chose to expose the victims to the danger of serious bodily harm without a lawful excuse. Under the common law, this subjective foresight and deliberate exposure are enough to establish the legal intention for murder.
- Because the judge's direction accurately reflected the law, the jury's verdict of murder was safe and lawful.

## Conclusion

The appeal was **dismissed** by a 3:2 majority. The appellant's conviction for murder was upheld.

### Judgment rule

You have the legal intent for murder if you **actually know** your actions are **highly probable** to cause death or really serious injury.

It does not matter if your actual goal or motive was just to scare someone, rather than to kill them.
