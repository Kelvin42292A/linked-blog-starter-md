High Court of Australia

## Material Facts

- The applicant (Royall) and the deceased (Kelly Healey) had a violent argument in their sixth-floor flat.
- Royall admitted to punching the deceased, shaking her, and pulling her hair.
- The deceased retreated to the small bathroom and locked the door. Royall forced the door open.
- Inside the bathroom, there was evidence of a fierce struggle, including gouge marks on the wall consistent with an ashtray being swung, and the deceased's blood splashed around the room.
- The deceased fell backwards out of the small, shoulder-width bathroom window and died.
- At trial, the Crown argued three possibilities for her death: (1) Royall pushed her out; (2) she fell while physically avoiding his attack; or (3) she jumped out to escape because she had a well-founded fear of life-threatening violence.
- Royall claimed she voluntarily jumped out of the window the moment he forced the door open, without him touching her.

## Issues

1. **Causation:** Did the trial judge properly direct the jury on how to determine if Royall's actions "caused" the deceased's death, especially if she jumped to escape his violence?
2. **Timing of Intent:** Did the judge misdirect the jury by telling them Royall's intent (to kill, do grievous bodily harm, or reckless indifference) had to exist at the moment she _fell_, rather than at the moment he _acted_?
3. **Reckless Indifference:** Did the judge correctly define "reckless indifference to human life" in accordance with the probability test established in _Crabbe_?

## Rule/Law

- **Causation is Common Sense:** Causation is not a philosophical or scientific question; it is a question of fact for the jury to decide by "applying their common sense to the facts as they find them" (approving _Campbell v The Queen_).
- **Causation in Escape/Fright (_Reg v Grimes and Lee_):** If an accused's conduct induces a "well-founded and reasonable apprehension" of danger in the victim, and the victim takes steps to escape that result in their death, the accused legally caused the death. The chain of causation is not broken by the victim's attempt at self-preservation.
- **Mode of Death is Irrelevant (_Reg v Demirian_):** "If a person creates a situation intended to kill and it does kill it is no answer to a charge of murder that it caused death at a time or in a way that was to some extent unexpected".
- **Reckless Indifference Means Probability (_Reg v Crabbe_):** The High Court affirmed that for "reckless indifference to human life" under the _Crimes Act 1900 (NSW)_, the accused must have actual knowledge/foresight of the **probability** of death, not just a possibility.
- **Coincidence of Act and Intent (Common Law):** The required intent must strictly coincide with the doing of the act that causes death.

## Application/Analysis

- **The Causation Direction:** The High Court ruled the trial judge's directions on causation were sufficient. The jury was entitled to use their common sense to conclude that jumping out of a window to escape a ferocious attack was a natural consequence of Royall's violence, making him legally responsible for the fall.
- **The Flawed Intent Timing:** The trial judge made a technical error by telling the jury that Royall's intent had to exist at the time the deceased _fell_ from the window. The law requires the intent to exist when the accused commits the _act_ that causes the death. However, because the time gap between Royall's attack in the bathroom and the deceased falling was so incredibly short, this error did not cause a miscarriage of justice.
- **The Recklessness Direction:** Even though the judge used slightly clunky wording ("a willingness to run the risk of the probability"), the High Court found this sufficiently brought home to the jury that Royall had to foresee death as a **probable** consequence, properly satisfying the strict rule from _Crabbe_.

## Conclusion

The appeal was **dismissed** by the High Court. Royall's conviction for murder and his sentence of life imprisonment were upheld.

### Judges Rules

- **The Probability Test (_Reg. v Crabbe_ and _Reg. v Annakin_):** To prove "reckless indifference to human life" under **s 18(1)(a) of the _Crimes Act 1900 (N.S.W.)_**, the accused must actually know that their act will **probably** (likely) cause death. Knowing that death is just a "possibility" is not enough.
- **Death, Not Just GBH (_Reg. v Solomon_):** Unlike the standard common law rule, the statutory rule in New South Wales requires foresight of the probability of _death_ specifically; "reckless indifference to grievous bodily harm is insufficient".
- **Willingness to Risk:** The accused must have an actual "willingness to run the risk of the probability of those consequences occurring" and show that they "did not care whether his act threatened the life concerned".
