High Court of Australia

## Material Facts

- The appellant (Royall) and the deceased (Kelly Louise Healey) shared a sixth-floor flat. Following a period of separation and arguments, the deceased returned to the flat.
- In the early hours of the morning, a violent argument ensued. The appellant admitted to punching the deceased in the face, shaking her, and pulling her hair. Blood was found in various parts of the living area.
- The deceased retreated to the bathroom, locked the door, undressed, and began to shower.
- The appellant forcibly broke the bathroom door open. Inside the extremely confined bathroom, there was strong evidence of a further violent attack, including the deceased's blood splashed on the walls, a chipped glass ashtray, and gouge marks on the wall consistent with a weapon being swung.
- The deceased fell six floors from the bathroom window to her death. The window was very small (shoulder-width), making an accidental fall extremely unlikely.
- The Crown alleged the appellant murdered her in one of three ways: (1) he pushed or forced her out the window, (2) she fell out while physically retreating from his attack, or (3) she jumped out to escape a well-founded and reasonable apprehension of life-threatening violence.
- The appellant was convicted of murder. He appealed to the High Court, arguing the trial judge misdirected the jury regarding the legal rules of causation and his intent.

## Issues

1. **Causation (Self-Preservation):** Does a victim's act of jumping from a window to escape a violent attack break the chain of causation, or is the accused legally responsible if the victim had a well-founded apprehension of life-threatening violence?
2. **Temporal Coincidence (Act and Intent):** Did the trial judge misdirect the jury by stating that the relevant time to assess the accused's intent was when the victim _fell_ from the window, rather than when the accused _committed the act_ that caused her to fall?
3. **Reckless Indifference:** Did the trial judge err in directing the jury on "reckless indifference to human life" by failing to explicitly instruct that the accused must have foreseen the _probability_ (rather than the mere possibility) of death?
4. **Unsafe/Unsatisfactory Verdict:** Was the murder conviction unsafe because the trial judge allowed the jury to choose between three different factual scenarios (pushing, retreating, or escaping) without giving specific directions on how to assess intent for each separate scenario?

## Rule/Law

- **s 18(1)(a) _Crimes Act 1900_ (NSW):** The statutory definition of murder. It requires that the act causing death be done with an intent to kill, an intent to inflict grievous bodily harm, or with reckless indifference to human life.
- **Causation in Fright/Self-Preservation Cases (_R v Grimes and Lee_; _Mackie_):** When an accused's violent conduct induces a well-founded and reasonable apprehension of physical harm in the victim, and the victim is injured or dies while taking steps to escape, the accused's act is considered the legal cause of the death. The victim's act of self-preservation does not break the chain of causation (it is not a _novus actus interveniens_) unless their reaction was completely unreasonable or disproportionate.
- **Reckless Indifference (_R v Crabbe_):** The mental element of "reckless indifference to human life" strictly requires the accused to foresee the **probability** (not the mere possibility) that their actions will cause death, and nevertheless run the risk.
- **Temporal Coincidence:** The required mental state (intent or recklessness) must concur specifically with the _act of the accused_ that causes the death, not simply at the exact moment the victim ultimately dies.
- **Unintended Mode of Death (_R v Demirian_):** If an accused performs an act with the requisite intent to kill or cause grievous bodily harm, and it causes the victim's death, it is no defence to a murder charge that the actual manner or mechanism of death was unexpected.

## Application/Analysis

- **Causation (The "Self-Preservation" Rule):** The High Court held that even if the deceased jumped from the window to escape (the Crown's third hypothesis), the applicant still legally "caused" her death. Because his ferocious attack induced a well-founded and reasonable apprehension of life-threatening harm, her desperate attempt to escape was a natural consequence of his violent actions. Her jump was not a voluntary intervening act (_novus actus interveniens_) that would break the chain of causation.
- **Unintended Mode of Death:** The Court ruled that as long as the applicant possessed the requisite intent (e.g., to inflict grievous bodily harm) when he attacked her in the bathroom, he is guilty of murder. It is no defence for him to argue that the actual _manner_ of her death (falling from a sixth-floor window) was unintended or unexpected.
- **Temporal Coincidence Misdirection:** The trial judge made a technical error by telling the jury to assess the applicant's intent at the exact moment the victim _fell_, rather than when the applicant actually _committed the violent acts_. However, the Court held this did not cause a miscarriage of justice because the time gap between his attack and her fall was practically instantaneous and immaterial.
- **Reckless Indifference Direction:** Although the trial judge used slightly clumsy wording ("willingness to run the risk of the probability"), the High Court found that, in context, it adequately conveyed the strict legal requirement. It successfully instructed the jury that to find "reckless indifference," the applicant must have actually foreseen the _probability_—not just the mere _possibility_—that his actions would cause death.

## Conclusion

The appeal was **dismissed**. The High Court upheld the murder conviction, ruling that while the trial judge made some errors in directing the jury, they did not result in a miscarriage of justice.
