Court of Criminal Appeal for New South Wales

## Material Facts

- **The Attempted Escape:** The appellant (Rogers) was caught attempting to escape from a correctional centre.
- **The Threat:** Rogers had a dangerous reputation as an informer ("dog") and had been attacked in prison previously. He attempted to escape because he received threats that a major attack on his life was going to occur over the weekend.
- **The Refusal of Protection:** Various protective custody systems were available to him within the prison network. However, Rogers consistently refused to go on protection because of personal preferences, a dislike of the other inmates in protection, and a fear that entering protection would confirm the informer rumours.
- **The Legal Battle:** The trial judge ruled that the defense of necessity was not available to Rogers and took the issue away from the jury. Rogers appealed, arguing the jury should have been allowed to consider his defense.

## Issues

Can a prisoner rely on the defense of necessity to excuse an escape from custody if they have the option to seek safety through the prison’s internal protective custody systems?

## Rule/Law

- **Necessity Can Excuse Escape:** In highly limited, extreme circumstances, the common law allows the defense of necessity to excuse escaping from lawful custody.
- **The Three-Part Test:** The defense of necessity requires:
    1. **Irreparable Evil:** The act must be done strictly to avoid an irreparable evil, such as death or serious injury.
    2. **Imminent Peril:** The accused must honestly believe on reasonable grounds that they are in imminent peril.
    3. **No Lawful Alternative:** The criminal act must be proportional to the threat, meaning the accused was afforded **no reasonable opportunity for an alternative, lawful course of action**.

## Application/Analysis

- **Real Threat of Death:** Rogers established the first element, as he was facing a genuine threat to his life.
- **A Lawful Option Existed:** Rogers failed the crucial third element because he had a clear, lawful alternative: reporting the threat to prison authorities and entering protective segregation.
- **Preference is Not Necessity:** While entering protective custody involves isolation and hardship, the law does not allow a prisoner to choose between the discomfort of protection and the illegality of escaping. Because a viable protective system was available, escape was not his only reasonable alternative.

## Conclusion

The trial judge was correct to take the necessity defense away from the jury. The appeal against conviction was dismissed.

### Judges Rules

- **The "No Lawful Alternative" Mandate:** You can never claim legal necessity if there was any reasonable, lawful path available to avoid the threatened danger.
- **Hardship Does Not Justify Crime:** A lawful alternative does not have to be comfortable or desirable. The fact that a lawful safety option (like protective custody or police protection) is highly unpleasant does not legally justify committing a crime to avoid it.
- **Strict Controls on Prisons:** Because prison environments are inherently hazardous, courts strictly limit the defense of necessity for escapees to prevent the complete subversion of the penal system.
