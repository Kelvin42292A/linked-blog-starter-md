Court of Criminal Appeal for New South Wales

## Material Facts

- The appellant (a drug addict) procured the 15-year-old victim for a party hosted by a drug dealer.
- In the early hours of the morning, the appellant was called to pick up the victim, who was found moaning and unconscious in the public foyer of a building after taking a heroin overdose.
- Instead of calling for help or leaving her in the public foyer, the appellant put her in a taxi, took her back to a private residence (the "dog shop"), placed her on a bed, and tried unsuccessfully to revive her.
- He delayed calling a doctor for several hours because he mistakenly thought she would be alright once she slept off the drugs. By the time a doctor was finally called, she was dead.
- At trial, the medical evidence regarding the exact time of her death was highly uncertain, with estimates varying by several hours.
- The appellant was convicted of manslaughter by criminal negligence (by omission) and appealed the conviction.

## Issues

1. **Duty of Care:** Did the appellant owe a legally recognized duty of care to seek medical help for the victim after moving her from a public place to a private room?
2. **Criminal Negligence:** Did the appellant's failure to call a doctor immediately amount to the "wicked" or gross criminal negligence required for manslaughter?
3. **Causation:** Because the exact time of death was unknown, could it be proven beyond a reasonable doubt that the appellant's delay in getting medical help actually caused or accelerated her death?

## Rule/Law

- **Elements of Manslaughter by Omission (_Nydam v The Queen_):** To prove manslaughter by criminal negligence, the Crown must prove three things: (1) the accused owed a legal duty of care, (2) their omission caused the death, and (3) their omission fell so far short of the standard of care of a reasonable person that it merits criminal punishment.
- **Assuming a Duty of Care (_Jones v United States of America_ & _R v Stone & Dobinson_):** The law recognizes a duty of care in several specific situations. Relevant to this case, a legal duty arises if a person voluntarily assumes the care of a helpless stranger **and** secludes them in a way that prevents others from rendering or obtaining aid.
- **The Standard of "Wicked" Negligence (_R v Nicholls_ & _Andrews v DPP_):** Mere carelessness or a simple mistake is not enough for a criminal conviction. If a duty of care is owed, the failure to get medical help must amount to such a high degree of negligence or recklessness that it can be called "wicked".

## Application/Analysis

- **He Assumed the Duty:** The Court found that the appellant _did_ owe a duty of care to the victim. By taking her unconscious body from a public foyer (where others might have found and helped her) and secluding her in a private room, he voluntarily assumed her care and deprived her of help from others.
- **No Gross Negligence:** Despite owing a duty, his actions were not criminally negligent. He made physical efforts to revive her and genuinely believed she would wake up. Because he lacked medical knowledge and didn't realize the dose was fatal, his behavior did not reach the level of "wicked" or gross negligence.
- **Uncertain Causation:** Because the medical experts could not confidently state when she died, the jury could not safely conclude that the appellant actually had enough time to save her life even if he had called a doctor sooner.

## Conclusion

The appeal was **allowed**, and the conviction was **quashed.** The Court entered a verdict of acquittal because, although the appellant owed a duty of care, the Crown failed to prove gross criminal negligence or that his delay was the actual cause of death.
