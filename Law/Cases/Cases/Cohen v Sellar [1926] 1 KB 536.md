King’s Bench Divison (UK)

## Material Facts

- The plaintiff (Cissie Cohen) and the defendant (Nathan Sellar) agreed to marry in August 1923.
- In December 1923, the defendant gave the plaintiff a diamond engagement ring worth £30. It was given solely in contemplation of marriage.
- The couple had quick tempers, fought frequently, and eventually separated in December 1924.
- A jury determined as a matter of fact that it was the **defendant (the man)** who refused to carry out the promise to marry, and he had no legal justification for breaking it off.
- The defendant counter-claimed in court, demanding that the plaintiff give the engagement ring back.

## Issues

1. **Ownership of the Ring:** If an engagement ring is given as a conditional gift in contemplation of marriage, what happens if the engagement is broken off?
2. **The Defaulting Party:** Can a man who breaks off an engagement without legal justification demand the return of the ring?

## Rule/Law

The judge laid out clear, strict rules regarding engagement rings (conditional gifts):

- **If the woman breaks it off:** She must return the engagement ring.
- **If the man breaks it off:** If he refuses to carry out his promise without recognized legal justification, he **cannot** demand the return of the ring.
- **Mutual Consent:** If the engagement is dissolved by mutual consent, the ring must be returned.
- **Death/Disability:** If the marriage doesn't take place because of the death or recognized legal disability of the giver, the ring must be returned.
- **Marriage Happens:** If the marriage takes place, the ring becomes the absolute property of the recipient.

## Application/Analysis

- **No Advantage from Your Own Fault:** The court ruled that the law of marriage promises shares characteristics with commercial contracts. It is a fundamental principle of law that "no one in such a case can take advantage of the existence of a state of things which he himself produced".
- **Losing the Deposit:** An engagement ring acts similarly to a pledge or deposit to bind a bargain. A person who wrongfully refuses to carry out a bargain loses their deposit.
- Because the jury found that the defendant (Sellar) was the one who broke the engagement without a valid excuse, he essentially caused the "self-induced frustration" of the marriage, and therefore forfeited his right to ask for the ring back.

## Conclusion

Judgment was given for the **plaintiff (Miss Cohen)** on both the claim and the counter-claim. She was legally entitled to keep the engagement ring, and was also awarded damages for the breach of promise.
