Supreme court of New South Wales (Court of Criminal Appeal)

## Material Facts

- The appellant (Foster) and a man named Jones went to the house of the deceased (Quinn), who was a drug supplier, to complain about the poor quality of amphetamines they had recently purchased and to "negotiate" a replacement.
- The appellant gave Jones a knife concealed in a jumper, which she claimed was strictly for "protection" in case the deceased became violent.
- In the kitchen, a fight broke out. Jones took the amphetamines and stabbed the deceased three times, causing a fatal wound.
- According to the appellant's version of events, Jones had already taken physical possession of the amphetamines _before_ the deceased lunged at him and _before_ Jones produced the knife.
- The appellant was convicted of murder. The Crown's case was based on both "common purpose" murder and "felony murder" (based on an underlying armed robbery or attempted armed robbery).

## Issues

- For the offence of robbery (and therefore felony murder based on robbery), can the violence or threat of violence occur _after_ the property has already been taken?
- Did the trial judge err in directing the jury that an attempted armed robbery is established merely if the knife was used "in connection with" the attempt to take the drugs?

## Rule/Law

- **The Element of Robbery:** The fundamental essence of robbery is that the violence or threat of violence must _cause_ the victim to part with the property.
- **_Smith v Desmond_ & _Regina v Emery_ (Followed):** It is strictly insufficient to establish robbery if the violence is inflicted, or the threat of violence is made, _after_ the property has already been taken. Both elements (the threat/force and the taking) must coincide.
- **s 18 _Crimes Act 1900_ (NSW):** For felony murder based on an _attempted_ felony, the act causing death must be done "in" the attempt to commit the felony.

## Application/Analysis

- **The Temporal Defect:** The trial judge made a critical error by refusing to direct the jury on the necessary "temporal connection" between the violence and the taking. The judge told the jury it was enough if the stabbing occurred "in connection with" the attempt to steal.
- **Larceny vs. Robbery:** The Court of Criminal Appeal held that if the jury believed the appellant's version—that Jones simply snatched the drugs first and only pulled the knife later when the deceased attacked him—then in law, there was no robbery. That scenario is simply a completed larceny (stealing) followed by a separate act of violence, not an armed robbery.
- **Misapplying Precedent:** The trial judge incorrectly relied on _Ryan v The Queen_ to avoid what he thought were "artificial" distinctions about fragments of time. The appellate court clarified that _Ryan_ did not overrule the long-standing law that a robbery strictly requires the victim to be compelled by force or fear to submit to the theft.

_(Note on Jury Procedure):_ The Court also heavily criticized the trial judge for placing the jury foreperson on oath and isolating her to ask about the jury's difficulties reaching a verdict, as this virtually imposed the judge's own view of the problem onto the jury's private deliberations.

## Conclusion

The appeal was **allowed** and the conviction was **quashed**. dBecause the misdirection denied the appellant a chance of acquittal on the felony murder basis, the Court ordered a **new trial** on the charge of murder.
