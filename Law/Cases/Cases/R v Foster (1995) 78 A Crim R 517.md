Supreme court of New South Wales (Court of Criminal Appeal)

## Material Facts

- The appellant, Shannon Elizabeth Foster, and Peter Edward Jones went to the home of the deceased, Glenn Quinn, at Condell Park on 28 September 1992
- Foster was a regular purchaser of amphetamines from Quinn. She believed she had received poor quality drugs and wanted a replacement supply for free or at a reduced price
- Jones was carrying a knife (supplied by Foster and concealed in a jumper) and a bag for coins
- While Foster was in the lounge room, Jones went into the kitchen with Quinn. Foster later entered the kitchen
- A fight broke out in the kitchen. Jones stabbed Quinn three times, one wound proving fatal
- Foster and Jones then left the house

## Issues

1. Whether the Crown proved beyond reasonable doubt that Peter Jones stabbed Glenn Quinn to death.
2. Whether the Crown proved beyond reasonable doubt that there was a common purpose between the appellant and Peter Jones to rob Glenn Quinn.
3. Whether the Crown proved beyond reasonable doubt that, during the course of that armed robbery (or attempted armed robbery), Peter Jones stabbed Glenn Quinn with the intention of killing him or causing him really serious bodily harm.
4. Whether the Crown proved beyond reasonable doubt that the appellant contemplated that Peter Jones might use the knife with the intention of killing Quinn or causing him really serious bodily harm

## Rule/Law

- Felony Murder (under s 18 of the Crimes Act 1900 (NSW)) occurs when death is caused by an act done in an attempt to commit a felony (here, armed robbery).
- **Robbery** requires that **violence or the threat of violent** must cause the victim to part with the property. It is not sufficient if the violence or threat occurs after the property has already been taken (_Smith v Desmond_ [1965] AC 960; _R v Emery_ (1975) 11 SASR 169 followed).
- For **armed robbery** (or an attempt to commit it), the offender must be armed with an offensive weapon, and there must be a threat or use of force at the time the property is taken (or during the attempt)
- In **common purpose murder,** the Crown must prove the accused contemplated that the co-offender might use the weapon with intent to kill or cause really serious bodily harm
- Intention is not limited to a specific moment of decision. It can be inferred from the carrying and use of weapon in the circumstances (Ryan v The Queen (1967) 121 CLR 205).

## Application/Analysis

There was no evidence that Jones produced or threatened to use the knife **before or at the time** he took the amphetamines. On the appellant’s version of events, Jones had already taken the drugs when the deceased lunged at him, and only then did the knife come into play.

Applying the rule from _Smith v Desmond_ and _R v Emery_:

- The violence or threat of violence must **cause** the victim to part with the property.
- Here, the taking of the drugs was complete before any threat or use of the knife occurred.
- Therefore, the elements of **armed robbery** (or attempted armed robbery) were not satisfied.

As a result, the Crown could not prove felony murder. The trial judge’s failure to clearly direct the jury on this temporal requirement was a significant error.

## Conclusion

Conviction squashed, the crown failed to prove armed robbery or attempted armed robbery.
