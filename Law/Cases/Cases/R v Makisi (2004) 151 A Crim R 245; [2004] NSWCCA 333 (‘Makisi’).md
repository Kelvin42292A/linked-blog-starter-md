## Material Facts

Manuel Makisi and a group of men assaulted and robbed three people. Makisi, who was moderately drunk, claimed he was too intoxicated to remember much. At trial, the judge instructed the jury on intoxication for the main charge, telling them to decide whether Makisi had the **"capacity to act intentionally"** and that if he lacked this capacity, he could not have acted intentionally. Makisi was convicted and appealed, arguing the judge confused the jury by focusing on his _capacity_ to form intent rather than whether he _actually_ formed it.

## Issues

Did the judge's instruction about the "capacity" to form intent wrongly distract the jury from deciding whether the accused **actually** intended to rob the victim?

## Rule/Law

- **Actual Intent is the Key:** The prosecution must always prove beyond reasonable doubt that the accused **actually formed** the specific intent to commit the crime—proving they merely had the _capacity_ to form it is not enough.
- **The "Capacity" Warning:** While judges should avoid using the word "capacity" because it is unnecessary and confusing, mentioning it is not a fatal error if the rest of the instructions make it absolutely clear that actual intent is what must be proven.

## Application/Analysis

While the trial judge's reference to "capacity" was unnecessary, he had already used the word "intent" or "intention" multiple times in his summing-up. Read in context, the jury would have clearly understood that their job was to decide if the prosecution proved Makisi actually intended to rob the victim.

## Conclusion

The instruction was logically correct and did not mislead the jury. The appeal against his conviction was dismissed.

### Judges Rules

- **Rule 1: The "Actual Intent" vs. "Capacity" Rule**  
    When a court is looking at self-induced intoxication for specific intent crimes, **the legal question is always whether the defendant _actually_ formed the intention to commit the crime, not just whether they had the _capacity_ to do so**. Even if a drunk person has the mental capacity to form an intent, the prosecution must still prove they actually did form it.
- **Rule 2: Summing-Up Context Rule**  
    A trial judge's instructions to a jury must be looked at as a whole. A technically confusing word or phrasing (like "capacity") will not overturn a conviction if the rest of the summing-up clearly and correctly explains the burden of proof and the elements of the crime.
