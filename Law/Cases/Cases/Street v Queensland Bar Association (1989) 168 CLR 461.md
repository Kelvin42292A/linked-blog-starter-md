## Material Facts

Mr. Street, a New South Wales barrister, was refused admission to the Queensland Bar because he did not intend to live in Queensland or abandon his NSW practice. Queensland's admission rules were later amended to require applicants to show an intention to practise "principally" in Queensland, which he also could not satisfy.

## Issues

Did these Queensland bar admission rules subject Mr. Street to an unconstitutional "disability or discrimination" under Section 117 of the Constitution?

## Rule/Law

Under **Section 117**, an out-of-state resident is protected from any legal disability or discrimination—including indirect or factual discrimination—that would not apply to them if they resided in that State. This case overruled _Henry v Boehm_ to establish that Section 117 protects against the real-world, substantive impact of discriminatory rules, rather than just their formal legal wording.

## Application/Analysis

The Queensland rules placed a heavy, unfair burden on Mr. Street solely because he lived in NSW. If he had lived in Queensland, he would not have been forced to give up his home or his interstate practice to work. There was no genuine professional or ethical justification for this barrier.

## Conclusion

The High Court allowed the appeal and declared both the old and amended "principal practice" rules inapplicable to Mr. Street.

### Judges Rules

- **The Substantive Equality Rule:** **Section 117 protects Australian citizens from both direct and indirect discrimination based on their State of residence**.
- **The Factual Impact Test:** A court must look at the **real-world, practical effect** of a rule on an out-of-state resident rather than just its formal text. Even if a rule is worded neutrally (such as requiring "principal practice" in a State), it is unconstitutional if its factual impact is to force an out-of-state resident to give up their home or work.
