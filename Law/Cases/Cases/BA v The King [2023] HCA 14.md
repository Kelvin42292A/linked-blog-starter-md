High Court of Australia

## Material Facts

- The appellant (BA) and his former partner (the complainant) were co-tenants of an apartment under a residential tenancy agreement,.
- Following a relationship breakdown, the appellant moved out of the apartment, took most of his belongings, and ceased paying rent, but legally remained a co-tenant on the lease,.
- On 8 July 2019, the appellant arrived at the apartment and demanded entry, but the complainant refused to let him in,.
- The appellant violently kicked open the locked door, shattering the doorframe, and then physically assaulted the complainant and smashed her phone,.
- He was charged with breaking and entering a dwelling-house and committing a serious indictable offence under **s 112(2)** of the **_Crimes Act 1900_ (NSW)**,.
- The trial judge directed an acquittal, reasoning that as a legal tenant, the appellant had a right to enter,. The Court of Criminal Appeal initially overturned this, ruling the entry was without the occupant's consent,.

## Issues

- For the purposes of the statutory offence of break and enter, must the "breaking" involve a **trespass** (i.e., an entry without lawful authority)?,.
- Does a co-tenant lose their lawful authority to enter if they move out, the current occupant refuses consent, or if they enter with the purpose of committing a crime rather than residing?,.

## Rule/Law

- **s 112 _Crimes Act 1900_ (NSW):** The offence of breaking and entering any dwelling-house and committing a serious indictable offence,.
- **The Element of "Breaking":** At common law and under the statute, a "break" must be a "break in law," meaning the entry must constitute a **trespass**,. A person who possesses lawful authority or a right to immediate possession of the premises cannot commit a trespass, and therefore cannot legally "break" into it,.
- **_Residential Tenancies Act 2010_ (NSW):** A residential tenancy agreement grants a "right of occupation" which equates to a right of **exclusive possession** (a lease at common law),.

## Application/Analysis

- **The Trespass Requirement:** The High Court affirmed that to commit a break and enter, the accused must be a trespasser,. Because the appellant was still legally a co-tenant on the active residential tenancy agreement, he possessed a legal right of exclusive possession and a corresponding lawful liberty to enter the apartment. Therefore, he was not a trespasser.
- **Irrelevance of Consent:** The complainant's refusal to let him in did not legally revoke his right to enter. A co-tenant's legal liberty to enter leased premises is derived from the tenancy agreement, not from the consent of the other occupants.
- **Irrelevance of Purpose or Damage:** The Crown argued the appellant lost his right to enter because he entered to commit an assault (not for a "residential purpose") and because he intentionally damaged the door. The High Court rejected this. A tenant's liberty to enter is not strictly conditional on their subjective intentions or on strict compliance with the rule not to damage the property. While causing damage breaches the lease and can lead to eviction, it does not retroactively make the entry a trespass.

_(Note: The appellant did plead guilty to common assault, intimidation, and destruction of property, but he could not be legally convicted of the specific crime of "break and enter")._

## Conclusion

The appeal was **allowed**. The High Court ruled that because the appellant was a co-tenant, he had lawful authority to enter the premises. As he was not a trespasser, he did not "break" into the apartment as a matter of law. The original verdict of **acquittal** for the s 112 charge was restored.
