High Court of Australia

## Material Facts

- **The Plan:** Four men agreed to break into a "grow-house" to steal a commercial crop of cannabis. The house was guarded by a resident.
- **The Killing:** During the break-in, the guard was violently beaten and died from blows to his head. The prosecution could not identify which of the five men physically struck the fatal blow.
- **The Conviction:** The prosecution relied on combining **extended joint criminal enterprise (EJCE)** (which relies on what an accomplice foresaw) with **constructive murder** (causing death during a major crime). The men were convicted of murder.

## Issues

- Can the common law doctrine of extended joint criminal enterprise (based on subjective foresight of a possibility) be combined with statutory constructive murder to convict a participant of murder?
- What must a secondary participant foresee to be convicted of common law murder under EJCE?

## Rule/Law

- **No "Double Deeming"**: The doctrine of extended joint criminal enterprise **cannot** be combined with statutory constructive murder. There is no legal doctrine of "constructive, constructive murder".
- **Constructive Murder Limit:** For a secondary participant to be liable for constructive murder, the intentional act of violence must fall **within the scope of their actual agreement** (Joint Criminal Enterprise).
- **Strict EJCE Requirements:** To convict an accomplice of common law murder under EJCE, the prosecution must prove they subjectively foresaw **both** that their partner might act with murderous intent _and_ that their partner's acts might cause really serious injury or death.

## Application/Analysis

- **Creating an Unlawful Fiction:** Combining EJCE (foresight of a risk) with constructive murder (where no intent to kill is required) is an impermissible expansion of liability. It would unfairly convict an accomplice of murder based on the mere foresight of any minor act of violence (like a minor slap or kick).
- **Wrong Instructions:** The trial judge erred by telling the jury they could convict if the appellants foresaw any minor act of violence (like striking a leg). The judge also failed to instruct them that EJCE murder strictly requires foresight of the actual consequence of death or really serious injury.

## Conclusion

The High Court of Australia **allowed the appeals, quashed the convictions, and ordered a new trial**.

### Judges Rules

- **No "Constructive, Constructive Murder":** You cannot combine extended joint criminal enterprise (foresight of a risk) with constructive murder (death caused during a major crime). Complicity in constructive murder requires that the act of violence was **part of your actual agreement**, not just something you foresaw.
- **The Agreement Must Include Violence:** To convict an accomplice of constructive murder under a standard joint criminal enterprise, the prosecution must prove your joint agreement (express or unspoken) **included the possible commission of an intentional act of violence** of the same general nature as the one that caused death.
- **EJCE Requires Foresight of the Result:** To be convicted of common law murder under extended joint criminal enterprise, you must subjectively foresee **both** that your partner might act with murderous intent _and_ that their acts might actually cause death or really serious bodily injury.
