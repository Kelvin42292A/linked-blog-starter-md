### Material Facts

Veen, a 20-year-old, stabbed a man to death after a dispute over payment. The jury acquitted him of murder and convicted him of manslaughter because they accepted his plea of diminished responsibility (due to suspected brain damage). However, the trial judge still sentenced Veen to life imprisonment to protect the community from his violent urges.

### Issues

Can a judge sentence an offender to life imprisonment solely to protect the public, even if that sentence is disproportionate to the actual crime?

### Rule/Law

Punishment must always be proportionate to the gravity of the crime committed. Protecting the community is an important factor, but it does not justify extending a sentence beyond what the offender deserves for that specific offence.

### Application/Analysis

The life sentence was a form of preventive detention. It ignored Veen's diminished responsibility and sacrificed the rule of proportionality by punishing him for future danger rather than his actual guilt.

### Conclusion

The High Court allowed the appeal, quashed the life sentence, and imposed a fixed 12-year prison sentence instead.

#### Judges Rules

Rule 1: Proportionality in Sentencing

A court cannot increase a sentence beyond what is fair for the offence committed just because the offender is dangerous or mentally ill. Criminal punishment must fit the actual guilt of the crime, not future predicted danger.

Rule 2: Questioning the Jury on Verdicts

When a jury returns a manslaughter verdict where both provocation and diminished responsibility were raised, the trial judge should ask the jury foreman to clarify the exact grounds of their verdict. This ensures the judge sentences the offender on the correct factual basis.
