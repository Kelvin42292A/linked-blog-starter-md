## Material Facts

Gregory Wayne Kable was convicted of the manslaughter of his wife. Near his release date, the New South Wales Parliament passed the _Community Protection Act 1994_ (NSW), which applied only to him. The Act allowed the Supreme Court to order Kable's preventive detention in prison for up to six months at a time if satisfied he was more likely than not to commit a serious act of violence. Under this Act, the Supreme Court ordered Kable to be detained.

## Issues

Can a State Parliament pass a law that gives a State court a function (such as detaining a single named individual without a trial) that is incompatible with the court's role under Chapter III of the Commonwealth Constitution?

## Rule/Law

The Commonwealth Constitution establishes an integrated Australian judicial system where State courts can exercise federal judicial power. Therefore, State Parliaments cannot pass laws that confer powers or functions on State courts that are incompatible with, or repugnant to, the institutional integrity, independence, and impartiality of those courts as potential repositories of federal judicial power.

## Application/Analysis

The Act singled out Kable for imprisonment without a standard criminal trial or a finding of criminal guilt. It forced the Supreme Court to predict his future dangerousness using relaxed civil standards of proof and evidence. This compromised the court's institutional impartiality, making it appear to be an instrument of executive policy and undermining public confidence in the judicial process.

## Conclusion

The High Court held the Act was invalid because it violated Chapter III of the Constitution. The appeal was allowed.

### Judges Rules

 Under Chapter III of the Australian Constitution, **a State Parliament cannot pass a law that undermines the institutional integrity of a State court** or forces it to act in a way that is incompatible with its constitutional role as an independent, impartial repository of federal judicial power.
