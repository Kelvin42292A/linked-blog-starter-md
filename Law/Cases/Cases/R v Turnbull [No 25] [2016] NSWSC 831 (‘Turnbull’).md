Supreme Court of New South Wales

## Material Facts

- **The Conflict:** Ian Robert Turnbull and his family were under intensive, multi-year regulatory investigations by the Office of Environment and Heritage (OEH) for illegal land clearing on their properties.
- **The Surveillance:** Glendon Turner, an OEH compliance officer, had been monitoring the properties using ground-level inspections, photographs, and aerial surveillance flights.
- **The Killing:** On 29 July 2014, Turnbull spotted Turner’s vehicle parked on a public lane near his property conducting surveillance. Turnbull claimed "something snapped", retrieved his rifle, and shot Turner to death.
- **The Defense:** At his murder trial, Turnbull argued he acted under **extreme provocation**. He claimed Turner's persistent, hostile regulatory actions and surveillance flights over two years amounted to the serious crime of criminal stalking or harassment.
- **The Application:** At the close of evidence, Turnbull’s legal team asked the judge to leave the partial defense of extreme provocation to the jury.

## Issues

Can the lawful regulatory investigations and surveillance of a public compliance officer constitute a "serious indictable offence" to satisfy the legal threshold of **conduct amounting to extreme provocation**?

## Rule/Law

- **The "Serious Indictable Offence" Threshold:** Under Section 23 of the _Crimes Act 1900_ (NSW) (as amended in 2014), the partial defense of extreme provocation can only be left to a jury if the deceased's provocative conduct was a **serious indictable offence**.
- **The Lawful Duty Restriction:** Legitimate actions taken by public officers carrying out their lawful compliance, enforcement, or investigative duties cannot constitute a criminal offence (like stalking or harassment).
- **Exceeding Lawful Bounds:** A public officer's conduct can only constitute a serious indictable offence if it falls **completely outside** the lawful scope of their public duties. Being rude, overzealous, or annoying is not enough.

## Application/Analysis

- **Legitimate Duties:** Turner's inspections, surveillance flights, and land monitoring were standard, lawful methods used by the OEH to gather objective evidence of illegal clearing.
- **Subjective Stress vs. Objective Crime:** While Turner's investigations naturally caused the Turnbull family to feel stressed, harassed, and apprehensive, Turner was acting strictly within his legal authority.
- **Element Failed:** Because Turner's actions were lawful, his conduct could not legally be characterized as a criminal offence of stalking or harassment. Therefore, the defense failed to prove the absolute prerequisite that the deceased committed a serious indictable offence.

## Conclusion

Johnson J refused the application, ruling that the partial defense of extreme provocation **should not be left to the jury**

### Judges Rules

- **Must Be a Serious Crime:** Unlike ordinary provocation, "extreme provocation" has an exceptionally high threshold: the deceased's provocative behavior must actually constitute a **serious indictable offence** (an offence carrying a heavy prison sentence).
- **Lawful Public Duties Are Excluded:** Legal compliance, investigation, or law enforcement actions carried out by public officials can **never** constitute extreme provocation—even if those actions make an accused feel intensely harassed, pressured, or anxious.
- **Abuse of Office Exception:** For a public official’s actions to constitute a serious indictable offence, their behavior must fall **entirely outside** the lawful performance of their public functions. Simply being rude, abrupt, or overzealous is not enough.
- **Subjective Perception is Insufficient:** If the deceased did not actually commit a serious indictable crime, the defense is completely unavailable—regardless of whether the accused honestly (but mistakenly) believed the deceased was acting illegally or maliciously.
