### Material

- The applicant (Ryan) decided to rob a service station using a sawn-off .22 rifle.
- He entered the station, presented the loaded and cocked rifle (with the safety catch off), and demanded money from the attendant (Taylor).
- After Taylor handed over the money, Ryan told him to turn around to be tied up. While Ryan reached for a cord with one hand, keeping his finger on the trigger of the gun with his other hand, Taylor suddenly crouched and swung around.
- Startled by the sudden movement, Ryan stepped back and the gun discharged, killing Taylor instantly.
- Ryan claimed he did not intend to shoot; he argued the gun went off by "accident" as an involuntary "reflex action" to being startled,,.

### Issue

- Is a "reflex action" caused by being startled considered an "involuntary" act that absolves a person of criminal responsibility?
- For the offence of armed robbery with wounding (**s 98**), does the prosecution need to prove the accused had a _specific intent_ to wound?
- Does an accidental or unintended discharge of a firearm during an armed robbery constitute murder under the felony-murder rule (**s 18**)?

### Rule

- **s 18(1)(a) _Crimes Act 1900_ (NSW):** Defines murder. It includes the "felony-murder" rule: a homicide is murder if the act causing death was done during or immediately after a crime punishable by penal servitude for life.
- **s 98 _Crimes Act 1900_ (NSW):** The offence of armed robbery with wounding. It is punishable by penal servitude for life.
- **The "Voluntary Act" Requirement:** To be guilty of a crime, the act causing death must be voluntary (a willed act). However, an act is **not** involuntary merely because the mind worked quickly or impulsively,. True involuntary acts are akin to sleepwalking or epileptic seizures.
- **No Specific Intent for Wounding:** The offence under s 98 requires the wounding to be caused by a voluntary act, but it does **not** require a specific intent to wound.

### Application

- **The Act was Voluntary:** The High Court rejected the argument that Ryan's "reflex action" was involuntary. Ryan consciously created a highly dangerous situation: holding a cocked, loaded gun with his finger on the trigger, pointed at a man he was threatening. Pressing the trigger in a spontaneous, startled response to the victim's movement is a foreseeable consequence, and legally remains a conscious, voluntary act.
- **Satisfying s 98 (Felony Murder):** Because Ryan's voluntary act (pressing the trigger) wounded Taylor while Ryan was committing an armed robbery, the elements of **s 98** were entirely satisfied, even though he didn't specifically intend to inflict the wound.
- **Resulting in Murder:** Because the death was caused during the commission of a crime punishable by life (s 98), the statutory definition of murder under **s 18(1)(a)** was automatically triggered. It did not matter whether the killing itself was "accidental" or unintended; committing an armed robbery with a loaded gun is done at the peril of committing murder if it goes off.

### Conclusion

The application for special leave to appeal was **dismissed**. The High Court ruled the trial judge's directions were adequate and upheld the original conviction for **murder**,.
