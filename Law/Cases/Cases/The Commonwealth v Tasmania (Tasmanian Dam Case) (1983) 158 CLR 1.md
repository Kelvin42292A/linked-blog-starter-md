### Material Facts

The Tasmanian government wanted to build a large hydro-electric dam in a wilderness area. To stop it, the Commonwealth government passed heritage laws and regulations using its international obligations under the World Heritage Convention. Tasmania argued that the Commonwealth had no power to control state lands or interfere with state projects.

### Issues

Did the Commonwealth have the constitutional power under the external affairs power (s 51(xxix)), the corporations power (s 51(xx)), and the race power (s 51(xxvi)) to stop the State from building the dam?

### Rule/Law

External Affairs (s 51(xxix)): The Commonwealth can pass laws to implement obligations under a genuine international treaty, even if the subject matter is purely domestic.

Corporations Power (s 51(xx)): The Commonwealth can regulate the activities of trading corporations, including acts done for the purposes of their trading activities.

Race Power (s 51(xxvi)): The Commonwealth can make special laws to protect the cultural and spiritual heritage of a specific race, such as Aboriginal heritage sites.

### Application/Analysis

The wilderness area was listed on the World Heritage List, creating a binding international obligation under the treaty to protect it.

The Tasmanian Hydro-Electric Commission (HEC) is legally a trading corporation because it sells electricity on a massive scale. The dam was being built for its trading activities.

The caves in the area held deep archaeological and historical significance for the Aboriginal people.

### Conclusion

The High Court held that the key Commonwealth laws were valid. Tasmania could not lawfully build the dam without federal consent.

#### Judges Rules

Rule 1: The Treaty Implementation Rule (External Affairs) The Commonwealth's power to make laws under s 51(xxix) is activated whenever Australia enters into a genuine international treaty. This allows the federal government to regulate domestic matters (like environmental protection) if required by the treaty.

Rule 2: The Government-Owned Trading Corporation Rule A government-owned public authority is still classified as a "trading corporation" if its trading activities (such as selling electricity) form a significant part of its overall business. The Commonwealth can regulate any of its activities done for the purpose of trade.

Rule 3: The Cultural Heritage Rule (Race Power) The power to make special laws for the people of a race includes the power to protect physical properties, sites, and relics that hold unique cultural or spiritual significance for them.
