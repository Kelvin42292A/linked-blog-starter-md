Supreme Court of New South Wales

## Material Facts

- **The Parties and Charge:** Bonjon, a Wadora man, was charged with the shooting murder of Yammowing, a member of the Colijon people, at Geelong. Both parties were Aboriginal.
- **The Jurisdictional Objection:** Mr. Redmond Barry, acting as defense counsel, argued that because New South Wales was acquired by British settlement (occupation) rather than conquest or treaty, the Crown acquired a right to the soil but did not obtain legal authority to govern the internal affairs of the Indigenous population.
- **Lack of Consent:** The defense emphasized that there was no treaty, statute, or agreement demonstrating that the Aboriginal people had ever consented to or submitted to British authority.

## Issues

1. Is a single judge of the Supreme Court of New South Wales at Port Phillip bound by the earlier Full Court decision in _R v Murrell_ (1836)?
2. Does the colonial court have criminal jurisdiction over crimes committed by Aboriginal people against one another (_inter se_)?

## Rule/Law

- **Precedent Flexibility:** A single judge of the Supreme Court is not legally bound by the decision in _R v Murrell_.
- **Status of Indigenous Peoples:** In a colony acquired without conquest or treaty, the indigenous inhabitants are regarded as **distinct though dependent allies, not British subjects**.
- **The Requirement of Consent:** For the Crown to exercise absolute criminal jurisdiction over the internal disputes of an Indigenous population, there must be an **express legislative enactment or a treaty** proving their submission to British law.
- **Preservation of Customary Law:** In the absence of a treaty or statute abolishing their traditional customs, Indigenous people retain the legal right to govern themselves under their own traditional laws and usages.

## Application/Analysis

- **Nature of Acquisition:** New South Wales was neither acquired by conquest or treaty, nor was it unoccupied when British settlement began. Therefore, the British did not automatically sweep away pre-existing native societies.
- **No Universal Subjection:** While English law applies to protect and govern conflicts _between_ British colonists and Aboriginal people, it does not automatically extend to cover private disputes confined strictly among the native population.
- **Colonial Precedents:** Historically, the British Crown has recognized the self-governing status of other traditional groups (such as the Maroons in Jamaica and the Charibs in St. Vincent) through treaties, proving that dependent communities can legally govern themselves within British territory.
- **No Extinguishment of Custom:** Since no local or imperial legislation has explicitly abolished Aboriginal laws or declared them subject to the colonial criminal code, their traditional legal systems remain active and valid among themselves.

## Conclusion

The court held that the Supreme Court of New South Wales **has no jurisdiction** over crimes committed by Aboriginal people against one another. Because of the gravity of the question, Willis J reserved his final decision to further consider the matter, strongly doubting his legal authority to try the prisoner. The prisoner subsequently pleaded to the charge subject to this express reservation of jurisdiction.

### Judges Rules

- **No Power Over Internal Disputes:** The colonial Supreme Court **cannot prosecute crimes** committed strictly between Indigenous people.
- **Self-Governing Allies, Not Subjects:** Because the land was already occupied (not empty, conquered, or ceded), Indigenous people are **distinct, self-governing allies** who keep the right to live by their own traditional laws.
- **Consent is Crucial:** English law cannot be forced upon Indigenous people's internal affairs without their **consent, a treaty, or an Act of Parliament**.
- **Settler Conflicts Only:** English law only steps in when there is a **direct conflict between an Indigenous person and a British settler**.
