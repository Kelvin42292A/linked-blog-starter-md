NSW Court of Criminal Appeal

## Material Facts

- The appellant was driving a stolen vehicle in Fairfield at an excessive speed and in an erratic manner.
- The appellant’s car collided with a vehicle traveling in the same direction, crossed double white lines, and collided head-on with a car driven by Mrs. Vongratsavai, who was 38 weeks pregnant.
- Mrs. Vongratsavai was taken to the hospital for an emergency caesarean, and a male infant (Mathew) was delivered in a poor condition (pale and limp).
- **Crucial Medical Facts:** For the two hours before he was pronounced dead, Mathew had a detectable heartbeat and blood circulation. However, he was immediately placed on artificial respiration and **never breathed unassisted**. Additionally, an EEG taken an hour after birth showed a "flat trace," indicating little to no electrical activity in the brain.
- The appellant was charged with several offences, including manslaughter and an alternative count of aggravated dangerous driving causing death.
- He pleaded not guilty and elected for a trial by a judge alone. The judge found him guilty of manslaughter, determining the baby was "born alive," and the appellant is appealing that decision,.

## Issues

1. **The "Born Alive" Rule:** Does a newly delivered infant satisfy the common law "born alive" rule if they exhibit a heartbeat but require mechanical ventilation and never breathe unassisted?
2. **Statutory Definition of Death:** Should the common law "born alive" rule be adapted to align with the _Human Tissue Act 1983_ (NSW), which would require the Crown to prove independent brain function?

## Rule/Law

- **The "Born Alive" Rule:** **_R v Hutty_:** Murder or manslaughter can only be committed on a legally recognized person, meaning the child must have an existence separate from and independent of its mother, fully extruded, and living by virtue of its own organs.
- **The First Limb (Delivery):** **_R v Trilloe_:** The foetus must have completely left its mother’s body, although the umbilical cord does not have to be cut for the child to be considered independent.
- **The Second Limb (Signs of Life):** **_Brock v Kellock_ (Applied):** To satisfy the rule, any clear sign of independent life is sufficient. Proof of a single vital function, such as a **heartbeat**, is enough; there is no legal requirement that the baby must breathe unassisted.
- **Statutory Definition of Death:** **s 33 _Human Tissue Act 1983_ (NSW):** Defines "death" as the irreversible cessation of all brain function or blood circulation. However, this statutory definition of _death_ **does not** alter or adapt the common law definition of _life_ for the purposes of the "born alive" rule.

## Application/Analysis

- **The "Any Sign of Life" Standard:** The Court of Criminal Appeal rejected the argument that a newly delivered baby must breathe unassisted to be legally "born alive". Relying on historical precedents like _Brock v Kellock_, the Court affirmed that _any_ sign of independent life—most notably a beating heart—is legally sufficient. Because the trial judge found as a fact that the infant maintained a heartbeat for two hours after being completely delivered, the legal requirements of the "born alive" rule were fully satisfied.
- **Rejecting the Brain Death Argument:** The Court firmly rejected the appellant's attempt to use the _Human Tissue Act 1983_ to argue that the baby was never alive due to a lack of brain function . The Court warned against the temptation to create "meretricious symmetry" (a superficial or false symmetry) between the statutory definition of _death_ and the common law definition of _life_ .
- **Statutory Purpose vs. Common Law:** The statutory definition of death was created for specific modern medical purposes (like organ transplants) to determine when a living person has died . It was never intended to alter the ancient, common law "born alive" rule, which uses an artificial standard to determine when life legally _begins_. Therefore, the Crown did not need to prove the baby had independent brain function for the manslaughter charge to succeed .

## Conclusion

The appeal was **dismissed**. The Court upheld the manslaughter conviction, ruling that the presence of a heartbeat was sufficient to prove the baby was legally "born alive".
