High Court of Australia

## Material Facts

- Farah Constructions (controlled by Mr. Elias) and Say-Dee entered into a joint venture to purchase and redevelop a property at No 11 Deane Street.
- The local Council rejected the development application because No 11 was too narrow, stating it needed to be amalgamated with adjoining properties to reach its full development potential.
- Mr. Elias's wife, his daughters, and another company he controlled (Lesmint) subsequently purchased the adjoining properties (No 13, No 15, and No 20).
- Say-Dee claimed they were kept in the dark and sued, alleging Farah breached its fiduciary duty by exploiting the Council's information to secretly buy the adjoining properties for Mr. Elias's family.
- Say-Dee argued the wife and daughters should hold their units on constructive trust for the joint venture because they received the property through Mr. Elias's breach of duty.
- The trial judge found that Mr. Elias _did_ invite Say-Dee to participate in buying the other properties, but Say-Dee declined due to financial difficulties. The Court of Appeal wrongly reversed this finding, so the High Court had to step in.

## Issues

1. **Fiduciary Duty:** Did Farah breach its fiduciary duty by keeping the adjoining properties for Mr. Elias's family, or did Say-Dee give informed consent to this?
2. **Knowing Receipt:** Are the wife and daughters liable as third parties under the "first limb" of _Barnes v Addy_ for receiving trust property?
3. **Knowing Assistance:** Are the wife and daughters liable under the "second limb" of _Barnes v Addy_ for assisting in a dishonest and fraudulent design?

## Rule/Law

- **Informed Consent (_Maguire v Makaronis_ (1997)):** A fiduciary cannot exploit opportunities without the informed consent of their partner . "What is required for a fully informed consent is a question of fact in all the circumstances of each case"
- **First Limb of _Barnes v Addy_ (1874) - "Knowing Receipt":** A third party (stranger to the trust) is liable if they "receive and become chargeable with some part of the trust property" with notice of the breach . The High Court clarified that **confidential information or public knowledge (like a Council's zoning attitude) is _not_ considered "trust property"** .
- **Second Limb of _Barnes v Addy_ (1874) - "Knowing Assistance":** A third party is liable if they "assist with knowledge in a dishonest and fraudulent design on the part of the trustees".
- **The Test for Knowledge (_Consul Development Pty Ltd v DPC Estates Pty Ltd_ (1975)):** To have "knowledge" under the second limb, the third party must have actual knowledge, wilfully shut their eyes to the obvious, or know circumstances that would indicate the facts to an honest/reasonable person . Crucially, merely knowing circumstances that would put an honest person _on inquiry_ (constructive notice) is **not** enough to make a third party liable.

## Application/Analysis

- **No Breach of Duty:** The High Court ruled that Farah provided sufficient disclosure to Say-Dee about the Council's requirements and the opportunity to buy the adjoining properties . Because Say-Dee's principals were experienced businesswomen who declined the offer due to financial trouble, their refusal amounted to "informed consent" for Farah to proceed on its own .
- **First Limb Fails (No Property Received):** Say-Dee argued the family "received" the valuable information from the Council . The Court firmly rejected this, ruling that the Council's planning attitude was publicly available information, not a trade secret, and information is generally not "property" . Therefore, the family did not receive any "trust property" . Furthermore, the wife and daughters had no notice of any alleged breach .
- **Second Limb Fails (No Dishonesty or Knowledge):** Because Mr. Elias made adequate disclosures, his conduct was not a "dishonest and fraudulent design" . Even if it was, there was absolutely no evidence that his wife and daughters had any actual knowledge or wilfully shut their eyes to his business dealings to satisfy the _Consul_ test.

## Conclusion

The High Court **allowed the appeal** by Farah and the Elias family. Say-Dee's claims were dismissed. The wife and daughters were completely cleared of liability and were allowed to keep their properties .
