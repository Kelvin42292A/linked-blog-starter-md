High Court of Australia

## Material Facts

- **The Incident:** Daryl Wilson punched a middle-aged, heavily intoxicated man once in the face during a brief street dispute.
- **The Death:** The punch caused the man to fall backward and strike his head on the ground. He later died of brain damage from the impact.
- **The Conviction & Appeal:** Wilson was acquitted of murder but convicted of involuntary manslaughter by an "unlawful and dangerous act". He appealed, arguing that the trial judge failed to properly explain what makes an unlawful act "dangerous".

## Issues

1. **The Dangerousness Standard:** What is the correct legal test to determine if an unlawful act is "dangerous" enough to support a manslaughter conviction?
2. **Battery Manslaughter:** Does the common law recognize a separate crime of "battery manslaughter" (causing death by a deliberate, minor blow meant to hurt but not cause serious injury)?

## Rule/Law

- **The "Appreciable Risk of Serious Injury" Test:** An unlawful act is "dangerous" only if a reasonable person in the accused's position would have realized that the act exposed another person to an **appreciable risk of serious injury**. A risk of "some harm" (minor or trivial injury) is not enough.
- **No Battery Manslaughter:** The common law **does not** recognize "battery manslaughter" as a separate category of involuntary manslaughter. Any unlawful application of force resulting in death must satisfy the objective "dangerousness" test above.

## Application/Analysis

- **Defective Jury Instructions:** The trial judge simply instructed the jury to decide whether Wilson's punch was a "dangerous act," without defining the level of risk or harm required.
- **Risk of Injustice:** Without clear guidance, the jury might have wrongly convicted Wilson on the basis that _any_ punch carrying a minor risk of trivial injury (like a bruised lip) is "dangerous" under the law.
- **A High Standard Needed:** The jury should have been directed to evaluate whether a reasonable person in Wilson's shoes would have foreseen that a single punch carried a real and appreciable risk of **serious injury**.

## Conclusion

The High Court **allowed the appeal**, quashed Wilson's manslaughter conviction, and ordered a new trial

### Judges Rules (Unlawful and dangerous act doctrine)

- **Real Risk of Serious Injury:** To convict someone of manslaughter by an unlawful and dangerous act, the prosecution must prove the act carried a real ("appreciable") risk of **serious injury**, not just minor or trivial harm.
- **Strictly Objective Perspective:** This danger is judged from the perspective of a **reasonable person** in the accused's position.
- **No Exceptions for Minor Blows:** The law does not recognize a separate, lower-threshold crime of "battery manslaughter" for minor punches. A punch must meet the full "serious injury" standard to count as manslaughter.
