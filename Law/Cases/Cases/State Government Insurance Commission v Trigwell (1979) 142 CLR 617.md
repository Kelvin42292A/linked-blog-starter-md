Hight Court of Australia

### Material Facts

- Miss Rooke was driving her car at night on a main highway at about 55 m.p.h.
- She collided with two sheep that were on the road, lost control of her car, veered onto the wrong side of the road, and crashed head-on into the Trigwells' car. Miss Rooke was killed, and the Trigwell family was injured.
- The sheep belonged to the Kerins, who owned the adjoining land. The fences were in poor condition, and the Kerins knew the sheep regularly escaped to graze on the side of the road.
- The Trigwells sued Miss Rooke's insurer (SGIC) for her negligent driving, and also sued the Kerins for negligence and nuisance for letting the sheep stray.
- The trial judge found Miss Rooke was negligent, but held that the Kerins were not liable because of an old English legal rule from a case called _Searle v Wallbank_.

### Issue

1. **Reception of English Law:** Did the old English rule in _Searle v Wallbank_ (which says landowners have no duty to fence their animals out of the highway) become part of the common law of South Australia when the colony was settled in 1836?
2. **Judicial Law Reform:** Even if the rule was received into Australian law, should the High Court abolish or modernize it because fast-moving cars have fundamentally changed the danger of straying animals?
3. **Driver Negligence:** Was Miss Rooke actually driving negligently before she hit the sheep?

### Rule (Searle v Wallbank)

- **The Rule in _Searle v Wallbank_:** The owner of land abutting a highway is under no legal obligation to users of the highway to maintain fences, and owes no duty of care to take reasonable steps to prevent ordinary, non-dangerous animals (like sheep) from straying onto the road.
- **Reception of Common Law:** Settlers carry with them to a new colony only so much of the English common law as is applicable to their own situation and the condition of the infant colony at the time of settlement.
- **The Role of the Court vs. Parliament:** Courts must not act as legislatures. If a settled common law rule needs to be changed because social or economic conditions have changed, it is the job of Parliament—not the judges—to change it, because Parliament can balance the competing interests of farmers and motorists.

### Application

- **The Rule Applies in Australia:** The High Court ruled that the physical conditions in South Australia in 1836 (unenclosed land, straying stock) were not so different from England as to make the rule inapplicable. Therefore, _Searle v Wallbank_ legally became, and remains, the law of South Australia.
- **No Exceptions:** Because sheep are not known to be a vicious or dangerous animal, there were no "special circumstances" to make the Kerins liable. Furthermore, two sheep wandering on a road do not amount to a legal "public nuisance".
- **Leaving it to Parliament:** The Court acknowledged that the rule seems unfair today, but firmly stated that forcing farmers to fence thousands of miles of rural roads would be a massive economic shift. Balancing the rights of rural graziers and modern motorists must be left to Parliament. _(Note: Murphy J strongly dissented, arguing the court should just change the law to fit modern times)._
- **Miss Rooke Was Negligent:** The majority of the Court upheld the finding that Miss Rooke was negligent because she did not reduce her speed enough when she dipped her headlights for oncoming traffic, meaning she could not brake in time when the sheep appeared.

### Conclusion

The appeals were **dismissed**. The High Court ruled that the Kerins (the farmers) are **not liable** for the accident. The SGIC (Miss Rooke's insurer) is **liable** to pay damages to the Trigwells.
