New South Wales Court of Criminal Appeal

## Material Facts

- The complainant (Matthew Bernard Williams), a 19-year-old prisoner, arrived at Mannus Correctional Centre on 14 September 1994.
- On 16 September 1994, the appellant (Garry Raymond Clark) invited the complainant to his cell to watch a movie.
- The complainant alleged the appellant grabbed him from behind, flung him face down onto the bed, and had anal sexual intercourse with him without consent, causing sharp pain. The complainant screamed into the bed.
- The appellant denied the allegation and claimed the sexual intercourse was consensual after the complainant agreed in exchange for protection from other prisoners.
- The appellant was charged with having sexual intercourse with the complainant without consent, knowing he was not consenting.
- The jury returned a verdict of guilty.

## Issues

- Did the judge explain the law about “consent” clearly enough to the jury?
- Did the judge make a mistake by telling the jury they could use the appellant’s lies as proof he was guilty?
- Was the guilty verdict unsafe or unfair overall?

## Rule/Law

- Under **s 61I** of the _Crimes Act 1900_ (NSW), a person is guilty of an offence if they have sexual intercourse with another person without the consent of that person and knowing that the person is not consenting.
- Consent means **free choice**. Consent is **not** submission due to pressure or threats.
- A person who submits to sexual intercourse as a result of threats or terror (even threats from persons other than the accused) is regarded as **not consenting** (s 61R(2)(c)).
- The absence of physical resistance does not, by itself, mean the person consented (s 61R(2)(d)).
- Where lies by the accused are relied upon as evidence of consciousness of guilt, the lies must be clearly identified and capable of supporting such an inference (_Edwards v The Queen_).
- On appeal, the court must independently assess whether the verdict is unsafe and unsatisfactory — that is, whether it was open to the jury to be satisfied beyond reasonable doubt of the appellant’s guilt.

## Application/Analysis

- The trial judge’s summing-up on consent was inadequate. The direction that “consent is free choice” and “consent is not submission due to some pressure” failed to properly explain to the jury how consent could be vitiated when the complainant allegedly agreed to sex in exchange for protection from other prisoners.
- The jury’s question clearly showed they were confused about whether submitting to sex as the “lesser of two evils” amounted to consent or rape. The judge’s further direction did not sufficiently address this issue.
- The direction allowing the jury to use alleged lies by the appellant as evidence of consciousness of guilt was also erroneous. The lies were not clearly established by independent evidence.
- Although there was evidence on which the jury could have convicted the appellant on the complainant’s version, the misdirections on consent and lies resulted in a miscarriage of justice.

## Conclusion

The appeal was allowed. The conviction was quashed and a new trial ordered due to inadequate jury directions on consent.
