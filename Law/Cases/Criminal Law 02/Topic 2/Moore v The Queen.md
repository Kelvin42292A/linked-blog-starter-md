Court of Criminal Appeal for New South Wales

## Material Facts

- The appellant opened a “Complete Freedom” bank account with St George Bank in March 2010.
- The account became overdrawn in June 2010. The bank continued to honour withdrawals and direct debits (including large mortgage payments to RHG Mortgage Corp and later transfers to PayPal), charging a $9 “payment honour fee” for each debit and high interest (approx. 20% p.a.)
- Over time, the negative balance grew to exceed $2.1 million. The appellant used the funds for personal expenditure, including luxury vehicles, a boat, and memorabilia
- The bank issued regular statements showing the overdraft, fees, and interest. The appellant made no misrepresentations to induce further lending
- The appellant was convicted in the District Court of:
    1. Dishonestly obtaining a financial advantage by deception (s 192E(1)(b) Crimes Act 1900 (NSW)) – by causing the bank to continue lending via the overdrawn account
    2. Dealing with proceeds of crime (s 193B(2))

He appealed to the NSW Court of Criminal Appeal, arguing there was no “deception” because the bank was fully aware of the overdraft through its own statements and continued to approve transactions under the account contract.

## Issues

- Whether the appellant engaged in “deception” ([s 192B](https://anzlaw.thomsonreuters.com/Link/Document/FullText?refType=U7&docFamilyGuid=I297ec785d10b11e8a07cfaeacc13e49f&pubNum=1100190&originationContext=document&transitionType=DocumentItem&docVersion=Law+in+Force&ppcid=af65b7b3058343d7aa47f571843bcec9&contextData=\(sc.Default\)) Crimes Act 1900 (NSW)) by using the overdrawn bank account when the bank was aware of the overdraft through regular statements and continued to honour transactions
- Whether the appellant dishonestly obtained a financial advantage by deception, contrary to  [s 192E(1)(b) of the Crimes Act 1900 (NSW)](https://anzlaw.thomsonreuters.com/Link/Document/FullText?refType=U5&docFamilyGuid=I2c81f540d10b11e8a07cfaeacc13e49f&pubNum=1100190&originationContext=document&transitionType=DocumentItem&docVersion=Law+in+Force&ppcid=af65b7b3058343d7aa47f571843bcec9&contextData=\(sc.Default\))
- Whether the jury verdicts were unreasonable or unsupported by the evidence, particularly because the appellant was authorised (even if by the bank’s oversight or error) to operate the account in the manner he did.

## Rule/Law

[Section 192E(1)](https://anzlaw.thomsonreuters.com/Link/Document/FullText?refType=U7&docFamilyGuid=I2c81f540d10b11e8a07cfaeacc13e49f&pubNum=1100190&originationContext=document&transitionType=DocumentItem&docVersion=Law+in+Force&ppcid=af65b7b3058343d7aa47f571843bcec9&contextData=\(sc.Default\))(b) Crimes Act 1900 (NSW) includes a person who, **by any deception,** dishonestly obtains any financial advantage… is guilty of the offence of fraud

[Section 192B](https://anzlaw.thomsonreuters.com/Link/Document/FullText?refType=U7&docFamilyGuid=I297ec785d10b11e8a07cfaeacc13e49f&pubNum=1100190&originationContext=document&transitionType=DocumentItem&docVersion=Law%20in%20Force&ppcid=af65b7b3058343d7aa47f571843bcec9&contextData=\(sc.Default\)&comp=wlau) Crimes Act 1900 (NSW):

Deception means any deception (by words or other conduct) as to fact or as to law, including: (a) a deception as to intentions; or (b) conduct that causes a computer/machine/electronic device to respond in a way the person is not authorised to cause it to make. The deception must be intentional or reckless.

The offence requires **both** “deception” **and** “dishonesty”

**Contractual terms of the account** (bank’s Terms & Conditions)

The account expressly permitted overdrawing. Key clauses:

- The bank was **not obliged** to allow overdrafts but **could do so**
- If it did, it would charge a fee and interest
- The customer must repay the overdrawn amount immediately

## Application/Analysis

- The bank’s own Terms and Conditions (exhibit 3) **expressly permitted** overdrawing the account. Although the bank was not obliged to allow it, once it honoured the transactions it charges fees and interest - this amounted to authorisation
- There was **no deception** by the appellant:
    - He made no false representations (express or implied)
    - The bank received regular statements showing the true (overdrawn) position and continued to approve every transaction
    - The bank’s computer system responded exactly as the contractual terms allowed
- The appellant’s conduct was dishonest (he knew he could not repay), but **dishonesty alone is not enough** - the offence under  [s 192E(1)(b) of the Crimes Act 1900 (NSW)](https://anzlaw.thomsonreuters.com/Link/Document/FullText?refType=U5&docFamilyGuid=I2c81f540d10b11e8a07cfaeacc13e49f&pubNum=1100190&originationContext=document&transitionType=DocumentItem&docVersion=Law+in+Force&ppcid=af65b7b3058343d7aa47f571843bcec9&contextData=\(sc.Default\)) requires deception as an essential element.
- The jury verdicts were unreasonable and unsupported by the evidence. The Crown could not identify any causative deception

## Conclusion

Convictions quashed. Verdicts of acquittal entered.
