New South Wales Court of Criminal Appeal

## Material Facts

- On 3 February 2005, the victim was withdrawing $200 from an ATM in Surry Hills
- The applicant (then aged 32) entered the ATM area with her three-year-old son and used the child to distract the victim
- She grabbed the $200 from the victim’s hand, squeezed his hand causing pain, and claimed the money was hers
- An altercation followed. The applicant yelled for help and falsely alleged to police that the victim had tired to grab her son
- The applicant pleaded guilty in the Local Court on 7 March 2006 to **stealing from the person** (s 94 Crimes Act 1900 (NSW)) and was committed for sentence
- On 6 October 2006, Judge Taylor imposed a suspended sentence for 2 years’ imprisonment (non-parole period 15 months) with a good behaviour bound
- The applicant breached the bond by committing further offences. On 18 December 2008, Judge Hosking SC revoked the bond and ordered the sentence to be served (backdated to 20 August 2008)

## Issues

- Whether the sentencing judge erred by taking into account an aggravating circumstance (squeezing the victim’s hand/ use of violence) that elevated the offence to one more serious than stealing from the person (s94 Crimes Act 1900 (NSW)) - application of De Simoni principle.
- Whether the sentencing judge failed to give proper or sufficient weight to the applicant’s mental condition, intellectual disability, and related subjective factors
- Whether the sentence of 2 years’ imprisonment (with a 15-month non-parole period) was manifestly excessive
- Whether an extension of time should be granted to seek leave to appeal against 2006 sentence

## Rule/Law

- **s 94 Crimes Act 1900 (NSW)**: Stealing from the person — maximum 14 years (same as robbery).
- Stealing from the person is a **variant of robbery**, not simple theft. Seriousness depends on the facts (_R v Delk_ [1999] NSWCCA 134).
- **De Simoni principle**: Judge cannot use facts that make the offence more serious than the one charged (_The Queen v De Simoni_ [1981] HCA 31).
- **Mental disability**: Can reduce moral blame and need for general deterrence, but only if clearly linked to the crime (_R v Hemsley_ [2004] NSWCCA 228).
- **Extension of time** to appeal: Court looks at delay length, reason for delay, and chance of success (_R v Young_ [1999] NSWCCA 275).
- **Suspended sentence** (s 12): Real punishment. If breached, usually served in full unless very special reasons.

## Application/Analysis

- Squeezing the victim’s hand did **not** breach the *De Simoni *****principle. It was part of the offence and did not turn it into a more serious crime
- The judge gave **enough weight** to the applicant’s mental issues and disability
- The sentence of 2 years (15 months non-parole) was **not too harsh**. The offence was serious (used her child as a decoy + force + false complaint to police)
- No error in backdating or pre-sentence custody
- Extension of time to appeal refused - long delay and weak case

## Conclusion

Appeal dismissed. The sentence was **not too harsh**. The offence was serious.
