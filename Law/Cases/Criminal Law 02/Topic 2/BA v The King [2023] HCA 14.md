High Court of Australia

## Material Facts

- The appellant (BA) and the complainant (his former partner) were co-tenants of an apartment in Queanbeyan under a residential tenancy agreement pursuant to the _Residential Tenancies Act 2010_ (NSW).
- The relationship broke down. The appellant moved out in around May 2019, ceased paying rent, and removed most of his possessions. The complainant continued to live in the apartment as the sole occupant.
- On 8 July 2019, the appellant attended the apartment at about 6:00 am. The front door was locked. When the complainant refused him entry, the appellant kicked the door in (damaging the doorframe and deadlock), entered the apartment, grabbed the complainant by the shoulders, shook her, yelled at her, seized her mobile phone, and threw it to the floor.
- The appellant was charged with break and enter and commit a serious indictable offence (intimidation) in circumstances of aggravation (use of corporal violence) contrary to **s 112(2) Crimes Act 1900 (NSW)**.
- At trial, the District Court directed a verdict of not guilty on the basis that, as a co-tenant, the appellant had a right to enter the premises and therefore could not “break and enter”.
- The Crown appealed to the NSW Court of Criminal Appeal, which allowed the appeal. The High Court granted special leave and heard the appeal.

## Issues

- Whether the element of **“breaks and enters”** in **s 112(1)** _Crimes Act 1900_ (NSW) requires proof that the accused entered **as a trespasser** (i.e., **without lawful authority**).
- Whether a co-tenant under a residential tenancy agreement has **lawful authority** to enter the premises by force (kicking in a locked door) against the express refusal of the other co-tenant who is in sole occupation.
- The proper construction of the **right of occupation** granted by a residential tenancy agreement under the _Residential Tenancies Act 2010_ (NSW) and whether it provides a defence to a charge under s 112.
- Whether the appellant’s forcible entry and subsequent intimidation amounted to an offence under **s 112(2)** (aggravated break and enter).

## Rule/Law

- **Section 112(1) Crimes Act 1900 (NSW)**: A person who **breaks and enters** any dwelling-house (or other building) and commits a serious indictable offence therein is guilty of an offence (14 years).
- **Section 112(2)**: Aggravated offence if committed in circumstances of aggravation (e.g. use of corporal violence) — liable to 20 years.
- **“Breaks and enters”** requires an **actual breaking** (use of force to create or enlarge an opening) **and entry** into the dwelling-house **without lawful authority** (i.e., as a trespasser).
- The offence protects the **peaceful occupation/habitation** of a dwelling-house, not merely proprietary rights.
- A **residential tenancy agreement** under the _Residential Tenancies Act 2010_ (NSW) grants a **right of occupation** (s 13), but this does not authorise forcible entry against the will of the current occupant who is in sole actual occupation.
- A co-tenant who has moved out and is no longer in occupation does **not** have lawful authority to break in against the wishes of the remaining occupant.

## Application/Analysis

- By 8 July 2019, the appellant had moved out and was **no longer in occupation** of the apartment. The complainant was in **sole actual occupation**.
- The appellant’s status as a co-tenant under the residential tenancy agreement gave him a **right of occupation** at law, but this did **not** authorise him to **forcibly break** into the locked apartment against the complainant’s express refusal.
- Kicking in the locked door constituted an **actual breaking** (use of force to enlarge/create an opening). Entering against the occupant’s will meant the entry was **without lawful authority** (i.e., a trespass).
- The appellant then committed a serious indictable offence (intimidation) inside the dwelling-house, with circumstances of aggravation (corporal violence).
- The _Residential Tenancies Act_ does not override the common law and statutory requirement in s 112. A tenant’s right of occupation does **not** permit forcible entry into premises occupied by another against their will.
- The trial judge erred in directing a verdict of not guilty. The elements of s 112(2) were satisfied.

## Conclusion

Appeal dismissed. Conviction restored. Co-tenant status does not authorise breaking into premises occupied by another against their will.
