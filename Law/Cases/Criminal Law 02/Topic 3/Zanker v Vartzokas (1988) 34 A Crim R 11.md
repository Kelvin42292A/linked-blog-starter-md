Supreme Court of South Australia

## Material Facts

- A young woman accepted a lift from the respondent (Vartzokas) in his van.
- While driving, he made sexual advances and offered her money for sexual favours.
- She refused and demanded to be let out of the van.
- The respondent accelerated and refused to stop. He then said: **“I am going to take you to my mate’s house. He will really fix you up.”**
- The young woman, in fear of imminent sexual violence, opened the door and jumped from the moving van (travelling at about 60 km/h).
- She suffered bodily injuries as a result.
- The respondent was charged with **assault occasioning actual bodily harm**.
- The magistrate dismissed the charge, finding no apprehension of _immediate_ violence.
- The prosecutor appealed to the Supreme Court of South Australia.

## Issues

- Whether the respondent’s conduct (threatening the victim with sexual violence while unlawfully imprisoning her in a moving vehicle) caused her to apprehend **immediate** unlawful personal violence.
- Whether all the elements of the offence of **assault** were present, even though the threatened harm was to occur at a “mate’s house” a short time later.

## Rule/Law

- An **assault** is any act which intentionally causes another person to apprehend **immediate** (or relatively imminent) unlawful personal violence (Fagan v Metropolitan Police Commissioner [1969] 1 QB 439).
- The fear of violence must be **immediate** harm, not just harm in the distant future
- However, the immediacy of the threat depends on the **circumstances** of the case
- Words alone can constitute an assault if, together with the surrounding conduct, they cause the victim to reasonably apprehend immediate unlawful violence
- Where the victim is **unlawfully imprisoned** or trapped by the accused, a threat of violence to be carried out shortly afterwards can amount to an assault because the fear is continuing and present

## Application/Analysis

- The respondent threatened the victim with sexual violence while she was trapped in his moving van
- The victim was in **immediate fear** of harm because she could not escape and the threat was going to happen soon at the “mate’s house”
- Even though the actual violence was not going to happen right at that second, the fear was **present and continuing** while she remained imprisoned in a van
- This fear of relatively **imminent** violence was enough to satisfy the requirements of assault (Fagan principle)
- The magistrate was wrong to say there was no immediate threat

## Conclusion

The Supreme Court allowed the appeal. The respondent was guilty of assault as his threat caused the victim to fear immediate violence while trapped in the van.
