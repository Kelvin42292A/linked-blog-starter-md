House of Lords (UK)

## Material Facts

- The appellants and others engaged in consensual sado-masochistic homosexual activities in private.
- These activities involved the intentional infliction of actual bodily harm (including beatings, wounding, branding, and genital torture) for sexual gratification.
- The participants used code words and took some precautions, but the acts caused more than transient or trifling injuries.
- The appellants were charged with offences under s.47 (assault occasioning actual bodily harm) and s.20 (unlawful wounding / inflicting grievous bodily harm) of the Offences Against the Person Act 1861.
- At trial, the judge ruled that consent was not a defence. The appellants then pleaded guilty and appealed.

## Issues

Whether, in a sado-masochistic encounter, the prosecution must prove lack of consent on the part of the victim to establish guilt under s.20 and s.47 of the Offences Against the Person Act 1861.

## Rule/Law

- Consent is a defence to common assault but is not a defence to the intentional infliction of actual bodily harm or wounding under ss.47 and 20 of the 1861 Act, unless the acts fall within recognised exceptions (e.g. properly conducted surgery, lawful sports, reasonable chastisement, or tattooing).
- It is not in the public interest for people to cause or consent to the infliction of actual bodily harm for no good reason.
- Sado-masochistic activities involving actual bodily harm do not fall within the recognised exceptions and are therefore unlawful, even when consensual and done in private.

## Application/Analysis

- The appellants intentionally inflicted actual bodily harm and wounding on willing participants.
- Although the acts were consensual and occurred in private, they went beyond common assault and involved actual bodily harm.
- The activities were not analogous to recognised exceptions such as lawful sports or surgery.
- The court considered public interest factors, including the risk of serious injury, infection, corruption of others, and the glorification of violence and cruelty.
- Consent could not legalise the infliction of actual bodily harm in these circumstances.

## Conclusion

The appeals were dismissed, and the convictions were upheld. The House of Lords held that consent was not a defence to the charges.
