Court of Criminal Appeal for New South Wales

## Material Facts

- Between 1983 and 1984, the appellant (Knight) made numerous threatening and abusive telephone calls to a police constable (Rowles), a magistrate (Henderson SM), and a District Court judge (Muir), as well as their family members.
- The calls contained death threats, threats of violence, and extreme verbal abuse.
- The recipients were many kilometres away from the appellant at the time of the calls.
- Knight was charged with multiple counts of **assault** under s 61 of the _Crimes Act 1900_ (NSW) and making false statements under s 248A of the _Crimes Act_.
- He was convicted by a jury on 15 of the charges and sentenced to 8 years’ imprisonment.
- On appeal, the key issue was whether telephone threats made from a distance could constitute an **assault** (i.e. whether they caused the recipients to apprehend _immediate_ unlawful personal violence).

## Issues

- Whether the appellant’s threatening telephone calls were sufficient to constitute the offence of **assault** under s 61 of the _Crimes Act 1900_ (NSW).
- Whether the threats caused the recipients to apprehend **immediate** unlawful personal violence.
- Whether telephone threats made from a considerable distance can amount to an assault.

## Rule/Law

- An **assault** is any act which **intentionally** (or possibly recklessly) causes another person to apprehend **immediate** and unlawful personal violence (_Fagan v Metropolitan Police Commissioner_ [1969] 1 QB 439).
- Assault requires the victim to apprehend **immediate** unlawful force — not just a threat of future violence.
- A mere threat or words alone do not automatically amount to an assault; the threat must cause a reasonable apprehension of **immediate** violence.
- Assault is different from battery: assault is the apprehension of immediate unlawful force; battery is the actual application of that force (_Collins v Wilcock_ [1984] 79 Cr App R 229).
- An assault occurs when the threatener, by some act, intentionally causes the other person to believe that unlawful force is about to be inflicted upon them (_Pemble_ (1971) 124 CLR 107).

## Application/Analysis

- Assault requires the victim to fear **immediate** unlawful violence (_Fagan_).
- The appellant made threatening phone calls, but he was far away from the victims.
- The victims had no reason to fear **immediate** violence.
- The threats were only of possible future harm.
- Therefore, the calls did not amount to assault.

## Conclusion

The Court of Criminal Appeal allowed the appeal and quashed the assault convictions.
