New South Wales Court of Criminal Appeal

## Material Facts

- On 25 July 2008, the applicant (McIntyre) assaulted his mother (the victim) in their granny flat at Dean Park.
- He grabbed her by the throat, punched her at least twice to the face, and threatened to kill her with a large knife, saying he would stab her.
- The victim, who had significant physical disabilities (recent knee and shoulder replacements), feared for her life and jumped from the moving van after the applicant accelerated and refused to let her out.
- She suffered bruises to her face, shoulder and arm, and soreness to her throat.
- Later the same day, at Quakers Hill Police Station, the applicant punched a police officer (Senior Constable Perkins) to the face, causing bruising and swelling.
- He also used offensive language towards police in the presence of members of the public (taken into account on a Form 1).
- The applicant has a lengthy criminal history of violent offences, including prior assaults on his mother and police.
- He has a history of mental illness (bipolar/schizo-affective disorder) and substance abuse, but was not found to be mentally ill under the _Mental Health Act 2007_.

## Issues

- Whether the sentencing judge erred by taking into account the injury (bruising) caused to the police officer when assessing the objective seriousness of the **s.58** offence (assault police in execution of duty), in breach of the _De Simoni_ principle.
- Whether the sentencing judge erred in failing to properly consider the availability of summary disposal in the Local Court.
- Whether the sentences imposed were manifestly excessive.
- Whether the conduct amounted to an assault (including the distinction between assault and battery) for the purposes of s.58 _Crimes Act 1900_.

## Rule/Law

- **De Simoni Principle**: A court cannot take into account facts that amount to circumstances of aggravation which would have warranted conviction for a more serious offence (The Queen v De Simoni (1981) 147 CLR 383).
- For **s.58** _Crimes Act 1900_ (assault police in execution of duty), actual bodily harm (e.g. bruising) is **not** an element and cannot be used to increase the objective seriousness, as that would make it a s.60(2) offence (7 years max).
- **Assault** includes both:
    - Psychic assault (causing apprehension of immediate unlawful force), and
    - Battery (actual unlawful application of force, even slight touching).
- When sentencing for assault police, general deterrence is important and courts must support police in the execution of their duty.
- The availability of summary disposal in the Local Court may be considered as a mitigating factor in appropriate cases, but is not always relevant.

## Application/Analysis

- The sentencing judge erred by referring to the police officer’s **bruising and swelling** when assessing the objective seriousness of the **s.58** offence. This breached the *De Simoni *****principle because actual bodily harm is an element of the more serious s.60(2) offence
- The judge also erred by not properly considering the availability of summary disposal in the Local Court
- However, these errors were minor. Even after correcting them, the sentences were still appropriate given applicant’s lengthy violent criminal history and the need for general deterrence in assaults on police
- The conduct clearly amounted to a **battery** (actual punch), which is included within the definition of assault s.58

## Conclusion

The Court of Criminal Appeal granted leave to appeal but dismissed the appeal. The sentences were not manifestly excessive. No lesser sentence was warranted.
