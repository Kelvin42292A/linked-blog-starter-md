

### Issue

- The conviction issue: Did the appellant “drive” a “motor vehicle” under the _Road Traffic Act_ 1974-1978 when he was steering a car that had broken down and was being towed?

- The Sentencing Issue: Was the imposition of cumulative sentences, which totaled three years of imprisonment for three driving offences committed over a short timeframe excessive?

### Rule

- A “motor vehicle” is defined by its functional design (a self-propelled vehicle), not by its operational capacity or performance at any given moment

- The act of “driving” is not strictly defined in the Act but encompasses being “in control of” the vehicle.

- When sentencing a sequence of related offences occurring over a comparatively short period, the punishment should not be arbitrarily depend on the number of “stops and starts”; such sentences should often be concurrent rather than cumulative.

### Application

Applying the Rule to the Conviction: Burt CJ reasoned that a conventional car remains a “motor vehicle” even if it has a flat batter, is out of petrol, or is otherwise unable to propel itself. While the vehicle was under tow, the appellant was sitting in the driver’s seat, steering, and capable of apply the brakes and steering. Because he maintained this level of control over the towed vehicle’s movements, he was legally “driving” it.

Apply the Rule of the Sentence: The appellant’s three offences took place over three short journeys between 4:30 pm and 6:30 am the following morning. The judge noted that calculating punishment by strictly multiplying sentences for every stop and start in a broadly continuous course of conduct resulted in a disproportionate penalty. Therefore, making the three 12-month sentences cumulative was considered excessive.

### Conclusion

The appeal against the conviction on the final charge was dismissed, as the appellant was indeed driving a motor vehicle

The appeal against the sentence was allowed

The court ordered that the 12-month periods of imprisonment for each of the three offences be served concurrently rather than cumulatively, with a single minimum non-parole period fixed at six months.
