### Material Facts

- Hallett violently assaulted Whiting (the deceased), rendering him unconscious.

- Hallett left Whiting lying on the beach at the water’s edge with his feet in the sea.

- Hallett then went to sleep further up the beach.

- Whiting drowned (in shallow water while unconscious).

- No clear evidence whether he drowned due to the incoming tide, rolling into the sea, or otherwise.

- Hallett was convicted of murder and appealed.

### Issue

- Whether the appellant’s assault and leaving the unconscious victim at the water’s edge caused by deceased’s death by drowning

- Whether the appellant had the necessary malice aforethought for murder

### Rule

1. **Causation**
    
    Death is caused by accused if it's a natural/foreseeable consequence of their act. Ordinary natural forces (tide, sea) don't break the chain. Chain only breaks if victim consciously re-enters danger.
    
2. **Malice Afterthought**
    
    Exists if accused intends to kill, intends to cause GBH, or knows their act is likely to cause death/GBH (even if indifferent or hoping it won't).
    

### Application

1. **Causation**
    
    Hallett left unconscious Whiting at water’s edge. Whiting drowned while unconscious. Whether tide came in or he rolled doesn’t matter-both are natural consequences. Chain not broken. Causation established.
    
2. **Malice Afterthought**
    
    Hallett choked Whiting knowing it was likely to case death/GBH. Knowledge of likely harm = malice aforethought. Even if he hoped Whiting would survive, it’s still sufficient. Malice established.
    

### Conclusion

The appeal is dismissed. Both causation and malice afterthought are established. Hallett's assault caused Whiting's death and he possessed the necessary mens rea for murder. The conviction is upheld.
