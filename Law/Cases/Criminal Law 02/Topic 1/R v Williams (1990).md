Court of Criminal Appeal of New South Wales
### Material Facts
- The appellant and victim (French) had two fights in a licensed club
- **Fight 1:** French wounded appellant's face with a broken beer glass
- **Fight 2:** Williams started the second fight and bit off part of French's ear
- Williams was charged with **malicious wounding** and, in the alternative, **assault occasioning actual bodily harm**.
- The jury acquitted him of malicious wounding but convicted him of assault occasioning actual bodily harm. 
### Issue
- Was the appellant (Williams) guilty of assault occasioning actual bodily harm?
- Whether the act of biting was a **voluntary** (willed) act
- Whether the Crown had to prove that the appellant **intended** to cause actual bodily harm, or whether **recklessness** was sufficient
### Rule
1. The act that forms the basis of a criminal offence must be **voluntary** (willed) act. An involuntary act, such as reflex or convulsive movement, does not attract criminal liability [[Ryan v The Queen]]
2. For the offence of **assault occasioning actual bodily harm**, the Crown does **not** need to prove a specific reason to cause actual bodily harm. It is enough if the assault was committed **intentionally or recklessly** [Venna [1976] QB 421](https://anzlaw.thomsonreuters.com/Link/Document/FullText?findType=Y&serNum=1975027087&pubNum=0003898&originatingDoc=I14e6b750881711e8aca5bab3c9b3f468&refType=IC&originationContext=document&transitionType=DocumentItem&ppcid=259984d23a59404ca0ab487340be14cb&contextData=\(sc.UserEnteredCitation\)&comp=wlau) .
### Application
- The appellant started the second fight and bit off part of French's ear. The trail judge properly directed the jury that the biting had to be a **voluntary** act. The directions were adequate (**Ryan** applied)
- There was a actual **bodily harm**
- The Crown did not need to prove an intention to cause bodily harm. It was enough that the appellant acted **intentionally or recklessly** (**Venna** applied)
- The conviction for assault occasioning actual bodily harm was correct. 
### Conclusion
Appeal dismissed. Conviction upheld
