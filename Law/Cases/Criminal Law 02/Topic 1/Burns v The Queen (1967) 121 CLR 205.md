High Court of Australia
### Material

- Burns (appellant) was charged with manslaughter

- Burns was a part to joint criminal enterprise to supply methadone to the deceased

- The deceased died from the combined toxic effects of methadone and other prescription drugs

### Issue

1. Whether the appellant’s supply of the prohibited drug (methadone) to the deceased constituted an unlawful and dangerous act manslaughter.
2. Whether there was sufficient evidence that the appellant administered (or assisted in administering) the drug to the deceased to warrant a new trial.

### Rule

- Unlawful and dangerous act requires proof of an **unlawful act** that is **dangerous**

- Supplying a prohibited drug can constitute the unlawful act

- In joint enterprise cases, the accused must be a part to the enterprise that involved the dangerous act

- Causation must be established between the act and the death

### Application

The High Court held that supplying methadone as part of a joint criminal enterprise was an **unlawful act**. However, the act was **not dangerous** in the required sense because a reasonable person would not necessarily have appreciated an appreciable risk of serious injury or death from the supply alone. There was insufficient evidence that Burns personally administered or assisted in administering the methadone. There, the conviction could not stand.

### Conclusion

The High Court allowed the appeal, quashed the conviction, and entered a verdict or acquittal.
