## Material Facts

- Nedrick had a grudge against Viola Foreshaw and threatened to “burn her out”
- On 15 July 1984, he poured paraffin through her letterbox and front door and set the house alight (no warning given).
- Fire destroyed the house; Foreshaw’s 12-year-old son, Lloyd, died from asphyxiation and burns
- Convicted of murder at Stafford Crown Court and sentenced to life imprisonment
- On appeal, Court of Appeal quashed murder conviction, submitted manslaughter, and imposed 15 years’ imprisonment.

## Issues

- Whether the trial judge misdirected the jury on the mental element (intention) required for murder
- What direction should be given to a jury on intention where the defendant foresees death or serious harm as probable consequence of his act, but does not desire it.

## Rule/Law

- A person is guilty of murder if they intended to kill or cause serious bodily harm.
- Foresight of consequences is **not** the same as intention, but it is evidence from which intention may be inferred
- In cases where the defendant does not desire death/serious harm, the jury may infer the necessary intention **only** if they are sure that:
    - Death or serous bodily harm was a **virtual certainty** (barring unforeseen intervention) as a result of the defendant’s voluntary act; **and**
    - The defendant appreciated that this was the case

## Application/Analysis

- Nedrick’s purpose was to frighten, not to kill or seriously injure
- His act (pouring paraffin + igniting the house at night with occupants inside and no warning) made death/serious harm a virtual certainty
- Trial judge used wrong test (high probability = intention)
- Court of Appeal quashed murder conviction and substituted manslaughter

### Conclusion

Murder conviction quashed. Manslaughter substituted with 15 years’ imprisonment. The case established the “virtual certainty” test for inferring intention.
