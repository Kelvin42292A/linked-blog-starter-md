Hight Court of Australia

### Material Facts

The Trigwells were seriously injured when their car collide with a vehicle driven by Miss Rooke. Miss Rooke had swerved after hitting two sheep on the highway. The sheep belonged to the Kerins, who occupied land adjoining the highway.

The Trigwells sued:

- The Insurance Commission (as Miss Rooke’s compulsory third-party insurer) for negligence and
- The Kerins for negligence and/or nuisance (failing to prevent their sheep from straying onto the road)

### Issue

Do occupiers of land adjoining a highway own a duty of care to users of the highway to prevent their animals from straying onto it?

### Rule (Searle v Wallbank)

At common law, an occupier of land adjoining a highway is **under no duty** to users of the highway to take reasonable care to prevent animals (not known to be dangerous) from straying onto the road.

### Application

The High Court (majority) held that the rule in *Searle v Wallbank *****forms a part of the common law of South Australia. The Kerins therefore owed **no duty of care** to prevent their sheep straying. There was also no public nuisance.

The Court refused to abolish or modify the rule, stating that any change was a matter of Parliament, not the courts.

### Conclusion

The Kerins were not liable. Judgment against the Insurance Commission (as Rooke’s insurer) stood. The Trigwell’s appeal against the Kerins was dismissed.
