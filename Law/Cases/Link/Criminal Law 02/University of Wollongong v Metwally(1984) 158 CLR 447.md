## Material Facts

Mr. Metwally, an Egyptian postgraduate student, made complaints of racial discrimination and victimization against the University under the NSW _Anti-Discrimination Act 1977_. The State Tribunal ruled in his favor and awarded him damages. However, the High Court had previously ruled in _Viskauskas v Niland_ that the NSW racial discrimination laws were inconsistent with the Commonwealth's _Racial Discrimination Act 1975_ and therefore invalid under s 109 of the Constitution. The Commonwealth Parliament then quickly passed an amendment stating that its federal Act was **"not intended, and shall be deemed never to have been intended"** to exclude concurrent State laws. The University appealed, arguing that the Commonwealth could not retrospectively undo s 109's automatic invalidation of the State law during the years the discrimination actually occurred (1978–1981).

## Issues

Can the Commonwealth Parliament use retrospective legislation or a "deeming provision" (legal fiction) to retrospectively bypass Section 109 of the Constitution and validate a State law that was historically inconsistent and invalid?

## Rule/Law

- **Section 109 is Automatic:** When a State law is inconsistent with a Commonwealth law, the Constitution itself immediately and automatically renders the State law invalid (inoperative).
- **Constitutional Limit on Retrospectivity:** While the Commonwealth Parliament can retrospectively change or repeal its own laws, it **cannot retrospectively alter the historical operation of Section 109 of the Constitution**.
- **No Fictions:** Parliament cannot use a "deeming" provision (statutory fiction) to declare that a real inconsistency did not exist in the past, or to give valid operation to a State law during a period when the Constitution had already sterilized it.
- **Ancillary Provisions:** Any supporting or ancillary provisions in a State Act (like victimization rules) cannot operate during the period in which the primary substantive rules (like discrimination bans) are inoperative.

## Application/Analysis

During 1978–1981, the NSW racial discrimination laws were in fact inconsistent with the Commonwealth Act and were invalid under s 109. The Commonwealth’s 1983 Amendment Act could not retrospectively rewrite history to undo this constitutional invalidation. Because the primary discrimination provisions were inoperative at the time, the ancillary victimization provision (s 50) also had no legal operation.

## Conclusion

The NSW provisions were invalid prior to the 1983 amendment. The State Tribunal had **no power or jurisdiction** to hear Mr. Metwally's complaints or award him damages.

### Judges Rules

- **Rule 1: The Commonwealth Cannot Override Section 109** While the Commonwealth Parliament is sovereign and can retrospectively change its own statutes, **it cannot legislatively alter, bypass, or undo the past operation of the Constitution (specifically Section 109)**. Commonwealth statutes cannot prevail over the Constitution.
- **Rule 2: Statutory Fictions Cannot Alter Historical Facts** Parliament cannot use a "deeming" provision or legislative fiction to retrospectively declare that a factual, historical inconsistency did not exist. Whether an inconsistency existed at a specific point in time is a **matter of objective history, not of retroactive legislative intention**.
- **Rule 3: Ancillary Provisions Fall with Primary Provisions** If a primary section of a State Act is rendered inoperative by Section 109, any ancillary or supporting provisions that depend on or protect that primary section are **likewise inoperative during that same period**.
