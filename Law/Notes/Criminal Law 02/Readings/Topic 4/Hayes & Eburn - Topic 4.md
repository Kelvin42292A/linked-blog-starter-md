# Sexual Assault

**The Law:** Section 61I of the Crimes Act (NSW). Carries a maximum penalty of **14 years imprisonment**.

**The Concept:** Replaced the old law of "rape." It emphasizes that sexual offences are **crimes of violence** and are completely **gender-neutral**.

#### Elements of the Offence

To convict someone, the prosecution must prove two parts:

1. Physical Elements (What happened)

There was an act of sexual intercourse.

It happened **without consent**.

2. Fault Elements (What the accused was thinking)

The accused must have:

**Known** the victim wasn't consenting; OR

Been **reckless** about whether they consented; OR

Held an **unreasonable belief** that the victim consented.

# Physical act

### Act of sexual intercourse

Under **Section 61HA of the** Crimes Act, "sexual intercourse" is legally defined as:

**Penetration:** Any penetration (even slight) of a female's genitalia (including surgically constructed) or anyone's anus by:

Any part of another person's body.

Any object manipulated by another person (excluding proper medical procedures).

**Oral Sex:** The introduction of any part of a penis into the mouth of another person, or cunnilingus.

**Continuation:** Continuing any of the acts listed above.

Key Legal Requirements

**It Must Be Intentional:** The act must be a voluntary, willed action by the accused. It cannot be an accident.

**Crown Burden:** The prosecution (the Crown) must prove beyond reasonable doubt that the accused intended to perform the physical act.

### Without consent

**Consent** = A person **freely and voluntarily** agrees to the sexual activity.

#### What Does NOT Count as Consent

- No capacity (asleep, unconscious, cognitively impaired, substantially intoxicated)
- Given under threats, force, terror, intimidation or coercion
- Given due to abuse of authority or position of trust
- Based on mistaken belief (e.g. wrong identity, believes married, health/hygiene purposes, etc.)
- Mere passive submission or lack of physical resistance
- Consent obtained while substantially intoxicated by alcohol or drugs

#### Mental Element (What the Accused Must Have Known)

The prosecution must prove the accused:

- **Knew** the victim did not consent, **or**
- Was **reckless** (didn’t care whether consent was given), **or**
- Had **no reasonable grounds** to believe the victim consented.

#### Important Rules

- Consent must be **free, voluntary and ongoing** (can be withdrawn at any time).
- Honest but **unreasonable** belief in consent is **not a defence** (post-2018 law).
- The jury must consider **all circumstances** of the case.
- Applies to sexual intercourse, sexual touching, and sexual acts.

For sexual activity to be lawful in NSW, the other person must have **freely and voluntarily agreed** with full capacity. Anything less = no consent.

# Fault elements

### Knowledge of and recklessness as to consent

The **Crown must prove** the accused had a **fault element** regarding consent:

- **Knew** the victim was **not consenting**, **or**
- Was **reckless** as to whether the victim was consenting.

### Extended Meaning of “Knowledge” (s 61HE(3))

Includes:

- Actual knowledge the victim did **not** consent, **or**
- **Reckless** (didn’t care whether or not the victim consented), **or**
- Had **no reasonable grounds** for believing the victim consented.

### Recklessness (from case law)

- **Hemsley** & **Tolmie**: Accused is reckless if they realised the victim **might not** be consenting but proceeded anyway.

No liability unless the accused **knew or was reckless** about lack of consent. Honest but unreasonable belief in consent is **not** enough to avoid liability (post-2018 law).

### Non-advertent recklessness

Non-advertent recklessness occurs when the accused **fails to consider** (does not think about) whether the victim is consenting, but proceeds with the sexual activity anyway.

### Key Cases

- **Hemsley (1988)**: Accused is reckless if they realised the victim **might not** be consenting but continued.
- **Tolmie (1995)**: A person can be reckless even if they **did not think about consent at all** (i.e. failed to turn their mind to the issue).

It is enough that the accused was **indifferent** or **failed to advert** to the possibility of no consent and went ahead. This counts as the required fault element under s 61HE.

### Advertent reckless

Advertent recklessness occurs when the accused is **actually aware** of the **possibility** that the victim is **not consenting**, but decides to proceed with sexual intercourse anyway.

### Key Principles from _R v Banditt_ (2004)

- The accused must have **realised** there was a possibility of no consent.
- They then went ahead **regardless** (i.e. without caring enough to stop).
- It is **not** necessary to prove the accused had a firm “determination” to have sex whether or not she consented.
- The possibility must be a **real possibility** (not a remote or bare possibility, e.g. a 1% chance may be disregarded).

### Approved Jury Direction

> “If he is aware that there is a possibility that she is not consenting but he goes ahead anyway, that is recklessness.”

**Bottom Line**: Awareness of a real risk of no consent + proceeding anyway = sufficient fault element (recklessness) under the law.

### Honest but unreasonable belief of consent

#### Old Common Law (_DPP v Morgan_ 1976)

- An **honest** (but mistaken) belief that the victim was consenting was a complete defence.
- The belief did **not** need to be reasonable.
- If honestly held, it negated the fault element (no knowledge or recklessness).

#### Current Law in NSW – s 61HE(3)

- The law was changed in 2018 (previously s 61HA).
- **Honest but unreasonable** belief in consent is **no longer a defence**.
- If the accused believed the complainant was consenting, but had **no reasonable grounds** for that belief → the accused is taken to **know** there was no consent.

#### Requirements

- Once lack of consent is proven, the Crown must prove the accused **knew** or was **reckless** as to lack of consent.
- The trier of fact (jury or judge) must consider **all the circumstances** of the case, including:
    - Any **steps** taken by the accused to ascertain whether the complainant was consenting (s 61HE(4)).

**“Steps”**: Positive actions or reasonable consideration of what the accused heard, saw, or perceived.

#### Case Example – _R v Lazarus_ (2017)

- Shows that even if the complainant did not consent, the accused can be acquitted if they had an **honest and reasonable** belief based on the circumstances and steps taken.

**Bottom Line**: In NSW today, it is **not enough** to honestly believe the other person consented — your belief must also be based on **reasonable grounds** in all the circumstances. Unreasonable belief = guilty.

### Negation of consent

#### When a Person **Does NOT Consent**

A person does **not** consent to sexual activity if:

- They **lack capacity** (e.g. due to age, cognitive impairment, or substantial intoxication).
- They are **asleep** or **unconscious**.
- They are **unlawfully detained**.
- Consent was given because of **threats of force/terror**, intimidation, or coercive conduct.
- Consent was obtained through **abuse of authority** or position of trust.

#### Important Points

- These are **statutory circumstances** where apparent consent is **invalid**.
- The **Crown must prove** beyond reasonable doubt that one of these situations existed.
- **Age**: Children under 16 cannot consent for many sexual offences (strict liability under s 80AE). For other offences (e.g. s 61J), lack of consent must still be proven, but age is relevant to capacity.

#### Key Cases

- **McGrath (2010)**: Trial judge erred by directing that a child under 16 is automatically incapable of consent for **all** offences. For some offences, consent and knowledge of non-consent must still be proven.
- **Dean (2006)**: A sleeping person cannot consent. Whether the complainant was asleep is a **question of fact** for the jury (Crown bears the onus).

Even if someone appears to “agree”, consent is **negated** in the above situations. The Crown must prove both lack of consent **and** the accused’s fault element.

### Consent in relation to offences of attempted sexual assault

#### Current Law (s 61HE)

- Section **61HE** now **explicitly applies** to:
    - Completed sexual assault offences (ss 61I, 61J, 61JA), **and**
    - **Attempts** to commit those offences.

#### Previous Law (pre-2018, s 61HA)

- The consent rules only applied to completed offences.
- In **WO v DPP (2009)**, the Court held that s 61HA did **not** apply to attempts.
- Result: Self-induced intoxication **could** be considered when assessing whether the accused had the necessary _mens rea_ (knowledge or recklessness as to consent) for an **attempt**.

#### Important Change

- Under the current law (s 61HE), the rules on consent, knowledge, and recklessness apply equally to **attempted** sexual assault.
- **Self-induced intoxication** is now **irrelevant** when determining whether the accused knew or was reckless about lack of consent — for both completed offences **and** attempts.

The 2018 reforms extended the strict consent rules to attempts. Intoxication can no longer help an accused argue they didn’t have the required fault element in attempt cases.

### Grounds to establish absence of consent

Section 61HE(8) lists **factors** the jury can consider when deciding whether the complainant **did not consent** (it does not automatically negate consent).

#### Key Grounds / Factors

- **Substantial intoxication** by alcohol or drugs (s 61HE(8)(a))
    - Intoxication can lower inhibitions, but does **not** automatically mean no consent.
    - Consent is negated only if the person was so intoxicated they were **incapable** of freely and voluntarily agreeing (i.e. could not understand or decide).
    - Level of intoxication is a **question of fact**.
- **Intimidatory, coercive conduct, or other threats** (non-violent) (s 61HE(8)(b))
    - Submission due to intimidation or coercion may = no consent.
    - Whether the threat was strong enough to remove **free and voluntary** agreement is for the jury to decide.
- **Abuse of authority or trust** (s 61HE(8)(c))

#### Important Principles from Cases

- **Chant (1998)**: Intoxication is relevant but not decisive. A person can consent while affected by alcohol/drugs if they still consciously and voluntarily agree. Only when intoxication reaches the point of incapacity is there no consent.
- **Aiken (2005)**: Non-violent threats (e.g. threat to report shoplifting) do **not** automatically vitiate consent under the old law. Under current s 61HE(8), such threats are a factor for the jury.
- Consent must always be **free and voluntary**.

These are **evidentiary factors** the jury considers alongside all circumstances. The Crown must still prove **beyond reasonable doubt** that the complainant did **not** freely and voluntarily consent.

# Sexual touching and sexual act

#### Major Change

The 2018 amendments replaced the old offences of **Indecent Assault** and **Act of Indecency** with two new, clearer offences:

|Old Offence|New Offence|Type|Description|Penalty|
|---|---|---|---|---|
|Indecent Assault (s 61L)|**Sexual Touching** (s 61KC)|Contact offence|Any sexual physical contact with the victim|5 years|
|Act of Indecency (s 61N)|**Sexual Act** (s 61KE)|Non-contact offence|Sexual conduct **other than touching** (e.g. forcing/inciting victim to touch themselves or others, exposing, etc.)|5 years (higher if victim age under 10)|

#### Key Points

- The new offences focus on **sexual** conduct rather than the old vague concept of “indecency”.
- **Sexual Touching** requires physical contact of a sexual nature.
- **Sexual Act** covers non-contact sexual behaviour (including inciting the victim).
- The **consent rules** in **s 61HE** apply to both offences (same as sexual intercourse offences).
- Penalties are generally the same or slightly increased in serious cases.

The 2018 reforms modernised and simplified the law by replacing outdated “indecent” offences with two clear categories: **Sexual Touching** (contact) and **Sexual Act** (non-contact), both requiring lack of consent and the accused’s fault element.

### Sexual Touching

#### Offence

Any person who **intentionally**:

- Sexually touches another person, **or**
- Incites the victim to sexually touch them, **or**
- Incites a third person to sexually touch the victim, **or**
- Incites the victim to sexually touch a third person

**Without consent** and **knowing** the other person does not consent.

**Penalty**: 5 years imprisonment.

#### Definition of “Sexual Touching” – s 61HB

**Touching** (with any part of the body or anything else, including through clothing) that **a reasonable person** would consider to be **sexual**.

**Factors to consider** (not exhaustive):

- Whether it involves genital, anal area, or (for females/trans/intersex) breasts.
- Whether done for sexual arousal or gratification.
- Any other aspect or circumstances that make it sexual.

**Exception**: Touching for **genuine medical or hygienic purposes** is **not** sexual touching.

#### Comparison with Old Law

- Replaced **Indecent Assault** (s 61L).
- Removed the old vague concept of “indecency” (what right-minded people find contrary to community standards).
- Now uses a clearer **reasonable person + sexual nature** test.

Sexual touching requires **intentional sexual contact** (or incitement) **without consent**, with the accused **knowing** there was no consent. The touching must be sexual by objective community standards.

### Sexual Act

#### Offence

Any person who **intentionally**:

- Carries out a sexual act with or towards the victim, **or**
- Incites the victim to carry out a sexual act with or towards the offender, **or**
- Incites a third person to carry out a sexual act with or towards the victim, **or**
- Incites the victim to carry out a sexual act with or towards a third person

**Without consent** and **knowing** the victim does not consent.

**Maximum Penalty**: 18 months imprisonment (higher if victim under 10).

#### Definition of “Sexual Act” – s 61HC

An act (**other than sexual touching**) carried out in circumstances where a **reasonable person** would consider the act to be **sexual**.

**Factors to consider** (not exhaustive):

- Whether it involves genital, anal area, or (for females/trans/intersex) breasts.
- Whether done for sexual arousal or gratification.
- Any other aspect or circumstances that make it sexual.

**Exception**: Acts for **genuine medical or hygienic purposes** are **not** sexual acts.

#### Key Points

- This replaced the old offence of **Act of Indecency** (s 61N).
- Covers **non-contact** sexual conduct (e.g. exposing oneself, masturbating in front of someone, forcing/inciting someone to watch or perform a sexual act).
- Context matters — an act that is not sexual by itself can become a sexual act depending on the circumstances (see _Gillard_).
- The same **consent rules** in s 61HE apply.

Sexual Act covers non-touching sexual behaviour that a reasonable person would regard as sexual. It requires intentional conduct **without consent** and with knowledge of non-consent.

### Sexual act towards another person

#### Key Difference from Sexual Touching

- **Sexual Touching** (s 61KC) requires **physical contact** (an assault).
- **Sexual Act** (s 61KE) does **not** require touching — it covers acts done **“with or towards”** the victim.

#### Important Interpretation from _R v Barrass_ (2005)

- The word **“towards”** does **not** require the act to be in the **immediate physical presence** of the victim.
- It is enough that:
    - The act is directed at the victim.
    - The victim can see it.
    - The accused **intends** the victim to see it.

**Example from the case**: A man in a car next to a school bus (3–6 metres away) exposed and masturbated his penis while looking at two 6-year-old girls and smiling. This was held to be an act of indecency **“towards”** them (now a sexual act).

#### Bottom Line

For a **Sexual Act** offence, the conduct does not need physical contact or to occur in the same room/space. It is sufficient if the act is **directed towards** the victim with the intention that they witness it, and a reasonable person would consider it sexual.

This interpretation carries over to the current law under s 61KE.

### Fault element of sexual touching

The fault element for sexual touching is the **same** as for sexual intercourse offences. The accused must have **known** or been **reckless** about the lack of consent. Failure to think about consent at all can still amount to recklessness.

#### Elements the Crown Must Prove

- **Physical element**: Intentional **sexual touching** (as defined in s 61HB) **without consent**.
- **Fault element**: The accused **knew** the victim did not consent, **or** was **reckless** as to whether the victim consented.

#### Key Principles from Case Law

- Consent is central — if the victim consented, there is **no assault**, so no offence.
- **Recklessness** includes:
    - Realising the victim **might not** consent but proceeding anyway (**advertent**).
    - Failing to even consider whether the victim consented (**non-advertent** recklessness — see _Fitzgerald v Kennard_ and _Kitchener_).
- Honest but **unreasonable** belief in consent is **not** a defence (consistent with s 61HE).

# Child sexual assault and sexual offences - cognitive impairment etc

Children under 16 and people with cognitive impairment receive strong protection. Consent is often irrelevant, especially where the accused is in a position of care or authority. Honest reasonable mistake about age can be a defence in some cases.

#### Child Sexual Offences

- Strict rules apply to children under 16.
- **Consent is irrelevant** for many offences (s 80AE) — e.g. sexual intercourse with child <16, or <18 in special care situations.
- **Mistake about age**:
    - Mistake of law (e.g. “I thought 15 was legal”) = **no defence**.
    - Honest and reasonable mistake of **fact** about the child’s actual age = **defence** (_CTM v R_).

#### Similar Age Defence (s 80AG)

- Defence available for some offences (e.g. s 66C(3), 66DB, 73, 73A) if:
    - Victim ≥14 years, **and**
    - Age difference between accused and victim is **≤2 years**.
- Once raised, Crown must disprove it beyond reasonable doubt.

#### Sexual Offences — Cognitive Impairment (s 66F)

**Two main offences** (consent is **not** a defence):

|Offence|Description|Max Penalty|
|---|---|---|
|**Carer offence** (s 66F(2))|Sexual intercourse with person with cognitive impairment where accused is **responsible for their care**|10 years|
|**Taking advantage** (s 66F(3))|Sexual intercourse with person with cognitive impairment **intending to take advantage** of their impairment|8 years|

**Applies also to sexual touching & sexual acts** (s 66F(6)).

#### Definition of Cognitive Impairment (s 61HD)

Includes: intellectual disability, developmental disorder (e.g. autism), neurological disorder, dementia, severe mental illness, or brain injury — that requires supervision or support in daily life.

#### Defences (s 66F(7))

- Accused **did not know** the person had a cognitive impairment, **or**
- Accused was married/de facto partner, **or**
- Act was for genuine **medical or hygienic** purposes.

### Special Evidentiary & Procedural Rules in Sexual Assault Trials

These rules protect complainants from unnecessary trauma and irrelevant questioning while maintaining the accused’s right to a fair trial. They reflect the special sensitivity of sexual assault cases.

#### Key Provisions

- **s 293 (Rape Shield Law)**: Strict restrictions on evidence about the complainant’s **prior sexual experience** or sexual reputation. Such evidence is generally **inadmissible** unless it meets narrow exceptions.
- **s 294 (Delayed Complaint Warning)**: The judge **must** warn the jury that:
    - There may be **good reasons** why a victim of sexual assault may not complain immediately (or at all).
    - Delay in complaining does **not** necessarily mean the allegation is false.
- **s 294A (Cross-Examination Restriction)**: An accused who is **not legally represented** is **not allowed** to personally cross-examine the complainant. Questions must be asked through a lawyer or court-appointed person.

#### Case Note

- The restriction on personal cross-examination by unrepresented accused (**s 294A**) was held to be **constitutional** in _R v MSK and MAK_ [2004] NSWCCA 308.

# Drug offences

In NSW, most drug offences you will study are under the **DMTA**. The chapter focuses on state offences (possession, supply, etc.). Federal law is mainly relevant for importation cases.

#### Overview

This chapter deals with offences involving **prohibited drugs** (e.g. cannabis, cocaine, heroin, MDMA, amphetamines) under the **Drug Misuse and Trafficking Act 1985 (NSW)** (**DMTA**).

#### Key Distinction

- **Prohibited drugs**: Completely banned (possession, use, supply, etc. are criminal).
- **Pharmaceutical drugs**: Regulated but not generally prohibited.

#### Two Legislative Schemes

1. **DMTA (NSW)** – Main law used by NSW Police Covers:
    - Possession
    - Use
    - Supply
    - Cultivation
    - Manufacture
2. **Criminal Code (Cth) – Part 9.1**
    - Mostly overlaps with DMTA.
    - Federal offences (especially **importation** of border controlled drugs – Div 307) are handled by AFP / Border Force.
    - Rarely used for street-level supply/cultivation.

#### Important Legal Point

- State (DMTA) and Commonwealth (Code) laws can overlap with different penalties.
- There is **no constitutional inconsistency** (_R v El Helou_ [2010]).
- The Commonwealth Code is intended to operate **concurrently** with state laws.

# State legislation

### Drug Schedule of the DMTA

#### Key Feature

The **Drug Misuse and Trafficking Act 1985 (NSW)** uses a **schedule system** (Schedule 1) to classify prohibited drugs and set specific **quantity thresholds** for different offences and penalties.

#### Table Structure (Schedule 1)

For each prohibited drug, there are **six columns** that define key quantities:

|Column|Quantity Type|Purpose|
|---|---|---|
|1|**Traffickable quantity**|Threshold for “deemed supply” (possession of this amount or more = presumed supply)|
|2|**Small quantity**|Used for minor possession/use offences|
|3|**Indictable quantity**|Determines whether the offence is dealt with summarily or on indictment|
|4|**Commercial quantity**|Triggers much more serious supply/manufacture offences|
|5|**Large commercial quantity**|Even more serious offences with higher maximum penalties|
|6|**Discrete dosage unit (DDU)**|Used for drugs sold in tablet/pill form (e.g. ecstasy)|

#### **Importance**

The **quantity** of the drug is **critical** in DMTA offences. Different quantities lead to:

- Different offences (e.g. simple possession vs supply vs large commercial supply)
- Different maximum penalties
- Different court procedures (Local Court vs District Court)

This schedule-based system allows the law to distinguish between users, small dealers, and large-scale traffickers.

### Admixture provision

When calculating the **weight** of a prohibited drug, the DMTA includes **any mixture** containing the drug.

- The weight is the **total weight of the substance** found by police (including cutting agents, fillers, etc.).
- It does **not** require the pure weight of the actual drug.
- Example: LSD impregnated in cardboard — the weight includes the cardboard (_Finch v R_).

This makes it much easier for the prosecution to reach quantity thresholds.

#### Deemed Supply – Traffickable Quantity (s 29)

If a person is in **possession** of a quantity of a prohibited drug that is **equal to or greater than** the **traffickable quantity** (Column 1 in Schedule 1), they are **deemed** to be in possession **for the purpose of supply**.

- This is an **evidentiary presumption** — it does not create a new offence.
- The Crown must still prove possession beyond reasonable doubt.
- The **accused** then bears the onus to prove (on the **balance of probabilities**) that they had the drug **for a purpose other than supply** (e.g. personal use).

#### The “Carey Defence” (_R v Carey_ 1990)

The Court held that simply **holding drugs temporarily** for someone else (e.g. minding them overnight with the intention of returning them to the owner) does **not** amount to “supply”.

- Returning drugs to their owner (or person reasonably believed to be the owner) is **not** supply.
- However, this defence is **narrow** — it does not apply if the person is holding the drugs as part of a joint enterprise, on-selling, or for any other commercial purpose.

**Note**: The _Carey_ defence has been applied in many cases but is often unsuccessful if the jury does not accept the accused’s explanation.

# Amounts specified in the schedule of the DMTA

### Small quantity

#### Purpose of the Small Quantity

The **small quantity** (listed in Column 2 of Schedule 1) is the threshold used to determine whether an **indictable drug offence** can be dealt with **summarily** (in the Local Court) rather than on indictment (in the District Court).

#### Section 30 DMTA

- If the court is satisfied **on the balance of probabilities** that the amount of the prohibited drug (or plant) involved is **not more than the small quantity**, the offence **may be dealt with summarily**.
- Chapter 5 of the _Criminal Procedure Act 1986_ governs the procedure for summary dealing of these indictable offences.

#### Penalty When Dealt with Summarily

- Maximum penalty: **50 penalty units** or **2 years imprisonment**, or both.

This provision allows minor drug matters to be handled more efficiently in the lower court, while serious cases (larger quantities) are reserved for the higher court.

### Indictable quantity

#### Purpose of the Indictable Quantity

The **indictable quantity** (Column 3 in Schedule 1) sets the upper limit for when an indictable drug offence can still be dealt with **summarily** (in the Local Court), unless either party elects otherwise.

#### Key Rule – Section 31 DMTA

- Applies to certain offences (e.g. supply of a prohibited drug).
- If the amount of the drug is **more than the small quantity** but **not more than the indictable quantity**, the offence **can be dealt with summarily**.
- However, **either the prosecution or the accused** can elect to have the matter dealt with **on indictment** in the District Court.

#### Penalty When Dealt with Summarily

- Maximum penalty: **100 penalty units** or **2 years imprisonment**, or both.

#### Practical Effect

- Below the indictable quantity → Usually stays in the Local Court (unless election is made).
- Above the indictable quantity → Must be dealt with on indictment in the District Court (much higher maximum penalties apply).

This gives flexibility: minor-to-mid range cases can be finalised more quickly in the Local Court, while serious cases go to the higher court.

### Commercial quantity

#### Purpose

The **commercial quantity** (Column 4 in Schedule 1) is the threshold that triggers **much more serious supply offences** with significantly higher penalties.

#### Key Features

- Once the amount of the prohibited drug reaches or exceeds the **commercial quantity**, it becomes an element of the offence that the **Crown must prove**.
- The Crown must also prove that the accused **knew** (or was reckless as to) the fact that the quantity was a commercial quantity.

#### Example: Supply Offence (s 25(2))

- Supplying **less than** a commercial quantity → maximum penalty **15 years** imprisonment.
- Supplying **not less than** a commercial quantity → maximum penalty **20 years** imprisonment (for most drugs, other than cannabis leaf/plant).

#### Practical Effect

Crossing the commercial quantity threshold:

- Changes the offence into a far more serious one.
- Usually results in the matter being dealt with on indictment in the District Court.
- Leads to substantially higher maximum penalties.

This category is aimed at mid-to-large scale dealers and traffickers, distinguishing them from small-scale suppliers.

### Large commercial quantity

#### Purpose

The **large commercial quantity** (Column 5 in Schedule 1) represents the highest threshold in the DMTA. It applies to the most serious drug trafficking cases.

#### Key Features

- Unlike the commercial quantity, there is **no separate offence** of supplying a large commercial quantity under s 25.
- Instead, it operates as a **serious aggravating factor** on sentence.
- When the Crown alleges that the amount involved is a large commercial quantity, that fact **should be stated in the charge** and put before the jury (_R v Lee_).

#### Penalty

- For supplying a **large commercial quantity** of a prohibited drug (other than cannabis leaf or plant), the **maximum penalty is life imprisonment** (s 33(3)(a)).

#### Practical Effect

- This category targets major drug traffickers and large-scale operations.
- Proof that the quantity reaches or exceeds the large commercial amount significantly increases the seriousness of the offence and the likely sentence.

### Discrete dosage unit (DDU)

A **Discrete Dosage Unit (DDU)** is an amount of a prohibited drug (not exceeding the amount specified in Column 6 of Schedule 1) that is **prepared or apparently prepared** for the purpose of being administered as a **single dose**.

#### Purpose

- This category is used for drugs that are typically sold or consumed in individual doses (e.g. tablets, pills, or impregnated materials).
- It allows the prosecution to count the **number of doses** instead of (or in addition to) relying solely on weight.

#### Example: LSD

- For LSD, the Schedule lists quantities in **both grams and DDUs**.
- Traffickable quantity = **15 DDU** **or** **0.003 grams**.
- Common scenario: LSD impregnated into small cardboard squares — each square counts as **1 DDU**.

#### Practical Effect

- Makes it easier to prove that a particular quantity threshold (traffickable, commercial, etc.) has been reached when the drug is packaged in single-dose form.
- The DDU is especially important for synthetic drugs like ecstasy (MDMA) or LSD.

This provision recognises the way certain drugs are practically distributed and used in the community.

### Important definitions in the DMTA

#### Definition (s 6 DMTA)

A person **takes part in** the cultivation, manufacture, production, or supply of a prohibited drug or plant if they:

- Take or participate in **any step** in the process (or cause any step to be taken), **or**
- Provide or arrange **finance** for any such step, **or**
- Provide **premises** where any such step occurs, or **permit/suffer** such a step to occur in premises they own, lease, occupy, or manage.

#### Key Points

- This is a very **broad** provision. It catches people who assist or facilitate the offence, even if they are not the main offender.
- It applies to many serious offences (e.g. manufacture, supply, cultivation).

#### Important Case Law Distinctions

- **Preparatory acts** are generally **not** enough:
    - Buying ingredients or equipment for later manufacture is only “preparation”, not “taking part” (_R v BD_, _R v Spicer_).
    - Loading and transporting equipment to be used later is also preparatory.
- However, once actual steps in the **process** of manufacture/supply begin, it is caught.
- Using substances that **cannot** actually produce the drug may still lead to a **conspiracy** charge, even if manufacture was impossible.

“Take part in” significantly widens liability. It captures not only the main actors but also financiers, landlords, and those performing supporting steps — but stops short of mere preparatory conduct.

### Supply under the DMTA

Supply is very broadly defined. Even **possession for supply** or **agreeing to supply** can constitute the full offence. The quantity involved (especially traffickable or commercial) dramatically affects the seriousness and penalty.

#### Definition of “Supply” (s 3 DMTA)

“Supply” is defined **very broadly**. It includes:

- Sell and distribute
- Agreeing to supply or offering to supply
- Keeping or having in possession **for supply**
- Sending, forwarding, delivering or receiving for supply
- Authorising, directing, causing, suffering, permitting or attempting any of the above

#### Key Offence – Supply (s 25)

- It is an offence to supply a prohibited drug.
- Because of the wide definition, the **same offence** (s 25) can be proved in many different ways.
- When the Crown relies on the **deemed supply** provision (s 29 – traffickable quantity), the charge is still simply “supply”, but the particular (how it is proved) should be stated.

#### Conspiracy vs Agreeing to Supply

- There is a separate offence of **conspiracy to supply** (s 26).
- However, an **agreement to supply** can itself amount to the substantive offence of supply (because “agreeing to supply” is included in the definition).
- The distinction is not always easy and depends on the facts (_R v Carusi and Cassar_).

#### Ongoing Supply (s 25A)

- A separate, serious offence.
- Committed if a person supplies a prohibited drug (other than cannabis) on **3 or more separate occasions** within any **30-day period** for **financial or material reward**.
- Maximum penalty: 20 years imprisonment or 3,500 penalty units.

#### Evidence Commonly Used to Prove Supply

- Possession of scales, resealable bags, cutting agents (e.g. Glucodin).
- Large amounts of cash.
- Code words used in drug trade (police expert evidence admissible).
- Weapons or security devices (indicating involvement in the drug trade).

### Cultivate

Cultivation is broadly defined and covers almost any active involvement in growing prohibited plants. Indoor hydroponic setups or commercial-scale operations attract significantly higher penalties.

#### Offence

Section 23(1)(a) makes it an offence to:

- **Cultivate** a prohibited plant (mainly cannabis or opium poppies), **or**
- **Knowingly take part in** the cultivation of a prohibited plant.

#### Definition of “Cultivate” (s 3)

Includes:

- Sow or scatter the seed
- Plant, grow, tend, nurture, or harvest the plant

#### Aggravated Forms (s 23(1A))

More serious offences (higher penalties) apply if:

- The cultivation is by **enhanced indoor means** (e.g. hydroponics with nutrient-enriched water, artificial light/heat, or misting roots), **or**
- The plants are cultivated for a **commercial purpose**.

#### Key Points from Case Law

- Participating in **harvesting** plants is sufficient to constitute cultivation.
- Watering plants to promote growth also qualifies.
- For **commercial quantity** cultivation, the accused must have intended to cultivate a quantity that reaches the commercial threshold.

### Manufacture

#### Offence

Section 24(1) makes it an offence to:

- **Manufacture** or **produce** a prohibited drug, **or**
- **Knowingly take part in** the manufacture or production of a prohibited drug.

#### Definition of “Manufacture” (s 3)

Includes the **process of extracting or refining** the prohibited drug.

**Example**: Extracting LSD from blotting paper into which it had been impregnated counts as manufacture (_R v Buic_).

#### Mental Element (Fault)

The accused must **know** they are manufacturing or producing **what they believe to be a prohibited drug**. It is **not necessary** for the accused to:

- Know the **exact** identity of the drug, or
- Know that the substance is specifically prohibited under the DMTA.

#### Related Offence – s 24A

Possession of **precursors** (e.g. pseudoephedrine) or **apparatus** intended for use in the manufacture of a prohibited drug is a separate offence.

**Note**: Precursors and prohibited drugs are treated as **different classes** of substances, so different rules and mental elements apply.

### Possession

#### The Offence

It is an offence under **s 10** of the DMTA to be in **possession** of a prohibited drug. There are also various summary offences for possessing items related to drug use or production.

#### Definition and Elements of Possession

Possession requires **two main elements**:

1. **Physical control or custody** of the drug (actual or shared).
2. **Knowledge** that the substance is (or there is a real chance it is) a prohibited drug.

**Important points**:

- Control can be **shared**, but it must be to the **exclusion of people outside the group** sharing it.
- Mere **access** to a place where drugs are found (e.g. a shared house with multiple occupants) is **not enough** to prove possession by one person.
- The accused does **not** need to know the **exact** identity of the drug — it is sufficient that they knew or believed it was **a prohibited drug**.

#### Extended Meaning (s 7)

Possession includes situations where the drug is in the **custody of another person** on behalf of the accused.

#### Proof of Possession

Where the accused does not admit possession, the Crown usually proves it by **inference** from:

- The location where the drug was found.
- The accused’s connection to that location.
- The nature and packaging of the drug.
- The accused’s conduct and statements.

**Key Case Principle** (_R v Armanatidis_): Physical control + knowledge are essential. Shared access alone is generally insufficient.

# The structure of DMTA

### Part 1 of the act

**Part 1** of the _Drug Misuse and Trafficking Act 1985 (NSW)_ is titled **“Preliminary”**. It contains foundational definitions and provisions that apply across the entire Act.

### Key Sections in Part 1

- **s 3**: Contains the main **definitions** used throughout the Act.
- **s 4**: The **admixture provision** (already covered above) — weight includes any mixture containing the drug.
- **s 6**: Defines **“take part in”** cultivation, manufacture, or supply (already covered above).

### Important Cannabis Definitions (s 3)

- **Cannabis plant**: Any growing plant of the genus _Cannabis_.
- **Cannabis leaf**: Any part of the cannabis plant (including achene and seed), but **excludes**:
    - Cannabis oil
    - Fibre from which resin has been extracted
    - The whole growing cannabis plant
- **Cannabis oil**: Any liquid containing tetrahydrocannabinol (THC).

These distinctions are important because different parts of the cannabis plant can attract different offences and quantity thresholds.

### Part 2 of the act

Part 2 of the _Drug Misuse and Trafficking Act 1985 (NSW)_ is titled **“Offences”** and is divided into two main divisions.

#### Division 1 – Summary Offences

These are less serious drug offences typically dealt with in the Local Court. Examples include:

- Possession of a prohibited drug (s 10)
- Possession of equipment for administration (s 11)
- Self-administration of prohibited drugs (s 12)
- Obtaining a prohibited drug by false representation (s 16)
- Aiding, abetting, etc. commission of an offence in NSW (s 20)

**Maximum Penalty**: Fine of 20 penalty units, or 2 years imprisonment, or both (s 21).

#### Division 2 – Indictable Offences

These are more serious offences, usually dealt with in the District Court. Key examples include:

- Cultivation of a prohibited plant (s 23(1))
- Cultivation by enhanced indoor means (s 23(1A))
- Manufacture or production of prohibited drugs (s 24)
- Supply of prohibited drugs (s 25(1))
- Ongoing supply of prohibited drugs (s 25A)
- Conspiracy to commit a DMTA offence (s 26)

#### Penalties

- Many indictable offences can be dealt with **summarily** (in the Local Court) if the quantity is below certain thresholds (see ss 30–31).
- For offences **on indictment** (District Court):
    - Most serious offences involving drugs **other than cannabis** carry maximum penalties from **18 years** up to **life imprisonment**.
    - **Life imprisonment** applies to offences involving a **large commercial quantity** (s 33A).
    - Offences involving **cannabis leaf or plant** have lower maximum penalties (generally up to 20 years).

**Note**: Although fines are technically available for serious offences, they are rarely imposed on indictment. Profits from drug crime are now mainly recovered under the _Confiscation of Proceeds of Crime Act 1989_.

### Part 2 of the DMTA

#### Purpose

Part 2A establishes the legal framework for **medically supervised injecting centres** (also known as safe injecting rooms) in NSW. It is a **harm reduction** measure.

#### Objects of Part 2A (s 36B)

The main goals are to:

- Reduce deaths from **drug overdoses**.
- Provide a **gateway** to treatment and counselling for people who use the centre.
- Reduce the number of **discarded needles and syringes** and public injecting.
- Help reduce the spread of **blood-borne diseases** (such as HIV and Hepatitis C).

#### Licensing and Operation

- The Part sets out rules for the **issuing, conditions, review, and revocation** of licences for these centres (ss 36D – 36KA).

#### Important Exemptions (Division 4)

- **Criminal liability exemptions**: Users of the centre and staff/personnel involved in its operation are generally protected from prosecution for certain drug possession and use offences while on the premises.
- **Civil liability exemption** (s 36P): Protects the centre and its operators from civil claims arising from the conduct of the facility.

Part 2A creates a controlled, legal environment for supervised drug injection as a public health initiative, with strong protections from criminal and civil liability to encourage its operation and use.

# Related legislation

### Drug Court

#### Objects of the Drug Court (s 3)

The main goals are to:

- Reduce **drug dependency** of eligible offenders.
- Promote their **reintegration** into the community.
- Reduce the need to commit crime to support drug use.

It achieves this by diverting drug-dependent offenders into supervised **treatment and rehabilitation programs** rather than traditional imprisonment.

#### How It Works

- The Drug Court operates with the jurisdiction of both the **Local Court** and **District Court**.
- Eligible offenders who plead guilty can be referred to the Drug Court.
- If accepted, the court convicts the person but **suspends the sentence** on the condition they enter a structured drug rehabilitation program (which can be community-based or residential).
- The program includes conditions such as counselling, drug testing, supervision, and restrictions on residence/associations.

#### Eligibility (s 5)

A person must generally:

- Have pleaded guilty to an offence (not involving violent conduct or sexual assault).
- Be likely to receive a sentence of imprisonment.
- Appear to be **dependent on prohibited drugs**.

#### Process and Outcomes

- The court closely monitors progress.
- If the offender successfully completes the program, they may receive a lighter final sentence.
- If they fail to comply or wish to withdraw, the program can be terminated. The court then re-sentences them, taking into account their performance in the program (s 12).
- Sanctions can include returning them to custody.

#### Effectiveness

Evaluations (e.g. 2008 BOCSAR report) show the Drug Court is **more cost-effective** than traditional imprisonment and significantly reduces reoffending rates, particularly for drug-related and violent offences.

### Confiscation of profits

#### Objects of the Act (s 3)

The main purposes are to:

- Deprive offenders of the **proceeds and benefits** derived from serious crimes (especially drug offences).
- Forfeit property used in, or derived from, the commission of offences.
- Enable law enforcement to **trace** such assets.
- Enforce forfeiture and penalty orders from other jurisdictions.

#### Application to Drug Offences

The Act is frequently used in drug cases because of the high profits involved. It is triggered when a person is convicted of a **drug trafficking offence** — generally any indictable offence under the DMTA involving more than a **small quantity** of a prohibited drug or plant.

The Director of Public Prosecutions (DPP) can apply for two main orders:

#### 1. Forfeiture Orders

- Against **tainted property** — property that was:
    - Used in or in connection with the offence, or
    - Derived or realised (directly or indirectly) from the offence.
- Can also apply to **substitutable tainted property**.

**Examples**:

- A car used to transport drugs (_R v Hadad_).
- Cash that are proceeds of drug sales (_R v Sultana_).

#### 2. Drug Proceeds Orders (Pecuniary Penalty Orders)

- The court must assess the value of any **benefits** the offender derived from drug trafficking.
- It then orders the offender to pay a **pecuniary penalty** to the State equal to that assessed value.
- This is treated as a **civil debt** owed to the State.

When assessing benefits, the court can consider the offender’s assets, income, and expenditure over a relevant period (often the 6 years before proceedings).

**Example**: In _R v Hall_ (2013), the court made a drug proceeds order based on admissions, even with limited precise evidence of profit.
