# **Unlawful and Dangerous Act Manslaughter**

### **What is Unlawful and Dangerous Act Manslaughter?**

Manslaughter committed when a person causes death through an act that is both **unlawful** and **dangerous**, without any intent to kill.

### **Element 1: The Act Must Be Unlawful**

- **Must be a crime:** It must be a breach of criminal law, not just a civil wrong/tort (_R v Franklin_).
- **Must be direct:** It must be a direct physical act that immediately and inevitably causes injury (_R v Dalby_).
- _Note:_ An omission (failing to act) does not count.

### **Element 2: The Act Must Be Dangerous**

- **The Objective Test (_Wilson_):** Would a **reasonable person** in the accused's position realize that the act exposed others to an appreciable risk of serious injury?
- **Accused's awareness is irrelevant:** It does not matter if the accused did not personally realize the danger (_DPP v Newbury; DPP v Jones_).
- **Physical Position:** The reasonable person must be placed in the actual physical position of the accused at the scene (_R v Cornelissen_).

### **Element 3: Intent (Mens Rea)**

- The prosecution **does not** need to prove intent to kill or cause serious harm.
- The prosecution **only** needs to prove that the accused intended to commit the unlawful act itself.

### **Key Case: _R v Cornelissen_ NSWCCA 449**

- **Facts:** The accused punched a victim during a confrontation. The punch was unlawful (not self-defence) and led to a ruptured artery and death.
- **Crucial Rule:** When deciding if an act is "dangerous," the jury must place the **reasonable person in the accused's physical position**—taking into account what the accused could actually see and experience during the incident.

### **New Case & Rule: _Burns v R_ (2012)**

- **The Rule:** Supplying an illegal drug is **NOT** considered a "dangerous act" for the purposes of manslaughter if the person dies from taking/ingesting it.
- **Supplier's Knowledge:** This rule applies **even if** the supplier knew the recipient was going to use/take the drug.
- **The Core Logic:** The act of supplying the drug itself is not what causes the physical harm; the ingestion of the drug by the deceased is a separate issue of causation.

## Unlawful otherwise then as breach of statutory or regulatory provision

For a charge of unlawful and dangerous act manslaughter, the prosecution must establish that the accused caused death by an act that is both unlawful and dangerous.

**Criminal Breach Required**: The act must be a breach of the criminal law. An act that is merely a civil wrong (a tort) is insufficient to support a manslaughter charge (citing _R v Lamb_ and _Pembrey v R_).

### **Key Case: R v Pullman (1991) 25 NSWLR 89**

This case establishes how the "unlawful act" requirement applies to breaches of statutory or traffic regulations.

**Case Facts**

- The accused was driving a car and crossed an unbroken centre line.
- This maneuver forced an oncoming car to swerve.
- The oncoming car lost control, collided with, and killed a motorcyclist.
- The accused was convicted of manslaughter at trial, but appealed.

### **The Key Legal Questions**

1. Does a breach of a traffic regulation automatically count as an "unlawful act" for manslaughter?
2. If an act is a traffic breach, is it completely excluded from being an "unlawful act" (limiting the prosecution to criminal negligence manslaughter instead)?

### **The Ruling and Legal Principles**

The NSW Court of Criminal Appeal quashed the conviction and established two critical rules:

1. **Regulatory Breaches are Not Automatically Unlawful Acts**: A breach of a statutory or regulatory prohibition (such as a traffic regulation) does not, for that reason alone, constitute an unlawful act sufficient for UDAM.
2. **The "Unlawful in Itself" Requirement**: A regulatory breach can only qualify as the unlawful act if it is **unlawful in itself**—meaning it is unlawful for a reason other than just breaking that specific regulation.
    - _Example of an act unlawful in itself_: If a driver deliberately crosses a double line with criminal intent, such as to frighten an oncoming driver and force them to move over.
    - _Application to Pullman_: There was no evidence the driver crossed the line to deliberately frighten the other driver or had any such criminal intent. Therefore, crossing the line was a simple regulatory breach, not an act "unlawful in itself".

### **Supporting Authorities Discussed in Pullman**

- **Andrews v Director of Public Prosecutions (1937)**: Cited for the principle that a statutory traffic regulation breach is not automatically sufficient to establish the unlawful act for manslaughter.
- **R v Rau (1972)**: Discussed regarding whether traffic offenses should be entirely excluded from unlawful act manslaughter. The court in _Pullman_ rejected total exclusion, holding that if the driving conduct is "unlawful in itself" (beyond the mere regulation breach), it can still qualify.
- **R v Borkowski (2009)**: A later NSW Court of Criminal Appeal case where the majority approved the principles established in _R v Pullman_.

### **Quick Exam Checklist (R v Pullman Principles)**

- Is the alleged act a crime under criminal law, rather than just a civil tort?
- If the act is a breach of a traffic or statutory regulation, is there evidence making it "unlawful in itself" (e.g., criminal intent like deliberately trying to frighten someone)?
- If there is no criminal intent beyond the regulatory breach, the act cannot support a charge of unlawful and dangerous act manslaughter.

## Burden on Crown to prove death caused by unlawful act

### **The Core Legal Rule (R v Jones)**

A jury cannot find a defendant guilty of manslaughter simply because their act caused the victim's death. The prosecution must prove that the act causing death was both **unlawful** and **dangerous**.

### **The Three Requirements for Unlawful and Dangerous Act Manslaughter**

**The Act Must Be Unlawful**

- **Definition**: An act is unlawful if it involves a deliberate application of force to another person without that person's consent (for example, physical assault or battery).

**The Act Must Be Dangerous (The Objective Test)**

- **The Test**: An act is dangerous if a reasonable person in the position of the accused would have realized that the act exposed the victim to an appreciable (or significant) risk of serious injury.
- **No Personal Knowledge Required**: The prosecution does not need to prove that the accused personally knew the act was unlawful or dangerous, or that they knew it exposed the victim to risk. It is an entirely objective standard.

**Intent (Mens Rea)**

- Once the prosecution proves that the physical act was deliberate, they do not need to establish any further specific intention to cause death or serious harm.

**Case Focus: R v Jones (1995) 38 NSWLR 652**

**The Facts**

- The accused and another person went to the victim's flat after being dissatisfied with the quality of amphetamines they had purchased.
- An argument and scuffle broke out in the kitchen.
- The accused stabbed the victim three times (including twice in the chest), causing the victim to collapse and die.

**The Legal Error**

- The trial judge directed the jury that if they did not find an intent to cause really serious injury (murder), they must find the accused guilty of manslaughter simply because his act caused the death.
- **The Ruling**: The Court of Criminal Appeal quashed the conviction. The direction was inadequate because the judge failed to instruct the jury that the prosecution had to prove the act was both unlawful and dangerous.

**4. Self-Defence in Manslaughter**

If self-defence is raised, the prosecution must disprove it. To disprove self-defence for a manslaughter charge, the prosecution must establish either:

1. The accused did not subjectively believe that it was necessary to do the act in self-defence, or
2. There were no reasonable grounds for forming such a belief.

_Note: For manslaughter, the accused's belief regarding self-defence does not include any intention to kill or to inflict really serious bodily harm._

## MANSLAUGHTER BY CRIMINAL NEGLIGENCE

### **Core Rule (Nydam v R)**

Manslaughter by gross criminal negligence occurs when a person causes death through an act or omission that falls grossly short of the standard of care a reasonable person would exercise, carrying a very high risk of death or serious injury.

**The Two Key Elements:**

- **Grossly Negligent Act or Omission**: The accused must owe the victim a duty of care, and their action (or failure to act) must fall to a very high degree below the standard of a reasonable person.
- **High Risk of Death or Grievous Bodily Harm (GBH)**: The act must carry such a high risk of causing death or serious bodily injury that the negligence deserves criminal punishment rather than just civil compensation.

_Note: The prosecution does not need to prove an intention to cause death or grievous bodily harm._

### **Key Case: Do v R NSWCCA 19**

**The Facts**

- The accused and others spent the night consuming a large amount of alcohol, Rohypnol, and heroin.
- They produced a shotgun and "mucked around" with it.
- The shotgun discharged, killing the victim. The accused was convicted of manslaughter by criminal negligence.

**Key Takeaway**

- Deliberately pointing a loaded and cocked shotgun at someone and pulling the trigger—especially while heavily intoxicated—is a classic, extreme example of gross criminal negligence.
- The physical act was done consciously and voluntarily, without an intent to kill, but in circumstances showing a severe lack of care and extreme risk.

### **Statutory Offence: Dangerous Driving Causing Death**

Because juries are historically reluctant to convict drivers of manslaughter in motor vehicle homicide cases, legislatures introduced specific offences like **dangerous driving causing death** (e.g., s 52A Crimes Act NSW).

## **Involuntary Manslaughter and Criminal Negligence**

### **Categories of Involuntary Manslaughter**

Under s 18 of the Crimes Act 1900 (NSW), manslaughter is a residual category of punishable homicide defined by the common law. Unlike murder, involuntary manslaughter does not require any intention to cause death or grievous bodily harm.

At common law, there are two distinct categories of involuntary manslaughter:

1. **Manslaughter by an unlawful and dangerous act** (carrying an appreciable risk of serious injury).
2. **Manslaughter by criminal negligence**.

### **R v Lavender (2005) — Malice is Not Required**

This High Court case clarified the mental elements required for involuntary manslaughter.

**The Facts**

- The accused was operating a 25-ton front-end loader at a sand mine.
- He drove the loader into thick vegetation to chase away trespassing children, even though his vision was obscured by the loader's bucket.
- He ran over and killed a boy.

**The Core Ruling**

- **Malice is Not an Element**: The High Court held that malice (as defined in the Crimes Act) is not an element of manslaughter.
- **Objective Assessment**: Manslaughter by a negligent act is determined strictly objectively (based on what a reasonable person would do), not subjectively (based on the accused's actual state of mind).

### **Manslaughter by Criminal Negligence**

This occurs when an accused causes death through a grossly negligent act or omission that goes beyond a civil wrong and deserves criminal punishment.

**The Nydam Test for Criminal Negligence**

To establish criminal negligence, the prosecution must show the act causing death was:

1. Done **consciously and voluntarily**.
2. Done without any intention of causing death or grievous bodily harm.
3. Done in circumstances involving a **gross falling short** of the standard of care a reasonable person would have exercised.
4. Done in circumstances involving a **high risk of death or grievous bodily harm**.

**Case Example: Do v R**

- **Facts**: The accused and others consumed heavy amounts of alcohol, Rohypnol, and heroin. They "mucked around" with a shotgun, which discharged and killed the victim.
- **Ruling**: The court held that pointing a loaded and cocked shotgun at someone and pulling the trigger—especially while heavily intoxicated—is an extreme example of gross criminal negligence.

### **Statutory Alternative: Dangerous Driving**

- Legislatures introduced statutory offences like **dangerous driving causing death** (e.g., s 52A Crimes Act NSW) because juries are historically reluctant to convict drivers of manslaughter in vehicle accidents.

## **Manslaughter by Negligent Omission**

### **General Rule: No General Duty to Act**

At common law, you are not criminally liable for failing to act (an omission), even to save a stranger. You are only liable if you owe the victim a specific **legal duty to act**.

### **The Four Legal Duties (The Taktak Taxonomy)**

To be convicted of manslaughter by omission, you must owe a legal duty of care from one of these four categories:

1. **Statutory Duty**: A duty imposed by a specific statute.
2. **Status Relationship**: A recognized relationship of care, such as parent and child.
3. **Contractual Duty**: A duty arising out of an employment contract, such as a railway gatekeeper.
4. **Voluntary Assumption of Care**: Voluntarily taking charge of a helpless person and **secluding them** so that others cannot help.

### **Key Case: R v Taktak (1988) 14 NSWLR 226**

**Facts**

The accused fetched an unconscious prostitute who was suffering from a drug overdose, took her to an associate's house, and tried ineffectual first aid instead of calling an ambulance. She died.

**Legal Principle**

- **Assumption of Duty**: By taking the helpless woman into his exclusive custody and removing her from where others could have seen and helped her, he voluntarily assumed a duty of care.
- **Outcome**: His conviction was quashed because his actions did not show the extreme degree of gross negligence required for manslaughter.

### **Key Case: Burns v R (2012) 246 CLR 334**

**Facts**

The accused supplied methadone to a customer who voluntarily consumed it in her home, had an adverse reaction, and died after being asked to leave.

**Legal Principle**

- **No Duty on Drug Suppliers**: A drug supplier does **not** owe a legal duty of care to a customer who voluntarily consumes drugs.
- **Voluntary Action**: Since the deceased voluntarily took the drugs, he created the danger himself. Simply being present during an overdose does not create a legal duty to call for help.

## **Extended Liability and Common Design in Manslaughter**

### **Joint Criminal Enterprise**

When an accomplice agrees (even by a tacit, unspoken understanding) to commit a crime (the foundational offence) with a principal offender, they can be held criminally responsible if the principal kills someone during that crime (the incidental offence).

### **Murder vs. Manslaughter (R v Taufahema)**

Whether the accomplice is guilty of murder or manslaughter depends entirely on their foresight of the principal's intent:

- **Guilty of Murder**: If the accomplice **foresaw** that the principal might use lethal force (e.g., shoot) **with the specific intent** to kill or cause grievous bodily harm.
- **Guilty of Manslaughter**: If the accomplice **foresaw** that the principal might use lethal force (and possibly cause death or grievous bodily harm) but **did not foresee** that the principal would do so with a specific intent to kill or cause grievous bodily harm.

### **Key Case Authorities**

- **R v Taufahema**: Establishes this critical boundary of foresight between murder and manslaughter in a common design.
- **Other Key Authorities**: _R v Duong_ (1992), _R v Chai_ (2002), and _R v Rees_.

## **Crimes Act 1900 (NSW) Section 25A - Assault Causing Death**

### **Background (The "Coward Punch" Law)**

- **Origin**: Introduced following media and public outcry over fatal single-blow "coward punch" assaults in Sydney, where intoxicated offenders struck victims to the head, causing them to hit the pavement with fatal results.
- **Purpose**: Solves the prosecution's difficulty in proving an intention to kill or cause grievous bodily harm (required for murder) in single-blow cases.
- **Key Case**: _R v Loveridge_ (2014) highlighted the sentencing debate for manslaughter in these situations, which prompted the introduction and strict sentencing of this section.

### **Elements of the Basic Offence: s 25A(1)**

The prosecution must prove three elements:

1. **Intentional Hit**: The accused assaulted the victim by intentionally hitting them with any part of the accused's body or an object held by them.
2. **Unlawful**: The assault was not authorised or excused by law.
3. **Caused Death**: The assault caused the death of the victim.
4. **Maximum Penalty**: 20 years imprisonment.

### **Elements of the Aggravated Offence (Intoxicated): s 25A(2)**

An aggravated offence applies if:

1. The accused is **18 years or older**, and
2. The accused was **intoxicated** at the time of committing the basic offence.
3. **Maximum Penalty**: 25 years imprisonment.
4. **Mandatory Minimum (s 25B)**: Carries a mandatory minimum non-parole period of **8 years**.
5. **Presumption of Intoxication**: The accused is conclusively presumed intoxicated by alcohol if their breath or blood alcohol concentration is **0.15 grams** or more.

### **Key Legal Rules & Differences from Manslaughter**

- **Broad Causation (s 25A(3))**: Death is caused by the assault whether it results directly from the physical hit or from hitting the ground/object as a consequence of the assault.
- **No Foreseeability Required (s 25A(4))**: Unlike involuntary manslaughter (which requires an objective risk of harm), there is **no requirement** to prove that the death was reasonably foreseeable.
- **No Intent to Injure Required**: Unlike common law battery manslaughter, there is no need to prove an intention to cause injury to any degree. The only intent required is the intention to assault by striking.
- **Intoxication Rules**: Because it is an offence of basic intent, evidence of self-induced intoxication cannot be used to dispute the intent to strike.
- **Alternative Verdict (s 25A(7))**: If a jury is not satisfied that a defendant is guilty of murder or manslaughter, they can acquit them and find them guilty under s 25A instead.

### **Defences to s 25A(2) (The Aggravated Offence)**

It is a defence to the aggravated (intoxicated) charge if:

- The accused's intoxication was **not self-induced**, or
- The accused had a **significant cognitive impairment** (including intellectual disability, developmental disorders, neurological disorders, dementia, mental illness, or brain injury) at the time.

## **Summary of Culpable Killing in New South Wales**

### **Major Homicide Offences in NSW**

The legal framework in NSW recognizes several major offences for the culpable killing of another person:

- **Murder**
- **Voluntary Manslaughter**
- **Involuntary Manslaughter**, which includes:
    - Manslaughter by unlawful and dangerous act
    - Manslaughter by criminal negligence
- **Assault Causing Death**

### **Other Heavy-Penalty Killing Offences**

In addition to the core homicide offences above, NSW law includes other statutory offences that impose heavy penalties for causing death:

- **Dangerous driving causing death** (under Section 52A of the Crimes Act)
- **Dangerous navigation causing death** (under Section 52B of the Crimes Act)

_(Note: These specific driving and navigation offences are noted as being beyond the scope of this textbook.)_

# Non-fatal offences against the person

### **Core Concept**

- **Definition**: Offences where the conduct of the accused does not cause death.
- **The Foundation**: Modern NSW law in this area is built on the common law of assault, with various statutory offences added over time to fill gaps.

### **Problems and the Need for Reform**

The current structure of non-fatal offences in NSW is heavily criticized by courts and legal scholars as being "antiquated and illogical".

- **Outdated Framework**: The law is based on an adaptation of the English _Offences Against the Person Act 1861_, which consolidated centuries-old rules rather than establishing a modern code.
- **Massive Overlap**: There is a confusingly large number of statutory offences with significant overlap and lack of clear underlying principles.
- **Anachronisms**: The law preserves historic distinctions and offences that are no longer relevant, such as specific offences for assaulting a magistrate preserving a shipwreck, or distinguishing "assault" from "wounding" (which dates back to days dominated by edged weapons).
- **Inconsistent Coverage**:
    - _Over-inclusive_: Common law assault extends to the slightest application of force.
    - _Under-inclusive_: Acts that endanger lives are covered patchily by highly specific categories rather than a consistent general principle.
- **Jurisdictional Differences**: Victoria reformed and modernized its laws in 1985, but NSW and South Australia remain the closest to the original, outdated 1861 English legislation.

### **Key Examples of NSW Statutory Offences (Crimes Act 1900)**

Because of the piecemeal additions to the law, the Crimes Act contains a wide list of specific non-fatal offences, including:

- **Reckless or Intentional Harm**: Grievous bodily harm (GBH) or wounding with intent (s 33) or recklessly (s 35).
- **Specific Public Safety Threats**: Throwing rocks at vehicles (s 49A), or spiking food/drink (s 38A).
- **Anachronistic Protections**: Impeding escape from a shipwreck (s 32) or assaulting a member of the clergy (s 56).
