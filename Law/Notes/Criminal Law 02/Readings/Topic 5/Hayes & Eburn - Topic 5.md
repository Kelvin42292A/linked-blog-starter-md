# Murder vs. Manslaughter

Homicide (the unlawful killing of a person) is primarily divided into murder and manslaughter. Both crimes share the same physical act of causing death, but they differ in the person's state of mind. Murder is the most serious charge and requires a highly guilty mind, such as intending to kill, intending to cause serious bodily harm, or acting with a reckless indifference to human life. Manslaughter applies when a death is caused but the person lacked this specific intent, or when partial defenses apply.

# The Requirement of a Voluntary Act

To be guilty of murder, the accused must have committed a **voluntary act** (or a culpable omission) that directly caused the death. A death that happens purely by "misfortune" or accident is not a crime.

# Accidents and Reflexes

A major legal challenge arises in shooting cases when a defendant claims they fired a gun accidentally as a "reflex" or automatic reaction, rather than by choice. The courts have established that juries must first identify the exact act that caused the death (causation) and then decide if that act was deliberately willed (voluntariness). The law generally maintains that if someone deliberately points a loaded gun at another person and fires it as a sudden, impulsive reaction to a movement, it is still considered a **voluntary and deliberate act** rather than an innocent reflex.
