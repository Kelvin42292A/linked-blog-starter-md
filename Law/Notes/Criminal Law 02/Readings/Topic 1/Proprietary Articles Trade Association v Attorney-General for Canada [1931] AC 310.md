### Material Facts

- **The Legislation**: The Canadian Parliament enacted the Combines Investigation Act (R.S. Can., 1927, c. 26) and Section 498 of the Criminal Code

- **The Offence:** These laws made it an indictable offence to participate in a “combine” (a monopoly or agreement) that operated to the detriment of the public by restraining trade, limiting manufacture, or enhancing prices.

- **The Penalties:** Punishments included fines and imprisonment. Additionally, sections 29 and 30 of the Act allowed for the reduction of customs duties or revocation of patent licenses if they were used to facilitate a combine.

- **The Challenge:** The Proprietary Articles Trade Association and the Attorneys-General of Ontario and Quebec challenged the laws, arguing they were ultra vires (beyond the power) of the federal Parliament because they interfered with “Property and Civil Rights,” which are exclusive provincial powers.

### Issues

1. Is the Combines Investigation Act (1927) ultra vires the Parliament of Canada in whole or in part?
2. Is Section 498 of the Criminal Code ultra vires the Parliament of Canada?

### Rules

- **Section 91(27) of the British North America Act, 1867:** Grants the federal Parliament exclusive power over “Criminal Law, except the Constitution of Courts of Criminal Jurisdiction, but including the Procedure in Criminal Matters”.

- **Section 91(3) and 91(22):** Grant federal power over “The raising of Money by an Mode or System of Taxation” and “Patents of Invention and Discovery”.

- **Section 92(13):** Grants provincial legislatures exclusive power over “Property and Civil Rights in the Province”.

- **Pith and Substance Doctrine:** A legal principle used to determine which level of government has the authority to create a law by looking at the law’s true nature or primary purpose.

### Application

- **Definition of Criminal Law:** Their Lordships held that “criminal law” in Section 91(27) is used in its widest sense. It is not limited to acts that were historically criminal; the Parliament can declare any act a crime as long as it provides a penalty.

- **Pith and Substance:** The court found the primary purpose of the legislation was the prevention of public harm through the regulation of trade combines via criminal sanctions. Because it was in “pith and substance” criminal law, it did not matter that it incidentally affected provincial “property and civil rights”.

- **Sections 29 and 30:** These specific sections were supported by federal powers over taxation and patents rather than just criminal law.

- **Distinguishing Previous Cases:** The court distinguished this case from In re Board of Commerce Act (1919), noting that new legislation was properly framed as criminal law rather than a general interference with all trade.

### Conclusion

- **Validity:** Both the Combines Investigation Act and Section 498 of the Criminal Code are **intra vires** (within the power of ) the Parliament of Canada

- **Ruling:** The appeal was dismissed, and the decision of the Supreme Court of Canada was affirmed.
