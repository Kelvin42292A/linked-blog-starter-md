# Definition of a crime

### What is a Crime?

- **The Rule:** A Crime is whatever the statute says it is. Moral don’t matter.
- **Proprietary Articles:** Two elements make a crime: (1) State prohibited + (2) Punishable
- **Evans:** Regulations (delegated legislation) cannot create vague crimes (e.g., “annoying” people) without clear authorization from act

### Criminal vs. Civil Penalty

- **The Test:** Look at the statute’s exact wording
- **Labrador Liquor / Gapes:** If it says **“offence”** or **“conviction”**, it’s a criminal matter.
- Parliament can remove criminal protections (like standard of prof), but use explicit language

### Procedural Differences (Fair Work Case)

**Criminal Prosecutions:**

- **System:** Accusatorial (accused doesn’t have to help prove guilt)
- **Proof:** Beyond reasonable doubt
- **Purpose:** Punishment, deterrence, retribution
- **Sentencing:** Judge decides alone. **No penalty deals** between prosecution and defence.

### Civil Penalty Proceedings

- **Systems:** Adversarial (a contest between parties)
- **Proof:** Balance of probabilities (easier to prove)
- **Purpose:** Compliance and protection
- **Sentencing:** Parties **can** agree on a penalty settlement for the judge to approve

# Role of criminal law

### The Dual Role of Criminal Law

Criminal law does two main, competing things

- **Protects Society:** Keeps the peace by prohibiting harmful conduct and threatening punishment
- **Protects the Individual (Limits the State):** It regulates state power, ensuring the government cannot arbitrarily punish or imprison citizens without following strict legal rules.

### Criminal Law is Reactive, not Preventative

- **The Rule:** Criminal law usually only acts after a crime happens. It relies on deterrence
- **Kable v DPP (Key Case)**: You generally cannot lock someone up just because you think they might commit a crime in the future. Guilt is determined by proven legal facts, not moral outrage or predictions of bad behaviour. Law must clearly state what is prohibited.

# Restraining the state

### Safeguards against state power

Because the State (police/prosecution) has massive resources, the law provides strict procedural safeguards for the accused:

- **Separation of Powers:**The Executive (police/DPP) investigates and prosecutes. The Judiciary (independent courts/judges) decides guilt. The prosecutor can never be judge.
- **Presumption of Innocence:** You are innocent until proven guilty. The burden is entirely on the State to prove guilt **beyond a reasonable doubt.**
- **The Right to Fair Trail: Dietrich v R (Key Case)** establishes that a fair trail is a fundamental, deeply rooted right. The court’s job is to stand as an independent referee between the powerful State and the individual citizen.

# Bill of Rights

### The “Bill of rights” problem

- **The issue:** Unlike Europe, Australia does not have a federal Bill of Rights
- **The Consequence:** If an Australian Parliament wants to bypass strict criminal trail protections (like “beyond reasonable doubt”), they can just write a law calling a severe punishment a “civil penalty”. Without a Bill of Rights, courts have a very little power to strike this down.

### Preventive Detention

Traditionally, criminal law only punishes past actions. Can the state keep you in prison after your sentence finishes just because they think you might offend again?

- **Fardon v Attorney-General (Qld) (2004) - The Key Case:**
    - **The Majority Ruling (The law): Yes.** The High Court ruled that state parliaments can pass laws allowing courts to order ongoing “preventive detention” for hight-risk offenders. It does not violate the separation of powers or the Constitution.
    - **Kirby J’s Dissent (Good for essays):** He strongly disagreed, arguing this is effectively “double punishment” and forces judges to guess the future, which goes against centuries of traditional legal principles

### The Reality Today

- Because the High Court upheld the law in Fardon, governments have run with it.
- NSW now has laws like Crimes (High Risk Offenders) Act 2006, which allows ongoing detention and extended supervision of violent or sex offenders after their sentences expire.

# Burden of proof

### The “Golden Thread” (Who proves what?)

- **The General Rule:** The prosecution (the Crown) bears the burden of proving the accused’s guilt. The accused does not have to prove their innocence.
- **The Key Authority: Wollmington v DPP [1935].** This case famously established the “golden thread” of criminal law - that it is always the prosecution’s duty to prove guilt.
- **Statute:** Evidence Act 1995 (NSW) s 141(1) officially requires the prosecution to prove its case beyond reasonable doubt.

### “Beyond reasonable Doubt” (What does it actually mean?)

- **The Rule:** Judges must **never** try to explain or define “beyond reasonable doubt” to a jury
- **The Key Authority: Green v R (1971).** The High Court ruled that trying to define it only confuses juries. It is a common, everyday phrase that ordinary people understand. A “reasonable doubt” is simply whatever the jury thinks is reasonable
- **What judges can say:** Later cases (R v Dookhe) clarified that a judge is allowed to contrast it with the civil standard (balance of probabilities) and clarify that it does not mean “beyond any doubt” - but they still cannot define the phrase itself.

### When the Burden shifts to the Accused (Exceptions)

- **The Rule:** Normally, a defendant does not have to offer any rebuttal. They just have to raise a doubt about the Crown’s case.
- **The exception:** For certain specific legal defences (e.g., the defence of mental illness), the burden of proofs shifts to the accused to prove they had the condition.
- **The Standard:** When the accused has to prove a defence, they do **not** have to prove it beyond reasonable doubt. They only have to prove it on the lower civil standard: the **balance of probabilities** (Evidence Act s 141(2))

# Criminal Justice system

1. **The Public:** They very first filter. Victims or witness use discretion when they decide whether or not to even report a crime to the police.
2. **The Police:** Decide whether to investigate or take no action
    1. Decide whether to issue a warning/caution or formally charge the suspect
    2. Decide what to charge them with (a minor **summary** offence or a serious **indictable** offence)
3. **The DPP (Director of Public Prosecutions):** For serious (indictable) offences, the DPP takes over from the police.
    1. They have massive discretion: they can decide to continue the prosecution, drop it entirely, or charge the specific charges
4. **The Magistrate (Committal Hearing):** Acts as a filter before big trail. They look at the evidence and decide: Is there a probability a resonable jury will convict?
    1. If yes → Goes to trail
    2. If no → Accused is discharged (The DPP can override a magistrate’s discharge using an “ex officio indictment” to force a trail anyway, though rare)
5. **The Judge:** Exercises discretion when deciding the final penalty/sentence if the accused is convicted
6. **The Appeals Court:** If appealed, they have the discretion to uphold the conviction completely acquit, or order a brand new trail

### The Ultimate Safeguard

**Double Jeopardy:** Once you are finally acquitted or convicted, the state cannot prosecute you again for the exact same offence. The process permanently stops.

# Role of lawyers and Prosecutor

### The Core Duty of a Prosecutor

- **Who they represent:** The State/Crown. They do **not** represent the victim
- **The “Minister of Justice” Rule:** Their job is **not** to win at all costs or hunt for a conviction. Their duty is to the court and to the truth. They must present the evidence fairly, impartially, and with absolute detachment.

### The Decision to Prosecute

A prosecutor should only proceed with a case if two test are met:

1. **Evidentiary Test:** Is there a reasonable prospect of conviction?
2. **Public Interest Test:** Is prosecuting this specific case in the public’s best interest?

### Strict Rules of Conduct (The “Must Dos”)

- **Full Disclosure:** The Crown must hand over all relevant evidence to the defence, even if that evidence destroys the Crown’s own case. You cannot hide evidence
- **Calling Witness:** They must call all relevant witness to the stand, not just the ones who help their argument.

### Livermore v R (The “What NOT to do” Case)

This is the landmark case on prosecutorial misconduct

- **What happened:** A Crown Prosecutor gave a highly unprofessional closing address. He gave his personal opinions, relied on stereotypes from movies, mocked the defence’s arguments (calling them “silly” and comparing them to Desperate Housewives), and belittled witnesses.
- **The High Court’s Ruling:** This behaviour is totally unacceptable. Prosecutors cannot use inflammatory language, ridicule the accused, introduce facts that aren’t in evidence, or use emotion to bias a jury. They must remain objective.
- **The Consequence:** Because the prosecutor broke the rules of fairness, the accused did not get a fair trail. The conviction was **quashed** (thrown out) and a new trial was ordered.

# Defence

### The Core Duty

You can (and should) defend a client even if you know they are guilty. Your job is to test the Crown’s evidence, but you **must never mislead the court.**

- **Scenario A: The Client Lies or Fakes Evidence in Court (Rule 20.1)**
    - You must ask the client for permission to tell the court the truth.
    - If they refuse: You **must withdraw** from the case.
    - _Exam Trap:_ You generally **cannot** tell the court about the lie without the client's permission (confidentiality). Just quit.
- **Scenario B: The Client Privately Confesses but Pleads "Not Guilty" (Rule 20.2)**
    - You _can_ still represent them, but your strategy is strictly limited.
    - **What you CAN do:** Sit back and force the prosecution to prove its case beyond a reasonable doubt. Argue that the evidence is weak.
    - **What you CANNOT do:** Point the finger at someone else, invent a fake alibi, or let your client take the witness stand to lie (perjury).
- **Scenario C: The Client Plans to Disobey a Court Order (Rule 20.3)**
    - Advise them not to do it, and definitely do not help them do it.
    - You must keep their plan a secret **unless** their actions pose a threat to someone's physical safety.

# Right to a defence lawyer?

### No Automatic Right

**The Law:** In Australia, you do **not** have a constitutional right to a free, publicly funded lawyer

### The Right to a Fair Trail

**The Dietrich Principle:** While you don’t have a right to a lawyer, you do have a fundamental right to a **fair trail.**

**The Problem:** The High Court recognised that an unrepresented person facing a serious criminal charge is severely disadvantaged. A judge cannot step in to help them without losing their neutrality. Therefore, forcing an unrepresented person through a complex trail usually results in an unfair trail.

### A “Stay of Proceedings”

If an accused is facing a **serious criminal offence,** cannot afford a lawyer (through no fault of their own), and legal aid is refused, the judge should order a **stay of proceedings.**

**What it means:** The trail is paused (adjourned indefinitely) until the accused can secure legal representation. If the judge forces the trail go ahead anyway, any resulting conviction will likely be quashed (thrown out) because the trail was unfair.

### The Limits of Dietrich

Dietrich does not apply to everything. Later cases (like NSW v Canellis) strictly limited this rule. A judge will **not** pause proceedings for lack of lawyer in:

- Civil proceedings
- Committal hearings (pre-trail hearings)
- Cases where the accused is only a witness
- Minor (non-serious) criminal offences

# Incompetence of a defence lawyer?

### General Rule: You’re Stuck with them

- **The Law:** An accused is bound by the way their lawyer conducts the trail
- **TKWJ v R:** Bad tactical decisions (like choosing not to call a specific witness to avoid risk) do **not** make a trail unfair. Appellate courts will not second-guess a lawyer’s trail strategy just because it failed.

### The Exception: “Miscarriage of Justice”

To get a conviction overturned, you have to prove “flagrant incompetence” that was so bad it actually caused a **miscarriage of justice.**

### The Guilty Anyway Trap

**Nudd v R & Ali v R:** Even if your lawyer genuinely incompetent (e.g., completely misunderstood the charges), you might still lose your appeal. Because if the prosecution’s evidence against you was so overwhelmingly strong that you were going to be convicted anyway, the court will rule that no actual miscarriage of justice occurred. The trail was fair enough.

# Elements of crime introduction

Every crime is like a recipe made up of specific “ingredients” called elements. The Crown must prove **every single element** beyond a reasonable doubt. If even on element is missing or unproven, the accused must be found **Not Guilty.**

### Things to prove

For almost all crimes, the prosecution must prove two things happened at the exact same time:

- **The Physical Element:** The prohibited physical action (or failure to act/omission) - traditional latin name : **Actus Reus**
- **The Fault Element:** The guilty state of mind (e.g., intention, recklessness) when doing physical act - traditional latin name: **Mens Rea**

# Physical elements

There are 3 Types of Physical elements

### An Action (conduct)

The physical act itself is strictly prohibited

_Example:_ Driving over the speed limit. The action of speeding is the crime.

### Consequence (result)

The law doesn’t care exactly how you did it; it prohibits the end result you caused

_Example:_ Murder. The crime is the _consequence_ (causing a death), whether you used a weapon, poison, or your bare hands.

### A Circumstance

The action is normally completely legal, but becomes a crime only because of the specific surrounding facts

_Example:_ Consensual sex is lawful conduct. However, if the _circumstance_ is that one person is under 16, that external fact transforms the lawful act into a criminal one.

# Fault elements

### The 4 levels of fault

Different crimes require different levels of fault. From highest culpability to lowest:

1. **Intention:** You foresaw the consequence and actively desired it to happen. (You wanted to do it).
    
2. **Recklessness:** You foresaw the risk that the consequence could happen, but you went ahead and did it anyway
    
    Tips: For most crimes (like sexual assault), recklessness just means foreseeing the _possibility_ of the consequence. But for **murder**, it strictly requires foreseeing the _**probability**_ of death.
    
3. **Knowledge:** You were aware of the facts (e.g., You knew the bag in your car contained illegal drugs)
    
4. **Negligence:** You did not foresee the consequence, but a “**reasonable person**” in you shoes would have
    

### Wilful Bindness

Wilful blindness is when you deliberately look the other way to avoid finding out the truth.

It is **not** a separate fault category. However, prosecutors can use evidence of wilful blindness to convince a jury that you actually possessed the required "knowledge" or "recklessness."

# Motive

### Motive is NOT Intention

- **Intention** = What you meant to do (e.g., "I meant to pull the trigger"). This is a mandatory fault element for many crimes.
- **Motive** = Why you did it (e.g., "I wanted the life insurance money"). Motive is **never** a required legal element of a crime.

### De Grunchy v R (The Motive Case)

**The Rule:** The prosecution does **not** have to prove a motive to get a conviction

**The Facts:** The man killed his family members. He argued on appeal that because the Crown couldn’t prove why he did it (no apparent motive), he shouldn’t be convicted.

**The Court’s Decision:** The court rejected his argument. While a proven motive is great circumstantial evidence that helps a jury infer guilt or intention, the absence of a motive doesn’t mean your are innocent.

### Zaburoni v R (Intention vs. Recklessness & Desire)

This case highlights how hard it is to prove true “intention”.

- **The Facts:** A man with HIV repeatedly engaged in unprotected sex with his partner and transmitted the virus. He was convicted of intentionally transmitting a serious disease.
- **The Argument:** His motive/purpose for unprotected sex was simply pleasure, not a desire to infect her.
- **The High Court’s Decision:** The conviction was quashed. The court ruled that just because he engaged in frequent, highly risky conduct (which prove he was **reckless),** that does not automatically prove he had the specific **intention** to transmit the disease. Foresight of a risk is not the same as intending the result.
