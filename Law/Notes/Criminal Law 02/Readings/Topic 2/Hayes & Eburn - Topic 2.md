### Dishonesty

**Common Law (Peters v R)**: Dishonesty is judged by the **standards of ordinary decent people**

- Jury must be satisfied the accused **knew** their conduct was dishonest by those standards
- Subjective element: Accused must appreciate their act was dishonest (not just their personal view)

**Statutory Definition (S 4B Crimes Act 1900 NSW)**:

1. Dishonest according to **standards of ordinary people; and**
2. Defendant **knew** it was dishonest according to those standards

Applies to fraud, larceny (as “fraudulent”), and many property offences

**Ghosh** test (older English) is **not** used in NSW - Peters test applies

### Vella & Siskos v R [2015] NSWCCA 148

Insurance fraud case (planned suicide to claim policy)

Court confirmed **Peters** test applies to statutory “dishonesty” offences

Emphasised that dishonesty requires both:

- Ordinary person would see it as dishonest, **and**
- Accused knew it was dishonest

“Good faith” and non-disclosure of material facts were relevant to the dishonesty finding

### Claim of Right **(Defence to Larceny/Fraud)**

Genuine **honest belief** that the accused had a **legal right** to the property/money = **no dishonest intent**

Key principles (from _R v Fuge_, _Walden v Hensler_, etc.):

- Belief must be **genuine/honesty held** (does **not** need to be reasonable
- Must be a belief in **legal entitlement** (not just moral entitlement)
- Applies to larceny and any offence where dishonesty/fraudulent taking is an element
- Can apply even if the belief is mistaken in law or fact
- Work as a complete defence if it negates the mens rea

# Finding and Larceny

When a person finds goods and decides to keep them, it may or may not amount to larceny. The key case is R v MacDonald [1983] 1 NSWLR 729. In that case, the accused found a camera hanging on a fence, asked a nearby person if it belonged to them, and kept it. The court held this could be larceny.

**Rule**

- Larceny is committed if the finder takes the property with **intent to permanently deprive** the owner **and** believes the true owner **can be found**
- There is **no larceny** if the finder **honestly believes** the goods are lost **and** the owner **cannot reasonably be found**

The jury considers practical factors such as:

- The value and nature of the item
- Where it was found
- Any identifying marks or features
- Whether the finder made any enquiries

**Important Notes**

- **Abandoned goods** (owner has given up ownership) cannot be stolen.
- Lost goods still belong to the original owner.
- This topic links closely with the **Peters** test for dishonesty — keeping the item is judged by ordinary decent standards.

# Mistake and the requirement of coincidence between physical and fault elements

In larceny, the physical act (taking property without consent) and the fault element (dishonest intent) must **coincide** at the same time. Mistake cases often create problems with this requirement

### Key Rules on Mistake

- **Unilateral mistake** (only one party realises the mistake): The person who keeps the extra property (e.g. too much change) can be guilty of larceny if they act dishonestly
- **Bilateral (mutual) mistake** (neither realises at the time): Usually **no larceny** because the initial taking is with consent, and later dishonest intent cannot turn an innocent act into theft

### Cases

- **Middleton and Ashwell** (old English cases): Once heavily criticised. They treated some mistakes as vitiating consent, leading to larceny
- **Ilich v R (1987) 162 CLR 110** (High Court): A mistake is only fundamental (and can lead to larceny) if it relates to:
    - The **identity of the person** receiving the property, **or**
    - The **identity of the thing** delivered, **or**
    - The **quantity** of the thing (especially money - but treated carefully)

In Ilich, the accused received an overpayment of money. The High Court held there was no larceny because ownership had passed and there was no fundamental mistake that prevented consent.

### Modern Position (NSW)

- Money is special because ownership usually passes even if handed over by mistake (due to negotiability of currency).
- Keeping money received by mistake is generally judged by the **Peters** test for dishonesty.
- Later dishonest intention after an innocent receipt does **not** create larceny.

# Larceny by Trick

Larceny by trick occurs when a person uses deception to obtain **possession** (but not ownership) of property, while intending to permanently deprive the owner.

The deception must case the victim to hand over **only possession**, not full ownership. If the victim intends to transfer **ownership**, it is **not** larceny (because you cannot steal something you own). Instead, it falls under false pretences or modern fraud offences.

**Explanation:**

- Person tricks victim → Victim gives up **possession** only.
- Trickster always intended to keep the property permanently.
- This satisfies the trespassory taking required for common law larceny.

**Example:**

Someone asks to borrow your watch “for valuation” or to show a jeweller, but secretly intends to keep it. This is **larceny by trick** because you only handed over possession, not ownership.

---

At Common law, the distinction between larceny by trick and obtaining by false pretences depended on whether the victim intended to pass **possession** or **ownership**

In NSW today, most of these situations are now covered by the broader **statutory fraud offence** under s 192E of the Crimes Act.

# Statutory Offences

In 2009, NSW introduced a modern fraud scheme in **part 4AA** of the Crimes Act to replace many old, technical property offences. The main offence is **s 192E Fraud**

### s 192E Fraud

A person is guilty of fraud if they:

- By any **deception**, and **Dishonestly,**
    - Obtain property belonging to another, **or**
    - Obtain financial advantage, **or**
    - Cause a financial disadvantage to another

**Maximum penalty**: 10 years imprisonment

### Definitions

**Dishonesty** (s 4B)

- Dishonest according to the standards of ordinary decent people, **and**
- The defendant knew it was dishonest by those standards (Peter test)

**Deception** (s 192B): Very broad includes:

- Words or conduct (verbal or non-verbal)
- Deception as to fact or law
- Causing a computer, Causing a computer, machine or electronic device to respond in a way it is not authorised to (e.g. exploiting a bank system glitch).

**Obtains property** (s 192C): Includes obtaining ownership, possession or control.

**Financial advantage / disadvantage** (s 192D): Covers both permanent and temporary gains or losses.

### Other Statutory Offences

- **s 157 Embezzlement by clerks/servants**: Specific offence for employees who fraudulently deal with employer’s property in their custody
- **s 125 Larceny by bailee**: Covers bailees who fraudulently convert goods
- **s 124 Fraudulent appropriation**: Allows conviction where someone later dishonestly treats property as their own

### Overpayment at Checkout

D realises the cashier is giving her too much change but keeps it. **Likely guilty of larceny/fraud.**

She becomes aware of the mistake and dishonestly decides to keep the money (unilateral mistake). Later dishonest intent + Peters dishonesty test applies

### Doris & Van - Money Tin

Doris repeatedly takes $200 weekly contributions from Van and only uses a small part for expenses, keeping the rest.

**Guilty of fraud (s 192E) or larceny.**

Clear deception/dishonesty + obtaining financial advantage. No claim of right

### Doris & Van - Sports Car

Doris borrows Van’s expensive car with permission, then has it stripped and rebuilt with stolen parts.

**Guilty of larceny (or damage/destroy property).**

She had lawful possession as a bailee but fraudulently converted it (s 125 larceny by bailee). Serious interference with the owner’s rights.

# Mental illness, voluntariness, automatism and intoxication

Criminal responsibility requires that the accused’s action were **voluntary** and that they had the necessary **mental state** (mens rea). Certain mental conditions can affect this and may lead to acquittal or reduced liability.

### Mental Illness (Insanity) Defence

This is a complete defence. The accused must show (on the balance of probabilities) that at the time of the offence, they were suffering from a **disease of the mind** (mental illness) so that they:

**M’Naghten Rules** (the main test in NSW):

- Did **not know the nature and quality** of what they were doing, **or**
- Did **not know that what they were doing was wrong.**

Mental illness = a condition that seriously impairs the mind (delusions, hallucinations, severe thought disorder, etc.) - s 4 Mental Health Act 2007 (NSW)

Leads to a **special verdict**: “Not guilty by reason of mental illness”

The person may be detained in a mental health facility (s 29 Mental Health (Forensic Provisions) Act).

**R v Porter (1933)** is the leading Australian case explaining the test.

---

### Voluntariness & Automatism

All criminal offences require a **voluntary act** (or omission)

**Automatism**: The accused acts without conscious control (e.g. sleepwalking, seizure, concussion)

- If caused by **mental illness →** Insanity defence
- I caused by external factor (e.g. blow to head) → Complete defence (non-insane automatism)

### Intoxication

**Voluntary intoxication** usually does **not** provide defence, except where it prevents the formation of specific intent (e.g. murder)

**Involuntary intoxication** (e.g. spiked drink) can be a defence if it negates mens rea or voluntariness

For **murder**:

- Check voluntariness → Mental illness → Specific intent → Self-defence → Substantial impairment (manslaughter only).

For **other offences**:

- Voluntariness / Automatism / Mental illness / Intoxication can lead to full acquittal.
