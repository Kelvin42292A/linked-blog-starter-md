**1. The Actus Reus: Acts and Culpable Omissions**

In general, the criminal law draws a sharp distinction between a positive act that causes death and the omission to prevent death. An innocent bystander has no legal duty to rescue a drowning person.

However, an **omission** can constitute the _actus reus_ of homicide (murder or manslaughter) if the accused had a **legal duty to act**,.

- **Leading Case (_R v Taktak_):** Confirms liability arises when the accused undertakes a duty of care. This includes:
    1. A statutory duty to care for another.
    2. A status relationship (e.g., parent and child),.
    3. A contractual duty,.
    4. Voluntarily assuming care and secluding the helpless person so as to prevent others from rendering aid,.
- **Wrongful Act Exception:** A duty also arises if the accused puts a person in danger by a wrongful act and fails to rescue them (e.g., _R v Taber; R v Styman_, where the accused bound and gagged the victim and left her to die),.

**2. Causation**

To establish homicide, the Crown must prove the accused's act or omission caused the death. The jury determines this using their "common sense". It is not necessary for the accused's act to be the _sole_ or principal cause of death; accelerating death is sufficient,.

**The Tests for Causation**

When an intervening event (_novus actus interveniens_) occurs, the courts apply tests to determine if the chain of causation is broken:

- **Operating and Substantial Cause:** Did the accused's act substantially contribute to the death and remain an operating cause?.
- **Natural Forces (_R v Hallett_):** The ordinary operation of natural causes (like the tide coming in and drowning an unconscious victim left on the beach) does **not** break the chain of causation,. Only an "extraordinary" natural event, like a tidal wave caused by an earthquake, would break the chain.
- **Escape and Self-Preservation (_Royall v R_):** If the accused's violent conduct induces a well-founded fear that causes the victim to flee and accidentally die (e.g., falling from a window), the accused remains causally responsible,. The victim's reaction must be a reasonably foreseeable consequence, keeping in mind that people in fear of their lives do not always act rationally,.
- **Voluntary Act of the Deceased (_Burns v R_):** The voluntary and informed act of a sane adult generally breaks the causal chain,. Supplying a prohibited drug (like methadone) is not legally the cause of death if the adult victim voluntarily chooses to consume it.
- **Pre-existing Susceptibility (_R v Moffatt_):** You must take your victim as you find them (the "eggshell skull" rule). If an accused assaults a victim who has a severe pre-existing heart condition and the assault accelerates their death, the accused has still caused the death,.

**3. The Victim: Death of a "Living Person"**

To commit murder, the victim must be a "reasonable creature in being" (a living person).

- **Beginning of Life:** Under the "born alive" rule, an infant must be fully born and show any sign of life (such as breathing or a heartbeat) to be considered a legal person (_R v Iby_),,. Foetuses killed _in utero_ cannot be murdered, though this may constitute grievous bodily harm to the mother.
- **End of Life:** Under s 33 of the _Human Tissue Act 1983_ (NSW), a person is legally dead when there is an irreversible cessation of all brain function or blood circulation.

**4. Fault Elements (Mens Rea) for Murder**

Under s 18 of the _Crimes Act 1900_ (NSW), an act or omission causing death is murder only if accompanied by one of the following four states of mind,:

**A. Reckless Indifference to Human Life**

- The accused must foresee that their actions will **probably** (likely) cause death, and proceed regardless,,.
- **Crucial Distinction (_R v Crabbe_):** Foresight of a mere _possibility_ of death is insufficient for murder,. Furthermore, in NSW, the accused must foresee the probability of _death_, not merely the probability of grievous bodily harm,.

**B. Intention to Kill**

- The accused must have the foresight of death as a consequence and the desire to bring it about.
- Premeditation is not required; forming the intent in the heat of the moment (e.g., to escape) is sufficient.

**C. Intention to Cause Grievous Bodily Harm (GBH)**

- The accused intended to cause "really serious bodily harm" or permanent/serious disfiguring, which subsequently resulted in death.
- **Note on Proof:** The Crown must prove the accused _subjectively_ held this intent. Juries cannot be directed to strictly presume that a person intends the natural and probable consequences of their acts, though they can infer intent from the severity of the acts (e.g., hitting someone with a hammer),.

**D. Constructive Murder (Felony Murder)**

- Murder is committed if the death is caused during or immediately after the commission (by the accused or an accomplice) of a foundational crime punishable by life or 25 years imprisonment (e.g., armed robbery),.
- **No specific intent required:** The accused does not need to have intended to kill or foresee the possibility of death,. Proving the _mens rea_ for the foundational offence establishes the necessary legal malice for the murder charge (_IL v R_, _Aubrey v R_),.
- **Joint Enterprise (_R v Jacobs and Mehajer_):** If accomplices commit a severe robbery together and one person strikes a fatal blow, all participants can be liable for constructive murder, even if the exact perpetrator of the blow cannot be identified,,.

**5. Distinction from Manslaughter**

Every other punishable homicide that does not meet the fault elements of murder is taken to be manslaughter.

- **Voluntary Manslaughter:** Occurs when the _mens rea_ for murder is present, but culpability is reduced due to a partial defence (extreme provocation, substantial impairment by abnormality of mind, or excessive self-defence).
- **Involuntary Manslaughter:** Occurs when the accused causes death unlawfully but _lacks_ the intent or reckless indifference required for murder (e.g., by criminal negligence or an unlawful/dangerous act),.
