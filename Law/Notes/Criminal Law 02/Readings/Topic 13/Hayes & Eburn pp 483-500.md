**🍺 1. Intoxication & Intent (Chapter 9)**

In New South Wales, the relevance of intoxication is governed by **Part 11A of the _Crimes Act 1900_ (NSW)**.

**🔹 Specific vs. Basic Intent**

- **Specific Intent Offences (s 428C):** Evidence of intoxication **is admissible** to determine if the accused actually formed the specific intent required (e.g., murder, assault with intent to rob).
    - _Exception:_ It is **not** admissible if the accused decided to commit the crime before becoming intoxicated, or drank for "Dutch courage".
- **Basic Intent Offences (s 428D):** If the intoxication was **self-induced**, it **cannot** be used to negate the _mens rea_ (criminal intent) for basic intent crimes (e.g., manslaughter, common assault).

**🔹 Key Case Law**

- **_R v Makisi_ (NSWCCA):**
    - **Rule:** The jury's focus must be on whether the accused **actually formed** the specific intent, not just whether they had the "capacity" to form it. Capacity is simply evidence used to decide if actual intent existed.
- **_Ward v R_ (NSWCCA):**
    - **Rule:** Where an accused has a mental illness (e.g., schizophrenia) and is also intoxicated, the expert medical evidence regarding how these two factors interact is highly relevant to whether they formed the specific intent.
- **_R v Conlon_ (NSW):**
    - **Rule (s 428G):** Self-induced intoxication **cannot** be used to establish a defense of mental illness. However, a pre-existing underlying mental condition (like brain damage or schizophrenia) is considered under s 428C alongside intoxication when assessing if specific intent was formed.

**👤 2. The Defence of Duress (Chapter 10)**

Duress is a common law excuse where an accused argues they were forced to commit a crime because of an imminent threat of death or serious injury.

**🔹 The Two-Limb Test (_R v Lawrence_ )**

To successfully claim duress, the defence must satisfy two tests:

1. **Subjective Limb:**
    - Did the threat of death or serious injury actually overbear the accused's will, causing them to act as they did?
    - Did the accused believe they had **no safe avenue of escape** or opportunity to seek protection?
2. **Objective Limb:**
    - Would a **person of ordinary firmness**—of the same age, sex, and in the same circumstances as the accused—have yielded to the threat in the same way?

**🔹 Limitations & Exclusions**

- **Nature of the Threat:** The threat must be of **death or really serious physical injury** (threats to property or reputation do not count).
- **Scope of Offences:** Duress is **not** a defence to **murder** (whether as principal or accessory) or **attempted murder**.
- **Voluntary Association:** If the accused voluntarily associated with criminals (e.g., joining a violent gang) and could foresee being coerced, the defence is unavailable.
- **_R v Hurley and Murray_ (VR):** Established that the threatened harm can be directed at the accused or their immediate family/associates, provided there was no safe escape.
