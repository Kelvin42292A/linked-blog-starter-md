# **Extreme Provocation (Section 23 of the Crimes Act)**

**Overview**

- **What it is:** A **partial defence** to murder that came into effect on June 13, 2014 .
- **The Result:** If successful, the jury must acquit the accused of murder and instead find them guilty of **manslaughter**.

**The 4-Part Test**  
For an act to be considered "extreme provocation," all of the following must apply:

1. The act causing death was in response to the deceased's conduct towards the accused.
2. The deceased's conduct was a **serious indictable offence**.
3. The conduct caused the accused to actually lose self-control.
4. The conduct could have caused an **ordinary person** to lose self-control to the extent of intending to kill or inflict grievous bodily harm.

**Important Rules & Exceptions**

- **What DOES NOT count:** A "non-violent sexual advance," or if the accused intentionally incited the conduct just to create an excuse to use violence.
- **Timing:** The provoking conduct does _not_ need to occur immediately before the act that caused the death.
- **Intoxication:** The accused's self-induced intoxication cannot be taken into account.
- **Burden of Proof:** If there is any evidence of extreme provocation, the onus is on the **prosecution** to prove beyond a reasonable doubt that the act was _not_ in response to extreme provocation.

**Legislative Intent (Why the law changed)**

- The previous law was heavily restricted after a controversial case where a man used the provocation defence after his estranged wife confessed she did not love him and was leaving him.
- The defence was kept (rather than abolished entirely) primarily to assist victims of long-standing domestic violence who might not meet the strict legal criteria for self-defence.

### **The Two-Part Test for Provocation (Earlier Law)**

To successfully argue provocation, both parts of the following test must be met:

**1. Actual Loss of Self-Control (The Subjective Test)**

- **The Rule:** There must be evidence that the accused's act (or omission) was the direct result of a loss of self-control.
- **The Trigger:** This loss of self-control must have been induced by the victim's conduct.
- **What it includes:** The provocative conduct can include "grossly insulting words or gestures towards or affecting the accused".

**2. The Ordinary Person Standard (The Objective Test)**

- **The Rule:** The victim's conduct must be severe enough that it could have induced an _ordinary person in the accused's position_ to lose self-control to the point of forming an intent to kill or inflict grievous bodily harm.
- **Crucial Clarification:** The test does _not_ require that an ordinary person would have actually killed the victim. It only requires that an ordinary person could possibly have formed the _intent_ to kill or cause grievous bodily harm.
- **Timing:** The victim's provoking conduct does not need to happen immediately before the killing; it could have occurred at any previous time depending on the circumstances.

**How the Crown Defeats the Defence:**  
To disprove provocation, the prosecution (the Crown) must prove beyond a reasonable doubt that at least one of these two parts did _not_ apply at the time of the killing.

### **Provocation & Sustained Abuse: The "Slow-Burn" (R v Chhay)**

**Overview: The Case of _R v Chhay (1994)_**

- **The Facts:** A wife was convicted of murdering her husband with a meat cleaver while he was asleep or dozing, following a long history of severe domestic violence.
- **The Problem with Old Laws:** Historically, provocation laws favored a male-centric view of violence (e.g., sudden bar fights between men) and failed to protect victims of prolonged domestic abuse (usually women) who react differently.

**Key Principle 1: No Need for Immediate "Suddenness"**

- The provoking conduct does **not** need to happen immediately before the act of killing.
- There is no strict requirement for a temporal relationship; a loss of self-control can develop after a lengthy period of abuse without a specific, immediate triggering incident.
- **The Challenge:** While a delay is legally allowed, the longer the gap between the abuse and the killing, the harder it is for the defense to prove the act was a true "loss of self-control" rather than deliberate, cold-blooded revenge.

**Key Principle 2: The "Slow-Burn" Reaction**

- Research shows that battered women typically do not react with instant violence to abuse, knowing it could lead to a worse beating.
- Instead, they often experience a **"slow-burn"** of fear, despair, and anger that erupts later on—frequently when the abuser is asleep, drunk, or indisposed.

**Key Principle 3: Defining "Loss of Self-Control"**

- **What it is:** An emotional response that makes the accused temporarily "not master of his mind". It doesn't have to be just anger; it can also be driven by fear or panic.
- **What it is NOT:** It is not a state of "automatism" (the resulting killing is still intentional). Furthermore, emotions like hatred or a calculated desire for revenge do _not_ count as a loss of self-control.
- **Role of Experts:** Psychiatric evidence (like a PTSD diagnosis) can be used to help a jury understand how a person can lose self-control after a long delay.

**The Test Reaffirmed (Subjective & Objective)**

- **Subjective:** Did the accused _actually_ lose self-control due to the deceased's conduct?
- **Objective:** Could the deceased's conduct have caused an _ordinary person_ in the accused's position to lose self-control and form an intent to kill or inflict grievous bodily harm?

### **The "Ordinary Person" Test & Recent Legal Changes**

**The Major Change: A Purely Objective Test**

- **The Old Law:** Previously, the test asked if an ordinary person _“in the position of the accused”_ would have lost self-control.
- **The New Law (Section 23(2)(d)):** The phrase _“in the position of the accused”_ was intentionally removed.
- **The Result:** The test is now **purely objective**, based strictly on ordinary community standards. The jury no longer has to put themselves into the shoes of the accused or consider the accused's special sensitivities.

**Understanding the Shift: Overturning _Green v R_**

- **How it used to work (e.g., _Stingel_ and _Green v R_):** Under the old law, courts separated the test into two ideas:
    1. _Gravity:_ You could look at the accused's personal characteristics (age, sex, past trauma) to understand how severe ("grave") the provocation felt to them. For example, in _Green v R_, the accused’s special sensitivity to sexual abuse (because of his family history) was considered relevant to how severely he felt provoked by a sexual advance.
    2. _Self-Control:_ You then asked if an ordinary person facing that same level of "gravity" would have lost self-control.
- **How it works now:** The new legislation completely **overturns** the ruling in _Green v R_ [1]. Because the words _“in the position of the accused”_ are gone, the actual, subjective impact of the provocation on the accused—and their specific personal background or weaknesses—is **now irrelevant** to the ordinary person test.

### **The New "Extreme Provocation" Rules**

**1. The Strict "Ordinary Person" Test**

- **100% Objective:** The law removed the phrase _“in the position of the accused”_.
- **No Personal Excuses:** The jury must judge the situation based on ordinary community standards, **not** by putting themselves in the accused's shoes.
- **Background Doesn't Matter:** The accused's personal history, traumas, or special sensitivities are now completely irrelevant to the test. (_Note: Being exceptionally young or immature might be the only exception_).

**2. What Does NOT Count Anymore**

- **Being Intoxicated:** Your own alcohol or drug use is ignored. The law assumes the "ordinary person" is completely sober.
- **Insulting Words:** Simply using "grossly insulting words" is no longer enough to claim provocation.
- **Non-Violent Sexual Advances:** The law specifically states this can never be used as an excuse.

**3. The "Serious Crime" Rule**

- For an act to trigger the extreme provocation defence, the victim's conduct must be a **"serious indictable offence"**.
- This means the victim's provoking act must be a major crime that is punishable by **at least 5 years (or life) in prison**.

**Mistake and Provocation (The _R v Dib_ Rule)**

**The Big Question** Can you claim provocation if you were **mistaken** about what the victim did, or who they were (e.g., believing X provoked you when it was actually Y)?

**The Short Answer** **No.** A mistaken belief cannot be used to claim extreme provocation.

**Why Mistake is Not Allowed**

**1. Plain English Rule**

- The law requires the provocation to be "conduct of the deceased."
- As a matter of basic English, if the deceased didn't actually do the act, it cannot be called "conduct of the deceased," even if you mistakenly believed they did it.

**2. The "Innocent Act" Rule**

- Usually, a "mistaken belief" can only excuse a crime if the mistaken facts would make your actions **completely innocent** (like self-defence).
- Because provocation only lowers murder to manslaughter (which is still a serious crime, not "innocent"), the mistake doctrine does not apply.

**3. Strict Standards (Even if allowed)**

- Even if a court were to somehow consider a mistake, the mistake would have to be **both honest and reasonable**. An unreasonable or irrational mistake will always be rejected.

**Key Case: _R v Dib_ (2002)**

- **The Facts:** The accused stabbed a victim, mistakenly believing the victim was part of a group attacking his brother.
- **The Ruling:** The judge refused to allow a manslaughter plea based on provocation, ruling that a person cannot rely on a mistaken belief of facts as a defense for provocation.

**Withholding Provocation from the Jury**

**1. The General Rule**

- **When it MUST go to the jury:** If there is **any reasonable evidence** of provocation, the trial judge is legally required to let the jury consider it.
- **Most Favorable View:** The judge must evaluate the evidence in the light **most favorable to the accused** when deciding whether to let the jury hear it.

**2. When a Judge can "Withhold" (Take it away from the jury)**

- A judge can refuse to let the jury consider provocation if, as a matter of law, **no reasonable jury could find that an "ordinary person" would have lost self-control**.
- Even if the accused _actually_ lost self-control, the judge will withdraw the defense if a normal person would not have reacted with murderous intent under those same circumstances.

**3. Common Situations for Withholding**

- **Mere Words:** Insulting, hurtful, or offensive words alone are generally not enough to satisfy the "ordinary person" test, allowing judges to withhold the defense.
- **Domestic Disputes (_R v Kumar_):** In this case, the victim locked the accused out, refused to feed him, and insulted his family. The judge correctly withdrew the defense because these actions fell far below the threshold of provocation that would cause an ordinary person to kill or mutilate someone.

**The Abolition Debate**

- **The Criticisms:** The Model Criminal Code Officers Committee (MCCOC) heavily criticized provocation for failing to reflect contemporary values.
- **Historical Bias:** The defence originated in Anglo-Saxon and Norman times to regulate drunken pub brawls and duels. Because of these origins, the doctrine is deeply geared toward male patterns of aggression and sudden losses of self-control at the expense of human life.
- **Culpability Gap:** Critics argue that the defence often reduces culpability to manslaughter for defendants whose actions are morally no different from murder.
- **Abolition:** Due to these critical flaws, jurisdictions like Victoria completely abolished the provocation defence in 2005.

**Substantial Impairment by Abnormality of Mind (Section 23A)**

**1. Core Concept & Result**

- **What it is:** A **partial defence** to murder (previously known in older textbooks as "diminished responsibility").
- **The Outcome:** If successful, it reduces the charge from **murder to manslaughter**.
- **Difference from Mental Illness:** Mental illness is a _complete_ defence (leads to acquittal and indefinite detention). Substantial impairment is a _partial_ defence (leads to a manslaughter conviction, allowing a set prison term and parole).

**2. The 3-Part Test** To use this defence, the accused must prove that at the time of the killing:

- **An Abnormality of Mind:** They had an abnormality of mind arising from an **underlying condition** (defined as a pre-existing mental or physiological condition, not something temporary).
- **Impaired Capacity:** This abnormality substantially impaired their capacity to do at least one of the following:
    1. **Understand** events.
    2. **Judge** whether their actions were right or wrong.
    3. **Control** themselves.
- **Warrants Reduction:** The impairment was so significant that it legally warrants reducing murder to manslaughter. (_Note: Expert witnesses can give psychiatric opinions on the impairment, but they are not allowed to tell the jury whether they think it "warrants" a reduction—that decision is strictly for the jury_).

**3. Key Rules & Exclusions**

- **Burden of Proof is on the Accused:** Unlike most defences where the prosecution must disprove it, the **accused** must prove substantial impairment. However, they only have to prove it on the **balance of probabilities** (meaning it is more likely true than not).
- **No Intoxication:** Any effects from self-induced drug or alcohol use at the time of the killing must be completely ignored when deciding if the accused was substantially impaired.

**1. Excessive Self-Defence (Section 421)**

- **What it is:** You **honestly believed** you needed to defend yourself (or someone else), but the force you used was **unreasonable**.
- **The Result:** You are acquitted of murder and convicted of **manslaughter** instead.
- **State Comparison:** Victoria completely abolished this defence in 2014.

**2. Infanticide (Section 22A)**

- **What it is:** A mother causes the death of her child **under 12 months of age**.
- **The Cause:** At the time of the act, her mind was **disturbed** due to not fully recovering from childbirth or because of the effects of breastfeeding (lactation).
- **The Result:** She is convicted of infanticide instead of murder, which carries the **same penalty as manslaughter**.

**Involuntary Manslaughter (Chapter 4)**

**What is it?**

- **The Definition:** An unlawful killing where the mental element required for murder (intent or reckless indifference) is **not** present, but the accused's actions are still highly blameworthy.
- **The Two Forms:** Under Australian common law, there are **two ways** to commit involuntary manslaughter:
    1. **Manslaughter by an Unlawful and Dangerous Act**
    2. **Manslaughter by Criminal Negligence**

**1. Manslaughter by an Unlawful & Dangerous Act**

To be found guilty of this form of manslaughter, the prosecution must prove two things:

- **The Act was "Unlawful":** It must be a breach of criminal law, not just a civil wrong. A common example is a physical assault, like a punch.
- **The Act was "Dangerous" (The _Wilson_ Test):**
    - **The Rule:** The test is **purely objective**.
    - **The Standard:** Would a reasonable, sober person in the accused's position have realized that the act exposed the victim to an appreciable risk of **serious injury**?
    - **Key Detail:** The accused does _not_ need to personally know the act was dangerous.
    - **No Proportionality Required:** The prosecution does _not_ need to prove that the force used was proportional to any provocation.
