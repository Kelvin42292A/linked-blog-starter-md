**Complicity** means **helping or encouraging someone else to commit a crime**. Under New South Wales (NSW) criminal law, people involved in a crime are divided into **four main categories**:

1. **Principal in the First Degree:** The person who actually carries out the physical acts of the crime. This also includes someone who uses an **"innocent agent"** (like a child or someone who lacks capacity) to perform the physical act for them.
2. **Principal in the Second Degree (Aider and Abettor):** Someone who is **present at the scene** of the crime and intentionally assists, encourages, or helps with its commission.
3. **Accessory Before the Fact:** Someone who helps plan, advises, or commands the crime **before it happens**, but is not present when it is committed.
4. **Accessory After the Fact:** Someone who **helps the offender after the crime is completed** (such as helping them escape or hide from the police).

**Joint Criminal Enterprise (JCE)**

A **Joint Criminal Enterprise** occurs when two or more people **agree to commit a crime together**.

- **The Agreement:** Under this doctrine, if a group agrees to a crime, **all members are equally liable as principals** even if only one person physically performs the criminal act (for example, if one person enters a house to steal while another acts as a lookout).
- **Extended Liability (Common Purpose):** If the group agrees to commit "Crime A," but during the process, one member commits "Crime B," the other members can also be found guilty of "Crime B" if they **contemplated (foresaw) it as a possible consequence** of carrying out the agreed plan.
- **The _Johns v R_ Case:** This rule is illustrated by the landmark case of _Johns v R_, which established that a participant in a planned robbery can be held responsible for a murder committed by their accomplice if they foresaw that killing or serious bodily harm was a possible consequence of executing their plan.
