**1. Substantial Impairment by Abnormality of Mind (`s 23A Crimes Act 1900 (NSW)`)**

This is a **partial defence to murder** that reduces the conviction to **manslaughter**. It does not lead to a full acquittal.

- **The Statutory Test (s 23A(1))**: The accused is not to be convicted of murder if:
    - **Abnormality of Mind**: At the time of the act, the accused's capacity to **understand events**, **judge right from wrong**, or **control themselves** was **substantially impaired** by an abnormality of mind arising from an **underlying condition**.
    - **Manslaughter Warranted**: The impairment was **so substantial** as to warrant reducing liability from murder to manslaughter.
- **Key Concepts**:
    - **Underlying Condition**: Defined as a **pre-existing mental or physiological condition** that is **not transitory/temporary**.
    - **Burden of Proof**: Lies on the **accused** on the **balance of probabilities**.
    - **Role of Expert Evidence**: While experts are needed to identify the abnormality of mind and its underlying condition, the ultimate question of whether the impairment was "so substantial as to warrant manslaughter" is a **value judgment for the jury or judge**, not the medical experts.
    - **Intoxication**: Self-induced temporary intoxication is **explicitly excluded** from being an "underlying condition".

**📂 Key Case Law**

- **R v Chayna (1993)**:
    - Clarified that "substantial" impairment means **more than trivial/minimal, but less than total**.
    - The jury must assess **all evidence** (including the accused's behavior before, during, and after the act), not just expert medical opinions.
- **R v Cheatham (2000)**:
    - The accused strangled his partner and claimed depression combined with drug/alcohol consumption.
    - Reiterated that **temporary self-induced drug or alcohol effects do not qualify** as an underlying condition under s 23A.
- **Patton v R (2010)**:
    - The jury is **entitled to reject expert opinions** if the accused’s real-world actions (such as planning, concealment, or rational behavior after the crime) demonstrate they had rational capacity at the time of the killing.

**2. Fitness to be Tried**

- **Focus**: Focuses on the accused's mental capacity **at the time of the trial**, whereas s 23A focuses on their state **at the time of the offence**.
- **The Standard (R v Presser)**: To be fit for trial, the accused must be able to **understand the charge**, **follow court proceedings**, **instruct their legal team**, and **challenge jurors**.
- **Consequence**: If an accused is found unfit, they cannot be tried in the ordinary manner.

---

**3. Voluntariness and Automatism**

- **Distinction from Mental Illness**:
    - **Mental Illness / s 23A**: Affects the **workings of the mind** (reasoning, judgement, and capacity to control).
    - **Automatism**: Affects the **physical capacity to perform a voluntary act** (involuntary, unconscious muscle movements).
- **The Rule**: Every crime requires a **voluntary act** (_actus reus_).
- **Consequence**: Because an act done in a state of automatism is involuntary, it **negates the actus reus entirely**, leading to a **complete acquittal**. (Unlike mental illness which leads to a special verdict of detention, or s 23A which leads to a manslaughter conviction).
