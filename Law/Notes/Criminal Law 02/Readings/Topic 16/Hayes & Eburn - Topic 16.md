**Section 1: Complicity & Joint Criminal Enterprise**

- **Joint Criminal Enterprise (JCE):** Liability arises when two or more people agree to commit a crime together. Each participant is liable for the acts of the others done in execution of that shared plan.
- **Extended Joint Criminal Enterprise (EJCE) / Extended Common Purpose:**
    - **The Rule:** If a co-offender commits an _incidental_ crime (e.g., murder) during the execution of the primary crime, an accomplice is also liable if they **foresaw the possibility** of that incidental crime being committed and continued to participate.
    - **Landmark Case:** **_McAuliffe v R_ (1995)** established this "foresight of possibility" test in Australia.
- **The Australian vs. UK Split:**
    - **UK Position:** The UK Supreme Court abolished EJCE in **_R v Jogee_ (2016)**, ruling that foresight is merely evidence of intent, and actual intent to assist/encourage is required for complicity.
    - **Australian Position:** The High Court of Australia in **_Miller v The Queen_ (2016)** declined to follow _Jogee_. In Australia, **foresight of possibility** remains the sufficient test for EJCE.
- **Constructive Murder in JCE:**
    - If a death is caused during a foundational crime (like robbery), an accomplice can be found guilty of murder if they foresaw the acts causing death as a possible incident of the enterprise.
    - **Key Case:** **_R v Jacobs and Mahajer_ (2004)**.
- **The Rule:** To successfully escape liability by withdrawing from a joint criminal enterprise, an accused must do two things:
    1. **Timely Communication:** Clearly communicate their intention to withdraw to the other participants.
    2. **Reasonable Preventative Action:** Take "all reasonable steps" to prevent the commission of the crime or undo their previous participation (e.g., contacting the police or trying to stop the crime).
- **Landmark Case:** **_White v Ridley_ (1978)**.
- **Accessory After the Fact:**
    - **Definition:** Assisting a principal offender _after_ a crime is committed, with the specific intent to help them escape detection, arrest, prosecution, or punishment.
    - **Key Cases:** **_R v Stone_ (1981)**; **_R v Barlow and Maguire_ (1990)**.
- **Misprision of Felony:**
    - It is an offence under **s 316 of the _Crimes Act 1900_ (NSW)** to fail to disclose information to police regarding a serious indictable offence without a reasonable excuse.

**Section 2: Criminal Procedure, Police Powers & Arrest**

- **Statutory Power:** Under **s 21 and s 36 of LEPRA** (_Law Enforcement (Powers and Responsibilities) Act 2002_ (NSW)), police may stop, search, and detain a person or vehicle without a warrant.
- **The Threshold:** Police must have **"reasonable grounds to suspect"** that the person possesses stolen property, drugs, or a dangerous weapon.
- **What is "Reasonable Suspicion"?**
    - It is less than a reasonable belief, but **more than a mere hunch or possibility**.
    - There must be factual grounds that would create a suspicion in the mind of a reasonable observer.
    - **Key Cases:** **_George v Rockett_ (1990)** and **_R v Rondo_ (2001)**.
- **Emergency Entry:** Police have statutory powers to enter premises without a warrant in emergencies, such as to investigate/prevent domestic violence under **s 82 of LEPRA**.
- **Withdrawal of Consent:**
    - If police enter premises with permission (consent), and that permission is withdrawn, **they must leave within a reasonable time**.
    - Remaining on the property without a statutory power or an active breach of the peace makes them trespassers.
    - **Landmark Case:** **_Kuru v State of New South Wales_ (2008)**.
- **Statutory Power:** Governed by **s 99 of LEPRA**.
- **The Two-Step Test:** An arrest is only lawful if:
    1. The officer **reasonably suspects** the person has committed or is committing an offence.
    2. The arrest is **reasonably necessary** for one of the statutory reasons (e.g., to prevent flight, preserve evidence, stop a repeat offence, or protect safety).
- **Duty to State Reasons:**
    - Police must inform the arrested person of the reason they are being arrested at the time of arrest, or as soon as practicable.
    - **Key Cases:** **_State of New South Wales v Robinson_ (2019)**; _**Christie v Leachinsky**_.
- **Entry to Arrest:** Police can enter a property to arrest someone under **s 10 of LEPRA**, but must comply with strict statutory protections. Case: **_Lippl v Haines_ (1989)**.
