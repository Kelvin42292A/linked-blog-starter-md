# Element of assault

### **Physical Element of Assault**

**assault** in the broader (general) sense includes:

1.  **Assault (restricted/narrow sense)** A **voluntary act** that causes another person to **apprehend immediate unlawful (non-consensual) contact**.

2.  **Battery** The **actual application of unlawful (non-consensual) force/contact** to another person.

- The act must be **voluntary**.
- No **violence or injury** is required (even minor touching or a threat qualifies).
- Can be committed by **words alone** (no physical movement needed).
- The victim **does not need to actually be afraid** — it is sufficient that the conduct would cause a reasonable person to apprehend immediate unlawful contact.
- The threatened contact must be **immediate** (not in the distant future).
- The contact (or threatened contact) must be **without consent**.

### Fault element of assault

According to the case of _Fagan_, to be guilty of assault, the accused must perform the physical act with one of two states of mind:

1. **Intentionally:** They deliberately meant to create the fear of immediate unlawful violence in the victim's mind.
2. **Recklessly:** They didn't necessarily _want_ to scare the victim, but they foresaw that their actions might cause that fear, and they went ahead and did it anyway.

#### _**MacPherson v Brown**_

The bulk of the text focuses on pinning down exactly what "reckless" means in criminal law, as it can be a confusing term. The case of _MacPherson v Brown_ clarifies this:

- **It requires foresight (Subjective):** Recklessness means the defendant actually foresaw the probable consequences of their actions (that the victim would be put in fear) even if they didn't desire that outcome.
- **It is not just carelessness (Objective):** Chief Justice Bray makes a crucial distinction. Recklessness should _not_ be confused with mere negligence or "inadvertent conduct" (where the defendant didn't realize the risk, even if a reasonable person would have).

### Stalking, intimidation and domestic violence

**The Problem with Standard Assault**

- Standard assault requires a threat of _immediate_ harm.
- This makes it difficult to protect victims from stalkers or domestic abusers who make future threats or harass them over time.

**The Solution: A Specific Offence**

- The law created a separate crime for **stalking or intimidation** with the intent to cause fear of physical or mental harm.
- Intimidation isn't just physical threats—it also covers things like cyberbullying, online harassment, and threatening to damage someone's property.

**Apprehended Violence Orders (AVOs)**

- Instead of waiting for a crime to happen, courts can issue AVOs (restraining orders) to protect people from future harm.
- **ADVO:** For people who are (or were) in a domestic relationship.
- **APVO:** For people not in a domestic relationship (like neighbours or coworkers).

**The Test to Get an AVO** To grant an AVO, the court looks at the victim's state of mind, not the offender's intent. The court must be satisfied of two things:

- **Subjective Test:** The victim _actually_ fears violence, stalking, or intimidation.
- **Objective Test:** It is _reasonable_ for them to have that fear.

**Breaching an AVO**

- A court can tailor the AVO with specific rules (e.g., banning the person from the victim's workplace or home).
- Breaking an AVO is a criminal offence carrying up to two years in prison or a fine.
- If a person breaks the AVO by committing an act of actual physical violence, the law states they should go to jail.

# Battery
[[R v Williams (1990) 50 A Crim R 213]]

### Physical elements of battery

- **The Core Rule:** Battery is the actual, non-consensual physical contact with another person.
- **Assault vs. Battery:** While assault is making someone _fear_ immediate contact, battery is the _actual_ physical touching.
- **Any Touch Counts:** The law considers every person's body to be inviolate (sacred). Because it's too hard to draw a line between different degrees of violence, even the absolute slightest touch can be a battery. It does not have to cause an injury.
- **Direct Result:** The contact must be the direct and immediate result of a voluntary act by the accused.

**The Exception: Implied Consent** Because a strict "no-touching" rule would make daily life impossible, the law makes exceptions for ordinary social contact:

- **Everyday Life:** You cannot claim battery for the normal, unavoidable contacts of daily life. For example, getting bumped in a crowded supermarket or receiving a friendly slap on the back at a party is generally legally acceptable because society "impliedly consents" to it.
- **Getting Attention:** Tapping someone on the shoulder merely to get their attention is usually fine, provided you don't use more force than necessary.
- **The Limit (No Restraint):** There is a firm line between touching someone to get their attention and grabbing them to physically restrain them. Unless someone has lawful authority (like a police officer making a valid arrest), grabbing someone to hold them back is a battery.
- **Persistence:** Even a tap on the shoulder can turn into a battery if the person continues to do it after it becomes obvious the contact is unwanted.

### Fault element of battery

- **The Core Rule:** To be guilty of battery, the accused must apply physical force to the victim either **intentionally** or **recklessly**.
- **Intentional:** The person deliberately meant to physically strike, touch, or apply force to the victim without their consent.
- **Reckless (Subjective Test):** The person didn't necessarily plan or want to hit the victim, but they _actually foresaw_ (knew) that their actions _might_ result in hitting someone, and they went ahead and did it anyway (they didn't care about the risk).
- **The Standard for Recklessness:** In the context of battery, recklessness just means foreseeing the _possibility_ of unlawful physical contact. It is about what was actually in the accused person's mind at the time, not just what a reasonable person would have realized.

#### Aggravated assaults

An aggravated assault is simply a basic assault (or battery) plus an **"aggravating factor"** that makes the crime more serious.

**Types of Aggravating Factors** The text gives two main examples of things that aggravate an assault:

1. **The Injury Caused:** e.g., the assault resulted in Actual Bodily Harm (ABH), Wounding, or Grievous Bodily Harm (GBH).
2. **The Victim's Job:** e.g., the person assaulted was a police officer or law enforcement officer.

**The _Williams_ Case**

- If you are charged with an aggravated assault based on the injury you caused (like ABH), the prosecution **does not** need to prove you _intended_ to cause that specific injury.
- They only need to prove you had the mental state (intent or recklessness) for a _basic_ assault. If you commit a basic assault and ABH happens as a result, you are guilty of the aggravated offence.

### Wounding

- **The Core Rule:** A legal "wound" is an injury that completely breaks the continuity of the skin.
- **The "Two-Layer" Requirement:** A superficial scratch is not enough. The injury must break through _both_ the outer layer (epidermis) and the inner layer (dermis) of the skin.
- **Internal Skin Breaks Count:** While a purely internal bruised organ or unbroken skin over a broken bone doesn't count, a break to the skin _inside_ the body does. For example, if a blow breaks a jaw and tears the skin inside the mouth, it is legally a wound even if the external face is untouched.
- **No Weapons Necessary:** An offender does not need to use a knife or tool to cause a wound. A bare fist that results in a split lip perfectly fits the technical definition.

### Grievous Bodily Harm (GBH)

- **The Basic Definition:** GBH simply means a "really serious" bodily injury.
- **Specific Legal Examples:** The Crimes Act specifically states that GBH includes:
    - The destruction of a foetus (causing a miscarriage).
    - Any permanent or serious disfigurement.
    - Passing on a severe bodily disease (like HIV).

#### The Fault Element: Recklessness & GBH

- **What Recklessness Means Here:** Acting "recklessly" means the accused actually foresaw the _possibility_ that their actions would cause harm, but they went ahead and took the risk anyway. (They only need to foresee it as a _possibility_, not a _probability_).
- **The "Golden Rule" for Reckless GBH (The 2012 Amendment):** To be convicted of "reckless GBH" or "reckless wounding," the prosecution **only** has to prove you were reckless about causing _some basic physical harm_ (Actual Bodily Harm). They **do not** have to prove you actually foresaw causing the "really serious" injury (GBH) that ended up happening.

### Assault and battery summarised

### The Broad Definition

In criminal law, the word **"Assault"** is an umbrella term that covers two different things:

1. **Assault** (the threat of contact).
2. **Battery** (the actual physical contact).

- **No Anger Needed:** You do not need to have "hostile intent" (meaning you don't have to be angry or hateful) to commit an assault or battery.
- **The Recklessness Rule:** If you are acting recklessly, it means you _actually foresaw_ the risk of causing contact (or fear of contact) and ignored it. Just being careless or not paying attention is not enough.

### Assault (The Threat)

- **The Core Rule:** It is an intentional or reckless act that makes the victim expect _immediate_, unwanted physical contact.
- **No Touch Needed:** You don't have to touch the person at all.
- **Awareness is Key:** The victim must be aware of the threat. Interestingly, it can still be an assault even if the victim isn't personally "scared" (e.g., a brave police officer facing down a threat), as long as they apprehend the contact.
- **Continuing Fear:** If a victim is unlawfully trapped, the threat is considered "immediate" the entire time they are trapped.
- **Future Threats Don't Count:** Vague threats about doing something in the future are not assaults. (This is exactly why the separate "Stalking and Intimidation" laws were created!).

### Battery (The Physical Contact)

- **The Core Rule:** The direct, intentional, or reckless application of physical force without the victim's consent.
- **Continuous Acts:** A battery can be a continuous act. If you accidentally touch someone or apply force, but then you _intentionally_ keep doing it once you realize what has happened, it becomes a crime (this comes from the famous _Fagan_ case where a man accidentally drove onto a police officer's foot, but then deliberately refused to move the car).
- **The Limits of Consent:** The prosecution must prove the victim didn't consent. However, the law says a person generally cannot legally consent to having serious injuries inflicted upon themselves.

# Consent
[[R v Brown [1993] UKHL 19]]

### Limits of consent

**The General Rule** While you can consent to minor touching (which prevents it from being a simple battery), there is a strict limit. As a general rule, **you cannot legally consent to having Actual Bodily Harm (ABH), Wounding, or Grievous Bodily Harm (GBH) inflicted upon you.** If the harm is serious enough, it is a crime regardless of whether the victim agreed to it.

**The Recognized Exceptions** Because a strict ban on all bodily harm would make society impossible, the law has carved out special "lawful exceptions." You _can_ legally consent to injuries in the following situations:

- **Surgery and Medical Treatment**
- **Body Modification:** Tattoos, ritual circumcision, and ear-piercing.
- **Contact Sports:** E.g., Boxing or football (as long as the contact is within the rules and expected risks of the game).
- **Rough Horseplay:** Everyday physical jokes or playground games.

_Note:_ You cannot consent to street fighting, duelling, or prize-fighting. The law treats these as crimes because they are purely for hurting each other and disturb the public peace.

**Consent in Private Sexual Acts / BDSM** The law draws a very fine line regarding what adults can consent to behind closed doors:

- _**R v Brown:**_ A famous case involving a group of men engaging in consensual sado-masochism (BDSM). The court ruled they were guilty of assault. The law states that society must protect itself against a "cult of violence," and you cannot consent to cruelty or severe harm just for sexual gratification.
- _**R v Emmett:**_ Even in a heterosexual marriage, if the consensual sexual acts carry a high risk of serious injury or death (like near-asphyxiation or burns), consent is not a defense.
- **_R v Wilson_ (The Exception):** A man branded his initials onto his wife's buttocks with a hot knife with her full consent. The court allowed this, viewing it as a private matrimonial matter closer to "consensual tattooing" rather than unlawful violence.

**Sexually Transmitted Diseases** Consenting to sexual intercourse is **not** legally consenting to catching a disease. Intentionally or recklessly transmitting a serious disease (like HIV) to a partner is legally considered inflicting Grievous Bodily Harm (GBH).

**The Ultimate Limit** You can **never** legally consent to your own death. Consensual killing is always murder.

### Sexually transmitted diseases and consent
[[Zaburoni v The Queen [2016] HCA 12]]
[[Aubrey v The Queen [2017] HCA 18; (2017) 260 CLR 305]]

**The Old Rule (No Longer Valid)**

- Historically (under an old 1888 case called _R v Clarence_), the law said that if a woman consented to sexual intercourse with her husband, she could not claim assault if he gave her an STD, because she had consented to the physical act.
- **This is no longer the law in New South Wales.** **The Modern Law: STDs are Grievous Bodily Harm (GBH)**
- Under the NSW _Crimes Act_, a "grievous bodily disease" (like HIV) is legally classified as Grievous Bodily Harm (GBH).
- Consenting to sexual intercourse is **not** the same as consenting to catch a serious disease. If a person does not know their partner has an STD, they cannot legally consent to the risk of catching it.
- Furthermore, you do not need to use physical violence (like a punch or a weapon) to "inflict" GBH. Passing on a disease through consensual sex legally counts as inflicting harm (_Aubrey v R_).

**Intentional vs. Reckless Transmission**

It is a serious crime to infect another person with a disease, but the law distinguishes between two states of mind:

- **Intentional Transmission:** The prosecution must prove your specific purpose or goal was to infect the other person. This is very hard to prove. For example, in _Zaburoni v R_, the court ruled that just because a man had frequent unprotected sex with his partner knowing he had HIV, it didn't automatically prove he _intended_ to infect her (he might have just been doing it for his own selfish gratification).
- **Reckless Transmission:** This is much more common. If you know you have a serious STD, you foresee the risk that you might pass it on to your partner, and you go ahead and have unprotected sex anyway without telling them, you are guilty of recklessly inflicting GBH.

### Consent reviewed

**The Core Conflict** The law constantly struggles to balance two things:

1. **The State’s Role:** Protecting individuals from making terrible, harmful decisions.
2. **Individual Freedom:** Allowing people the right to choose what happens to their own bodies.

**The Problem with Current Laws** The report criticizes the current court-made rules as messy and inconsistent. Right now, courts just use a vague "public policy" excuse to pick and choose what harm is acceptable (like surgery, tattooing, and rugby) and what isn't (like street fights and extreme BDSM). Cases like _Brown_ (where extreme BDSM was illegal) and _Wilson_ (where burning initials with a hot knife was legal) show how confusing the law has become.

**The Proposed Solution (A Clear List)** To fix this confusion, the Committee recommended throwing out the vague rules and using a simple, list-based approach:

- **The New Rule:** You can legally consent to conduct that causes _basic harm_, but you **cannot** consent to conduct that causes _serious harm_.
- **The Approved List:** You can only consent to _serious harm_ if it falls into one of these specific, approved categories:
    1. Medical and dental treatment
    2. Contact sports (played within the rules)
    3. Tattooing
    4. Genuine religious mortifications (rituals)

# Sexual offences

**The Need for Reform** Historically, sexual offence laws were outdated. Over the last few decades, the law has been heavily reformed due to three main factors:

1. **Changing social attitudes** in the 1970s.
2. **The Women’s Movement**, which highlighted how old rape laws were failing victims.
3. **Child Protection**, culminating in the 2018 legal reforms triggered by the Royal Commission into Institutional Responses to Child Sexual Abuse.

**Major Changes to the Law** To reflect modern understanding, several traditional legal concepts were completely overhauled:

- **New Terminology:** The term "rape" was generally replaced with "sexual assault." Old terms like "indecent assault" were replaced with "sexual touching" and "sexual act."
- **Broader Definitions:** The legal definition of sexual assault was expanded to include penetration by objects or other body parts, not just a penis.
- **Marriage Exemptions Removed:** The law finally recognized that rape can occur within a marriage.
- **Age Exemptions Removed:** The old legal presumption that a boy under 14 was incapable of committing rape was abolished.
- **Courtroom Protections:** Evidentiary rules were changed to reduce trauma for victims (e.g., allowing children to give evidence via CCTV instead of facing the accused in court).

**The Core Objectives of Modern Sexual Offence Laws** The Model Criminal Code outlines several key goals for these laws:

- **Protect Autonomy:** No one should be forced to submit to a sexual act without their true consent.
- **Graded Seriousness:** The law ranks crimes by severity: sexual _penetration_ is the most serious, followed by sexual _touching_, and finally sexual _acts_ (which involve neither penetration nor touching).
- **Gender Neutrality:** The laws must apply equally regardless of the gender or sexuality of the victim or the perpetrator.
- **Balance of Rights:** The law must protect vulnerable victims (like children and women, who make up the majority of victims) while still respecting the fundamental rights of the accused, such as the presumption of innocence.

Because criminal law is state-based, the changes have been "piecemeal." This means that while the general themes above apply nationwide, different states still have different specific definitions, fault elements, and ages of consent.

### Statutory and common law framework

**The Five Main Categories** Following the major reforms in 2018, the _Crimes Act 1900 (NSW)_ organizes sexual offences into five basic categories based on the type of conduct:

1. **Sexual Assault** (and Aggravated Sexual Assault)
2. **Assault with Intent** to have sexual intercourse
3. **Sexual Touching** (and Aggravated Sexual Touching)
4. **Sexual Act** (and Aggravated Sexual Act)
5. **Offences against Children and Vulnerable Persons**

**Key Changes to Offence Names** The 2018 reforms modernized the language used in the law. Two older, outdated offences were replaced:

- The old offence of **"Indecent Assault"** has been replaced by **"Sexual Touching."**
- The old offence of **"Act of Indecency"** has been replaced by **"Sexual Act."**

**Abolishing Old Immunities** The _Crimes Act_ specifically wiped out two old, outdated common law rules:

- **The Age Immunity:** It used to be legally presumed that a boy under 14 could not commit rape. This is no longer true.
- **The Marriage Immunity:** It used to be presumed that a husband could not legally rape his wife. This immunity has been abolished.

**Other Specific Offences** The law also covers situations where a victim is forced into sexual acts even if actual intercourse doesn't occur, such as forced self-manipulation (masturbation), and strictly prohibits female genital mutilation.

### Intention and recklessness, capacity and consent, knowledge and mistake

#### 1. Capacity to Consent

Before looking at whether someone _did_ consent, the law asks if they _could_ consent.

- **The Test:** To have capacity, the victim must have enough knowledge or understanding to comprehend two things: the physical act itself, and the fact that it is of a _sexual_ nature. (If a disability or intoxication prevents this understanding, they lack capacity).

#### 2. What Constitutes "Consent"

The courts in _Mueller_ laid down some strict boundaries on what consent actually looks like in practice:

- **Submission is NOT Consent:** Just because someone gives in or stops fighting back due to fear or authority does not mean they consented.
- **Silence is NOT Consent:** The absence of a "no" or a lack of physical resistance does not automatically mean "yes."
- **Reluctant Consent:** This is a tricky area. The law states that if someone ultimately agrees, even if they do so reluctantly or only after a lot of verbal persuasion, it is still technically "consent" (provided there was no threats or duress).

#### 3. The Fault Element: Knowledge, Mistake & Recklessness

For a sexual assault charge, the prosecution must prove the accused performed the act with a guilty mind. This means proving they either **knew** there was no consent, or were **reckless** about it.

The biggest takeaway from cases like _Kitchener_ and _Fitzgerald v Kennard_ is how the law defines **Recklessness**:

- **Advertent Recklessness:** The accused actually thought, _"She might not be consenting,"_ but went ahead and did it anyway.
- **Inadvertent Recklessness (The "Couldn't Care Less" Rule):** What if the accused didn't even think about whether the victim was consenting or not? The courts ruled that this is _still_ recklessness. Failing to even consider if your partner is consenting shows a total indifference to their rights. If the thought "didn't enter my mind," the law treats you as reckless.

A person is guilty if they forced the act knowing there was no consent, if they realized the risk of non-consent and ignored it, OR if they were so indifferent that they didn't even bother to think about whether the other person was consenting at all.
