# Larceny (Crimes Act - Section 117)

Larceny is primarily governed by Section 117 of the Crimes Act. To prove larceny, the prosecution must prove beyond a reasonable doubt that both the physical acts (Actus Reus) and the mental intentions (Mens Rea) were present.

## 1. Actus Reus (The Physical Elements)

The prosecution must prove all three of the following physical elements:

- **Property capable of being stolen:** Section 4 of the Crimes Act defines property as "every description of real and personal property". This includes illegal property (like illicit drugs) and even body parts. Property that you jointly own or have a lawful right to possess generally cannot be stolen.
- **Asportation (Taken and carried away):** This refers to the physical movement of the property. Even a "slight sufficient movement" is enough to satisfy this element.
- **Without consent of the owner:** The property must be taken without the owner's permission. If you are in a store, the owner grants you an implied license to take an item to the cash register. If you act outside of that license (e.g., concealing the item in your pocket instead of taking it to the counter), you have taken it without consent.

## 2. Mens Rea (The Mental Elements)

The prosecution must also prove all three of the following mental elements:

- **Intention to permanently deprive:** The defendant must intend to exercise _ownership_ over the property, not merely _possession_. Under Section 118, if you take an item with the intention to exercise ownership, it is still larceny even if you plan to return it later.
- **Dishonesty:** Section 4B of the Crimes Act defines this using a two-part test. The act must be considered dishonest according to the standards of ordinary people (objective test) , and the defendant must have known it was dishonest by those ordinary standards (subjective test).
- **No honest belief of claim of right:** The prosecution must prove the defendant did not have a genuine belief that they had a legal right to the property. This is a subjective test—the belief does not have to be reasonable, but it must be genuinely held. It must be a belief in a _legal_ right (not just a moral one), and the defendant cannot take more than the exact value they believe they are owed.

## 3. Timing of the Offence

- **The Contemporaneous Rule:** Generally, the Actus Reus and Mens Rea must occur at the exact same time.
- **Exception (Doctrine of Continuing Trespass):** If a person accidentally takes property without the intent to steal it, but later discovers what they have and _then_ decides to keep it, the law applies the mental intent retroactively to the initial physical taking.

## 4. Special Types of Larceny

- **Larceny by Finding:** "Finders keepers" is not the law. If you find property and the rightful owner can be identified, you are obliged to return it.
- **Larceny by Mistake:** If property is handed over due to a mistake, consent is removed if it is a "fundamental mistake". A mistake is fundamental if it involves the identity of the property, the identity of the person, or the quantity of the property. **Exception:** Overpaying someone in cash is not considered a fundamental mistake.

## 5. Key Cases to Remember

- **_Illich_:** The critical case establishing the elements of larceny and the rules for fundamental mistakes (specifically the cash exception).
- **_Foster_:** The key case explaining the "intention to permanently deprive," distinguishing between exercising mere possession versus exercising ownership.
- **_Fuge_:** The defining case that outlines the elements required to argue an "honest belief of claim of right".
