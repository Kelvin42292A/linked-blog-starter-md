# ⚖️ Legal Research & Writing — Lecture Notes

## 🔍 1. Statutory Research & Legislation Tools
* **NSW Legislation Website**: 
    * Official government portal; updates faster than general databases.
    * Best for viewing an entire Act in context and tracking **point-in-time versions** (e.g., viewing a section's wording from 2013).
* **AustLII**: 
    * Great for quick searches of specific statutory sections or printing single provisions.
    * Con: Updates slightly slower than official platforms.
* **USYD Library Subscriptions**: 
    * Access via Google `library USYD` → Browse → Law.
    * Provides free student access to premium, high-cost legal search engines.
* **Lexis+ Australia vs. Westlaw Australia**: 
    * The two primary database suites used in professional practice.
    * They hold different publisher collections (e.g., Lexis carries *Australian Law Reports* [ALR]; Westlaw holds *Commonwealth Law Reports* [CLR]).

### 🛠️ Statutory Interpretation Example (*Graffiti Control Act*)
* **Element Breakdown**: Every statutory offense must be pulled apart into explicit legal elements (e.g., "a person" + "without reasonable excuse" + "intentionally mark" + "property").
* **Definitions**: Always use internal statutory definitions hyperlinked within the Act rather than general dictionary meanings.
* **Onus of Proof**: Pay close attention to clauses that shift the burden of proof to the defendant (e.g., proving a "reasonable excuse").
* **Penalty Units**: NSW uses standard monetary "units" instead of flat dollar amounts. This allows the government to adjust fine inflation globally across all legislation without rewriting individual Acts.

## 📑 2. Legal Citation & AGLC4 Basics
* **Precision**: Minor structural or citation errors immediately signal a lack of professionalism to a judge or senior partner.
* **Sources to Avoid**: Do not cite law firm blogs or random websites. They oversimplify legal principles and are often drafted by unverified junior staff.
* **Hierarchy of Authority**: Prioritize decisions from superior courts, peer-reviewed legal journals, and official legislation.

### 🏛️ Reported vs. Unreported Judgments
* **Reported Cases**: Formally published in authorized legal volumes. They include a professional summary called a **headnote**.
* **Unreported Cases**: The raw text of a judgment uploaded immediately after delivery (found quickly on AustLII or NSW CaseLaw).

### 🖋️ Formatting Examples
* **Pinpoint References**: The trailing number in a citation indicates the exact page (reported) or paragraph bracket (medium-neutral) you are referencing. Always include them.
* **In-Text Citation**: `Leichhardt Municipal Council v Montgomery (2007) 233 ALR 200, 210.`
* **Bibliography Citation**: `Leichhardt Municipal Council v Montgomery (2007) 233 ALR 200.`
* **Statute Abbreviations**: 
    * Use "**Section**" if beginning a sentence.
    * Use "**s**" for a single section in the middle/end of a sentence (e.g., s 4).
    * Use "**ss**" for multiple sections (e.g., ss 12–15).

## ✍️ 3. Legal Writing Structure: IRAC
The foundational structural template for writing objective legal advice or problem-style law assignments. Do **not** use these terms as explicit subheadings in your text; let them flow naturally.

* **I — Issue**: Pinpoint and clearly state the specific legal problem to be solved.
* **R — Rule**: Outline the relevant statutory provisions or case law precedents.
* **A — Application**: Critically analyze how the legal rules apply directly to the unique facts of your client's situation.
    * *Tip*: When using a precedent case, do not just summarize its facts. Highlight how your client’s facts are either distinct from or similar to that precedent to build your argument.
* **C — Conclusion**: Provide a definitive, logical answer to the issue.

⚠️ **Avoid Over-Certainty**: Law is rarely black and white. If a point is highly arguable, state alternative outcomes or map out backup arguments (e.g., *"If the court finds that acceptance did not occur, the secondary issue of consideration must be evaluated..."*).
