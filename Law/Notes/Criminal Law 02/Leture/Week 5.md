**Criminal Law Lecture Notes: Sexual Consent and Statutory Reforms**

**Core Legal Principles**

- **Statute over Common Law:** When addressing criminal law problems, always look to the statutory framework (the Crimes Act) first, identify the relevant provision, and then look at supplementary common law.
- **Legislative Amendments:** Laws often change as a reflection of societal shifts or in response to case law where the judiciary signals a need for parliament to improve the framework.

**The Affirmative Consent Model (2021 Reforms)**

The most significant takeaway from this area of law is the **radical shift toward an affirmative consent model** enacted via the _Crimes Legislation Amendment (Sexual Consent Reforms) Act 2021_ (commenced June 1, 2022).

- **Affirmative Communication Required:** Consent is not valid unless a person says or does something to actively communicate their consent.
- **No Presumption:** You cannot assume consent from a lack of resistance or a lack of protest.
- **Ongoing and Specific:** Consent must be mutual, ongoing, and specific. Consenting to one sexual activity on one occasion does not mean consent is given for another activity, or on another occasion. Consent previously given can be withdrawn at any time.
- **Totality of Circumstances:** Courts will look at the entire context, including the relationship between the parties and all communications before, during, and after the act, to determine if consent was obtained.

**Elements of Sexual Assault Offenses**

To secure a conviction, the Crown must prove the elements of the offense, but the statutory reforms have significantly shifted how the mental element is handled.

- **Actus Reus (The Physical Act):** This is generally the more straightforward element to establish (the physical sexual act).
- **Mens Rea (The Mental Element):** The Crown must prove beyond a reasonable doubt that the accused _knew_ the victim was not consenting, was _reckless_ as to consent, or had a belief about consent that was _not reasonable in the circumstances_.
- **The Burden Shift:** There is now a "seismic shift" placing a strict onus on the defense to prove that the accused actively took steps to obtain affirmative consent.
- **Recklessness:** This is divided into two distinct categories:
    - **Advertent recklessness:** The accused considered the possibility that the other person was not consenting but continued anyway.
    - **Inadvertent recklessness:** The accused did not consider at all whether the person was consenting.
- **Intoxication:** **Self-induced intoxication of the accused is not a valid excuse** and cannot be used to negate the _mens rea_ element.

**Negation of Consent**

Consent must be freely and voluntarily given. Consent is negated (invalidated) if the victim:

- **Lacks capacity:** e.g., due to being too young, too old, or having cognitive impairments.
- **Lacks opportunity:** e.g., is unconscious or asleep.
- Is subjected to **threats of force or terror**, or is unlawfully detained.
- Is heavily **intoxicated** (impairing cognitive ability to freely consent).
- Consents due to a **mistaken belief** or due to an **abuse of a position of authority/trust** (coercive conduct).

**Child Sexual Abuse & Grooming**

Major reforms were also introduced via the _Criminal Legislation Amendment (Child Sexual Abuse) Act 2018_, largely in response to the Royal Commission into Institutional Responses to Child Sexual Abuse.

- **New Offenses:** Includes failing to report/protect children from abuse, sexting, and grooming.
- **Grooming:** Defined as behaviors (even over short periods) designed to establish trust or access to a child for unlawful sexual activity, such as offering material or financial benefits. The mere age difference establishes a severe power imbalance.

**Practical Tips for Law Students**

- **Tab Your Legislation & Textbooks:** Print and tab the Crimes Act heavily so you don't waste time in open-book exams searching for provisions.
- **Use the Judicial Commission Bench Books:** This is a free, up-to-date resource showing the exact directions judges give to juries regarding offenses like sexual assault.
- **Read Judgments:** Reading case law (including dissenting judgments) is crucial for understanding how the judicial bench thinks.
- **1-Page Weekly Summaries:** Create a single-page summary of the main points and cases at the end of each week, and review previous weeks while doing so to build a mental chronology.
- **Go to Court:** Visit open courts during semester breaks. Observing real cross-examinations and courtroom dynamics will rapidly improve your practical legal understanding.
