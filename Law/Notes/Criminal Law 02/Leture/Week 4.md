**The Framework of Assault (New South Wales)**

Assault is categorized into two types: **Physical assault** (actual unlawful physical contact) and **Psychic assault** (words or actions causing the victim to apprehend immediate unlawful contact).

When answering an exam question, you should identify all potential offences, starting with Common Assault and working your way up to more serious charges.

**1. The Four Key Statutory Offences (Crimes Act)**

The Crimes Act outlines the primary assault charges based on the level of injury and the defendant's state of mind:

- **Section 61 (Common Assault):** The baseline offence with a maximum penalty of two years imprisonment. This covers minor incidents without lasting injury.
- **Section 59 (Actual Bodily Harm - ABH):** Carries a maximum penalty of five years. ABH is defined by common law as any injury that is "more than merely transient or trifling" (_**Donovan**_). It also includes recognized psychiatric illnesses like PTSD resulting from actions like silent phone calls (_**Ireland**_).
- **Section 35 (Reckless Grievous Bodily Harm or Wounding):** Carries a penalty of 7 to 14 years.
    - **Wounding** requires breaking both the inner and outer layers of the skin (_**Shepherd**_).
    - **Grievous Bodily Harm (GBH)** is defined as a "very serious injury" (_**Smith**_, _**Blackwell**_) and can include transmitting serious diseases like HIV.
    - _Crucial Exam Note:_ Following the _**Blackwell**_ case and 2012 amendments, the prosecution only needs to prove the defendant was reckless as to causing _Actual Bodily Harm_, even if GBH was the actual result.
- **Section 33 (Intentional GBH or Wounding):** The most severe charge, carrying a maximum of 25 years. It requires proof that the defendant specifically intended to cause a very serious injury.

**2. The Elements of Assault (Common Law)**

Because Section 61 does not define the elements of assault, you must rely on common law, primarily the case of _**Fagan**_. To secure a conviction, the prosecution must prove both the Actus Reus and the Mens Rea beyond a reasonable doubt.

**Actus Reus (The Physical Act):**

- **For Psychic Assault:** The threat must instill a fear of _immediate_ physical violence. Vague, future threats are generally not assault (_**Knight**_). However, look at the context; being trapped in a speeding car makes future threats immediate (_**Zanker v Vartzokas**_).
- **For Physical Assault:** Unlawful physical contact without consent. While consent usually makes contact lawful, public policy exceptions exist. For example, individuals cannot legally consent to extreme sadomasochistic harm or public street fighting (_**Brown**_).
- **Conditional Threats:** If a threat has a condition that negates immediate fear ("If the police weren't here, I would strike you"), it is not an assault (_**Tuberville v Savage**_). However, if the condition is violently disproportionate to the situation, it can still be assault (_**Rosa v Samuels**_).
- **Victim's Fear:** Conflicting authorities exist on whether the victim must actually be scared. _**Brady v Schatzel**_ establishes that it is sufficient if the victim simply _apprehends_ (expects) immediate unlawful violence, even if they are brave and not frightened.

**Mens Rea (The Mental State):**

- **Intention:** The defendant meant to produce the particular result (_**Zabaroni**_).
- **Recklessness:** A subjective test evaluating whether the defendant foresaw a "real possibility" that their actions could cause harm, but went ahead anyway (_**MacPherson v Brown**_, _**Chen**_).
- **Wicked Negligence (Rare):** Under Section 54, an unlawful or negligent act/omission causing GBH can carry a two-year penalty (_**R v D**_), though this is rarely prosecuted compared to other charges.

**3. Intimidation (A Powerful Alternative Offence)**

Always check if the facts support a charge under **Section 13 of the Crimes (Domestic and Personal Violence) Act**.

- It covers stalking or intimidating conduct (including cyberbullying and harassment) intending to cause the victim to fear physical or mental harm.
- _Exam advantage:_ It is easier for the prosecution to prove than psychic assault because it **does not require the fear to be immediate**, and carries a strict penalty of five years.
