**1. Involuntary Manslaughter & UDA Updates**

- **The Unlawful and Dangerous Act (UDA) Test is 100% Objective:** Under _**R v Besson (2004)**_, a dangerous act is one that a reasonable person would have realized exposed another to an appreciable risk of serious injury.
- **No Subjective Context Allowed:** The court refused to allow the subjective features of a battered spouse (past abuse, torment, torment history) to be factored into the UDA liability test. The trial judge ruled that domestic violence history is strictly a matter for **sentencing/penalty**, not for deciding the objective seriousness of the act under UDA.

**2. Assault Causing Death (The "One-Punch" Law - s 25A)**

Introduced in NSW in 2012 as a separate statutory offense to UDA manslaughter following high-profile fatal street assaults.

- **The 4 Elements:**
    1. The defendant intentionally hit another person (could be 1 punch or 20 punches).
    2. The assault was unlawful.
    3. The defendant was _not_ acting in self-defense.
    4. The assault caused the victim's death.
- **Intoxication Rules:** Self-induced intoxication is **not** a defense. The accused is legally assessed as a sober person.
- **Defenses:**
    - The intoxication was _not_ self-induced (e.g., drink was spiked).
    - The defendant suffered from a cognitive/intellectual impairment at the time of the assault (e.g., acquired brain injury).
- **The Penalty:** If the defendant is 18 years or older, the maximum penalty is **25 years** in prison.
- **Alternative Verdict Rule:** An accused cannot be convicted of both murder/manslaughter and Section 25A for the same act (no double convictions).

**3. Extreme Provocation (s 23 of the Crimes Act)**

Under the **2014 legislative amendments**, the defense was renamed and substantially narrowed from "provocation" to **"extreme provocation"**. It operates purely to reduce a murder charge to voluntary manslaughter.

**The 4 Statutory Elements (s 23(2)):**

- **Element A (Conduct):** The killing must be in response to the conduct of the deceased towards or affecting the accused. _(Note: Hearsay provocation remains a gray area under R v Davis—where an accused is merely told about provocative conduct that occurred in their absence)_.
- **Element B (The Serious Crime Trigger):** The deceased's conduct must amount to a **serious indictable offense** punishable by **5 or more years** of imprisonment (defined in s 4 of the _Crimes Act_).
    - _Exclusions:_ This 5-year requirement completely excludes "mere words" (insults about your mother, religion, etc.) from ever being enough.
    - _**R v Turnbull (2016):**_ A farmer slowly shot an environmental officer to death, claiming he was provoked by the officer's "harassment" (using drones to monitor illegal tree clearing). The judge (Johnson J) refused to let extreme provocation go to the jury. Lawful execution of public duties by an official is _not_ stalking or harassment and cannot constitute a serious indictable offense.
- **Element C (Subjective Loss of Control):** The conduct must have actually caused the accused to lose self-control at the time of the act.
- **Element D (The Two-Limb "Ordinary Person" Test):** The deceased's conduct could have caused an _ordinary person_ to lose self-control and form an intent to kill or cause GBH.

**Applying the Two-Limb Test (s 23(2)(d))—Critical Exam Distinction:**

- **Limb 1: Assessing "Gravity" (Subjective):** Juries **can** look at the personal characteristics of the accused (age, sex, race, culture, immaturity, relationship history, ethnic background) to determine how severely the provocation hit them.
- **Limb 2: Assessing "Self-Control" (Strictly Objective):** When deciding how much self-control is expected of the "ordinary person," the jury **can only consider the accused's age and maturity**.
    - _**R v Rogers (2021):**_ An obsessively jealous husband smothered his wife with a pillow after discovering a non-sexual "loving friendship" with a school friend. Juries must completely disregard personal weaknesses like obsessive-compulsive disorders, morbid jealousy, or depressive states. A jealous or obsessive personality cannot be imputed to the hypothetical ordinary person.

**4. Key Exclusions to Extreme Provocation (s 23(3))**

1. **Non-Violent Sexual Advances:** Explicitly barred from the defense (reversing the controversial common law precedent of _**Green v R**_).
2. **Self-Induced Intoxication:** The accused's alcohol or drug use cannot be considered; the hypothetical ordinary person is assumed to be sober.
3. **Self-Induced Provocation (The "Trap" Rule):** The defense is barred if the accused deliberately incited the deceased's conduct specifically to provide an excuse to use violence against them.
4. **Mistake of Fact (_R v Dib_):** If the accused stabs the wrong person during a group altercation, they cannot claim provocation unless they held an _honest and reasonable belief_ that the specific deceased was the person who actually engaged in the provocative conduct.
