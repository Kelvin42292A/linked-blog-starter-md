## 3) Larceny (and robbery)

### Larceny — exam structure

**Actus reus**

- **Property** capable of being stolen (within the statutory definition).
- **Taking** (and “carrying away/asportation” if your course treats it as a separate element).
- **Without the owner’s consent**.

**Mens rea**

- **Dishonesty** (_s 4B Crimes Act 1900 (NSW)_).
- Intention to **permanently deprive**.
- No honest belief in a **claim of right** (if raised, address it directly).

**Quick issue-spotting prompts**

- Whose **property/possession** was it at each moment?
- Was it **handed over**? If yes, was it **possession only** (possible larceny by trick) or also **title**?

### Key points / cases (as noted in class)

- **Ilich**: apply “common sense” to _s 117_.
    - Must prove **actus reus** and **mens rea** **beyond reasonable doubt**.
- **Foster v R**: _s 118 Crimes Act_ (intention to deprive).
- **Subjective focus**: what the accused honestly believed/intended (rather than what a reasonable person would do).
- **R v Fuge (Wood CJ)**: claim of right must be an **honest** belief; cannot exceed the **bona fide** claim amount.
- **Langham’s case**: taking property worth **more** than legally owed can defeat claim of right.

### Robbery

- **Robbery = larceny + assault**:
    - Larceny supplies the **dishonest taking**.
    - Assault supplies the **violence/threat** component.

**Exam approach**

1. Prove **larceny** first (all elements).
2. Then prove **assault** (apprehension + immediacy).
3. Connect the assault to the taking: violence/threat used **to obtain or retain** property.

### Extra larceny notes (tidy)

**Consent vs possession (larceny by trick)**

- If the owner **hands over possession only** (not ownership/title) because of a deception, this can support **larceny by trick** (subject to your NSW course framing).
- Exam approach: identify what was intended to be transferred — **possession**, **property**, and/or **title** — and whether any “consent” was vitiated by the trick.

**Mistake as to the person / identity (Ilich note)**

- Where property is **intended for X** but is **mistakenly handed to Y**, focus on:
    - what the owner **intended to consent to** (and to whom),
    - what Y **took/kept**, including any issue about taking **more than** what was intended to be handed over.

**Overpayment / money as “property” (Ilich note to confirm)**

- Note your class point: an **overpayment of money** may be treated differently from “handing over too much of a physical thing”, because **money is a special type of property** (confirm the exact NSW authority/ratio you’re meant to use).

## 4) Fraud (intro notes)

### Provision

- _Crimes Act 1900 (NSW) s 192E_ (obtain property or financial advantage by deception) — confirm any related sections your lecturer grouped with it (you noted “s 3”).

### Key idea (from class)

- “Obtaining a **financial advantage**” can be **temporary** (i.e. not necessarily permanent).

**Conduct / actus reus (as discussed)**

- Making/doing something you **know is false**, or
- **Inducing** another to believe something you know is false,
- By **dishonest and deceptive** conduct.

**Case**

- **DPP v Ray (1974)** — confirm the specific principle your lecturer attached to this case.

**Exam approach**

1. Identify the **representation/inducement** (what was said/done?).
2. Prove **knowledge of falsity**.
3. Prove **dishonesty** (use your course framing).
4. State the **gain/loss** (what was obtained or what detriment occurred?).

**One-line conclusion template**

- “Fraud under _s 192E_ is _[made out / not made out]_ because the accused, by deception, _[obtained property / obtained a financial advantage / caused a financial disadvantage]_, and acted dishonestly.”

## 5) Assault (NSW) + conditional threats

### Provisions mentioned (to verify)

**Crimes Act 1900 (NSW)**

- _s 61_ — **assault** (you noted this as “physical assault”; confirm the exact scope/wording your course uses for _s 61_).
- _s 59_ — **assault occasioning actual bodily harm (ABH)** (confirm the full provision and any elements/definitions emphasised in your materials).

### Main test (to confirm in NSW materials)

- **Fagan v Metropolitan Police Commissioner** (often cited for the definition/requirements of assault) — confirm this is the “main test” your course wants you to apply and note the exact elements your lecturer emphasised.

### Key concept: “present” threat in a practical sense

- Conditional/threat scenarios can be a **grey area**.
- **No assault if it is absolutely clear the threatened violence will not be carried out**.

**Case**

- **Tuberville v Savage**: words accompanying an act can **negate** an apparent threat (i.e., make clear there is no immediate intention to apply unlawful force).
- **Ryan v Kuhl** (Victorian authority): no assault where the complainant **did not apprehend/fear for their safety** (use as a contrast; confirm relevance/weight in your NSW materials).

### Exam checklist (quick)

- Was there an **act** causing the victim to **apprehend immediate unlawful violence**?
- Do the **words** make the threat immediate, or do they **negate** it (Tuberville)?
- If relying on a diagnosed injury (e.g., **depression**), note:
    - what the evidence is (diagnosis, symptoms),
    - how it links to the alleged assault conduct,
    - and whether it affects charge/gravity (confirm with NSW authority).

**One-line conclusion template**

- “Assault is _[made out / not made out]_ because the complainant _[did / did not]_ apprehend **immediate** unlawful violence, and any accompanying words _[did / did not]_ negate immediacy.”

**Key phrase to use (to confirm)**

### Concept Comparison: Larceny vs. Larceny by Trick vs. Fraud (s 192E)

When reading a fact scenario, track the property from the victim's hand to the accused's hand and ask:

| Offence | Did the victim voluntarily hand it over? | What did the victim intend to transfer? |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Normal Larceny** | **No.** (It was taken behind their back or by force/snatching). | **Nothing.** |
| **Larceny by Trick** | **Yes.** (But only because of a lie). | **Possession only.** (Expected it back / temporary). |
| **Fraud (s 192E)** | **Yes.** (But only because of a lie). | **Full Title / Ownership.** (Intended to part with it permanently). |

> [!tip] Issue-Spotting Quick Rule
> **Money:** If the exam facts involve money being handed over (e.g., the victim is tricked into paying $100 for a fake concert ticket), head straight to **Fraud (s 192E)**. When people hand over cash, they intend to pass full ownership of those specific notes/coins.
