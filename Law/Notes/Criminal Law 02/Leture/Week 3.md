### Larceny by Finding

- It is a **subjective test** focused on the defendant’s state of mind: whether the defendant honestly believed the property was abandoned or that the owner could be found.
- Mens rea is inferred from all facts and circumstances surrounding the finding and taking (actus reus).
- Reference to Justice Yeldon: belief is inferred from what the finder does and does not do, compared to actions of an honest finder.
- Example: surfboard left on beach for weeks → inference of abandonment possible.
- On balance of probabilities, defendant needs to show their version is consistent with evidence.
- **Section 124 Crimes Act** creates an alternate offence if the defendant initially lacked fraudulent intent but later fraudulently appropriates or retains property to secure a reward (e.g., diamond ring or dog cases). Relevant for high-distinction answers when facts involve finding + later demand for unreasonable reward.

### Larceny by Trick

- Key cases: **Pear (1779)** and **Justilius**.
- Principle: trickery induces handing over **possession only**, not ownership.
- Water slide example: impersonating staff to hold watch/phone while person goes on slide, then running off.
- Common law originally did not recognise larceny where consent (even if obtained by fraud) was given for possession.
- In **Justilius** (encyclopedias on approval): if owner intends ownership to pass (subject to payment), it is not larceny by trick.

### Fraud (s192E Crimes Act)

- Broad offence: any person who **by deception, dishonestly** obtains property belonging to another, or obtains financial advantage / causes financial disadvantage.
- Easier for prosecution than larceny; covers both possession and ownership.
- **Dishonesty**: defined in Crimes Act (s4B) – objective (ordinary standards) + subjective (known by defendant).
- **Deception**: defined in s192B (intentional or reckless representation by words/conduct as to fact or law). Key case **DPP v Ray** (restaurant meal – implied continuing representation of intention to pay; deception by omission when intention changed).
- **Obtaining property**: s192C – ownership, possession, or control (or inducing another to cause this).
- **Financial advantage/disadvantage**: defined in s192D.
- **Causation** essential: causal nexus between deception and obtaining property/advantage/disadvantage must be proved beyond reasonable doubt (Di Simeone case – false charity representation).
- **Honest belief of claim of right** remains a defence (common law preserved); prosecution must negate it. Discussed in context of Fuge and other cases.
- Contrast with **Moore** case: clear dishonesty (spending bank error money) but no deception → no fraud.

### Break and Enter

- Requires **breaking** + **entering** a dwelling house or building, with intent to commit a serious indictable offence (or committing one therein).
- **Stanford** case: no breaking if window already left open (no forcing/securing device overcome).
- Entering: physical entry of part of body or instrument.
- **BA v King** (High Court): co-tenant with current lease has lawful authority to enter → no trespass, no break and enter despite relationship breakdown and kicking door.
- Aggravated versions (armed, in company, wounding/GBH) carry higher penalties (20/25 years).

### Robbery (s94 Crimes Act)

- Larceny + assault (or threat) where violence/threat causes property to pass.
- Key case: **Smith & Desmond** – elements include intent to steal, threat/force putting person in fear, taking from the person.
- “Steal from the person” also under s94 (immediate physical presence/control) – e.g., snatch and grab with confrontation (Delk).
- Causation required: assault must cause property to pass (continuing transaction possible).
- **Honest belief of claim of right** applies (Langham case – crossbow/gun to recover exact money owed).
- Aggravated robbery (s95–98): in company, weapon, wounding/GBH, etc.

### General Advice

- Always use **ILAC** (Issue, Law – legislation first then cases, Application, Conclusion): assess if prosecution can prove every element beyond reasonable doubt.
- Identify all potential offences (e.g., larceny + fraud, or larceny + assault vs robbery).
- Refer to key cases for principles/definitions only; apply to facts.
