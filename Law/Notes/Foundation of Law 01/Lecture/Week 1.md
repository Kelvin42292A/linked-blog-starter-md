### Core Legal Concepts

- **Common Law vs. Criminal Code**: While some Australian states use a Criminal Code, NSW relies on its own Crimes Act supplemented by common law cases to define elements of offences and available defences.

- **Criminal vs. Civil Law**: The lecturer distinguished criminal law as being focused on punishment (imprisonment and fines) rather than the damages and compensation found in torts (civil law).

- **Standard of Proof**: The prosecution must prove every essential element of an offence **beyond a reasonable doubt**.


### Elements of a Crime

To establish criminal liability, the prosecution must typically prove two components:

1. **Actus Reus (Physical Element)**: The physical act must be voluntary. This includes "omissions" (failing to act), though these are generally only relevant in homicide cases where a specific duty of care exists.

2. **Mens Rea (Mental Element)**: The defendant's state of mind at the time of the act. This ranges from:
	- **Intention**: Meaning to produce a specific result.
	- **Recklessness**: Realizing a consequence might occur but proceeding anyway.
	- **Negligence**: A "wicked" falling short of the standard of care a reasonable person would exercise.


### Key Cases Mentioned

- **_Illich_**: Defines the elements of larceny.
- **_Ryan_ and _Katarzynski_**: Address the requirement for voluntary conduct.
- **_Royal_ and _Howlett_**: Focus on causation and whether a "break in the chain of causation" exists.
- **_Zabaroni_**: Provides a clear definition of legal intention.
- **_Nydum_ and _Lavender_**: Clarify the tests for criminal negligence.
