**Overview & Origins**

- **Effective Date:** The Australian Constitution came into effect on **1 January 1901**.
- **Significance:** It serves as the foundational legal document and supreme law of the land, often referred to as the "birth certificate of the nation".
- **Origins:** Initially enacted as **Clause 9 of a British Act of Parliament** (Commonwealth of Australia Constitution Act 1900) because Australia previously consisted of six self-governing British colonies. Today, its status as fundamental law rests on the sovereignty of the **Australian people**, who drafted and approved it.

**1. Amending the Constitution (Section 128)**

- The Constitution has a rigorous, deliberate amendment process to protect stability while allowing democratically supported reforms.
- **Parliamentary Approval:** An amendment must first pass with an absolute majority in both the House of Representatives and the Senate.
- **The Referendum:** It must then be presented to the electorate within two to six months.
- **The Double Majority Rule:** To succeed, the amendment requires both:
    1. A **majority of votes nationwide**.
    2. A **majority of voters in at least four of the six states**.
- _Note on Success Rate:_ Constitutional amendments are rare; since 1901, only 8 out of 44 proposed amendments have passed, such as the landmark 1967 referendum allowing the Commonwealth to legislate for Aboriginal Australians.

**2. Federalism & The Division of Powers**

The Constitution establishes a federal system, dividing powers between a central authority (the Commonwealth) and the six states.

**Commonwealth Powers**

- **Concurrent Powers (Section 51):** Enumerates specific areas where the Commonwealth can legislate, including trade, taxation, defence, immigration, and **external affairs**.
- **Exclusive Powers (Section 52):** Powers strictly vested in the national government, such as matters relating to the seat of government and the federal public service.

**State Powers & Dominance of the Commonwealth**

- **Saved Powers (Section 107):** States retain broad, residual legislative authority over areas not explicitly granted to the Commonwealth (e.g., education, health, criminal law).
- **Inconsistency of Laws (Section 109):** **If a state law is inconsistent with a Commonwealth law, the Commonwealth law prevails**, and the state law is invalid to the extent of the inconsistency.
- **Commonwealth Expansion:** The federal government has increasingly dominated the states by broadly interpreting the "external affairs" power (e.g., the _Tasmanian Dam_ case, using an international treaty to stop state-level dam construction).
- **Financial Dominance:** The Commonwealth also expands its reach by monopolizing income tax and using **Section 96 tied grants** to conditionally fund the states, forcing them to adopt federal policies in areas like education.

**3. The Separation of Powers**

Governmental authority is divided into three distinct branches across the first three chapters of the Constitution.

**I. The Legislature (Chapter 1)**

- **Role:** Vested in the Parliament to make, amend, and repeal laws.
- **Composition:** The Crown (Governor-General), the Senate, and the House of Representatives.

**II. The Executive (Chapter 2)**

- **Role:** Vested in the Queen and exercised by the Governor-General, but practically carried out by the Prime Minister, Cabinet ministers, and government departments to implement and administer laws.
- **Responsible Government:** The Australian system heavily overlaps the Executive and Legislative branches. **Ministers must be members of Parliament (Section 64)** and are accountable to it. The Parliament delegates rule-making authority to the executive (subordinate legislation), subject to parliamentary oversight like the power of disallowance.

**III. The Judiciary (Chapter 3)**

- **Role:** Vested exclusively in the courts (principally the High Court) to interpret laws, apply common law, and impartially adjudicate disputes. The separation between the judiciary and the other branches is strictly maintained.

**4. The High Court & Judicial Independence**

- **Jurisdiction:** The High Court is the ultimate appellate court (Section 73) and holds original jurisdiction over constitutional matters, treaties, and disputes between states or the Commonwealth (Sections 75 & 76).
- **Writs against Commonwealth Officers:** Section 75 empowers the court to issue writs like **mandamus** (an order to perform a duty) to prevent the legislature or executive from usurping judicial authority.
- **Cross-Vesting (_Re Wakim_):** Under Section 77, Parliament can confer federal jurisdiction on state courts, but importantly, **the Commonwealth cannot confer state jurisdiction on federal courts**.
- **Judicial Independence (Section 72):** Judges are appointed by the Governor-General, their pay cannot be diminished while in office, and they can only be removed for **"proved misbehaviour or incapacity"** by a vote from both houses of Parliament.

**5. The Crown, Governor-General, & Reserve Powers**

- **Symbolic Role:** The King is the head of state, operating as a nonpartisan institution, with duties carried out by the Governor-General.
- **Duties:** The Governor-General provides **Royal Assent** to make passed bills into law, appoints the Prime Minister, and dissolves Parliament, almost entirely based on ministerial advice.
- **Reserve Powers:** In highly rare circumstances, the Governor-General can act without ministerial advice. The most famous example is the **1975 dismissal of Prime Minister Gough Whitlam** by Governor-General Sir John Kerr during a Senate deadlock over the supply bill.

**6. Parliament & The Legislative Process**

**Representative Government**

- Sections 7 and 24 mandate that representatives are **directly chosen by the people**, ensuring democratic accountability.
- **House of Representatives:** Seats are allocated based on population (minimum of 5 per state). Members serve 3-year terms.
- **Senate:** Protects regional interests by giving **equal representation to all six states** (currently 12 senators each, plus 2 per territory), serving staggered 6-year terms.

**Passing a Bill**

1. **First Reading:** Title is formally read; no debate.
2. **Second Reading:** Crucial stage where the minister outlines the bill's intent (often used later for statutory interpretation), followed by debate and a vote.
3. **Committee Stage:** Detailed scrutiny and amendments.
4. **Third Reading:** Final vote on the amended bill.
5. _Passes to the other House to repeat the process, then receives Royal Assent_.

**Resolving Deadlocks (Section 57)**

- If the Senate rejects a bill passed by the House twice (with a 3-month interval), the Governor-General can trigger a **double dissolution** election. If the deadlock continues post-election, a joint sitting of both houses is convened to vote on the bill.
- _Note on Money Bills (Section 53):_ The Senate cannot initiate or amend bills appropriating revenue or imposing taxation, as financial control lies with the House of Representatives.

**7. Administrative Law & Accountability**

- **Oversight Bodies:** Include Ombudsmen (investigating unfair treatment by agencies), Parliamentary Committees (scrutinizing expenditure and policy), and Auditors-General (financial and performance audits).
- **Royal Commissions:** The highest form of inquiry in Australia, holding broad powers to compel witnesses to testify about major public concerns (e.g., corruption).
- **Judicial Review:** Courts examine the **legality** (not the merits) of executive decisions to ensure decision-makers acted within their statutory authority. The new Administrative Review Tribunal (ART) handles these reviews.

**8. Human Rights Protections**

- Australia **does not have a comprehensive Bill of Rights**. Protections rely on the rule of law, common law, and limited constitutional provisions.
- **Express Constitutional Rights:**
    - **Section 51(xxxi):** Acquisition of property by the Commonwealth must be on **just terms**.
    - **Section 80:** Right to a trial by jury for indictable federal offences.
    - **Section 116:** Freedom of religion (Commonwealth cannot establish a religion or impose religious tests for office).
    - **Section 117:** Prohibits states from discriminating against residents of other states.
- **Implied Rights:** The High Court has recognized unwritten rights derived from the Constitution's structure. For example, there is no express right to vote, but because Sections 7 and 24 require representatives to be "chosen by the people," the Court recognizes an **implied right to the freedom of political communication**.
