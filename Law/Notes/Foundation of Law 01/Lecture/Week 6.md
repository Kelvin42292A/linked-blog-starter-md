**Milestone 1: Feudalism & Land Ownership**

- **The Foundation:** Following the Battle of Hastings (1066), William the Conqueror declared himself the ultimate owner of all land in England.
- **The Feudal System:** A hierarchy of land grants (fiefs) given by the King to lords in exchange for services (military, agricultural, or ceremonial), secured by an oath of fealty. No one owned land outright; they merely held a right to possess and use it (_seisin_).
- **The Australian Connection:** This system was transplanted to Australia. The Crown remains the ultimate owner of all land, and individuals hold "estates in fee simple" (perpetual rights to use/possess). However, this is modified by Native Title (_Mabo_), which recognizes the pre-existing land rights of First Nations peoples.

**Milestone 2: The Birth of Common Law & Courts**

- **Curia Regis (The King’s Court):** Created by Norman Kings to centralize governance and resolve disputes. Originally, it was _itinerant_ (it traveled around with the King), which made justice hard to access when the King was away.
- **Specialized Courts:** To handle the growing workload, the Curia Regis split into three distinct courts based in Westminster:
    1. **The Exchequer:** Handled royal finances, taxes, and feudal dues.
    2. **The Court of Common Pleas:** Handled civil disputes between individuals (land, contracts, property).
    3. **The King's Bench:** Handled criminal matters, breaches of the peace, and cases involving the Crown.
- **Precedent:** Judges began recording their decisions, creating a body of _precedent_ that became the basis of English Common Law.

**Milestone 3: Limiting the King (Magna Carta & Habeas Corpus)**

- **Magna Carta (1215):** The first formal limit on the King’s power. Key clauses established that even the King is not above the law:
    - **Clause 17:** Held courts at a fixed location (Westminster), ensuring consistent civil justice.
    - **Clause 39:** Created early _due process_—no one can be deprived of liberty except by the law of the land.
    - **Clause 40:** Promised that justice would not be sold, denied, or delayed.
- **Habeas Corpus:** Built on Magna Carta principles and formalized in 1679. It allows any detained person to challenge their imprisonment in court. The detaining authority bears the burden of proving that the detention is legally justified.

**Milestone 4: The Rise of Equity & The Chancery**

- **The Writs & Rigidity:** To start a lawsuit, a plaintiff needed a specific, pre-existing document called a _writ_. The _Provisions of Oxford (1258)_ banned the King's Chancery from creating new types of writs without approval. This made the common law system highly rigid—if your problem didn't fit an existing writ, you got no justice.
- **The Birth of Equity:** Blocked by the common law, citizens petitioned the King’s Chancellor for help based on "fairness and conscience". This evolved into the **Court of Chancery (Equity)**.
- **The Earl of Oxford’s Case (1615):** Settled a massive power struggle between common law judges and equity. The King ruled that **whenever common law and equity conflict, equity prevails** to prevent injustice.
- **The 7 Key Maxims of Equity:** Guides used by equity judges to make fair decisions:
    1. _Equity will not suffer a wrong to be without a remedy_ (fixes gaps in the common law).
    2. _Equity follows the law_ (respects legal rights, but stops people from using them unconscionably).
    3. _Those who seek equity must do equity_ (plaintiffs must perform their own legal duties).
    4. _Those who come to equity must come with clean hands_ (improper or bad behavior bars you from relief).
    5. _Equity assists the diligent, not the tardy_ (_laches_—you cannot unreasonably delay taking action).
    6. _Equity looks to the intent rather than the form_ (prioritizes substance over strict technical paperwork).
    7. _Equity regards as done that which ought to be done_ (treats intended fair outcomes as already achieved).

**Milestone 5: Parliamentary Supremacy**

- **The Divine Right of Kings:** The old belief that monarchs got their power directly from God and were answerable only to Him.
- **The English Civil War (1642–1651):** A bloody struggle over whether supreme authority belonged to the King or to Parliament. It resulted in the execution of King Charles I and the temporary rise of the Commonwealth under Oliver Cromwell.
- **The Bill of Rights (1689):** Emerged from the Glorious Revolution, forever cementing **Parliamentary Sovereignty**.
- **Plenary Power:** It established that Parliament has supreme, unlimited legislative authority. No court or monarch can invalidate or override an Act of Parliament.
