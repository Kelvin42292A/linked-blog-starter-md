## 1) The “modern” starting point: text, context, purpose

> “The process of construction begins with a consideration of the ordinary and grammatical meaning of the words of the provision having regard to their context and legislative purpose.”

> _Australian Education Union v Department of Education and Children's Services_ [2012] HCA 3 at [26] (French CJ, Hayne, Kiefel & Bell JJ)

**Core idea**

- Interpretation is not “literal only” or “purpose only”: it is an integrated exercise of **text + context + purpose**.

**What counts as “context”?**

- **Intrinsic** (within the Act): long title, objects clause, headings, structure, definitions, etc.
- **Extrinsic** (outside the Act): second reading speech, explanatory memorandum, law reform reports, international materials, etc (subject to the relevant Interpretation Act rules/thresholds).

---

## 2) Interpretation legislation (directing the method)

- **NSW**: _Interpretation Act 1987 (NSW)_
- **Commonwealth**: _Acts Interpretation Act 1901 (Cth)_
    - **s 15AA** (purposive interpretation to be preferred)
    - **s 15AB** (use of extrinsic materials in certain circumstances)

### Barker’s caution (important nuance)

- Provisions like **s 15AA / s 15AB** do not “automatically decide” the result; they also require interpretation.
- “Purposive” does not mean purpose always overrides text; the statute’s own wording still controls, and interpretation Acts often operate **subject to contrary intention**.

(Reference in slides: Barker J paper, “First you see it, then you don’t…” (2012) at [16].)

---

## 3) Problem method for statutory interpretation (IRAC-by-element)

**Workflow**

1. Identify **preliminary issues**
2. Then apply **IRAC** to _each element_ of the provision you are interpreting:
    - **Issue**: what element/term is in dispute?
    - **Rule**: what statutory interpretation principles apply to that element?
    - **Application**: apply those principles to the facts
    - **Conclusion**: reached for that element, then overall.

---

## 4) Worked case study framework (as per slides)

### A) Preliminary issues (before construing elements)

- **Jurisdiction**: is it a NSW Act or Cth Act? (determines which Interpretation Act provisions you apply)
- **Commencement**: check commencement provisions; if silent, use Interpretation Act default rules (e.g. the “28-day rule” example used in class).

### B) Element-by-element construction (example structure)

For each element:

1. **Check definitions** in the Act (and then Interpretation Act if applicable).
2. Start with **ordinary meaning** (dictionary may be used to confirm ordinary meaning, depending on the relevant rules).
3. Test whether context/purpose suggests **another available construction**.
4. Use intrinsic aids first; only then consider whether/when to use extrinsic aids.

---

## 5) Text first, then (if needed) extrinsic aids — key authorities (as noted)

### “Exhaust ordinary construction first”

- _Saeed v Minister for Immigration and Citizenship_ [2010] HCA 23 at [33]: it is erroneous to look at extrinsic materials before exhausting ordinary statutory construction.
- _Catlow v Accident Compensation Commission_ [1989] HCA 43 at [6] (Brennan & Gaudron JJ): extrinsic material cannot alter plain meaning; it assists only where meaning is doubtful / relevant threshold is met.

### Context/purpose may displace purely literal meaning

- _Project Blue Sky Inc v Australian Broadcasting Authority_ (1998) 194 CLR 355 at [78]: text is the starting point, but context, consequences, purpose, and canons may require a meaning that does not match grammatical meaning.

### Don’t let extrinsic materials “rewrite” the statute

- _Certain Lloyd’s Underwriters v Cross_ (2012) 248 CLR 378 (Kiefel J): extrinsic materials can help identify policy, but do not justify giving a wider operation than the statute’s language permits.

### “Legislative intention” as a conclusion of applying rules

- _Lacey v Attorney-General (Qld)_ (2011) 242 CLR 573 at [43]–[44]: “legislative intention” is not a real collective mental state; it is a conclusion reached by applying accepted rules of construction, including identifying purpose from text/structure (and sometimes extrinsic materials).

---

## 6) Canons / presumptions (example used)

- **Ejusdem generis**: where general words follow specific words, the general words may be limited to the same kind/class as the specific words (used in the “any other transport” example).

---

## 7) Mini-checklist for your exam/notes

- Identify **Act + jurisdiction** (NSW/Cth) → pick correct Interpretation Act.
- Start with **text (ordinary meaning)**.
- Read the provision in **intrinsic context** (whole Act).
- Identify **purpose** (from text/structure; confirm with extrinsic if permitted/threshold met).
- Consider **canons/presumptions** where relevant.
- Use **extrinsic materials** only when allowed and for the proper purpose (confirm meaning; resolve ambiguity/absurdity; identify policy).
- Conclude **element-by-element**, then overall.
