## Key Legal Philosophers & Theories

- **Ronald Dworkin (1931–2013):** Viewed law as an interpretative concept. Judges do not just apply strict rules; they must interpret laws by considering principles of justice, fairness, and morality.
- **Joseph Raz (1939–2022):** A legal positivist. Argued that law provides an authoritative system to mediate conflicts and guide behavior, even when individuals disagree with the underlying reasons.
- **Oliver Wendell Holmes (1841–1935):** An American jurist associated with legal pragmatism/realism. Defined law practically as a "prediction" of what courts will actually do, prioritizing real-world outcomes over moral or theoretical elements.
- **Thomas Aquinas (1225–1274):** Rooted in **Natural Law theory**. Argued that law is a rational system aimed at the common good and must possess a moral dimension. Under this view, unjust laws that contradict moral or divine principles are not truly valid laws.
- **Early Command Theory (Unnamed in text):** Views law strictly as a tool of governance prioritizing order and control, enforced through fear of punishment and sanctions rather than moral reasoning.

## What is Jurisprudence?

- Jurisprudence is the theoretical and philosophical study of law.
- It focuses on the fundamental principles underpinning the legal system, asking not just _what_ the law is, but _why_ it is that way and what validates it.
- Different schools of thought interpret law through different lenses: sociology (social/political interactions), positivism (formal rules/behavior), and natural law (morality/justice).

## Four Main Schools of Western Legal Thought

- **Natural Law:** Law is inherently tied to universal moral principles. Law must promote the common good, and an unjust rule lacks the valid foundation to be considered law.
- **Positivism:** Law is separated from morality. It is a system of rules created by a recognized sovereign authority. Law is defined by its source (who made it), not by its moral content.
- **Common Law:** A system built on judicial precedent. Judges play an essential role in shaping the law incrementally through individual court decisions.
- **Legal Realism:** Challenges formal abstract rules. Argues that actual judicial decisions are heavily influenced by outside social, political, personal, and economic factors.

## Historical Origins of the Common Law

- **Pre-1066 England:** The legal system was completely decentralized. Justice relied on fragmented local customs and "folk moots" (early local courts), lacking consistency.
- **The Norman Conquest (1066):** William the Conqueror defeated King Harold at the Battle of Hastings (depicted in the Bayeux Tapestry) and claimed the English throne.
- **Centralization:** To maintain order, the Normans replaced local Anglo-Saxon customs with a unified legal system. Royal judges traveled the country to administer justice uniformly.
- **The Domesday Book (1086):** A comprehensive survey of land and resources that served as an early foundation for centralized property law.

## The Three Meanings of "Common Law"

- **1. A Uniform System Across the Land:** Stemming from the 1066 Norman Conquest, it refers to the centralized legal system that replaced fragmented regional customs to become "common" across all of England.
- **2. Judge-Made Law (Case Law):** Law developed organically through court rulings rather than legislative statutes. It relies on **stare decisis** (standing by past decisions) to ensure consistency. It is flexible but slow to evolve. While powerful (e.g., establishing the modern duty of care in _Donoghue v Stevenson_), judge-made law is ultimately subordinate to Parliament (e.g., the Native Title Act overriding common law).
- **3. Common Law vs. Equity:** A technical division within judge-made law. Early common law courts adhered strictly to rigid rules and offered limited remedies (primarily financial damages). **Equity** later developed as a separate but parallel system of justice to provide fairness and alternative remedies in situations where strict common law rules produced unjust outcomes.
