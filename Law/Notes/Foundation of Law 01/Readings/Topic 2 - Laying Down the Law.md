# Distinction between matters of fact and matter of law

### Newer Version (Chapter 6, §§6.57–6.59)

- An important conceptual distinction between **fact** and **law**.
- In a jury trial: facts are for the **jury**, law is for the **judge**.
- This distinction determines:
    - Responsibilities of judge vs jury
    - Scope of appeals (appellate courts have greater power on questions of law)
    - Judicial review of administrative decisions (generally limited to errors of law)

**Key Criminal Example (Identity in Murder Trial)**

- Basic issue of **identity** (who committed the act) → **Question of fact** for the jury.
- However, if the prosecution relies on **joint criminal enterprise** or **extended joint criminal enterprise**, the legal rules are explained by the judge.
- Whether those legal requirements are satisfied on the evidence → **Question of fact** for the jury.
- Note: Extended joint criminal enterprise doctrine is particularly controversial and has been considered by the High Court in recent years.

**General Difficulty**

- Many issues are easy to classify, but many others involve **mixed questions of fact and law**.
- No satisfactory universal test exists to draw the line.

**Key Case**_Collector of Customs v Agfa-Gevaert Ltd_ (1996) “The distinction between questions of fact and questions of law is a vital distinction in many fields of law.”

**Practical Importance**

- Administrative law: Courts usually review only for **errors of law**; tribunals can review **merits** (facts + law).
- Appeals: Greater scope for intervention on legal issues than factual findings.

### **Older Version – What Changed / Was Added** (Chapter 8)

The older version included extra detail that has been **removed or shortened** in the new edition:

- **Five specific reasons** why the distinction matters (Twining & Miers):
    1. Facts do not constitute precedent.
    2. Facts normally reserved for jurors, law for judges.
    3. Law decisions generally require public justification; facts do not.
    4. Appeals on law are generally more favourable to the convicted appellant.
    5. Appeal courts will not reverse a finding of fact unless it was unsustainable on the evidence or so unreasonable that the court did not understand the rule’s requirements.
- **Detailed Case Example** (now removed): _Director General Department of Land and Water Conservation v Bailey_ (Native vegetation clearing on rural land – “rural structures” = fact; “designated development” = law)
- **Pozzolanic Propositions** (endorsed in _Agfa-Gevaert_) – now largely omitted:
    1. Whether a statutory word has ordinary or technical/legal meaning → Question of Law.
    2. Ordinary meaning of a word → Question of Fact.
    3. Meaning of a technical legal term → Question of Law.

# How does a judge identify the binding legal rule?

A trial judge has **relatively little discretion** in the law to be applied. Under the doctrine of **stare decisis**, if a superior court in the same hierarchy has previously decided an **identical question of law**, the trial judge **must follow** that decision and apply the law exactly as stated.

### Do judges make law

- Most judges and legal commentators now **reject the “fairytale” declaratory theory** and accept that **judges do make law**.

**Key Points:**

- **Justice Kirby**: If there is no apparent law, the judge is **duty-bound to create it**.
- On **novel issues** (especially interpreting legislation), **even trial judges** may create law.
- Primary responsibility for developing law lies with **superior appellate courts**, especially the **ultimate appellate court** (Heydon).

**Constraints:**

- Judges must ensure any new rule **coheres with existing legal principles**.
- Judges **must decide** cases — they cannot walk away, postpone indefinitely, or say it is too hard. There is **never an absence of law**.

# Precedent Across Different Australian Court Hierarchies (Uniform & Commonwealth Legislation)

### **Newer Version (12th ed, Chapter 8, §§8.45–8.51)**

- According to the traditional doctrine of precedent, binding force is determined by reference to the **appeal hierarchy**.
- However, difficulties arise when courts in **different hierarchies** (or different jurisdictions) deal with the **same or identical law** — especially **Commonwealth legislation**, **uniform national legislation**, or **common law**.
- Strong emphasis on **consistency and uniformity** of decision-making across Australia.

**Key Principle (High Court Guidance)**:

- Intermediate appellate courts (and trial judges) should **not depart** from decisions of other Australian intermediate appellate courts on **uniform national legislation** or **Commonwealth legislation** unless convinced that the other court’s interpretation is **plainly wrong**.
- This principle applies strongly to **uniform national legislation** (e.g. _Evidence Act 1995_ (Cth) and state equivalents).
- There is a **single common law of Australia**, so similar considerations apply to non-statutory common law.

**Important Cases**:

- _Australian Securities Commission v Marlborough Gold Mines Ltd_ (1993) – foundational case on uniformity.
- _Farah Constructions Pty Ltd v Say-Dee Pty Ltd_ (2007) – High Court emphasised consistency for both uniform legislation and common law.
- _CAL No 14 Pty Ltd v Motor Accidents Insurance Board_ (2009) – High Court criticised the Tasmanian Full Court for casually departing from NSW Court of Appeal on a common law issue.

**Practical Approach** (e.g. _Evidence Act_ example):

- A judge may regard an interstate appellate decision on identical legislation as **highly persuasive** (even if not strictly binding) and follow it unless clearly wrong.

### **Older Version – What Changed / Was Added** (10th ed, Chapter 8 [8.7]–[8.17])

The older version covered this topic **earlier and in less depth** within the general discussion of precedent. The newer edition moved and **expanded** this specific issue into a dedicated later section (§§8.45–8.51) with more detailed analysis and recent cases.

Main differences in the older version:

- Less emphasis on the **“plainly wrong”** test and uniformity principles.
- Fewer detailed High Court examples (_Farah Constructions_ and _CAL No 14_ received less or no treatment).
- More general discussion of cross-hierarchy and interstate precedents without the strong High Court guidance statements now highlighted.
- The topic was blended into the broader explanation of hierarchies rather than given its own focused treatment.

**Summary of Change**: The newer version is **more detailed, better structured**, and gives stronger practical guidance on how Australian courts should handle uniform laws and interstate decisions. It reflects developments in High Court jurisprudence since the 10th edition.

# Non-Uniform Legislation

### Newer Version – 12th ed, Chapter 8, §§8.52–8.58

- The **Farah Constructions** “plainly wrong” principle applies strongly to **uniform national legislation** and **Commonwealth legislation**, and to the common law of Australia.
- However, the position is **less clear** for **non-uniform legislation** (similar but not identical laws across jurisdictions).
- Courts often still look to decisions from other Australian jurisdictions on **similarly worded legislation** for guidance.
- There is **no strict rule** requiring courts to follow interstate decisions on non-uniform statutes.
- Intermediate appellate courts may depart more readily, but still tend to aim for consistency where possible.

**Key Case Example: Tillman v Attorney General (NSW) (2010)**

- NSW Court of Appeal considered a Victorian Court of Appeal decision on very similar (but not identical) legislation regarding continuing detention of serious sex offenders.
- The Court held it was **not bound** but ultimately followed the Victorian decision because it was not clearly wrong.

**Later Developments**

- Cases such as _Harrison v Melhem_ and others show courts continue to wrestle with how much weight to give similar (but non-uniform) legislation from other states.
- The High Court has not laid down a single bright-line rule for non-uniform cases.

### **Older Version – What Changed / Was Added**

The older version (10th ed) had much less coverage of this distinction.

- It did not have a dedicated section separating uniform vs non-uniform legislation.
- The discussion of cross-jurisdictional precedents was more general and blended together.
- The nuanced treatment of _Tillman_, _Harrison v Melhem_, and the limits of the _Farah_ principle on non-uniform statutes was not present (or much shorter).
- Older discussions of departing from precedents due to social change or unsatisfactory rules were more prominent.

**Summary of Change:** The 12th edition added a clear, dedicated discussion on the limits of the uniformity principle when legislation is similar but not part of a national uniform scheme. This gives students a more sophisticated understanding of when the “plainly wrong” test applies and when courts have more flexibility.

# ‘Plainly Wrong’ and Per Incuriam Precedents, and Judicial Comity

### **Newer Version (12th ed, Chapter 8, §§8.59–8.67)**

- Courts are only **strictly bound** by precedents from higher courts in the **same hierarchy**
- Even where a precedent is not strictly **binding** (e.g. from another jurisdiction or hierarchy), courts should treat it with **respect.**
- A court may depart from such a precedent only if it is convinced the earlier decision is **‘plainly’, ‘clearly’, or ‘manifestly’ wrong.**

### Key Principles

- The “**plainly wrong”** test applies to precedents on **common law, Commonwealth legislation, national scheme legislation,** and can extend to **similar or identical non-uniform legislation**
- **Per Incuriam** (”through lack of care”) is closely related - it applies where a court failed to consider relevant legislation or binding authority, leading to flawed reasoning.
- Applying the “plainly wrong” label to another court’s decision should be done with **real and deliberate caution**

**What makes a precedent ‘plainly wrong?’** (Non-exhaustive list from _BHP Biliton Iron Ore Pty Ltd v National Competition CouncilI)_

- Failure to consider relevant statutory provisions or incorrect statutory instruments
- Failure to apply binding High Court or Full Court authority
- Other serious errors that caused the decision to miscarry

**Limitations:**

- Lower courts **cannot** use _per incuriam_ to ignore binding precedents from higher courts in their own hierarchy.
- Strong judicial comity (mutual respect) exists between Australian courts — casually labelling another court’s decision as “plainly wrong” is discouraged.

### **Older Version – What Changed / Was Added** (10th ed)

The older version had much less detailed coverage of this topic.

- It mentioned the idea of departing from persuasive precedents if “plainly wrong” but did not provide the expanded analysis or the helpful non-exhaustive list from _BHP v NCC_.
- The relationship between the “plainly wrong” test and _per incuriam_ was not explained as clearly.
- There was less discussion on judicial comity (mutual respect between courts) and the caution required before labelling another court’s decision as plainly wrong.
- Fewer recent examples and less practical guidance on how intermediate appellate courts should approach this issue.
- The older version also discussed other grounds for departing from non-binding precedents, such as when a precedent is **unsatisfactory** (e.g. _Brodie v Singleton Shire Council_ – non-feasance immunity) or when there have been **significant changes in social conditions** (e.g. _Mabo v Queensland (No 2)_ on terra nullius and _R v L_ on marital rape immunity). These specific examples and techniques are now integrated into the broader “plainly wrong” and judicial comity framework in the new edition.

**Summary of Change:** The newer edition significantly expanded and clarified this area, providing a more detailed, practical, and cautious framework for when courts can depart from non-binding precedents. It better integrates the concepts of “plainly wrong”, _per incuriam_, judicial comity, and older techniques such as unsatisfactory precedents and social change.

# **Distinguishing a Precedent on Its Facts**

This technique is **open to any court** at any level. Judges frequently distinguish cases on their facts to avoid undesirable precedents.

- A judge can avoid a precedent by ruling that the **facts** of the earlier case are **materially different** from the current case.
- The principle from the old case then does **not apply**.
- At the extreme, an unpopular precedent can be **confined** only to cases with **identical facts**.

**Key Example:** _Thornton v Shoe Lane Parking Ltd_

- Earlier cases involved tickets issued by **human operators**.
- _Thornton_ involved a ticket from an **automatic machine**.
- Court distinguished the cases: the automatic process was irrevocable and gave no real chance to reject the conditions.

# Statement of law is too wide

Courts can limit overly general statements of law so they do not apply to the current case.

- A judge may avoid a precedent by holding that the **statement of law** in the earlier case is **too broad**.
- The judge can then **narrow** it or apply it only to its specific facts.

**Key Example:** _Attorney-General (NSW) v Mundey_

- The Attorney-General relied on a broad statement from an earlier Full Court decision.
- Hope JA ruled that the earlier statement was **too wide**.
- He preferred a **narrower test** (requiring intention or real risk of interfering with justice).
- The broad statement was treated as going beyond what was necessary (_obiter_).

# Statement of Law is obiter dictum

- Judges can avoid a precedent by saying the the statement of law is **orbiter dictum** (not necessary for the actual decision)
- Orbiter statements are **not binding** and do not have to be followed

### Key notes

- Sometimes clearly orbiter (e.g. “this issue is not necessary to decide”)
- Can be arguable if the statement goes beyond what was needed
- Lower courts can use this, but must treat seriously considered obiter from the High Court with great respect
