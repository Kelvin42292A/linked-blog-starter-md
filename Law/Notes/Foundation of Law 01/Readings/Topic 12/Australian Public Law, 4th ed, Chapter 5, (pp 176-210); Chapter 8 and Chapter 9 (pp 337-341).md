# _Australian Public Law, 4th ed, Chapter 5, (pp 176-210); Chapter 8 and Chapter 9 (pp 337-341)_

**🏛️ Theme 1: The Australian Federal System**

**1. Basic Structure & Division of Power**

- **Dual-Level System:** Establishes a federation splitting power between the **Commonwealth (Federal) Government** and the **six original States**.
- **Territorial Governments:** The ACT and Northern Territory are granted self-governance via s 122 of the Constitution; however, this power is conferred by the Commonwealth and can be overridden or revoked at any time.
- **Specific vs. Residual Powers:** The Commonwealth holds **enumerated powers** (specifically listed under ss 51, 52, and 122). The States retain **residual powers** over areas not mentioned (e.g., health, education, crime).
- **Section 109 Inconsistency:** If Federal and State laws conflict, the State law is invalid/inoperative to the extent of the inconsistency. The High Court interprets this broadly: inconsistency exists if a state law "alters, impairs, or detracts from" a federal law.

**2. Economic, Social & Legal Union**

- **s 92 (Free Trade):** Mandates that interstate trade and commerce must be "absolutely free," preventing states from setting up protectionist trade barriers.
- **s 90 (Excise Monopoly):** Grants the Commonwealth exclusive power to levy customs and excise duties, stripping states of the power to tax goods.
- **s 117 (No Discrimination):** Prevents states from discriminating against residents of other states.
- **s 51(ii) & s 99 (No Preference):** The Commonwealth cannot discriminate between states in tax laws or give preference to one state over another in trade or revenue.
- **s 128 (Referendums):** To amend the Constitution, a proposed change must pass by a "double majority"—a national majority of all voters, plus a majority of voters in a majority of states (4 out of 6 states). This is highly restrictive; only 8 out of 44 referendums have succeeded.

**3. Expansion of Commonwealth Power (Key Cases)**

- **The Engineers' Case (1920):** A landmark shift in constitutional interpretation. The High Court rejected the old "reserved state powers" doctrine in favor of a literal, broad reading of Commonwealth powers, massively shifting the balance of power toward the federal government.
- **The Corporations Power (s 51(xx)):** Exploded in scope under the **Work Choices Case (2006)**. The Court ruled the Commonwealth can regulate any industrial relations and policies that involve a "constitutional corporation," bypassing traditional state controls.
- **The External Affairs Power (s 51(xxix)):** Allows the Commonwealth to legislate on purely domestic matters to implement international treaty obligations (e.g., using a cultural heritage treaty to block Tasmania from building a dam in the **Tasmanian Dam Case (1983)**).
- **The Defense Power (s 51(vi)):** A "purpose-based" power that expands dramatically during war/tension and narrows in peace. In **Thomas v Mowbray**, the Court accepted its extension to allow federal control orders to combat terrorism.

**4. Financial Relations & Imbalance**

- **Vertical Fiscal Imbalance (VFI):** The Commonwealth holds massive financial dominance because it took exclusive control of income tax during WWII (upheld by the _Uniform Tax Cases_) and holds exclusive excise power. States must rely on Federal **s 96 grants** to fund their responsibilities.
- **Tied Grants (s 96):** The Commonwealth often attaches strict policy conditions (Specific Purpose Payments) to these grants, effectively forcing states to implement national policies in areas where the Commonwealth has no legislative power (e.g., universities and school reforms).
- **Horizontal Fiscal Equalisation (HFI):** Managed by the Commonwealth Grants Commission to distribute grant money equitably, ensuring poorer states can provide basic services, though wealthier states (like WA) often argue they are subsidizing them.

**5. Intergovernmental Relations**

- **Coordinate vs. Cooperative:** Shifted from "coordinate" (separate spheres of action) to "cooperative federalism" where both levels coordinate national schemes through referrals of power (s 51(xxxvii)).
- **COAG (Council of Australian Governments):** Established in 1992 to coordinate national funding and policy. It is criticized as an executive-driven process that bypasses parliament and is heavily subject to party politics (e.g., Rudd's push to "end the blame game" vs. Howard's warning of "wall-to-wall" state ALP governments without checks).

**👔 Theme 2: The Executive & Constitutional Powers**

**1. Executive Power (s 61) & Limits**

- **Vesting:** Executive power is formally vested in the King/Queen and represented by the Governor-General.
- **The Williams Case (2012):** The High Court ruled that the Commonwealth executive cannot spend public money on programs without direct legislative authorization from Parliament.
- **Melbourne Corporation Doctrine:** An implied constitutional limit protecting the federation; the Commonwealth cannot pass laws that target or place a special burden on State governments that would destroy their capacity to function as independent political entities.

**2. The Governor-General & Conventions**

- **Formal Powers:** Proroguing/dissolving Parliament, commanding defense forces, appointing/removing judges, and assenting to Bills.
- **Unwritten Conventions:** By convention, the Governor-General is a symbolic figurehead who **must** act only on the advice of Cabinet and the Prime Minister.

**3. Reserve Powers (Acting Without Advice)**

- **Definition:** Very rare circumstances where the G-G can act without or against ministerial advice.
- **Hung Parliaments:** Determining who can form government when no political party has a clear lower-house majority.
- **Dismissal of Governments:** Used on two highly controversial occasions in Australian history:
    1. **Premier Jack Lang (NSW, 1932):** Dismissed by the NSW Governor for instructing public servants to disobey a federal law.
    2. **Prime Minister Gough Whitlam (Federal, 1975):** Dismissed by the G-G during a constitutional deadlock where the opposition-controlled Senate blocked supply (the budget).
- **Codification Debate:** The 1999 Republic Referendum debated whether these unwritten reserve powers should be codified (written down) to prevent future constitutional crises or kept unwritten to preserve flexibility.

**4. Statutory Powers**

- Most day-to-day executive actions (social security benefits, tax collection, migration detention, and police powers) do not come from the Constitution, but are **statutory powers** explicitly delegated to executive departments by Acts of Parliament (e.g., _Migration Act_, _Defence Act_).
