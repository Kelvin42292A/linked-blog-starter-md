## Chapter 7

1. Case Law & Reading a Case

Case Law: Law developed by judges when resolving individual disputes, which sets models (precedents) for future cases.

Key Parties: The plaintiff brings the action, and the defendant defends against it. On appeal, they are the appellant and respondent.

Case Analysis (IRAC): To analyze a case, students and lawyers should identify the material facts, procedural history, and grounds for appeal, often using the IRAC method: Issue, Rule, Application, Conclusion.

2. The Doctrine of Precedent (Stare Decisis)

Stare decisis literally translates to "stand by the thing decided and do not disturb the calm".

Policy Rationale: It promotes certainty (citizens know the law), equality (treating like cases alike), efficiency (courts don't reinvent the wheel), and impartiality.

General Rules:

Courts are bound by the decisions of courts higher up in their same hierarchy.

Decisions from different hierarchies, or lower down in the same hierarchy, are merely persuasive, not binding.

A court is generally not bound by its own past decisions, but will only depart from them with reluctance.

3. Court Hierarchy

Precedent relies on a clear hierarchy of courts (e.g., Local Court → District Court → Supreme Court → High Court of Australia).

Appellate courts have the power to reverse the decisions of lower courts.

4. Ratio Decidendi vs. Obiter Dicta

Ratio Decidendi (Binding): The "reason for the decision." It is the pronouncement of legal principle that is absolutely necessary for the judge's decision on the established facts. Only the ratio is binding.

Obiter Dicta (Not Binding): "Remarks in passing." These are legal principles or hypothetical discussions not strictly necessary for the judge's final decision. They are not binding, though they can be highly persuasive in future cases.

Distinguishing a Case: A judge can avoid applying a precedent by finding that the "material facts" of the present case are different from the past case, meaning the old rule does not directly apply.

Divided Courts: When a panel of judges disagrees, the ratio is found by extracting the legal principles agreed upon by the majority of the judges.

5. Judicial Law-Making & Reporting

Filling Gaps: While parliament primarily creates legislation, judges often have to "fill in the gaps" when applying ambiguous statutes or adapting common law to new social changes.

Tension: This creates an ongoing tension between "judicial conservatism" (strictly following past rules) and "judicial activism" (updating the law for changing social values).

Law Reporting: For the doctrine of precedent to work, cases must be systematically recorded. They are published in authorized and unauthorized law report series, and increasingly found on the internet.

## Chapter 8

1. The High Court of Australia

Apex Court: The High Court is the ultimate appeal court in Australia.

Overruling Itself: The High Court is not strictly bound by its own previous decisions, but it will only depart from them with caution and strong reasons (e.g., if an earlier decision rests on a principle that has not worked out in practice).

Constitutional vs. Statutory Cases: The High Court is more willing to overrule its own past decisions in constitutional cases than in statutory interpretation cases. This is because only the High Court or a public referendum can change the Constitution, whereas Parliament can easily pass a new law to fix a statutory misinterpretation.

2. The Appellate Hierarchy and Other Australian Courts

Strict Binding: A lower court is strictly bound by the decisions of higher courts within the same hierarchy.

Persuasive Precedents: Courts are not strictly bound by decisions of courts in other Australian hierarchies (e.g., a NSW court is not bound by a Victorian court) or courts at the same level.

Single Judges: A single judge of a court is generally not bound by the decisions of another single judge of the same court, though they will usually follow them for consistency unless they believe the earlier decision is "plainly wrong".

3. Uniform National Legislation and "Plainly Wrong" Decisions

Uniformity: Australia has multiple state and territory hierarchies but only one common law. When interpreting Commonwealth legislation or uniform national laws, courts across different states strive for uniformity.

The "Plainly Wrong" Test: An intermediate appellate court (like a state Court of Appeal) should follow the interpretation of another intermediate appellate court on uniform legislation unless they are convinced the earlier decision is "plainly wrong". A decision might be considered plainly wrong if it ignored relevant legislation or a binding precedent (per incuriam).

4. Inferior Courts and Administrative Tribunals

Inferior Courts: Local and Magistrates courts are strictly bound by the decisions of the superior courts above them.

Tribunals: Administrative tribunals (like the AAT) are not courts and do not exercise judicial power, so the strict doctrine of precedent does not technically apply to them. However, for the sake of consistency and efficiency, tribunals are encouraged to follow their own previous decisions and are bound to follow the decisions of actual courts on questions of law.

5. Decisions from Other Jurisdictions (UK)

Not Binding: No decision of any foreign court is legally binding on Australian courts.

Historical UK Ties: Historically, decisions of the UK's Privy Council and House of Lords were binding. Today, they are no longer binding, but their past decisions can still be treated as highly persuasive depending on the context.

Key takeaway to remember: The doctrine of precedent aims to balance consistency and predictability in the law with the need for flexibility to correct errors and adapt to changes over time.
