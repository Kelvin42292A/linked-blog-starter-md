**Core Legal Principles**

- **Stare decisis:** Literally translates to "let the decision stand." This is the Latin name for the doctrine of precedent, which can refer to decisions being strictly binding or merely persuasive.
- **Judicial Comity:** The respect judges show to decisions from other courts or judges. This often leads them to follow decisions that are not strictly binding (e.g., from a different court hierarchy).
- **Res judicata:** A final decision between parties. Once a decision is made and all appeal rights have expired, the matter between those specific parties is permanently closed. Even if a higher court later overrules the legal principle in a _different_ case, it does not change the result for the original parties.

**Outcomes of an Appeal (Same Case)**

- **Affirmed:** The lower court's decision is upheld (agreed with) on appeal.
- **Reversed:** The lower court's decision is overturned on appeal.

**Treatment of Earlier Cases (Different Cases)**

These terms describe how a judge in a _current_ case treats a completely different, _earlier_ case:

- **Overruled:** A court decides that a decision in an earlier case was wrong. This term has a very precise legal meaning.
- **Approved:** A later case states that an earlier case was decided correctly.
- **Followed:** The court not only approves of an earlier decision but also applies its legal principles to the facts of the current case.
- **Applied:** The legal principle from an earlier case is applied to a new set of facts and circumstances.
- **Distinguished:** The judge decides the facts of the current case are "materially different" from the earlier case, meaning a different legal principle should apply instead.
- **Doubted:** A court thinks an earlier decision is incorrect, but does not go so far as to definitely overrule it.
- **Considered:** An earlier case is discussed by the judge, but without any adverse criticism.
- **Explained:** An earlier case is not necessarily doubted, but the judge justifies its outcome by pointing out a specific, non-obvious point of fact or law.
- **Referred:** An earlier case is simply mentioned without any significant discussion.

**Important Note for Exams:** Be careful not to confuse "Reversed" with "Overruled." A decision is _reversed_ when it goes up on appeal in the exact same case, whereas a legal principle is _overruled_ when a later, separate case decides the earlier case's reasoning was wrong. Furthermore, terms like "affirmed", "reversed", and "overruled" have uniform, precise legal meanings, while terms like "approved", "followed", and "considered" are a bit less precise and rely on the author's judgment.
