**1. The Core Definition of _Ratio Decidendi_**

Neil MacCormick provides a precise formula for defining the _ratio decidendi_ of a case. He defines it as:

"[A] ruling expressly or impliedly given by a judge which is sufficient to settle a point of law put in issue by the parties' arguments in a case, being a point on which a ruling was necessary to his/her justification (or one of his/her alternative justifications) of the decision in the case".

- **Ruling vs. Rule:** MacCormick emphasizes that the _ratio_ is a **ruling** made by the judge, rather than just "any rule" upon which a conclusion depends. For example, when a court applies a statute, the statute itself is not the _ratio_; rather, the _ratio_ is the court's specific interpretation of that statute.
- **Scope of the Ruling:** Judges are only obligated to enunciate a conclusion that is sufficient to justify accepting or rejecting the claims advanced by the parties. Rulings that go beyond the points actively argued by the parties carry the risk of being adopted without fully adequate consideration of counter-arguments.

**2. Refining "Necessity" and "Sufficiency"**

MacCormick arrives at his definition by refining the ideas of previous legal theorists:

- **Critique of Rupert Cross:** Sir Rupert Cross defined the _ratio_ as any rule treated by the judge as a "necessary step" in reaching their conclusion. MacCormick argues this definition is too wide; as noted above, a statute is "necessary" to a conclusion, but it is not the _ratio_.
- **The Shift to "Sufficiency" (Brian Simpson):** Brian Simpson argued that looking only for "necessity" lets in too much, proposing instead that the focus should be on what is **sufficient** for the decision.
- **MacCormick's Synthesis:** MacCormick combines both concepts. A _ratio_ must be **sufficient** to settle the specific point of law in dispute between the parties, but it must also be **necessary** to justify the final decision. Importantly, "necessity" here refers to the judge's _obligation_ to decide the specific dispute between the parties, not a strict causal or logical necessity.

**3. The Ratio in Practice: Multi-Judge Courts and Implicit Rulings**

- **Multiple Rationes:** A _ratio_ is a function of a judge's decision and the justification they offer. Therefore, a single judge may produce multiple _rationes_ if they rule on multiple points in issue.
- **Multi-Judge Appellate Courts:** In courts with several judges, there may be multiple _rationes_ if judges take different paths to reach the same conclusion. MacCormick notes it is impossible to construct a single "composite ruling" from these different approaches. However, if the various justifications are mutually consistent, they should all be deemed highly persuasive for future courts to develop.
- **The "Goodhart Method" for Implicit Rulings:** In cases where a judge does not explicitly state a clear ruling, MacCormick endorses the "Goodhart method". This involves implying a universalized proposition of law: "If facts _F_ occur then decision _D_ ought to be given". Facts are deemed "material" if the judge represents them as the specific facts _because of which_ they came to their decision.

**4. Distinguishing _Obiter Dicta_**

- **Definition:** Arguments evaluating other legal authorities, evaluating legal principles, or weighing the consequences of a favored ruling fall under the category of _obiter dicta_ (other things said). This remains true even if the judge needed those arguments to justify the ruling itself.
- **The Value of Obiter Dicta:** MacCormick stresses that _obiter dicta_ should not be dismissed just because they lack strictly binding force. They are highly persuasive and essential to legal discourse because they:
    - Demonstrate how judges weigh competing principles.
    - Represent attempts to formulate legal principles that were previously only implicit.
    - Play a vital role in argument by analogy, which is a major component of legal reasoning.

**5. Defending the Reality of the Ratio Against Skepticism**

- **Julius Stone's Skepticism:** Legal theorists like Julius Stone argued that _ratio decidendi_ is an "illusory reference" or a "concealed multiple reference". Stone argued that because the facts of a case can be categorized in an infinite number of broad or narrow ways, any precedent is infinitely flexible and indeterminate.
- **MacCormick's Rebuttal:** While MacCormick acknowledges that judicial incompetence exists and judges sometimes "bumble through" without resolving points satisfactorily, he rejects Stone's overarching skepticism. He insists that judges are frequently capable of providing clear, crisp, and determinate rulings on points of law.
- **Revisability:** Finally, MacCormick argues that having a clear and binding precedent does not mean it can never be revised. The idea of non-revisability is "unwise". Binding precedents can legitimately provoke new reflections when new circumstances arise, and revising a precedent does not mean the fundamental doctrine of precedent should be abandoned.
