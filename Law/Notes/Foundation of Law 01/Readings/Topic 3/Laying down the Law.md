# Evolution of legal practice

### Origins of the legal profession

Alongside the development of legal system, there is a system developed to help provide guidance on its operation and reaching the courts. Where litigants used to suffered from inconveniences because they either have to follow the King’s Bench or go to Westminster in London. This leads to a new role called **attorneys** to represent litigants.

#### Attorneys

An **attorney** is a legal representative allowed to stand in another person's place (from the French à tourner) to guide litigants and conduct legal business through the court system.

#### Sergeants-at-law

**Serjeants-at-law** were an elite, king-appointed class of top-ranking lawyers who held an exclusive monopoly over the Court of Common Pleas from 1400 until the early 20th century.

#### Barristers

**Barristers** emerged as specialized oral advocates who argued cases at the bar of the court. They organized into London's four **Inns of Court** (Inner Temple, Middle Temple, Gray's Inn, and Lincoln's Inn), which still regulate the profession today—a tradition reflected in Australia's barrister chambers.

#### Solicitor

**Solicitors** emerged in the 15th century as legal assistants. Over time, they absorbed the role of attorneys to become the standard term for non-courtroom lawyers in Britain, Australia, Canada, and New Zealand, while America retained the older term "attorney."

#### Fusion of barristers and solicitors

By the late 19th century, England’s legal profession was split into **barristers** (higher-court advocates) and **solicitors**.

While Australia has legally fused the two roles to allow practitioners to do both, a strong practical division remains. Most Australian lawyers still choose to practice distinctly as either a solicitor or a specialist barrister.

#### King’s Counsel and Senior Counsel

**King's Counsel (KC)**—or **Senior Counsel (SC)**—is the highest rank for senior barristers, often called **"taking silk"** because of their silk gowns.

Australia replaced the title of KC with SC in the 1990s and 2000s, but several states have since brought KC back for better international recognition. Today, top barristers can often choose between the two titles.

### **Civil vs. Criminal Practice Evolution**

Unlike civil trials, 16th-century criminal trials were **"lawyer-free"** altercations where defense counsel was barred.

This changed between the 17th and 19th centuries due to wrongful executions, the rise of professional Crown prosecutors, and highly complex new evidence laws. To ensure fairness, defense lawyers became essential, transforming criminal trials into the adversarial, lawyer-driven system we have today.

Legal profession in lower court of Australia is still lacking of presence. In Australia hearing summary offences are now legal professionals. However, many defendants cannot afford a lawyer while in case law the high court held that if the defendant have a possibility facing prison, they have rights to have a legal representation

#### **The Legal Profession in Lower Courts**

**Unrepresented Defendants:** While the High Court (Dietrich v The Queen) mandates free counsel for poor defendants facing prison, **Legal Aid** budget constraints mean this rarely covers minor **summary offences**.

**Unqualified Prosecutors:** Unlike higher courts that use professional lawyers, lower courts rely on **police prosecutors** who lack legal degrees, independent oversight, and professional legal ethics. Australia is one of the few common law countries that still allows this.

### Legal profession uniform law

The **Legal Profession Uniform Law** project was started in 2009 to create a single, unified set of rules for lawyers across all of Australia.

So far, only New South Wales, Victoria, and Western Australia have joined. However, because these three states make up **75% of all Australian lawyers**, it has made practicing law across state borders much easier and more efficient.

### Technological change

Digitization and technology have transformed modern legal practice in four major ways:

- **Online Research:** Lawyers use digital databases (like LexisNexis and AustLII) instead of law books.
- **Easier Discovery:** Pre-trial document sorting (**discovery**) is now done instantly using searchable electronic databases.
- **Digital Filing:** Documents are submitted to courts online rather than in person.
- **Remote Courts:** Court hearings are frequently held via video-conferencing to save time and money.

### Automation and the law

**Robodebt** was an automated debt-recovery system used by the Australian Government starting in 2016. It is considered a massive, shameful failure of technology and public administration

#### How it Worked and The Flaw

- **The System**: A primitive computer program automatically matched annual tax data against fortnightly welfare payments
- **The Error**: If the numbers didn’t match, the system **automatically sent a debt notice**, forcing vulnerable people to prove they didn’t owe money. However, the system was flawed because it flatly divided annual income by 26, completely ignoring the fact that causal or short-term workers have fluctuating income

#### The Fallout

- **The Legal Defeat**: After a massive backlash and a class-action lawsuit, the government had to cancel **$1.763 billion** in fake debts, refund **$751 million** to 381,000 citizens, and pay a **$112 million** settlement
- **The Royal Commission (2023)**: An official inquiry found that public servants and politicians rushed the illegal scheme to save money, actively lied to cover it up, and that official watchdog agencies failed to stop it. The inquiry recommended criminal and civil prosecutions for responsible.

### Artificial intelligence and legal practice

Generative AI (like ChatGPT) marks the beginning of a **fourth industrial revolution**, heavily impacting the legal sector by automating both routine and complex tasks.

#### **What AI Can Do in Legal Practice**

- **Research and Writing:** Instantly summaries cases, details areas of law, and drafts serviceable contracts or court documents.
- **Litigation Prep:** Speeds up document discovery, reviews case files, and drafts briefs

#### **The Impact on Legal Jobs**

Instead of replacing lawyers entirely, AI is forcing the profession to "move up the skills ladder." Humans will increasingly focus on tasks AI cannot replicate: **complex legal judgment, strategic guidance, and building client relationships.**

#### **The Danger: "Hallucinations" and Fake Cases**

AI operates on algorithmic patterns, not actual understanding, meaning it can make highly confident but completely false errors.

> **The Cautionary Tale (_Mata v Avianca_, 2023):** A U.S. lawyer used ChatGPT for legal research, and the AI completely fabricated six fake court cases with fictional quotes. When the lawyer asked ChatGPT if the cases were real, the AI lied and said they were. The judge fined the lawyers for wasting court time, tricking their client, and damaging the reputation of the justice system.

### Technology and access to justice

The high cost and complexity of the Australian legal system mean that access to justice is often determined by personal wealth, power, and connections. While technology offers a solution, it also presents severe structural risks.

#### **The Potential: Leveling the Playing Field**

- **Lowering Financial Barriers:** Emergent AI tools can automate core legal tasks—such as predicting case outcomes, generating pleadings, and creating discovery requests—drastically reducing litigation costs.
- **Guidance for Ordinary Citizens:** Legal-tech AI can analyze past disputes to provide clear, accessible procedural guidance to self-represented individuals who cannot afford a lawyer.

#### **The Risks: Inequality and the "Legal Singularity"**

- **The Digital Divide ("Haves" vs. "Have-Nots"):** Large law firms and corporations have the resources to build advanced AI. Because most legal disputes settle confidentially outside of court, these "repeat players" retain exclusive access to the data needed to train the best AI, potentially worsening societal inequality.
- **The "Legal Singularity" & Loss of Humanity:** At its extreme, total automation could lead to a system where all legal outcomes are perfectly predictable. Commentators warn this would drain the vitality from the law:
    - It substitutes algorithmic **prediction for human persuasion and reason-giving**.
    - It strips the legal system of its ability to **adapt to new social developments** through public, democratic deliberation.
    - Resolving conflicts in "server farms" rather than open courts risks destroying the **public legitimacy** of the justice system.

To ensure AI promotes the rule of law rather than just serving the wealthy, governments must regulate data. This requires maximizing public access to legal case outcomes so that AI training data is not monopolized by elite corporations.

# Entry to the legal profession

Completing a law degree or diploma opens the door to joining the legal profession. As members of a "learned profession," lawyers are bound by distinct values and responsibilities.

#### What Defines a "Profession"?

- **Specialized Knowledge:** Professionals possess an organized, complex branch of knowledge not shared by the general public.
- **A Vocation of Service:** Becoming a lawyer is traditionally viewed as a "calling" to serve others, prioritizing the client's and public's needs above the lawyer's own self-interest.

#### **The Importance of High Ethical Standards**

Lawyers owe strict duties to the courts, clients, the profession, and the public. As emphasized by former Chief Justice Owen Dixon:

- Society naturally suspects specialized knowledge if it cannot understand it.
- To maintain **public trust and confidence**, the profession must strictly enforce high ethical standards and professional judgment. Without this trust, the entire function of the legal profession fails.

### Good Fame and Character

To be admitted to the legal profession, an applicant must prove they are a **"fit and proper person"** of **"good fame and character."** This requirement demands absolute candor and honesty from the moment a student begins their legal studies.

#### **The Absolute Obligation of Candor**

Applicants must make a full written disclosure of anything relevant to their fitness to practice.

- **Criminal History:** Every interaction with a court for a criminal offence must be disclosed. While minor offenses (excluding basic parking tickets) may not automatically block admission, hiding them will.
- **Academic Integrity:** Past incidents of plagiarism, cheating, or collusion during university or practical legal training can lead to a refusal or delay of admission. Courts stress that they cannot admit individuals who respond to stress by acting dishonestly (_Re AJG_).
- **Consequences of Dishonesty:** Attempting to hide "skeletons in the closet" or making a misleading disclosure (_Re Legal Profession Act 2004_) is treated as a deliberate or reckless misrepresentation to the court and will result in the refusal or revocation of a lawyer's admission.

#### **Key Cautionary Case: Nicola Gobbo ("Lawyer X")**

The standard of fitness is continuous and extends far beyond initial admission. The "Lawyer X" scandal serves as a stark warning:

- **The Breach:** Former barrister Nicola Gobbo spent over a decade representing criminal defendants while covertly acting as a police informant against them.
- **The Ruling:** The High Court condemned her actions as "fundamental and appalling breaches" of her duties. The Supreme Court of Victoria permanently stripped her of her legal credentials, ruling that conduct which corrupts and debases the foundations of the criminal justice system demands total removal from the profession.

#### To Practice Law in Australia

To practice law in Australia, you must complete three distinct stages:

**1. The "Priestley 11" (Academic)**

You must first complete a law degree that covers the **Priestley 11**—the 11 mandatory foundational law subjects (such as Contracts, Torts, Criminal Law, and Ethics) required for minimum legal competence.

**2. Practical Legal Training (PLT)**

After your degree, you must complete a **PLT** course. This is a practical program (usually resulting in a Graduate Diploma) that teaches hands-on skills like drafting documents and dealing with clients, replacing the old system of working as an apprentice ("articles").

**3. Becoming a Barrister (Optional)**

If you want to become a barrister (a courtroom advocate), you must fulfill extra rules that depend on your state:

- **NSW & Victoria:** You must pass a difficult Bar Exam, take a short Bar Practice Course, and spend a year being supervised by a senior barrister ("pupillage").
- **Western Australia:** There is no formal exam or mandatory supervision; you simply need a formal application backed by two senior references.

### Professional Conduct

- **The Rules:** Once admitted, lawyers must follow strict ethical rules. Solicitors follow the _Australian Solicitors' Conduct Rules_, and barristers follow a similar set of rules developed by the Australian Bar Association.
- **Uniformity:** These rules are becoming increasingly uniform across Australia, especially in states using the Legal Profession Uniform Law (NSW, Victoria, and Western Australia).
- **The Core Philosophy:** Law is a **service, not just a business**. Making money must always come second to the ideal of serving the public and the justice system.

#### A Lawyer’s Multiple Duties

Lawyers owe duties to their clients, fellow practitioners, and the public. However, a solicitor's **paramount (highest) duty is to the court and the administration of justice**.

### Duty to the Client

- **The Retainer:** A lawyer's duty to a client is based on a **retainer** (the formal agreement to provide legal services). Because clients are dependent on a lawyer's specialized training, they must be able to place a high degree of trust in them.
- **Key Obligations:** Lawyers must act in their client's best interests, be honest and courteous, and deliver services competently, diligently, and promptly.
- **Handling Complexities:** If a conflict arises—such as when representing a child whose best interests clash with their parents' wishes—the lawyer must use independent personal judgment. If a lawyer lacks expertise in a specific area of a case, they must seek advice from another specialist, or they can be sued by the client for negligence or breach of contract.

### Duty to the Court

**Duty to the Court** means that when a lawyer is admitted to practice, they become an official **officer of the court**. This duty is **paramount**—it completely overrides and trumps any duty to the client. Even if a client gives conflicting instructions, the court comes first.

**What this means in practice:**

- **No Deception:** A lawyer must never mislead the court or hide documents/legal authorities, even if they hurt their client's case.
- **No Playing Games:** If a lawyer spots an error during a criminal trial, they must point it out immediately to be fixed, rather than keeping it "up their sleeve" to use as an excuse for an appeal later.
- **No Unfair Attacks:** They cannot make groundless, damaging accusations against witnesses or other parties.

**Consequences of breaking the rule:**

If a lawyer violates this duty, the judge can refer them to a **disciplinary body** or force the lawyer to personally **pay the legal costs** caused by their behavior.

### Duty to fellow practitioners and third parties

#### Duty to Other Lawyers

- **Professional Civility:** Lawyers must be honest, fair, and polite to the opposing side's lawyer—traditionally calling them **"my learned friend."** * **No Playing Tricks:** They must never mislead opposing lawyers, even if hiding information would help their own client.
- **Keeping Promises:** If a lawyer gives an **undertaking** (a formal promise to do something), they must strictly honor it. Persistent rudeness or dishonesty can lead to disciplinary penalties.

#### Duty to Third Parties

- **Acting as an Agent:** When negotiating deals (like property sales or contracts) with third parties, the lawyer acts as an **agent** for their client (the **principal**). They must follow the client's instructions and clearly tell the third party they are just acting as an agent.
- **Avoiding Conflicts of Interest:** A lawyer must not take on a case if they have a personal or business relationship with the third party. They must also avoid unprofessional, casual contact with personal friends who happen to be the opposing lawyer on a case.

### Duty to community

- **Be Competent:** Work hard and do a good job.
- **No Bad Advertising:** Never market services in a cheap or misleading way.
- **Watch Your Behavior:** Act appropriately in public and choose social contacts wisely, even outside of work.

### Model Litigant Obligations when action for government

When a lawyer represents the government, they must act as a **"model litigant."** Because the government has massive public power and resources, its lawyers must act as role models for fairness and honesty.

**Rules of "Fair Play" include:**

- **No games:** Don't use minor technical legal rules to stall or complicate the case.
- **Save time/money:** Try to settle disputes early and keep court costs low.
- **Be honest:** Don't force the other side to prove something the government already knows is true.
- **Help others:** Assist opponents who don't have their own lawyers, and never take unfair advantage of their lack of experience.

**The Main Rule:** The government cannot go back on its word or play tricks to win a case. Breaking these rules can result in judges punishing the government by making them pay the other side's legal bills

### Ethical behaviour

Lawyers frequently face **ethical dilemmas** that have no easy solutions. Resolving these issues requires balancing competing duties while completely ignoring self-interest.

#### 1. Doing the Right Thing

- **Seek Advice:** Ethical dilemmas are hard. Good lawyers don't have to know every answer instantly—they should ask senior colleagues for help.
- **Ignore Self-Interest:** Doing the right thing might cost time, lose a client, or hurt your career. An ethical lawyer must ignore those consequences entirely.

#### 2. Money vs. Ethics

- **The Business Pressure:** Today, law is a business. Firms heavily pressure staff to make profits, which can tempt lawyers to focus on money over honest advice.
- **Over-Aggression:** Financial pressure can make lawyers act too aggressively just to win, breaking the rules of courtesy and fair play.

#### 3. Personal Integrity

- **No Bullying:** Lawyers must never use their legal status to get special perks or intimidate regular people out of suing them.
- **Respect Juniors:** Senior lawyers must never abuse their power over junior staff.

### Ethics of Criminal Prosecution

A prosecutor represents the community. Their job is to **seek the truth and ensure a fair trial**, not just to "win" convictions.

- **Full Disclosure:** Prosecutors must immediately share all evidence with the defense, even if it helps prove the defendant is innocent. (e.g., The _Mallard_ conviction was thrown out because the prosecutor hid a forensic report that cleared the defendant).
- **Call All Key Witnesses:** They must call all vital witnesses to give a complete picture of the event, even if a witness's testimony damages the prosecution's case. (e.g., In _Gilham_, the prosecutor failed this duty by hiding an expert pathologist who disagreed with their theory).

### Ethics of Criminal Defence

A defence lawyer’s role is to provide fearless advocacy to protect a client’s liberty against the superior power of the state

- **The Cab Rank Rule**: Barristers cannot refuse a case just because they dislike the client or the crime. Everyone is entitled to a legal defence
- **Testing the Case**: The lawyer’s job is to force the prosecution to prove guilt beyond a reasonable doubt, which includes aggressively challenging witness credibility
- **Confessions of Guilt**: If a client confesses guilt to their lawyer but still wants to plead “not guilty”, the lawyer can continue to represent them under strict limits:
    - They **can** force the prosecution to prove its case
    - They **cannot** lie, present a false story, or blame someone else
    - The **must quit** the case if the client insists on lying under oath (perjury)
- **No Misleading**: Unlike prosecutors, defence lawyers do not have to volunteer damaging facts. However, they must never actively lie to or mislead the court.
