**Author:** Irene Watson (Associate Professor, drawing from Tanganekald and Meintangk Peoples’ perspectives). **Core Premise:** Aboriginal peoples have never ceded their sovereignty, and the Australian colonial state lacks lawful authority for its existence and dispossession of Aboriginal lands.

**1. Pre-existing Aboriginal Legal Systems & Treaties**

- **Complex Legal Systems:** Prior to the 1788 invasion, First Nations peoples had complex legal systems that evolved over thousands of years.
- **Evidence of Agreements:** While there are no written records, treaties and agreements between different Aboriginal peoples were recorded in **song lines**. These regulated shared, exclusive, and trading spaces.
- **No Treaties with the British:** The British never attempted to enter into treaties with First Nations Peoples in Australia. No consent was ever obtained to occupy or develop the land.

**2. The Hypocrisy of "British Subject" Status**

- **The Included-Excluded:** The colonial project gave Aboriginal people various forced identities, including "British subjects" and "Australians," to remove their sovereignty.
- **1840 Coorong Massacre:** Tanganekald people resisted incursions in South Australia, leading to the hanging of Tanganekald men without trial.
- **Justice Cooper’s 1840 Ruling:** When assessing the massacre, Justice Cooper ruled that the English law could not apply because the Aboriginal people had "never submitted themselves to our dominion".
- **Unfinished Business:** Aboriginal peoples were deemed "British subjects," yet they were routinely denied the legal protections against murder and theft that should come with that status.

**3. The Failure of Domestic Law (Native Title & ILUAs)**

- **Terra Nullius:** The legal fiction of _terra nullius_ was used to legally annihilate Aboriginal peoples. Even after _Mabo (No. 2)_ rejected the theory, it remains embedded as the legitimating foundation of the state.
- **Native Title Limitations:** _Mabo_ and Native Title legislation did not provide actual land rights; instead, they entrenched the colonisers' quest for legitimacy and continued assimilationist policies.
- **Indigenous Land Usage Agreements (ILUAs):** Watson warns that ILUAs are highly problematic. They are domestic arrangements (not international treaties) that risk being used to manufacture the "consent of the native" under duress, failing to address massive power imbalances between Aboriginal peoples and state/corporate negotiators.

**4. International Law and Future Possibilities**

- **Sovereign Subjects:** Aboriginal peoples are pre-existing subjects of international law; they did not lose their sovereignty just because colonial powers ignored it.
- **Burden of Proof:** Currently, Aboriginal people are forced to prove their ongoing sovereignty. Watson argues the **burden of proof should fall on the state** to prove by what lawful authority it gained jurisdiction.
- **UN Treaty Study (Miguel Martinez):** In 1999, Martinez highlighted the "ethical imperative" to undo historical wrongs and noted that humanity owes a debt to Indigenous peoples.
- **Need for an Independent International Mechanism:** Domestic laws frequently clash with international human rights (e.g., the Northern Territory Intervention ignored UN recommendations regarding racial discrimination). Therefore, an empowered, independent international body is urgently needed to review disputes between Indigenous peoples and the state.
