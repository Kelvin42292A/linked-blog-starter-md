### Common Law (UK)

Common law is a legal system that the judgements of the courts operate not only to solve the particular dispute, but also stands as a precedents for the future. It is influenced by Greek and Christian legal principles. Common law’s name comes from it being common being applied to all of England, Wales and slowly to Ireland. The major source of common law is cases but more modern way of law being set up is by legislation.

### Civil Law (Germany, Other EU and South America Countries)

Civil law is a system that is centered on ‘code’ or law that is made or registered rather than looking into past cases. Essentially civil law is more leaning towards Legislative Statutes. After French Revolution, napoleon has set up a civil law system in his country and thus set a base for civil law to become the foundation of most European countries. ### Development of case law

Judges make decision in order to resolve disputes, the secondary thought is to set a sample for future disputes. It is more of a legislature’s job to regulate the law. Case can be thought as a brick and a bunch of bricks could construct a wall which forms a firm legal framework. In 1860, the official reports is finally put out by The Incorporated Council of Law Reporting. This body is to publish case reports that has been checked by judges who made the decisions. While judges are bound by past cases (precedent), they also bring their own human perspective to the bench. Since precedent isn’t everything, and context and opinions matter because law has to evolve.

**Example:** The Problem: Ms. Donoghue drank beer of an opaque bottle, found a rotting snail at the bottom of the drink and got ill. Historically, if you bought a bad product, you sued the seller for “breach of contract”. But Ms. Donoghue didn’t buy the drink but her friend did. Therefore, she had no contract with the cafe owner, She tried to sure the manufacturer directly, but at the time in 1932, there was no established legal rule saying a manufacturer owned any duty to a consumer they didn’t have a direct contract with.

The Solution: To allow Ms. Donoghue to win, Lord Atkin had to invent a new legal principle. The text outlines the exact moment he created the modern concept of **Duty of Care.**

He took the moral/biblical rule “love your neighbour” and turned it into a legal rule: “you must not injure your neighbour.”

The Core Rule: The most concerning part about this text is “Who, then, in law is my neighbour?”

He states that your legal neighbour is anyone who is so closely and directly affected by your actions that you should reasonably foresee that they could get hurt if you aren’t careful.

### The Basic Case Note

**Citation:** The case name and year

**Court:** Which court heard it (e.g, Single judge, Court of Appeal)

**Facts:** A brief summary of what happened

**Issues:** The ground of appeal or the questions the court had to answer

**Reasons:** The legal principles used to make the decisions

**Decision:** The final result.

### The Detailed Case Note

**Procedural History:** What happened in the lower courts before it got here?

**Legal Analysis:** How did the court analyse the law

**Application:** Exactly how did the judge apply the legal principle to the specific facts?

**Orders:** The formal legal orders made by the court

**Context:** Any important social or cultural factors surrounding the case

### Terminology

There are two main forms of legislation: which is statute and Acts (made by Parliament) and delegated or subordinate legislation.

### Subordinate laws

They are made by someone other than Parliament, typically the Governor General, a Governor, a Minister of the Crown, or a statutory body such as a board, university or council. The authority of these law-makers are found in an Act or statute of Parliament. These law can be rules, regulations, statutory rules, subordinate law, legislative instruments, ordinances and by laws. They are usually called delegated, subordinate, or secondary legislation.

### Differences between legislation and case law

Legislative bodies can be proactive. They don’t have to wait for a dispute to arise before making new laws. They make law to prevent problems from occurring. It might seems like they have infinity power in a certain matter but in practice not really. The law should not intrude, confine the reach of legislation. It also constrain by political process, constitutional restrictions on legislative power. Where legislative rules affects what happens after, but not before ,usually. There should be a date of assent where the bill is turning into an Act of Parliament, where it is the final stage of the legislative process.

### Value or weight of legislative rules

**First principle** is that if there is an inconsistency between the later statute or subordinate law with the earlier law, the earlier law might be repealed. Courts are reluctant to repeal the law and would try to differentiate between the the older and the newer law that have some sort of inconsistency.

**Second principle** is that an Act or statute takes precedence over subordinate laws. There is sometimes where ‘as if enacted in this Act’ clause would give the subordinate laws equal status as the Act.

**Third principle** is that legislation made by higher Parliament in Australia case National Parliament prevails over state Parliaments.

### Henry VIII clause

It lets the government “chop off the head” of a law without asking Parliament for permission first. Chop off their head is a nickname for government’s power to kill or override an existing law. It means a government minister can use small rule to “behead” or cancel out a major law that was originally passed by Parliament, to pass the usual democratic debate.

### Legislation take precedence over case law

If there is an inconsistency between the legislation and case law, the legislation take precedence. As a result, Parliament can always change the law by legislation if they think the case law is undesirable.

This does not means that courts are under parliament. Courts have to resolve disputes about the meaning of the words in legislation and courts’ interpretation may be different from Parliament’s intention.

Moreover, the courts has to ensure that the legislation made only on topic within the constitution power, otherwise it can be struck down by court.

### Making statutes

Who makes the law

There are **nine** main groups (legislatures) that make laws in Australia:

- The Federal (Commonwealth) Parliament
- The 6 State Parliaments
- The 2 Territory Assemblies (ACT and NT)

One house or two?

- **Bicameral** (Two Houses): Most of the states have an “Upper House” and a “Lower House”. Both must agree before a law is passed
- **Unicameral** (One House): Queensland, the ACT, and the NT only have one house (one group of people voting).

What’s a “Bill”?

- **Bill:** A draft version of law. It isn’t a “statute” (official law) until it is passed
- **Clauses:** The name for the specific sections or “rules” inside the Bill
- **Money Bills:** Special drafts about taxes or government spending. In the Federal Parliament, the Senate (Upper House) has limited power over these; They can suggest changes but cannot start or officially amend them.

### Financial scrutiny of legislation

Two key federal bodies proposing government expenditure: the Parliamentary Budget Office (PBO) and the Senate estimates committees

### PBO

Independent, non-partisan analysis body for cost proposals. Set up by statute in 2012. Ensure policies properly costed before being made to public. The role of PBO make information available to assist voters, Members of Parliaments and parliamentary committees to understand the financial budgets and policies.

### Senate Legislation committees

Known informally as ‘estimates committees’. They exam income and expenditure of government departments and other government agencies. Committee has six senators: three from the **government** and two from the **opposition**, and one from a **minor party**. The hearings are open to public. Reports by the committees include issue of concern.
