## Australia's Journey to Legal Independence: Master Notion Notes

### 1. Summary: The Australia Acts 1986

Date of Effect: 5:00 am Greenwich Mean Time on 3 March 1986.

Enactment Method: Passed as virtually identical statutes by both the Commonwealth Parliament (under s 51(xxxviii) of the Constitution, with the request and consent of all State Parliaments) and the United Kingdom Parliament (Australia Act 1986 (UK)).

Significance: This dual-enactment marked the absolute completion of Australia’s journey to constitutional and legal independence, permanently severing all remaining legislative and judicial ties to the UK.

#### Key Statutory Provisions:

Section 1 (Legislative Independence): Terminated the power of the UK Parliament to pass any law extending to the Commonwealth, States, or Territories of Australia.

Section 3 (State Legislative Autonomy): Abolished the Colonial Laws Validity Act 1865 in relation to the Australian States. State Parliaments could now pass laws that were repugnant to (conflicted with) British statutes.

Section 11 (Judicial Finality): Completely terminated the final avenue of appeal from State Supreme Courts directly to the Privy Council in London.

### 2. Timeline A: Legislative Journey to Independence

[1823] New South Wales Act │ (Imperial Act establishing the first appointed legislative body in NSW) ▼ [1828] Australian Courts Act │ (Set the "Reception Date" of English law as 25 July 1828) ▼ [1850] Australian Constitutions Act (No 2) │ (Authorized colonies to draft constitutions and establish bicameral parliaments) ▼ [1865] Colonial Laws Validity Act │ (Established the "repugnancy" doctrine; validated colonial laws unless they clashed with Imperial statutes) ▼ [1901] Commonwealth Constitution │ (Established the federal legislative framework under an Imperial Act) ▼ [1931/1942] Statute of Westminster & Adoption Act │ (Granted legislative independence to the Commonwealth Parliament, but NOT the States) ▼ [1986] Australia Acts 1986 (Ended all UK legislative power over Australia; freed States from repugnancy)

|Date|Milestone / Event|Source / Jurisdiction|Significance & Legal Impact|
|---|---|---|---|
|1823|New South Wales Act 1823|Imperial (British)|Created a limited, non-representative Legislative Council of 5 to 7 appointed members to advise the Governor. This was the first shift away from absolute military rule towards civil administration.|
|1828|Australian Courts Act 1828|Imperial (British)|Formally established the reception of English law as of 25 July 1828. All statutory and common law of England in force on this date was "received" into NSW law, provided it was applicable to colonial conditions.|
|1850|Australian Constitutions Act (No 2) 1850|Imperial (British)|Granted colonies the authority to draft their own constitutions, establish bicameral legislatures, and introduce responsible/representative government, laying the groundwork for independent local law-making.|
|1865|Colonial Laws Validity Act 1865|Imperial (British)|Clarified colonial legislative powers. It ruled that colonial laws were valid even if they conflicted with English common law. However, they remained invalid (void for "repugnancy") if they conflicted with Imperial statutes applying to the colony by "express words or necessary intendment."|
|1901|Commonwealth of Australia Constitution Act 1900|Imperial (British)|Created the Federal Parliament of Australia. While self-governing, both the federal and state parliaments remained theoretically subject to the Colonial Laws Validity Act 1865 and British legislative supremacy.|
|1931|Statute of Westminster 1931|Imperial (British)|Freed the Federal Parliaments of British Dominions from the Colonial Laws Validity Act (no repugnancy) and declared that UK Acts would not extend to a Dominion without its request and consent.|
|1942|Statute of Westminster Adoption Act 1942|Commonwealth (Australian)|The Commonwealth Parliament formally adopted the Statute of Westminster (backdated to 3 Sept 1939). This granted full legislative independence to the Federal Parliament. Crucially, the Australian States remained colonies of the British Crown and were still bound by the Colonial Laws Validity Act.|
|1986|Australia Acts 1986|Commonwealth, State & UK|Completed legislative independence. Section 1 terminated all future UK legislative authority over Australia. Section 2 gave States extraterritorial power. Section 3 repealed the Colonial Laws Validity Act for the States, making them fully sovereign.|

### 3. Timeline B: Judicial Journey to Independence

[1823] Charter of Justice │ (Established the Supreme Court of NSW; appeals to Governor & Privy Council) ▼ [1901] Commonwealth Constitution (s 73 & s 74) │ (Created High Court; limited Privy Council appeals on constitutional matters) ▼ [1968] Privy Council (Limitation of Appeals) Act │ (Abolished Privy Council appeals in all federal and territory matters) ▼ [1975] Privy Council (Appeals from the High Court) Act │ (Ended all remaining appeals from the High Court to the Privy Council) ▼ [1986] Australia Acts 1986 (s 11) (Abolished direct appeals from State Supreme Courts to the Privy Council)

​

|Date|Milestone / Event|Source / Jurisdiction|Significance & Legal Impact|
|---|---|---|---|
|1823|Charter of Justice 1823 (NSW Act)|Imperial (British)|Established the Supreme Court of New South Wales as a court of record. Appeals lay to the Governor (assisted by the Judge Advocate) and, in larger civil matters, directly to the Judicial Committee of the Privy Council in London.|
|1901|Commonwealth Constitution (ss 73, 74)|Imperial / Commonwealth|Section 73 established the High Court of Australia as a general court of appeal for all colonial/state courts. Section 74 restricted appeals to the Privy Council on constitutional (inter se) questions unless the High Court issued a certificate.|
|1968|Privy Council (Limitation of Appeals) Act 1968|Commonwealth (Australian)|Abolished all appeals from the High Court of Australia to the Privy Council in matters involving federal law, Territory law, and federal constitutional matters.|
|1975|Privy Council (Appeals from the High Court) Act 1975|Commonwealth (Australian)|Closed the door on all remaining appeals from the High Court to the Privy Council in purely domestic/State law matters. The High Court was now the absolute apex court for any matter that reached it.|
|1986|Australia Acts 1986 (Section 11)|Commonwealth, State & UK|Terminated the final remaining "backdoor" avenue of appeal where litigants could appeal directly from State Supreme Courts to the Privy Council, bypassing the High Court. Since 3 March 1986, the High Court of Australia is the sole, absolute final court of appeal for all Australian legal matters.|

### 4. Key Comparative Legal Doctrines (For Exams & Notion)

#### A. Repugnancy vs. Sovereignty

Repugnancy (Colonial Era): Under the Colonial Laws Validity Act 1865, if a local state or colonial law conflicted with a British Imperial law designed to apply to Australia, the local law was void.

Sovereignty (Post-1986): Following Section 3 of the Australia Acts, the states are no longer subject to the repugnancy doctrine. State parliaments have sovereign power to alter or repeal any inherited British law, provided it falls within their constitutional limits.

#### B. Federal vs. State Independence Gaps (1942–1986)

For 44 years (between the adoption of the Statute of Westminster in 1942 and the Australia Acts in 1986), Australia had a "hybrid" legal status.

The Commonwealth Government was fully independent of Great Britain.

However, the individual Australian States remained technically dependent colonies of the British Crown. The British Parliament could still theoretically pass laws for the States, the States could not pass laws that conflicted with Imperial statutes, and appeals could still go from State courts to the Privy Council in London.

This constitutional anomaly was only resolved by the coordinated passage of the Australia Acts in 1986.
