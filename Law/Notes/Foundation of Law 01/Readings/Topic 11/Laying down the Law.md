# Chapter 2 Summary: The Origins & Historical Development of the Modern Australian Legal System

## **1. The Foundation of the Australian Legal System & Colonisation**

### **A. Indigenous Customary Law & Sovereignty**

- **Aboriginal Presence:** Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples have lived in Australia for over 60,000 years, practicing complex customary legal systems [3].
- **Disregard by Settlers:** The colonizing British state systematically failed to recognize Indigenous legal culture, customary law, and sovereignty [3]. This systemic disrespect created a complex set of ongoing legal and social problems that the modern Australian legal system continues to grapple with [3].

### **B. Transportation and Settlement**

- **Penal Outpost Origins:** The Australian legal system began as a penal outpost of the British Empire [3].
- **Imperial Authorization:** In 1717, the British Imperial Parliament passed a statute (4 Geo 1, c 11) allowing judges to commute death sentences to "transportation" to the colonies [3]. Following the American Revolution, Britain lost its primary destination for convicts, prompting the designation of New South Wales, Norfolk Island, and Bermuda as new penal sites in 1786 [3].
- **The First Fleet:** Led by Governor Arthur Phillip, the First Fleet arrived in May 1787 carrying 717 convicts and 290 free persons [3]. Phillip officially claimed the territory for King George III on 26 January 1788 [3].
- **Military Rules:** Early legal authority in the colony originated strictly in the military administration of penal law, heavily shaping subsequent legal and governmental structures [3]. Convict transportation to NSW was terminated in 1840, and to Van Diemen's Land (Tasmania) in 1852 [3].

## **2. The Reception of English Law & Terra Nullius**

### **A. The Status of Acquired Territories**

Under British common law, the laws applicable to a newly acquired territory depended on how it was acquired [7]:

1. **Conquest or Cession:** The existing laws of the territory remained in force until altered by the British Parliament [7].
2. **Settlement:** If an uninhabited country was discovered and planted by British subjects, all English laws then in force immediately applied, **but only so much of the English law as was applicable to the conditions of an infant colony** (William Blackstone's formulation) [7].

### **B. The Doctrine of Terra Nullius**

- **"Land Belonging to No One":** Because the British did not recognize Indigenous agricultural, structural, or political systems, they classified Australia as _terra nullius_ (desert and uncultivated) [7].
- **Legal Fiction of Settlement:** The British assumed that First Nations peoples had no organized political system or land ownership [7]. Consequently, New South Wales was treated as a **settled colony** rather than a conquered one (_Cooper v Stuart_) [7]. This legal fiction of "peaceful settlement" erased Indigenous land rights until modern reforms [7].

### **C. The Reception Date**

- **Australian Courts Act 1828 (Imp):** To resolve confusion about which English laws were applicable, s 24 of this Act established a formal date of reception [7].
- **The Date:** All English common law, equity, and statutes in force on **25 July 1828** were received into New South Wales and Van Diemen's Land, provided they were suitable for the colonial conditions [7].

## **3. The Evolution of an Independent Legislature**

The transition of New South Wales from a military autocracy to an independent, representative democracy occurred in several incremental stages:

```
[1788: Absolute Governor Power]
       │
[1823: First Legislative Council (5-7 members - advisory only)]
       │
[1825: Executive Council created to advise Governor]
       │
[1828: Legislative Council expanded (10-15 members with veto power)]
       │
[1842: Representative Legislature (2/3 elected, 1/3 Crown-nominated)]
       │
[1850s: Responsible Self-Government & Bicameral Parliament]
```

- **Absolute Autocracy (1788):** The early Governors wielded virtually absolute civil, military, and political power [5].
- **First Legislative Council (1823):** The _New South Wales Act 1823_ (Imp) created a Legislative Council consisting of 5 to 7 members appointed by the Crown to advise the Governor [5].
- **Executive Council (1825):** Established to advise the Governor on executive, military, civil, and commercial matters [5].
- **Expanding Legislative Veto (1828):** The Imperial Parliament expanded the Council to 10–15 members and gave them the power to veto laws proposed by the Governor [5]. It also fixed the date of reception of English law [5].
- **Representative Government (1842):** The _Australian Constitutions Act (No 1)_ (Imp) expanded the Legislative Council to 36 members (two-thirds elected, one-third nominated by the Crown) [5]. The Governor was removed from the legislature but retained the power to introduce bills, grant royal assent, or veto bills [5].
- **Responsible Government (1850s):** The _Australian Constitutions Act (No 2)_ (Imp) granted colonies the power to draft their own constitutions to establish independent, responsible self-government [5]. In 1853, NSW drafted a constitution establishing a **bicameral legislature** (with an elected Lower House and an appointed Upper House), which was enacted via the _New South Wales Constitution Statute 1855_ (Imp) [2].

## **4. Development of Judicial Independence**

- **Early Military Courts:** Early colonial courts lacked independence, being run by military officers and structured along military lines [2].
- **New South Wales Act 1823 (Imp):** This landmark Act established the Supreme Court of New South Wales as an independent, civil judicial system [2].
- **Sir Francis Forbes (First Chief Justice):** Forbes fiercely defended judicial independence from gubernatorial and military interference [2]. His tenure marked the transition from an autocratic penal colony to a society governed by the rule of law [2].
- **Trial by Jury:** In 1828, the _Australian Courts Act_ gave the Governor power to introduce trial by jury in criminal matters, which was finally fully established in 1833 [2].

## **5. Repugnancy & The Colonial Laws Validity Act 1865**

### **A. The Repugnancy Crisis**

- **The Constraint:** Colonial legislatures could not pass laws that were "repugnant" (contradictory) to English law [4].
- **Judicial Overreach (Justice Boothby):** In the mid-19th century, South Australian Supreme Court Justice Benjamin Boothby struck down a vast amount of South Australian legislation (including the revolutionary Torrens land title system) on the basis that they were repugnant to English laws [4].

### **B. The Legislative Fix: Colonial Laws Validity Act 1865 (Imp)**

To resolve Boothby's disruption, the Imperial Parliament passed this crucial clarifying Act [4]:

- **Narrowed Repugnancy:** A colonial law was only void for repugnancy if it directly contradicted an Imperial statute that applied to the colony by **express words or necessary intendment** [4].
- **Constitutional Autonomy:** It explicitly gave colonial legislatures the power to amend their own constitutions, provided they followed proper "manner and form" requirements [4].

## **6. The Judicial Committee of the Privy Council**

- **The Imperial Court of Appeal:** Located in London, the Judicial Committee of the Privy Council served as the ultimate court of appeal for the colonies [4].
- **Accessibility Barriers:** Due to the massive expense and geographical distance, it was highly inaccessible [8]. Prominent legal figures like Henry Higgins remarked: _"The poor man never goes to the Privy Council"_ [8].
- **Step-by-Step Abolition:** Appeals to the Privy Council were severed in three stages [8]:
    1. _Privy Council (Limitation of Appeals) Act 1968_ (Cth): Abolished appeals from the High Court on federal and constitutional matters [8].
    2. _Privy Council (Appeals from the High Court) Act 1975_ (Cth): Abolished all remaining appeals from the High Court of Australia [8].
    3. _Australia Acts 1986_: Terminated all remaining direct appeals from State Supreme Courts, making the High Court of Australia the ultimate domestic authority [8].

## **7. The Path to Constitutional Independence**

### **A. Federation (1901)**

- **The Union:** On 1 January 1901, the six self-governing colonies united to form the Commonwealth of Australia under the _Commonwealth of Australia Constitution Act 1900_ (Imp) [8].
- **Limited Autonomy:** While Australia gained domestic self-government, it remained legally subordinate to the British Crown and Parliament, with no independent international legal identity [1].

### **B. Statute of Westminster 1931 (Imp)**

- **The Break from Imperial Power:** A major legislative step toward full legal autonomy [1].
- **Key Provisions (Section 2, 3, & 4) [1]:**
    - Repealed the repugnancy doctrine for the Commonwealth Parliament.
    - Confirmed the Commonwealth’s power to make laws with extraterritorial effect.
    - Declared that the British Parliament would not legislate for the Commonwealth unless explicitly requested and consented to by Australia.
- **Adoption (1942):** To address wartime urgencies, Australia formally adopted these provisions via the _Statute of Westminster Adoption Act 1942_ (Cth), backdating the adoption to 3 September 1939 (the start of World War II) [1].

### **C. The Australia Acts 1986**

Despite the _Statute of Westminster_, individual Australian states retained direct colonial links to the British Crown and were still legally bound by the repugnancy doctrine [1]. This final anomaly was resolved by the simultaneous passage of the _Australia Act 1986_ by the Commonwealth, State, and UK Parliaments [1]:

- **Complete Legislative Independence:** Terminated the UK Parliament's power to legislate for any part of Australia [1].
- **State Autonomy:** Freed Australian states from the repugnancy doctrine and the _Colonial Laws Validity Act 1865_ [1].
- **Judicial Finality:** Terminated all remaining appeal pathways to the Privy Council in London, establishing the High Court of Australia as the undisputed pinnacle of the Australian judicial system [1, 6].
