# Lack of Organization

## 1. Avoid No Organization

- **The Risk:** Most costly mistake; causes you to miss key issues and connections.
- **The Fix:** Take time to **jot down a brief outline** before writing.
- **Methods:** Structure by **parties**, **legal issues**, **dates**, or **assets**.

## 2. Avoid Over-Organization

- **The Risk:** Too many subheadings break continuity.
- **Bad Practice:** Separating all pros (Plaintiff) from all cons (Defendant).
- **Good Practice:** Address flaws immediately after stating an argument. Discuss **liability** _before_ defenses.

# Reading the Facts

## 1. Avoid Rushing

- **The Risk:** Hurrying through the facts just to start writing is a "false economy" that leads to poor answers.
- **The Process:** Read once for the **general drift**, then reread with **extreme care**. Every word/date/party matters.

## 2. Question the Question

- Always ask: _"Why is this fact, date, or party being told to me?"_
- Make a conscious commitment to double-check details so you don't confuse names or places.

## 3. Avoid Over-Suspicion

- Do not over-analyze or doubt clear facts given in the prompt (e.g., if the text says someone is dead, accept it—don't write _"If they are in truth dead..."_).

# The Importance of Role

- **Follow the Assignment:** Approach the question strictly based on your assigned role (e.g., an advocate will write differently than an impartial judge or a legislative drafter).
- **Watch for Changes:** Stay alert, as examiners often change your role from one question to the next.
- **Analyze the Fact Stage:** Notice where the facts stand. Are they already decided by a lower court, or are you shaping them for an argument? If arguing, tailor your tone to your audience (e.g., a professional judge vs. a jury of laypersons).

# Read the Instructions

- **Create a Schedule:** Look at the relative weight of the questions and plan your time before you start writing.
- **Stick to the Clock:** Set strict deadlines for when you must move on to the next question (e.g., if you have 3 equal questions in 2.5 hours, spend exactly 45 minutes on each).
- **Avoid the Time Trap:** Leaving a question mostly blank and writing "Time!" at the end is a self-inflicted wound that will ruin your grade.

# Padding

- **Quantity ≠ Quality:** Examiners never give credit for word count. Extra paragraphs are just a waste of time.
- **The Rule:** Go immediately to the issues. Only mention a fact when it becomes relevant to your analysis.
- **Three Common Types of Padding to Avoid:**
    1. **Regurgitating Facts:** Never rewrite or summarize the facts. The examiner already knows them.
    2. **Wind-ups:** Avoid lengthy intros about general policies, broad values, or explaining how you plan to structure your essay.
    3. **Copying Text (Open Book Exams):** Never waste time writing out long quotations from statutes or casebooks. **Cite it, don’t copy it.**

# Inventing Facts

- **The Problem:** Manufacturing convenient facts just to avoid complex legal issues maddens examiners.
- **The Rule:** **Never add to the facts given.** If information is missing, do not invent it to make your life easier.
- **How to Handle Gaps:**
    - **Unknown Arguments:** If you don't know what position a party took in court, treat them as _possibilities_ instead of making up a definite statement.
    - **Unknown Jurisdiction:** If the location isn't specified and laws conflict, analyze the **conflict of authority**—do not just assign a convenient rule to weasel out of it.
- **When to Add Conditions:** It is acceptable to briefly state how a missing fact _would_ change your analysis if it were present, but do not dwell on it.

# Authority

- **The Goal:** Strike a balance between under-citing and over-citing to make your answer precise and lawyerly.
- **Good Practice:** On open-book exams (especially statutory courses), **always include specific section numbers** and relevant case names when discussing authority.
- **Bad Practice (Senseless Citation):** Do not casually drop case names or statutes just to show you know them. If you don't explain _how_ the authority is relevant to your argument, it weakens your answer.

# Negative Issue Spotting

- **Good Practice:** Briefly state that a specific issue _does not_ apply because a particular fact or factor rules it out. This shows a precise understanding.
- **The Error:** Writing a long explanation about a topic just because you studied it, even though it isn't relevant to the exam question.
- **The Rule:** If an issue isn't on the exam, move on. Do not waste a page explaining a concept just to prove you know it—the examiner already knows it isn't there.

# Knowledge

- **Knowledge Use > Memorization:** Examiners do not want you to simply "barf back" class notes. Strong essays show the ability to _apply_ your knowledge to a brand-new problem or set of facts.
- **Don't Dump Everything:** Never expect to use every single thing you learned in the course on the exam.
- **Focus on Advanced Concepts:** If a question probes for advanced knowledge, focus on that "higher ground." Do not waste time over-emphasizing basic concepts just because you memorized them—the basics are just the foundation required to solve the advanced problem.

# Procedure and Remedy

- **Cross-Cutting Issues:** Always check for common procedural issues that affect substantive legal rights:
    - **Standing:** Does the party have a sufficient interest to bring the case?
    - **Timing:** Has a delay triggered **laches** or **statute of limitations** problems?
- **Prioritize Remedies:** Almost every exam question will involve remedies. It is an essential component of a complete answer.
- **The Golden Question:** Once you identify a wrong (e.g., a tort or breach), immediately ask yourself: _"Which of the many things a court can do about this wrong is appropriate here, and why?"_

# Question-Begging

- **The Problem:** The most common error in legal analysis is stating a conclusion without justifying it (giving a conclusion without the reasoning).
- **The Rule:** The right answer isn't right unless you show _why_. You must explicitly show how the facts trigger the law and lead to your result.
- **Beware of "Clearly":** Avoid phrases like _"It is clear that..."_ or _"Clearly..."_ Law exams rarely have major issues that can be hand-waved away with an adverb.
- **Distinguish Issue Difficulty:**
    - **Easy Issues:** State the conclusion quickly and move on. A major hallmark of a weak essay is over-analyzing a straightforward point to death.
    - **Hard Issues:** Spend your time here. Signal your awareness of how close or open the issue is, and deeply analyze the arguments for both sides.

# Issue Spotting Is Not Enough

- **The Reality:** Spotting the issue is just the first step. You must move past merely identifying the rule and dig into its deep implications and difficulties for the parties.
- **The "A" Student Secret:** Examiners often write facts that _almost_ fit a rule, but not quite. To get top marks, show sensitivity to **distinguishing the facts** from the rule based on the rule's underlying purpose.
- **Avoid Abstract Doctrine:** Do not write generalized, abstract essays about legal theories.
- **The Rule:** Tie every single point back to the specific details of the prompt. Show exactly **which facts** raise the issue and **how** that issue alters the rights of the parties.

# The Other Side

- **The Hardest Part:** Keeping an open mind to all sides of an issue. Avoid the natural trap of immediately taking a single stand and blocking out opposing positions.
- **The "A" Student Secret:** A truly great answer does not just provide a preferred solution—it actively **develops both sides** of the problem by constantly asking: _"What would the other side argue against my view?"_
- **Master Arguing in the Alternative:**
    - If you resolve an issue one way, but know that a _different_ resolution would open up a whole new branch of legal issues, analyze that second branch anyway.
    - **The Formula:** Use phrases like: _"Even if the court finds that X does not apply, the plaintiff can alternatively argue Y because..."_
- **The Rule:** Never use your own conclusion as an excuse to duck or avoid addressing complex issues that the facts clearly invite you to discuss.

# Irresolution

- **Reach a Result:** Lawyers are paid to advise and judges are paid to decide. Do not leave your answer hanging by just saying a doctrine _might_ apply.
- **Answer the "Why" and "How":** Commit to an outcome. Explain exactly _why_ and _how_ the rule applies or fails to apply to the facts.
- **Signal Weight:** A strong essay constantly evaluates the strength of different arguments, rules, and issues.
- **The Goal:** Provide a well-reasoned, definitive prediction of the most probable outcome.

# Inspiration

- **The Opportunity:** Daring, unique, or unconventional insights—like explaining why a doctrine is wrong or offering a completely novel analysis—are the raw material for an **A+ grade**.
- **The Rule of Prudence:** Before sharing your revolutionary idea, always set out the **ordinary, conventional analysis** first.
- **The Reason:** Cover your bases in case the examiner (or a hypothetical court) doesn't view your "inspiration" as favorably as you do. Ensure the standard legal answer is fully addressed before taking a creative risk.

# The Seamless Web

- **Statutes Aren't Isolated:** In statutory courses, don't ignore factors just because they aren't explicitly tied to a code or section number. The code rarely covers every single angle of a problem.
- **Remove Course Blinders:** Do not let course titles restrict your thinking. Feel free to connect knowledge across different subjects (e.g., noting a contracts angle on a torts exam).
- **The Reality Check:** Keep a sense of proportion. If you are sitting a torts exam, the vast majority of your time and points will still come from analyzing the torts issues provided. Don't let outside subjects hijack your answer.

# Writing

- **Organize Visually:** Break up your answer into clear paragraphs that mirror the main points of your plan.
- **Be Professional:**
    - Use complete sentences and avoid abbreviating ordinary words.
    - Avoid slang (e.g., instead of writing "thrown out of court," use precise legal terms to show you understand the underlying distinctions).
- **If Handwriting:**
    - Write legibly so the examiner can focus on your analysis rather than deciphering your script.
    - If your handwriting is difficult to read, skip a line between each written line.
    - Leave a standard left-hand margin for the examiner's grading notes.

# Format

- **Choose Typing Over Handwriting:** Always opt to type your exam on a computer/word processor whenever it is allowed.
- **Speed Isn't a Barrier:** You do not need to be a fast, touch-typist to benefit, as law exams require analytical thinking rather than rapid copy-typing.
- **The Main Benefit:** Using a word processor inherently facilitates clarity, making it much easier to structure, edit, and present a readable answer.

# Avoid Jocularity

- **Take It Seriously:** Your examiner takes the exam questions seriously. Most attempts at humor or levity come across as strained, cloying, and a distraction from your analysis.
- **The High Correlation:** Efforts at humor are surprisingly highly correlated with poor exam-writing and analytical errors.
- **The Trap of Bizarre Names:** Examiners use unusual names for parties or places simply to help you keep track of them and avoid confusion. It is not an invitation to be funny—do not try to match or imitate this style in your answer.

# Panic

- **The Fallacy of Urgency:** Under stress, some students mistakenly believe that the importance of an exam justifies abandoning their analytical good sense, ruining their grammar, and adopting a messy "stream-of-consciousness" writing style.
- **The Reality:** Panic never helps. It does not show the examiner "how hard you are working"—it only makes your arguments less capable, less organized, and less persuasive.
- **The Antidote:** Steel yourself against the psychology of panic. Treat the exam as a structured analytical exercise that requires a calm, deliberate approach, regardless of the stakes.
