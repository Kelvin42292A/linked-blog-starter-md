### Page and paragraph references

Every case citation must include a specific reference to the page or paragraph where the cited statement or principle appears. Without this, the citation is incomplete and frustratingly difficult for a reader to verify, especially since High Court decisions can span up to 100 or even 385 pages.

### Key Requirements

- **For Page-Based Reports:** Include the page number on which the report of the case begins, followed by the specific page or pages you are quoting or referring to (known as a **pinpoint reference**).
- **For Medium Neutral Citations:** Include the equivalent of the first page (usually the judgment number). If you are quoting from a case or referring to a specific portion of a judgment, the citation must include a pinpoint reference to the **relevant paragraph** (and can also include the paragraph number).

### Examples Provided

**Standard Page-Based Pinpoint:**_Commonwealth v Western Australia_ (1999) 196 CLR 392, 398 _(Where `392` is the starting page, and `398` is the pinpoint page reference)._

**Medium Neutral Pinpoint:**_Re Culleton_ [2017] HCA 3 [38]–[43] _(Where `3` is the judgment number, and `[38]–[43]` are the specific paragraph pinpoint references)._

### Alternative citations and authorised reports

#### Preferred vs. Avoid

- **High Court**: Cite **CLR** - not ALR, ALJR, or FLR
- **Federal Court**: Cite **FCR** - not ALR or AAR
- **AAT (Tribunal):** Cite **ALD** - not AAR.

If you can't find the authorised citation, look it up using **AustLII (LawCite)**, **CaseBase**, or **FirstPoint**.

### Unreported decisions

If a case is **unreported** (not in a book) **AND** has **no medium neutral citation** (no digital court code), you must use specific personal and court details to identify it

**Required Elements:**

1. Case Name
2. Court/Tribunal
3. Judge’s Name
4. Exact Date of the decision
5. Court File Number (if available)

**Example**

_Rita McKindley v MJ Campbell Nominees Pty Ltd_ (**Supreme Court of the ACT**, **Miles CJ**, **5 December 1994**).

Because these old or rare cases lack standard tracking numbers, this detailed information is the only way a reader can track down the exact file from the court registry.

### Old English Cases

When citing an old English case found in the _English Reports_ (ER) or _Revised Reports_ (RR), you must list the **nominate report** first, followed by the English/Revised Reports citation.

### Formula & Example

**[Nominate Report]** ; **[English Reports]**

_Groenvelt v Burwell_ (1700) **1 Salk 144** ; **91 ER 134**

- **1 Salk 144:** The original, historical reporter where the case was first printed (Salkeld's Reports).
- **91 ER 134:** The massive, modern reprint collection (_English Reports_) where you can easily find that case today.

### Case Histories

To show a case's history, list the **highest court's decision first**, then work backward through the lower courts.

**Shorthand Connectors**

- **revd / revg:** Reversed / Reversing (overturned)
- **affd / affg:** Affirmed / Affirming (agreed)
- **vard / varg:** Varied / Varying (changed slightly)

**Example Breakdown**

MLC Assurance Co v Evatt [1971] AC 793, revg (1968) 122 CLR 556, affg (1967) 69 SR (NSW) 50 (CA) (sub nom Evatt v MLC Assurance Co [No 1]).

### Citing statutes

**_Name of Act Year_ (Jurisdiction) Pinpoint**

#### Quick Breakdown

- **The Name & Year:** Always written in _italics_ (e.g., _Export Control Act 2020_).
- **The Jurisdiction:** In plain text brackets (e.g., `(Cth)` for Federal, `(NSW)` for New South Wales, `(WA)` for Western Australia).
- **The Pinpoint:** The exact section you need (e.g., `s 638` or `s 22(2)`).

#### Examples

- _Export Control Act 2020_ (Cth)
- _Legal Profession Act 2008_ (WA) s 638
- _Work Health and Safety Act 2011_ (NSW) s 22(2)(e)(ii)

### Numbering status

Every statute gets an official, unique **number** based on the order it was passed by parliament in a specific year.

- **The Format:** It lists the Act's name, followed by its official number and year of enactment.
- **Example:** The official citation for the _Insolvency Law Reform Act 2016_ (Cth) is **No 11 of 2016**. This means it was the 11th Act passed by the Commonwealth parliament in 2016.
- **Northern Territory (NT) Exception:** In the NT, you can find this number printed in the "back note" at the very end of the substantive text.

### Short titles or names

Statutes are almost never referred to by their official "number." Instead, they are cited using their **short title** (the common name set out inside the Act itself).

- **General Practice:** The short title is usually found in Section 1 of the Act (e.g., _Immigration (Guardianship of Children) Act 1946_ (Cth)).
- **Victoria Exception:** For older Victorian Acts (passed before 1 September 1985), there was no separate section setting out a short title; the title is simply the heading at the start of the statute.
- **Northern Territory (NT) Exception:** Older NT practice omitted the year from the short title section of the Act. However, modern practice (for all NT Acts passed since 2006) now includes both the year of enactment and the short title.

Always **italicise the entire short title**, including the year (e.g., _Industrial Chemicals Act 2019_).

### Enacting jurisdiction

You must clearly state which parliament passed the law. If it isn't obvious from the text, place the jurisdiction in **plain text round brackets** right after the Act's name and year.

### Standard Australian Abbreviations

- **Cth:** Commonwealth (Federal)
- **NSW:** New South Wales
- **Vic:** Victoria
- **Qld:** Queensland
- **WA:** Western Australia
- **SA:** South Australia
- **Tas:** Tasmania
- **ACT:** Australian Capital Territory
- **NT:** Northern Territory

### Formatting Rule

Unlike the name and year of the Act, the jurisdiction inside the brackets is **never italicised**.

### Examples

- _Modern Slavery Act 2018_ **(Cth)**
- _Safety in Recreational Water Activities Act 2011_ **(Qld)**

### Section References

Always be clear whether you are talking about the **whole Act** or just a **specific part** of it.

#### Abbreviations

- **s** (Section - singular): Used for a single section or specific pinpoint (e.g., `s 20` or `s 20(3)(a)(ii)`).
- **ss** (Sections - plural): Used for multiple sections or ranges (e.g., `ss 33(1) and 37(2)` or `ss 46–52`).

#### Reading a Deep Pinpoint

Sections break down from biggest to smallest piece:

> **`s 20(3)(a)(ii)`**

- **s 20** = Section
- **(3)** = Subsection
- **(a)** = Paragraph
- **(ii)** = Sub-paragraph

_(Note: If you are already looking at a section, you can just say: "sub-paras (ii) and (iii) of s 20(3)(a)")._

### Citing British and Imperial statutes

When citing British statutes for work submitted in Australia, **follow the Australian (AGLC) style**: use the short title and italicise the entire name and year.

#### Key Formatting Rules (By Era)

- **Post-1963 Statutes:** Use **(UK)** in plain text brackets after the year.
    - _Example:_ There are no direct examples for post-1963 in the text, but it follows the standard pattern.
- **Pre-1963 Statutes:** If the law was passed by the British Parliament for an imperial territory, use **(Imp)** instead of (UK).
    - _Examples:_ _Statute of Westminster 1931_ **(Imp)**
    - _Colonial Laws Validity Act 1865_ **(Imp)**

#### The Imperial Exception (Traditional Citation)

The official, historical way to cite very old British statutes uses the **regnal year** (the year of the King or Queen's reign) and the **chapter number**.

- _Example:_ **4 Geo IV, c 96**
- _Meaning:_ The 96th Chapter (`c 96`) of the statute volume passed during the 4th year of the reign of King George IV (`4 Geo IV`).

### Constitutions

Constitutions are cited just like regular statutes: **Italicised Title** + **(Plain Jurisdiction)**.

- **Federal:** _**Commonwealth Constitution**_ (or simply _**The Constitution**_).
- **States:** Cited like standard state laws (e.g., _Constitution Act 1902_ (NSW)).
- **Territories:** Both the ACT and NT "constitutions" are actually Federal Acts, so they use **(Cth)**.
    - _Example:_ _Northern Territory (Self-Government) Act 1978_ (Cth).

### Bills and explanatory memoranda/notes

Before a law is passed, it is a **Bill**, and its sections are called **clauses**. Unlike Acts, Bills are written in **plain text (no italics)**.

**Bills:** Title in plain text + word "Bill" + Jurisdiction.

- _Example:_ Liability for Climate Change Damage (Make the Polluters Pay) Bill 2020 (Cth)

**Explanatory Documents:** Write the document type in plain text right before the Bill's name.

- _Example:_ Explanatory Note, Drug Court Legislation Amendment Bill 2014 (NSW)

### Regulations and other delegated legislation

Regulations use the exact same format as Acts: **_Italic Title and Year_ (Plain Jurisdiction)**.

#### Examples

- _Offshore Minerals Regulations 2010_ (WA)
- _Tobacco Plain Packaging Regulations 2011_ (Cth)

### Citing books

#### 1. Standard Books (Monographs)

**Formula:**

	Author, _Title_ (Publisher, Edition, Year) Pinpoint.

**Example:**

	JD Heydon, _Meagher, Gummow & Lehane’s Equity_ (LexisNexis, 5th ed, 2014) 129.

#### 2. Book Chapters (Edited Collections)

**Formula:**

	Chapter Author, 'Chapter Title' in Editor (ed), _Book Title_ (Publisher, Year) Start Page, Pinpoint Page.

**Example:**

	Robin Cooke, 'Tort Illusions' in Paul Finn (ed), _Essays on Torts_ (LawBook, 1989) 71, 74.

#### 3. Legal Encyclopedias

**Formula:**

	Publisher, _Title_, vol [No] (at [Date]) [Title No] [Chapter Name] [Paragraph].

**Example:**

	LexisNexis, _Halsbury’s Laws of Australia_, vol 1(1) (at 1 November 2002) 5 Aboriginals and Torres Strait Islanders [5–3760].

#### 4. Looseleaf Services (Binder Services)

**Formula:**

	Publisher, _Title_, vol [No] (at [Date/Service No]) [Chapter Name] [Paragraph].

**Example:**

	CCH, _Australian Federal Tax Reporter_, vol 1 (at 1149-4-2004) Exempt Income [9–870].

### Citing legal journals

**Formula:**

	Author, 'Article Title' (Year) Volume(Issue) _Full Journal Title_ Start Page, Pinpoint.

**Example:**

	Nick James, 'How Dare You Tell Me How to Teach!': Resistance to Educationalism within Australian Law Schools' (2013) 36(3) _University of New South Wales Law Journal_ 799, 802.

### Citing newspapers, magazines and non-legal periodicals

**Formula (With Author):**

	Author, 'Article Title', _Publication Name_ (City of Publication, Full Date) Pinpoint.

**Example (Newspaper):**

	Chris Merritt, 'Code for a Simpler, Better Contract Law is Presented', _The Australian_ (Sydney, 14 March 2014) 28.

**Formula (No Author / Editorial):**

	Editorial: 'Article Title', _Publication Name_ (City of Publication, Full Date) Pinpoint.

**Example (Editorial):**

	Editorial: 'The Lessons from Losing Flight MH370', _The Sydney Morning Herald_ (Sydney, 28 March 2014) 14.

**Example (Magazine):**

	Peter Smith, 'The Deplorable Victims of Free Trade', _Quadrant_, April 2017, 32.

### Citing material from the internet

**Formula:**

	Author/Authoring Body, 'Title of Web Page' (Year) _Name of Website_ <URL>.

**Example:**

	Liz Curran, 'Making Connections: The Benefits of Working Holistically to Resolve People's Legal Problems' [2005] _Murdoch University Electronic Journal of Law_ 5 [http://www.austlii.edu.au/au/journals/MurUEJL/2005/5.html](http://www.austlii.edu.au/au/journals/MurUEJL/2005/5.html).

### Citing parliamentary and government material

**Formula:**

	Jurisdiction, _Parliamentary Debates_, Chamber, Full Date, Page (Speaker Name).

**Example:**

	Commonwealth, _Parliamentary Debates_, Senate, 12 December 2013, 1575 (Senator Seselja).

#### Government Reports

**Formula:**

	Authoring Body, _Title of Report_ (Report/Inquiry No, Month Year) Pinpoint.

**Example:**

	Productivity Commission, _Migrant Intake into Australia_ (Inquiry Report No 77, 13 April 2016).

### Citing foreign material

**The Core Rule:**

	Try to fit the foreign source into standard Australian (AGLC) formatting rules first.

**The Exception Rule:**

If AGLC doesn't cover it, use the local country's official style guide:

- **US:** _The Bluebook_
- **UK:** _OSCOLA_
- **Canada:** _McGill Guide_

# Study and Exam Skills

**The Mindset:**

Thinking and legal exam-taking are skills that require active practice, not just passive reading.

**Key Skills to Develop:**

- **Organization:** Time management, active class participation, and building summaries early.
- **Analysis:** Spotting issues in fact scenarios and applying rules objectively.
- **Reflection:** Looking at the "bigger picture" of how laws operate in society.

### What are study skills?

**Definition:**

The practical, academic, and personal skills used to manage your learning, manage uncertainty, and excel at tertiary study.

**Core Academic Skills:**

- **Time Management:** Planning out your study schedules, reading workloads, and exam prep early.
- **Active Engagement:** Taking precise notes, listening for key themes, and participating in tutorials.
- **Critical Thinking:** Moving beyond memorization to understand the policy, reasoning, and societal impact behind a law.

### Organizing Study Time

- **Use a Study Plan:** Create a functional tool at the start of the semester to map out assessment deadlines and break down dense weekly readings.
- **Sustainability over Burnout:** Ensure your plan is flexible and factors in non-study activities like work, exercise, and social life.
- **The Limit of Study:** Discipline is key, but unreasonably extending your study hours without proper rest drops your efficiency and is counterproductive.

### Reading efficiently

**The Core Rule:**

Law requires reading a massive volume of material. Success depends on reading strategically and actively, not just trying to memorize every page.

**The 4 Pillars of Efficient Reading:**

- **Skim-Read First:** Use headings, intros, and conclusions to map out the text and identify key points before reading in detail.
- **Target the Relevance:** Focus your energy on _required readings_ for weekly classes; save _recommended readings_ for deep-dive research or assignments.
- **Stay Active:** Highlight key terms, ask questions as you go, and take structured notes to keep from slipping into passive reading.
- **Adapt Your Habits:** Figure out your workflow—whether you learn best by doing deep readings _before_ class to build a foundation, or _after_ class once you have context.
