# # Chapter 2: The Origins & Development of the Australian Legal System (Simple Summary)

## **1. The Birth of Common Law & Equity (England)**

- **1066 Norman Conquest:** William the Conqueror consolidated royal power. His successors sent traveling royal judges (_Justices in Eyre_) around England, merging diverse local customs into a single **"Common Law"** applied uniformly to all English subjects [2].
- **The Rigid Writ System:** To bring a lawsuit in common law courts, you had to purchase a specific written command (_writ_) from the King's Chancellor [3]. If your grievance did not perfectly fit into an existing procedural writ "pocket," you had no legal remedy [3].
- **The Rise of Equity:** Because common law courts became too rigid and procedural, litigants appealed directly to the King's Chancellor for justice [4]. This created **Equity**—a secondary body of law based on "good conscience" and flexible maxims (e.g., _"he who comes to equity must come with clean hands"_) [4].
- **The Merger:** Common law and equity were separate systems until they were combined under the **Judicature Acts of 1873–1875** [4]. If common law and equity conflict, **equity always prevails** [4].

---

## **2. Foundations of Constitutionalism (The Crown vs. Parliament)**

- **Magna Carta (1215):** King John was forced to sign this "Great Charter," establishing the revolutionary constitutional principle that **the King is not above the law** and securing basic rights like trial by peers and freedom from arbitrary taxation [5].
- **The Glorious Revolution & Bill of Rights (1689):** A massive power struggle ended with the Crown being subordinated to Parliament [6]. The _Bill of Rights 1689_ established **Parliamentary Supremacy**—the Crown could no longer suspend laws, levy taxes, or maintain a standing army without Parliament's consent [6].
- **Act of Settlement (1701):** Secured **Judicial Independence** by granting judges security of tenure ("during good behavior" rather than serving at the whim of the King) [6].
- **The Separation of Powers:** To prevent tyranny, government power is divided into three independent branches: the **Legislature** (makes law), the **Executive** (enforces law), and the **Judiciary** (interprets law) [1, 6].
- **The Rule of Law:** Dicey’s classic formulation demands that state power must be constrained by law, that laws must be public and clear, and that all people—including government officials—are equal before the law [6].

---

## **3. The Colonization of Australia (The Penal Shift)**

- **A Penal Colony:** The Australian legal system originated in 1788 as a military penal colony of the British Empire, with absolute executive power initially concentrated in the Governor [7].
- **The Doctrine of Terra Nullius:** Meaning "land belonging to no one" [7]. The British treated Australia as a "settled" (practically unoccupied) colony rather than a conquered territory, falsely asserting that Indigenous peoples had no recognized political or legal systems [7].
- **Reception of English Law:** Under English common law, British settlers instantly inherited the laws of England, but **only so far as those laws were suitable to the conditions of the infant colony** [7].

---

## **4. Universal Rules of Legal History**

- **The Precedent Standard (_Stare Decisis_):** Courts are strictly bound to follow the legal principles set by higher courts in earlier cases to ensure predictability and consistency [2].
- **No Remedy, No Right:** In early common law, your substantive legal rights were completely dependent on whether you could find a correct, pre-existing procedural _writ_ [3].
- **Equity Follows, But Corrects:** Equity exists to supplement, not destroy, the common law. Where they clash, equity's flexible rules of conscience prevail [4].
