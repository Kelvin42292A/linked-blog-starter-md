## Judicial Independence and the Rule of Law

> Source study guide based on: Justice Bruce Debelle, Judicial independence and the rule of law (2001) 75 ALJ 556 [5].

### 1. The Core Relationship: Rule of Law & The Judiciary

Interdependence: The rule of law cannot survive without an independent judiciary and an independent legal profession [5, 17].

The Last Bastion: The judiciary serves as the final shield between the ordinary citizen and arbitrary state power or powerful corporate entities [17]. If citizens cannot seek redress from an independent judge free from executive or corporate pressure, they have no safeguard against bureaucratic acts affecting their daily lives [17].

Restraining Power: The rule of law is not an enemy of liberty, but its partner; its purpose is to restrain and civilise power, safeguarding relationships and keeping the peace [13, 14].

### 2. Dicey’s Three Pillars of the Rule of Law

Justice Debelle adopts Professor A.V. Dicey's three-part definition of the rule of law [11]:

Supremacy of Law (No Arbitrary Power):

Government cannot exercise arbitrary power [11].

No individual can be punished except for a distinct breach of the ordinary law, established in the ordinary legal manner before the ordinary courts [11].

Contrast (Voltaire's Experience): In 18th-century France (lacking the rule of law), the writer Voltaire was sent to the Bastille twice without trial, once for a poem he did not write, and was thrashed by noblemen without legal redress [11].

Equality Before the Law:

No person is above the law [12].

Every individual, regardless of rank or condition (including the Prime Minister and the Governor-General), is subject to the ordinary law and the jurisdiction of ordinary courts [12].

Rights Stem from Court Decisions:

Constitutional and civil rights are the direct result of actual decisions made and enforced by courts in individual cases, rather than being handed down as an abstract gift from a governing authority [13].

### 3. The Historical Evolution of Judicial Independence

The modern independent judiciary evolved over a 900-year process of political and constitutional struggle [9, 18]:

#### Early Royal Justice (12th to 16th Century)

servants of the Crown: Early judges evolved from royal advisors (e.g., the Court of Common Pleas established by Henry II in 1178) [18, 19]. They were treated as standard civil servants whom the monarch could hire and fire at will [19, 23].

Irregular Pay & Bribery: Judicial salaries were paid irregularly [20]. For example, Chief Justice Roger of Brazabon was paid only four times in twelve years between 1294 and 1306 [20]. Consequently, judges depended on pensions from wealthy abbeys and were highly susceptible to bribery [20, 21].

Violent Royal Purges:

Under Edward I in 1289, an inquiry into corruption led to the sacking of most judges of the King’s Bench and Common Pleas [21].

In 1350, Chief Justice William de Thorpe was dismissed and sentenced to death for taking bribes [22].

In 1387, Richard II asked his judges to declare a parliamentary commission invalid [24]. When the parliament regained power, the judges who gave that advice were impeached and sentenced to death; Chief Justice Tresillian was executed [24].

#### The Stuart Struggles & Coke’s Resistance (17th Century)

Durante Bene Placito ("During Pleasure"): At the start of the 17th century, judges held office strictly at the "good pleasure" of the Crown [27].

Sir Edward Coke: Appointed Chief Justice of Common Pleas (1606) and King's Bench (1613), Coke became the champion of common law supremacy over royal prerogative [28]. Reaffirming Bracton's principle that "the King is subject to God and the law," Coke ruled that the King could not alter common law or create new offenses by proclamation [25, 28]. James I dismissed Coke in 1616 as a warning to all other judges [28].

Aggressive Royal Purges: To ensure favorable judgments, Stuart kings aggressively purged the bench [30]. Charles II sacked 11 judges in his final 11 years (1674–1685) [30]. James II sacked 12 judges in just a three-year reign [30].

#### The Landmark Remedy: The Act of Settlement (1701)

Following the Glorious Revolution of 1688, the Act of Settlement 1701 (s 3) permanently established statutory protections for judicial independence [31]:

Security of Tenure: Judges hold office during good behaviour (quamdiu se bene gesserint) rather than royal pleasure [31].

Bicameral Address for Removal: Judges can only be removed from office upon a formal address passed by both Houses of Parliament [31].

Established Salaries: Judicial salaries must be legally ascertained and established [31].

### 4. The Elements of Judicial Independence Today

To preserve the rule of law, modern democracies maintain three strict constitutional safeguards for judges [32, 33]:

Security of Tenure: Judges must be appointed for a defined period, preferably up to a statutory retirement age (70 years for the High Court and Federal Courts under s 72 of the Constitution; 72 years for Territory and most State courts) [33].

Financial Security: Judges must be paid an adequate salary secured out of public revenue to protect them from political or financial manipulation [33, 37].

Judicial Immunity: Judges must remain immune from civil lawsuits regarding actions performed and decisions made while presiding on the bench [33].

### 5. Safeguarding Public Confidence & Accountability

Because the judiciary has no army or police force, its authority relies entirely on public confidence and credibility [34, 41].

#### How Judges Maintain Credibility:

Decisions According to Law: Credibility is maintained by deciding cases strictly according to established legal principles, not by seeking popular approval or public favor [34, 36].

No Representative Mandates: Judges do not have constituencies or political agendas [35]. The courts should not be "representative" of interest groups; rather, they require individuals of the highest technical competence to adjudicate disputes fairly [35].

Open Justice (Accountability): Court proceedings must be transparent [38]. Litigation is conducted in public, judges are legally obligated to publish reasons for their decisions, and their rulings are open to public, academic, and appellate scrutiny [38].

#### Proactive Public Education (Combating Apathy):

Justice Debelle argues that judicial reticence should not be a muzzle [46]. To combat public apathy (which threatens democracy), the judiciary must actively educate the community about civics, constitutional history, and the rule of law [40, 42, 45].

Courts should utilize educational articles, press releases, websites, and community forums to explain how they work and why an independent bench is the citizen's ultimate safeguard [43, 45].

### 6. The Independence of the Legal Profession

Unpopular Clients & Causes: An independent legal profession must be free to advise and represent any client—regardless of how unpopular their cause is—without fearing personal, political, or economic recrimination [47, 48].

The Cab-Rank Rule: Professional conduct rules, such as the cab-rank rule and restrictions on practicing in partnership, are vital to ensuring that highly unpopular individuals still receive competent legal representation, reinforcing the rule of law [48].
