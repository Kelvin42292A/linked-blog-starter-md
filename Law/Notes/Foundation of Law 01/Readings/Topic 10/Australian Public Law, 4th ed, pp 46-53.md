## **Part 1: The Foundations of English Common Law**

### **1. Roots of English Law & Feudalism (1066)**

- **The Norman Conquest (1066):** William the Conqueror consolidated royal power without immediately abolishing Anglo-Saxon local customs.
- **Feudalism:** A highly hierarchical system where the **Crown theoretically possessed all land**. Lords held land from the King in exchange for military service and allegiance, forming the base of modern property law.
- **Traveling Justices & The Curia Regis:**
    - The King resolved disputes via the _Curia Regis_ (King's Advisory Court).
    - Traveling judges (**Justices in Eyre**) were sent to local areas to resolve disputes impartially.
    - Over time, their decisions merged into a single, uniform body of law across the realm: the **"Common Law"**.

### **2. Doctrine of Precedent (_Stare Decisis_)**

- **Definition:** "To stand by things decided."
- **Rule:** Royal courts are strictly obliged to follow principles established by prior decisions to ensure the law remains **predictable, consistent, and fair**.

### **3. The Standard Court Structure**

As the court system formalized, three distinct common law courts emerged:

1. **Common Pleas:** Handled civil disputes between private subjects.
2. **King's Bench:** Heard cases involving the Crown or direct breaches of the King's peace (criminal matters).
3. **Court of Exchequer:** Managed royal revenues and taxation.

## **Part 2: Essential Constitutional Milestones**

### **1. Limits on Monarchy & Parliamentary Supremacy**

- **The Bill of Rights 1689:**
    - Established **Parliamentary Supremacy** over the Crown.
    - Stripped the King of the prerogative power to suspend or dispense with laws.
    - Guaranteed parliamentary control over **taxation and public expenditure**.
- **The Act of Settlement 1701:**
    - Established **Judicial Independence**.
    - Granted judges security of tenure (_"during good behavior"_) rather than allowing the monarch to dismiss them at will (_"at the King's pleasure"_).

### **2. Crucial Common Law Case Law**

- **Dr Bonham's Case (1610):** Sir Edward Coke CJ famously declared that the common law could control and declare void an Act of Parliament if it was "against common right and reason" (the early precursor to judicial review).
- **Case of Proclamations (1611):** Coke CJ ruled that **the King cannot create new offences by proclamation** or change the common law; lawmaking power belongs to Parliament.
- **Entick v Carrington (1765):** Established the core Rule of Law principle that **any state/executive action must be explicitly authorized by law**. If the government cannot point to a specific law authorizing an intrusion, they are committing a trespass.

## **Part 3: The Foundations of the Australian Legal System**

### **1. Transportation and Settlement (1788)**

- **Penal Origins:** The Australian legal system began in 1788 as a British military penal colony under Governor Arthur Phillip, following the _Transportation Act 1717_ and the 1787 Orders in Council.
- **Reception of English Law:** English common law and statutes were received instantly in New South Wales, but **only so far as they were suitable and applicable** to the conditions of the young colony.

### **2. The Legal Fiction of _Terra Nullius_**

- **Acquisition of Territory:** Under 18th-century international law, there were three ways to acquire land: conquest, cession, or occupation (settlement).
- **Terra Nullius ("Land Belonging to No One"):** The British treated Australia as a "settled" colony by falsely asserting that the Indigenous population did not possess an organized political or legal system (_Cooper v Stuart_).
- **The Overturning:** This foundational legal fiction was eventually struck down by the High Court of Australia in **_Mabo v Queensland (No 2)_ (1992)**, which recognized Native Title.

### **3. Responsible Government & Conventions**

- **Responsible Government:** The executive branch (ministers) must be drawn from Parliament and remain directly accountable to it, maintaining the confidence of the lower house.
- **Constitutional Conventions:** Unwritten, legally unenforceable political customs (e.g., cabinet collective responsibility) that are strictly followed to ensure the democratic operation of the state.
- **Colonial Independence:** Australia transitioned from a military dictatorship to a representative democracy, starting with the establishment of the **Legislative Council under the _New South Wales Act 1823_**.
